In Which Passepartout Is Convinced He Has Found His Ideal at Last

"Well, upon my word," Passepartout said to himself, a little dazed at first, "I've seen figures over at Madame Tussaud's every bit as lively as my new master!"

It should be explained here that Madame Tussaud's "figures" are those wax models, much visited in London, that truly lack nothing but the power of speech.

During the few moments he had glimpsed Phileas Fogg, Passepartout had made a quick but careful study of his future master. He was a man of perhaps forty, with a noble, handsome face, tall, not the worse for a slight fullness of figure, fair of hair and side-whiskers, his forehead smooth and unlined at the temples, his complexion rather pale than ruddy, his teeth magnificent. He seemed to possess in the highest degree what physiognomists call "repose in action" — that quality common to all who make more work than noise. Calm, phlegmatic, clear-eyed, his eyelids never flickering, he was the finished type of those cold-blooded Englishmen one meets fairly often in the United Kingdom, whose faintly academic bearing Angelica Kauffmann has so wonderfully caught with her brush. Seen in the various acts of his existence, this gentleman gave the impression of a being perfectly balanced in all his parts, exactly weighted, as flawless as a chronometer by Leroy or by Earnshaw. And indeed, Phileas Fogg was precision personified, which showed plainly in "the expression of his feet and hands," for in man, as in animals, the very limbs are organs that express the passions.

Phileas Fogg was one of those mathematically exact men who, never in a hurry and always ready, are sparing of their steps and their movements. He never took one stride too many, always going by the shortest way. He never wasted a glance at the ceiling. He allowed himself not a single unnecessary gesture. No one had ever seen him stirred or troubled. He was the least hurried man in the world, yet he always arrived on time. Still, one can understand that he lived alone and, so to speak, outside all social contact. He knew that in life one must allow for friction, and since friction slows a man down, he rubbed up against no one.

As for Jean, called Passepartout, a true Parisian of Paris, in the five years he had lived in England, working in London as a valet, he had searched in vain for a master he could grow attached to.

Passepartout was none of those Frontins or Mascarilles — shoulders back, nose in the air, cocksure gaze, dry eye — who are nothing but impudent rogues. No. Passepartout was a good fellow, with a pleasant face, lips a little full and always ready to taste something or kiss somebody, a gentle and obliging soul, with one of those fine round heads you like to see on the shoulders of a friend. He had blue eyes, a lively color, a face plump enough that he could see his own cheekbones, a broad chest, a strong build, powerful muscles, and a Herculean strength that the exercises of his youth had developed to admiration. His brown hair was a bit unruly. If the sculptors of antiquity knew eighteen ways to arrange Minerva's hair, Passepartout knew only one for arranging his own: three passes of the comb, and he was done.

Whether this fellow's outgoing temperament would agree with that of Phileas Fogg — the most elementary prudence forbids one to say. Would Passepartout prove the thoroughly punctual servant his master required? Only time and wear would tell. After a fairly footloose youth, as we know, he now longed for peace. Having heard English orderliness praised, and the proverbial coolness of English gentlemen, he had come to seek his fortune in England. But so far fate had served him badly. He had been unable to put down roots anywhere. He had tried ten households. In every one of them the master was whimsical, erratic, a chaser after adventures or after distant countries — which no longer suited Passepartout. His last master, young Lord Longsferry, Member of Parliament, after spending his nights in the oyster-rooms of the Haymarket, was carried home all too often on the shoulders of the police. Passepartout, wanting above all to be able to respect his master, ventured a few respectful remarks that were badly received, and so he quit. In the meantime he learned that Phileas Fogg, Esq., was looking for a servant. He made inquiries about the gentleman. A man whose life was so regular, who never slept away from home, who never traveled, who was never absent, not even for a day, could only suit him. He presented himself, and was taken on under the circumstances already related.

Passepartout — half past eleven having struck — thus found himself alone in the house on Savile Row. He set about inspecting it at once. He went over it from cellar to attic. This clean, orderly, austere, puritanical house, so well arranged for service, pleased him. It struck him as a fine snail's shell — but a shell lit and heated by gas, for carbureted hydrogen supplied all its needs for light and warmth. On the second floor Passepartout had no trouble finding the room meant for him. It suited him. Electric bells and speaking-tubes put it in communication with the rooms on the mezzanine and the first floor. On the mantel an electric clock kept time with the clock in Phileas Fogg's bedroom, and the two struck the same instant, the same second.

"This suits me, this suits me!" said Passepartout to himself.

He noticed, too, in his room, a notice posted above the clock. It was the program of the daily service. It listed — from eight in the morning, the regulation hour at which Phileas Fogg rose, to half past eleven, the hour at which he left the house to go and lunch at the Reform Club — every detail of the service: tea and toast at eight twenty-three, shaving water at nine thirty-seven, hairdressing at twenty to ten, and so on. Then, from half past eleven in the morning to midnight — the hour at which the methodical gentleman went to bed — everything was noted, foreseen, regulated. Passepartout made it his delight to study this program and to fix each of its several articles in his mind.

As for the gentleman's wardrobe, it was very well stocked and admirably thought out. Each pair of trousers, coat, or waistcoat bore a serial number, entered in a register that recorded when it came and went, marking the date on which, according to the season, each garment was to be worn in its turn. The same system governed the footwear.

In short, this house on Savile Row — which in the days of the illustrious but dissipated Sheridan must have been the very temple of disorder — was now comfortably furnished, bespeaking easy means. No library, no books, which would have been of no use to Mr. Fogg, since the Reform Club put two libraries at his disposal, one devoted to literature, the other to law and politics. In the bedroom stood a safe of moderate size, built to defend its contents from fire as well as theft. There was not a weapon in the house, not a single implement of hunting or war. Everything about it pointed to the most peaceable habits.

After examining the place in detail, Passepartout rubbed his hands, his broad face lit up, and he repeated cheerfully:

"This suits me! This is just the thing for me! We'll get along perfectly, Mr. Fogg and I! A stay-at-home, regular sort of man! A real machine! Well, I've no objection to serving a machine!"
