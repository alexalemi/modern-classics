Which Affords Passepartout the Chance to Make an Atrocious, but Perhaps Original, Pun

Phileas Fogg was in prison. They had locked him up in the station house of the Custom House, the Liverpool customs office, and there he was to spend the night while he awaited his transfer to London.

At the moment of the arrest, Passepartout had tried to fling himself at the detective. Policemen held him back. Aouda, horrified by the sheer brutality of the thing and knowing nothing of its cause, could make no sense of it. Passepartout explained the situation to her. Mr. Fogg — that honest, courageous gentleman to whom she owed her life — had been arrested as a thief. The young woman protested against so monstrous a charge; her heart rose up in indignation, and tears streamed from her eyes when she saw that there was nothing she could do, nothing she could even attempt, to save her savior.

As for Fix, he had arrested the gentleman because his duty commanded him to make the arrest, guilty or not. The courts would decide.

And then a thought came to Passepartout — that terrible thought that he, and no one else, was the cause of the whole disaster. For why had he hidden this business from Mr. Fogg? When Fix had revealed both his standing as a police inspector and the mission he had been charged with, why had Passepartout taken it upon himself not to warn his master? Mr. Fogg, forewarned, would surely have given Fix proof of his innocence; he would have shown him his mistake; at the very least, he would not have carried this wretched agent along at his own expense and on his own heels — an agent whose first act had been to arrest him the moment he set foot on the soil of the United Kingdom. Brooding over his blunders, his recklessness, the poor fellow was seized with unbearable remorse. He wept; he was pitiful to see. He could have knocked his own head against the wall.

He and Aouda had stayed, in spite of the cold, under the portico of the customs house. Neither of them would leave the spot. They wanted to see Mr. Fogg one more time.

As for that gentleman, he was well and truly ruined — and ruined at the very moment he was about to reach his goal. This arrest destroyed him beyond recovery. Having reached Liverpool at eleven-forty in the morning on the 21st of December, he had until eight forty-five that evening to present himself at the Reform Club — nine hours and fifteen minutes — and he needed only six of them to reach London.

At that moment, anyone who had entered the station house would have found Mr. Fogg sitting motionless on a wooden bench, without anger, imperturbable. Resigned, you could not have called him; but even this last blow had failed to move him, at least on the surface. Had there formed within him one of those secret rages, all the more terrible for being contained, that break out only at the last instant with an irresistible force? No one could say. But Phileas Fogg was there, calm, waiting — for what? Did he still keep some hope? Did he still believe in success, now that the prison door had closed on him?

Whatever the case, Mr. Fogg had carefully laid his watch on a table and sat watching the hands go round. Not a word escaped his lips, but his gaze had a strange fixity to it.

At any rate, the situation was a terrible one, and to anyone who could not read that conscience, it came down to this:

An honest man, Phileas Fogg was ruined.

A dishonest man, he was caught.

Did the thought of escape occur to him then? Did he think of looking to see whether this station house offered any practicable way out? Did he think of running? One is tempted to believe he did, for at a certain moment he made a circuit of the room. But the door was solidly locked and the window fitted with iron bars. So he came and sat down again, and he drew from his pocketbook the itinerary of the journey. On the line that bore the words:

"December 21st, Saturday, Liverpool," he added:

"80th day, 11:40 in the morning," and he waited.

One o'clock struck on the Custom House clock. Mr. Fogg noted that his watch was two minutes fast against it.

Two o'clock! Supposing he boarded an express at this very moment, he could still reach London and the Reform Club before eight forty-five that evening. His brow creased faintly.

At thirty-three minutes past two, a noise rang out beyond the door, a clatter of doors flung open. He heard Passepartout's voice; he heard Fix's voice.

Phileas Fogg's gaze flashed for an instant.

The door of the station house opened, and he saw Aouda, Passepartout, and Fix rush toward him.

Fix was out of breath, his hair in wild disorder. He could not speak.

"Sir," he stammered, "sir — forgive me — a wretched resemblance — the thief was arrested three days ago — you — are free!"

Phileas Fogg was free! He walked up to the detective. He looked him full in the face and, making the one and only swift movement he had ever made or would ever make in his life, he drew both arms back and then, with the precision of an automaton, struck the unlucky inspector with both fists.

"Well struck!" cried Passepartout, who, allowing himself an atrocious pun quite worthy of a Frenchman, added: "By Jove, there's what you'd call a fine piece of English handiwork!"

Fix, knocked flat, did not utter a word. He had gotten no more than he deserved. But at once Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and Passepartout left the customs house. They threw themselves into a carriage and in a few minutes reached the Liverpool railway station.

Phileas Fogg asked whether there was an express ready to leave for London.

It was two-forty. The express had departed thirty-five minutes before.

Phileas Fogg then ordered a special train.

There were several high-speed locomotives under steam; but, given the demands of the service, the special could not leave the station before three o'clock.

At three o'clock, having said a few words to the engine driver about a certain bonus to be earned, Phileas Fogg was racing off toward London in the company of the young woman and his faithful servant.

The distance that separates Liverpool from London had to be covered in five and a half hours — an entirely feasible thing, when the line is clear the whole way. But there were forced delays, and when the gentleman reached the station, ten minutes to nine was striking on every clock in London.

Phileas Fogg, having completed this journey around the world, had arrived five minutes late!

He had lost.
