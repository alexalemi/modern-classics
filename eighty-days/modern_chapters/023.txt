During Which the Crossing of the Pacific Ocean Is Accomplished

What had happened off Shanghai is easy enough to understand. The signals flown by the Tankadère had been spotted from the Yokohama steamer. Seeing a flag at half-mast, the captain had borne down on the little schooner. A few minutes later Phileas Fogg, settling his passage at the price agreed upon, was tucking five hundred and fifty pounds into the pocket of the skipper, John Bunsby. Then the honorable gentleman, Aouda, and Fix had climbed aboard the steamer, which set course at once for Nagasaki and Yokohama.

Arriving that very morning, the 14th of November, right on schedule, Phileas Fogg had left Fix to his own business and gone straight aboard the Carnatic, where he learned — to Aouda's great joy, and perhaps to his own, though he let nothing of it show — that the Frenchman Passepartout had in fact reached Yokohama the day before.

Phileas Fogg, who was to leave again that same evening for San Francisco, set out immediately in search of his servant. He applied, but in vain, to the French and English consular agents, and after combing the streets of Yokohama to no purpose, he had given up hope of finding Passepartout when chance — or perhaps some kind of presentiment — led him into the booth of the honorable Batulcar. He would certainly never have recognized his servant under that outlandish herald's costume; but Passepartout, upside down as he was, caught sight of his master up in the gallery. He couldn't stop his nose from twitching. And there went the balance of the pyramid, and everything that followed.

All this Passepartout learned from Aouda's own lips, and she told him too how the crossing from Hong Kong to Yokohama had been made, in the company of one Mr. Fix, aboard the schooner Tankadère.

At the name of Fix, Passepartout didn't so much as blink. He judged the moment had not yet come to tell his master what had passed between the police inspector and himself. And so, in the account he gave of his own adventures, he blamed and excused himself only for having been caught out by an opium binge in a Yokohama smoking den.

Mr. Fogg listened coldly to this tale, without a word; then he opened his servant a line of credit large enough to fit him out in more suitable clothes aboard ship. And sure enough, before an hour had gone by, the honest fellow had cut off his nose and clipped his wings, and there was nothing left about him to recall the disciple of the god Tingou.

The steamer that ran between Yokohama and San Francisco belonged to the Pacific Mail Steam Company and was named the General Grant. She was a great paddle-wheel steamer of twenty-five hundred tons, well fitted out and very fast. An enormous walking-beam rose and fell above the deck; at one end it took the rod of a piston, and at the other that of a connecting-rod, which turned the straight-line motion into a circular one and applied it directly to the shaft of the wheels. The General Grant was rigged as a three-masted schooner and carried a broad spread of canvas that lent the steam a powerful hand. Making her twelve miles an hour, the steamer should take no more than twenty-one days to cross the Pacific. Phileas Fogg was therefore entitled to believe that, reaching San Francisco on the 2nd of December, he would be in New York on the 11th and in London on the 20th — beating that fatal date of December 21st by a few hours.

There were a good many passengers aboard the steamer: English, plenty of Americans, a whole tide of coolies emigrating to America, and a number of Indian Army officers spending their leave making the tour of the world.

Nothing happened at sea during the crossing. Held up on her broad wheels and steadied by her heavy canvas, the steamer rolled very little. The Pacific Ocean lived up to its name. Mr. Fogg was as calm and as sparing of words as ever. His young companion felt herself drawn to this man by bonds other than gratitude. That silent nature, so generous in the end, made a deeper impression on her than she knew, and it was almost without meaning to that she gave way to feelings the enigmatic Fogg seemed not to feel in the least.

What was more, Aouda took a prodigious interest in the gentleman's plans. She worried over the setbacks that might yet wreck the success of the journey. Often she talked with Passepartout, who was not incapable of reading between the lines of Aouda's heart. By now the good fellow had, where his master was concerned, the simple faith of a charcoal-burner; he never ran dry of praise for Phileas Fogg's honesty, his generosity, his devotion; then he set Aouda's mind at ease about the outcome of the trip, repeating that the hardest part was done, that they were out of those fantastical countries of China and Japan and heading back toward civilized lands, and that a train from San Francisco to New York and a steamer from New York to London would surely be enough to finish this impossible tour of the world within the appointed time.

Nine days after leaving Yokohama, Phileas Fogg had traveled exactly halfway round the globe.

For on the 23rd of November the General Grant crossed the one hundred and eightieth meridian — the one on which, in the southern hemisphere, the antipodes of London lie. Of the eighty days he had been allotted, Mr. Fogg had, it is true, used up fifty-two, leaving him only twenty-eight to spend. But it should be noted that if the gentleman was only halfway there "by the difference of the meridians," he had in reality covered more than two-thirds of the whole route. What forced detours, after all, from London to Aden, from Aden to Bombay, from Calcutta to Singapore, from Singapore to Yokohama! Following the fiftieth parallel — London's own — in a straight circle, the distance would have been only about twelve thousand miles, whereas Phileas Fogg, at the whim of his means of transport, was obliged to cover twenty-six thousand, of which he had done some seventeen thousand five hundred by this 23rd of November. But now the way was straight, and Fix was no longer there to pile up obstacles along it!

It happened, too, that on this 23rd of November Passepartout knew a great joy. It will be remembered that the stubborn fellow had insisted on keeping London time on his famous family watch, holding every other country's hours to be wrong. Well, that day, though he had never once put it forward or back, his watch agreed with the ship's chronometers.

That Passepartout crowed over it, one can well imagine. He'd have dearly liked to know what Fix might have said, had he been there.

"That scoundrel who fed me a heap of nonsense about meridians, about the sun, about the moon!" Passepartout kept saying. "Ha! Those people! If you listened to them, you'd keep fine time indeed! I was always sure that one day or another the sun would make up its mind to set itself by my watch!"

What Passepartout didn't know was this: if the face of his watch had been divided into twenty-four hours, like the Italian clocks, he'd have had no cause to crow at all, for the hands of his instrument, when it was nine in the morning aboard ship, would have pointed to nine in the evening — that is, the twenty-first hour past midnight — a difference exactly equal to the one between London and the one hundred and eightieth meridian.

But even if Fix had been able to explain this purely physical effect, Passepartout would no doubt have been incapable, if not of understanding it, at least of admitting it. And in any case, if by some impossibility the police inspector had shown up aboard just then, it's likely that Passepartout — quite rightly holding a grudge — would have taken up an altogether different subject with him, and in an altogether different manner.

Now, where was Fix at that moment?

Fix was, as it happened, aboard the General Grant.

For on reaching Yokohama, the agent had left Mr. Fogg, whom he counted on catching up with later in the day, and gone straight to the English consul. There he had at last found the warrant, which, chasing after him ever since Bombay, was already forty days old — the warrant that had been sent on to him from Hong Kong aboard the very Carnatic on which he'd been believed to be traveling. Imagine the detective's disappointment! The warrant was now useless! Mr. Fogg had left the English possessions! An extradition order would now be needed to arrest him!

"So be it!" Fix said to himself, once the first flush of anger had passed. "My warrant's no good here, but it will be in England. This scoundrel has every look of a man heading home, thinking he's shaken off the police. Good. I'll follow him all the way there. As for the money, God grant there's some left! But on travel, on rewards, on lawsuits, on fines, on an elephant, on expenses of every kind, my man has already dropped more than five thousand pounds along his road. After all, the Bank is rich!"

His mind made up, he had gone aboard the General Grant at once. He was on board when Mr. Fogg and Aouda arrived. To his extreme surprise, he recognized Passepartout in his herald's costume. He hid himself in his cabin without delay, to avoid an explanation that might spoil everything — and, thanks to the crowd of passengers, he fully expected to escape his enemy's notice, when that very day he found himself face to face with him on the forward deck.

Passepartout sprang at Fix's throat without another word, and, to the great delight of certain Americans who bet on him at once, he treated the wretched inspector to a magnificent thrashing that proved beyond doubt the high superiority of French boxing over English.

When Passepartout had finished, he felt calm, as though a weight had lifted. Fix picked himself up in fairly bad shape and, looking his opponent over, said coldly:

"Are you done?"

"Yes, for now."

"Then come and have a word with me."

"That I—"

"In your master's interest."

Passepartout, as though mastered by this coolness, followed the police inspector, and the two of them sat down at the front of the steamer.

"You've given me a hiding," said Fix. "Good. Now listen to me. Up to now I've been Mr. Fogg's adversary, but from here on I'm playing his hand."

"At last!" cried Passepartout. "You believe he's an honest man?"

"No," Fix answered coldly. "I believe he's a scoundrel. Quiet! Don't move, and let me speak. As long as Mr. Fogg was on English soil, it was in my interest to hold him there until a warrant came. I did everything toward it. I set the priests of Bombay on him, I got you drunk at Hong Kong, I split you off from your master, I made him miss the Yokohama steamer—"

Passepartout listened, fists clenched.

"Now," Fix went on, "Mr. Fogg seems to be heading back to England? So be it, I'll follow him. But from here out I'll put as much care and zeal into clearing the obstacles from his road as I've put until now into piling them up. You see, my game has changed, and it's changed because my interest demands it. I'll add that your interest runs the same as mine, for it's only in England that you'll learn whether you're in the service of a criminal or an honest man!"

Passepartout had listened to Fix very closely, and he was convinced that Fix spoke in complete good faith.

"Are we friends?" asked Fix.

"Friends, no," answered Passepartout. "Allies, yes, and on approval only, for at the first sign of treachery I'll wring your neck."

"Agreed," said the police inspector calmly.

Eleven days later, on the 3rd of December, the General Grant steamed into the bay of the Golden Gate and reached San Francisco.

Mr. Fogg had as yet neither gained nor lost a single day.
