In Which Phileas Fogg and His Companions Venture into the Forests of India, and What Comes of It

To shorten the distance, the guide kept to the right of the railway line still under construction. That line, thrown badly off course by the whimsical spurs of the Vindhia Mountains, did not follow the shortest road — the one Phileas Fogg had every reason to take. The Parsee, thoroughly at home on the country's roads and footpaths, claimed he could save a good twenty miles by cutting straight through the forest, and they took his word for it.

Phileas Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty, buried up to the neck in their howdahs, were shaken half to pieces by the elephant's stiff trot, which the mahout kept at a brisk pace. But they endured it with the most perfectly British calm, saying little, and scarcely able to see each other besides.

As for Passepartout, perched on the animal's back and taking every jolt and counter-jolt full force, he was careful — on his master's advice — not to let his tongue get between his teeth, or it would have been bitten clean through. The good fellow, flung now onto the elephant's neck, now back onto its rump, was doing gymnastics like a clown on a springboard. But he joked and laughed through his tumbling, and every so often he dug a lump of sugar out of his bag, which the clever Kiouni took with the tip of his trunk without once breaking his steady trot.

After two hours of this, the guide halted the elephant and gave him an hour's rest. The animal first slaked his thirst at a nearby pool, then set to devouring branches and shrubs. Sir Francis Cromarty made no complaint about the stop. He was worn out. Mr. Fogg looked as fresh as if he had just risen from his bed.

"The man is made of iron!" said the brigadier general, gazing at him in admiration.

"Wrought iron," said Passepartout, who was busy putting together a quick breakfast.

At noon the guide gave the signal to move on. The country soon took on a wild look. The great forests gave way to thickets of tamarind and dwarf palm, then to vast barren plains bristling with scrubby brush and strewn with great blocks of syenite. This whole stretch of upper Bundelkund, little traveled, is home to a fanatical population hardened in the most terrible practices of the Hindu religion. The English had never been able to establish any real authority over a territory still ruled by the sway of the rajahs, whom it would have been hard to reach in their unassailable retreats among the Vindhias.

Several times they caught sight of bands of fierce Indians, who made an angry gesture at the sight of the swift beast passing by. The Parsee steered clear of them whenever he could, judging them poor company to meet. Few animals showed themselves that day — barely a handful of monkeys, which fled with a thousand contortions and grimaces that Passepartout found highly entertaining.

One thought, among many others, kept nagging at him. What would Mr. Fogg do with the elephant once they reached the Allahabad station? Take him along? Impossible! The cost of transport added to the purchase price would make him a ruinously expensive animal. Sell him, then, or turn him loose? This admirable beast surely deserved some consideration. And if by chance Mr. Fogg were to make a present of him — to Passepartout, that is — the valet would be very much at a loss. The question would not stop turning over in his mind.

By eight in the evening the main ridge of the Vindhias had been crossed, and the travelers halted at the foot of the northern slope, in a ruined bungalow.

The distance covered that day was about twenty-five miles, and as many still remained before they would reach the Allahabad station.

The night was cold. Inside the bungalow the Parsee lit a fire of dry branches, whose warmth was much appreciated. Supper was made up of the provisions bought at Kholby. The travelers ate like men worn out and aching. The conversation, which began with a few broken phrases, soon ended in loud snoring. The guide kept watch beside Kiouni, who slept standing up, propped against the trunk of a great tree.

Nothing marked that night. A few roars from cheetahs and panthers now and then broke the silence, mixed with the shrill chattering of monkeys. But the big cats confined themselves to noise and made no hostile move against the guests of the bungalow. Sir Francis Cromarty slept heavily, like a good soldier worn out by his labors. Passepartout, in a restless sleep, relived in his dreams the tumbling of the day before. As for Mr. Fogg, he rested as peacefully as if he had been in his quiet house on Savile Row.

At six in the morning they set out again. The guide hoped to reach the Allahabad station that very evening. In that case Mr. Fogg would lose only a part of the forty-eight hours he had banked since the start of the journey.

They came down the last slopes of the Vindhias. Kiouni had picked up his brisk pace again. Toward noon the guide skirted the little town of Kallenger, on the Cani, one of the lesser tributaries of the Ganges. He kept clear of inhabited places, feeling safer in these deserted stretches of country that mark the first dips of the great river's basin. The Allahabad station now lay less than twelve miles to the northeast. They halted under a clump of banana trees, whose fruit — as wholesome as bread and, in travelers' words, "as luscious as cream" — was very much appreciated.

At two o'clock the guide passed under the cover of a thick forest, which he had to cross for several miles. He preferred to travel this way, sheltered by the woods. In any case he had met with no unpleasant encounter so far, and the journey seemed likely to pass off without mishap — when the elephant, showing some signs of unease, suddenly stopped.

It was four o'clock by then.

"What is it?" asked Sir Francis Cromarty, lifting his head above his howdah.

"I don't know, officer," answered the Parsee, straining to catch a confused murmur that came drifting under the thick canopy.

A few moments later the murmur grew more distinct. It sounded like a chorus of human voices and brass instruments, still a good way off.

Passepartout was all eyes and ears. Mr. Fogg waited patiently, without a word.

The Parsee dropped to the ground, tied the elephant to a tree, and slipped into the thickest of the underbrush. A few minutes later he came back and said:

"A procession of Brahmins, heading this way. If we can, let's not be seen."

The guide untied the elephant and led him into a thicket, warning the travelers not to set foot on the ground. He himself stood ready to spring onto his mount if flight became necessary. But he judged that the band of the faithful would pass without noticing them, for the dense foliage hid him completely.

The jarring din of voices and instruments came nearer. Monotonous chants mingled with the beat of drums and cymbals. Soon the head of the procession appeared beneath the trees, some fifty paces from the post held by Mr. Fogg and his companions. Through the branches they could easily make out the strange cast of this religious ceremony.

In the front rank came priests, crowned with miters and wrapped in long, gaudy robes. Around them pressed men, women, and children, raising a sort of funeral chant, broken at even intervals by the blows of tom-toms and cymbals. Behind them, on a car with broad wheels whose spokes and rim formed a tangle of serpents, rose a hideous statue, drawn by two yoke of zebus richly draped. The statue had four arms; its body was dyed a dark red, its eyes wild, its hair matted, its tongue lolling, its lips stained with henna and betel. Around its neck coiled a necklace of skulls; at its flanks, a belt of severed hands. It stood upright on a fallen giant whose head was missing.

Sir Francis Cromarty knew that statue.

"The goddess Kali," he murmured, "the goddess of love and death."

"Of death, I'll grant you, but of love — never!" said Passepartout. "The ugly old harridan!"

The Parsee signed to him to be quiet.

Around the statue writhed and struggled and convulsed a knot of old fakirs, striped with ochre and covered in cross-shaped gashes that let their blood escape drop by drop — dull-witted zealots who, in the great Hindu ceremonies, still fling themselves under the wheels of Juggernaut's car.

Behind them, a few Brahmins, in all the splendor of their Oriental dress, were dragging along a woman who could barely hold herself upright.

This woman was young, and white as a European. Her head, her neck, her shoulders, her ears, her arms, her hands, her toes were loaded down with jewels — necklaces, bracelets, earrings, and rings. A tunic threaded with gold, veiled under a light muslin, traced the lines of her figure.

Behind this young woman — a violent contrast to the eye — guards armed with naked sabers thrust through their belts and long inlaid pistols carried a corpse on a palanquin.

It was the body of an old man, dressed in the rich robes of a rajah, wearing, as in life, the turban embroidered with pearls, the robe woven of silk and gold, the cashmere sash set with diamonds, and the magnificent weapons of an Indian prince.

Then came musicians and a rear guard of fanatics, whose cries at times drowned out the deafening crash of the instruments, closing the cortege.

Sir Francis Cromarty watched all this pomp with a strangely saddened air, and turning to the guide:

"A suttee!" he said.

The Parsee nodded and put a finger to his lips. The long procession wound slowly on beneath the trees, and soon its last ranks vanished into the depths of the forest.

Little by little the chants died away. There were still a few bursts of distant shouting, and at last a deep silence settled over all the uproar.

Phileas Fogg had heard the word spoken by Sir Francis Cromarty, and the moment the procession had gone:

"What is a suttee?" he asked.

"A suttee, Mr. Fogg," answered the brigadier general, "is a human sacrifice — but a voluntary one. That woman you have just seen will be burned tomorrow at the first hours of daylight."

"The scoundrels!" cried Passepartout, unable to hold back the shout of outrage.

"And the corpse?" asked Mr. Fogg.

"That is the body of the prince, her husband," said the guide, "an independent rajah of Bundelkund."

"You mean to say," Phileas Fogg went on, without the least tremor in his voice, "that these barbarous customs still survive in India, and the English have not been able to stamp them out?"

"In most of India," answered Sir Francis Cromarty, "such sacrifices no longer take place. But we have no hold over these wild regions, and least of all over this Bundelkund territory. The whole northern face of the Vindhias is the scene of endless murder and pillage."

"The poor woman!" murmured Passepartout. "Burned alive!"

"Yes," the brigadier general went on, "burned — and if she were not, you could scarcely believe the wretched state her own people would reduce her to. They would shave off her hair; they would feed her barely a few handfuls of rice; they would cast her out. She would be treated as an unclean thing and would die in some corner like a mangy dog. It is often the prospect of that dreadful existence, far more than love or religious zeal, that drives these unhappy women to the pyre. Sometimes, though, the sacrifice is genuinely willing, and it takes the government's firm hand to prevent it. A few years ago, when I was living in Bombay, a young widow came to ask the governor's leave to burn herself with her husband's body. As you may imagine, the governor refused. So the widow left the city, took refuge with an independent rajah, and there carried out her sacrifice."

Throughout the brigadier general's account, the guide had been shaking his head, and when it was done:

"The sacrifice that will take place tomorrow at daybreak is not voluntary," he said.

"How do you know that?"

"It is a story everyone in Bundelkund knows," answered the guide.

"And yet the poor creature seemed to put up no resistance," Sir Francis Cromarty observed.

"That is because they have drugged her senseless with the smoke of hemp and opium."

"But where are they taking her?"

"To the pagoda of Pillaji, two miles from here. There she will spend the night, waiting for the hour of the sacrifice."

"And that sacrifice will take place —?"

"Tomorrow, at the first light of day."

After this answer, the guide brought the elephant out of the dense thicket and hoisted himself onto the animal's neck. But just as he was about to urge him on with a particular whistle, Mr. Fogg stopped him, and turning to Sir Francis Cromarty:

"What if we saved this woman?" he said.

"Save this woman, Mr. Fogg!" cried the brigadier general.

"I still have twelve hours in hand. I can spend them on that."

"Well! So you have a heart after all!" said Sir Francis Cromarty.

"Sometimes," Phileas Fogg answered simply. "When I have the time."
