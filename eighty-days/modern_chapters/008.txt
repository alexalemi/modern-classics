In Which the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean Prove Favorable to Phileas Fogg's Designs

The distance from Suez to Aden is exactly one thousand three hundred and ten miles, and the Company's contract allows its steamers a span of a hundred and thirty-eight hours to cover it. The Mongolia, her fires stoked hard, was making enough speed to beat the scheduled time.

Most of the passengers who had come aboard at Brindisi were bound for India. Some were going to Bombay, others to Calcutta — but by way of Bombay, for ever since a railway had been driven straight across the width of the Indian peninsula, there was no longer any need to round the point of Ceylon.

Among these passengers of the Mongolia were civil servants of various sorts and officers of every rank. Some belonged to the British army proper, others commanded the native regiments of sepoys, and all were handsomely paid, even now that the government had taken over the rights and the burdens of the old East India Company: second lieutenants at 7,000 francs, brigadiers at 60,000, generals at 100,000.

Life aboard the Mongolia, then, was a good one, in this company of officials, into which were mixed a few young Englishmen who, with a million in their pockets, were off to found trading houses in far-flung ports. The purser — the Company's trusted man, the equal of the captain aboard — did things in style. At the morning breakfast, at the two o'clock lunch, at the half-past five dinner, at the eight o'clock supper, the tables groaned under platters of fresh meat and side dishes turned out by the steamer's butchery and pantries. The lady passengers — there were a few — changed their dress twice a day. There was music, there was even dancing, whenever the sea allowed.

But the Red Sea is a moody thing, and too often a foul one, like all these long, narrow gulfs. When the wind blew off either the Asian or the African coast, the Mongolia — a long spindle of a screw steamer — was caught broadside and rolled fearfully. The ladies then vanished; the pianos fell silent; songs and dances stopped together. And yet, gale or no gale, swell or no swell, the steamer, driven by her powerful engine, ran on without delay toward the Strait of Bab-el-Mandeb.

And what was Phileas Fogg doing all this while? One might imagine that, forever fretful and anxious, he worried over the shifting winds that threatened the ship's pace, over the wild heave of the swell that might damage the engine, over every possible breakdown that could force the Mongolia into some port and so wreck his whole journey.

Not in the least — or if the gentleman did entertain such possibilities, he let nothing of it show. He was still the impassive man, the imperturbable member of the Reform Club, whom no incident or accident could surprise. He seemed no more moved than the ship's chronometers. He was seldom seen on deck. He gave little thought to this Red Sea, so rich in memory, this stage of the first historic scenes of humankind. He did not come up to make out the curious towns strewn along its shores, whose picturesque outlines sometimes stood against the horizon. He did not so much as dream of the dangers of this Arabian gulf, of which the ancient historians — Strabo, Arrian, Artemidorus, Edrisi — always spoke with dread, and onto which sailors of old never ventured without first consecrating their voyage with offerings to appease the gods.

So what did this odd fellow do, cooped up aboard the Mongolia? First, he took his four meals a day, without ever a roll or a pitch throwing a machine so wonderfully well ordered off its rhythm. Then he played whist.

Yes! He had found partners as rabid for the game as himself: a tax collector on his way to his post at Goa, a clergyman, the Reverend Decimus Smith, returning to Bombay, and a brigadier general of the English army rejoining his corps at Benares. These three passengers had the same passion for whist as Mr. Fogg, and they played for hours on end, no less silently than he.

As for Passepartout, seasickness had no hold on him whatsoever. He had a cabin forward and ate, for his part, conscientiously. And it must be said that this voyage, made on these terms, no longer displeased him in the slightest. He had made his peace with it. Well fed, well lodged, seeing the world — and besides, he kept assuring himself that this whole fancy would end at Bombay.

The day after leaving Suez, the 10th of October, it was with a certain pleasure that he ran into, up on deck, the obliging fellow he had turned to on landing in Egypt.

"I'm not mistaken," he said, coming up to him with his most amiable smile. "You're the very man, sir, who so kindly played guide for me at Suez?"

"Indeed I am," answered the detective. "I recognize you! You're the servant of that odd Englishman —"

"Precisely, Mr. —?"

"Fix."

"Mr. Fix," said Passepartout. "Delighted to find you aboard. And where are you headed?"

"Same as you — Bombay."

"Couldn't be better! Have you made this trip before?"

"Several times," answered Fix. "I'm an agent of the Peninsular Company."

"Then you know India?"

"Well... yes...," answered Fix, not wanting to commit himself too far.

"And is it a curious place, this India?"

"Very curious! Mosques, minarets, temples, fakirs, pagodas, tigers, snakes, dancing girls! But let's hope you'll have the time to see the country?"

"I hope so, Mr. Fix. You do understand that no man in his right mind can spend his life leaping from a steamer onto a railway and from a railway onto a steamer, all on the pretext of going around the world in eighty days! No. All this acrobatics will stop at Bombay, never you doubt it."

"And is he keeping well, Mr. Fogg?" asked Fix in the most natural tone.

"Very well, Mr. Fix. And so am I, for that matter. I'm eating like an ogre who's been fasting. It's the sea air."

"And your master — I never see him on deck."

"Never. He isn't curious."

"You know, Mr. Passepartout, that this so-called trip in eighty days might well be a cover for some secret mission — a diplomatic mission, say!"

"My word, Mr. Fix, I've no idea, I'll admit it, and frankly I wouldn't give half a crown to find out."

After that meeting, Passepartout and Fix chatted together often. The police inspector was keen to strike up a friendship with Mr. Fogg's servant. It might serve him when the moment came. So he would often stand him, in the bar-room of the Mongolia, a few glasses of whisky or pale ale, which the good fellow accepted without ceremony and even returned in kind, so as not to be in his debt — finding this Fix, moreover, a thoroughly decent gentleman.

Meanwhile the steamer pressed rapidly on. On the 13th, they sighted Mocha, appearing within its belt of ruined walls, above which a few green date palms stood out. Far off in the mountains spread vast fields of coffee trees. Passepartout was thrilled to gaze on this famous town, and he even decided that, with its circular walls and a dismantled fort curving like a handle, it looked like an enormous demitasse.

During the following night the Mongolia cleared the Strait of Bab-el-Mandeb, whose Arabic name means the Gate of Tears, and the next day, the 14th, she put in at Steamer Point, to the northwest of the roadstead of Aden. There she was to take on fuel again.

Feeding the furnaces of steamers at such distances from the centers of production is a grave and weighty business. For the Peninsular Company alone, it comes to an annual expense of eight hundred thousand pounds. Depots have had to be set up in several ports, and in these far-off seas coal runs to eighty francs a ton.

The Mongolia still had sixteen hundred and fifty miles to make before reaching Bombay, and she was to stay four hours at Steamer Point to fill her bunkers.

But this delay could do no harm whatever to Phileas Fogg's program. It had been allowed for. Besides, instead of reaching Aden only on the morning of the 15th of October, the Mongolia came in on the evening of the 14th. That was a gain of fifteen hours.

Mr. Fogg and his servant went ashore. The gentleman wished to have his passport stamped. Fix followed without being noticed. The formality of the visa done, Phileas Fogg returned aboard to take up his interrupted game.

Passepartout, for his part, strolled about as was his habit, among that population of Somalis, Banians, Parsees, Jews, Arabs, and Europeans who make up the twenty-five thousand inhabitants of Aden. He admired the fortifications that make this town the Gibraltar of the Indian Ocean, and the magnificent cisterns still being worked on by English engineers two thousand years after the engineers of King Solomon.

"Very curious, very curious!" Passepartout said to himself on his way back aboard. "I can see it's no bad thing to travel, if a man wants to see something new."

At six in the evening the Mongolia beat the waters of the Aden roadstead with the blades of her screw and was soon running out over the Indian Ocean. She was allowed a hundred and sixty-eight hours to make the crossing from Aden to Bombay. And this Indian sea proved kind to her. The wind held in the northwest. The sails came to the aid of the steam.

Better steadied, the ship rolled less. The lady passengers, in fresh gowns, reappeared on deck. The songs and the dances began again.

So the voyage went off in the best of conditions. Passepartout was delighted with the agreeable companion chance had thrown his way in the person of Fix.

On Sunday the 20th of October, toward noon, they raised the Indian coast. Two hours later the pilot came aboard the Mongolia. On the horizon a backdrop of hills traced itself harmoniously against the sky. Soon the ranks of palms that cover the town stood out sharply. The steamer entered the roadstead formed by the islands of Salsette, Colaba, Elephanta, and Butcher, and at half-past four she was drawing alongside the quays of Bombay.

Phileas Fogg was just then finishing the thirty-third rubber of the day, and he and his partner, thanks to one bold maneuver, had taken all thirteen tricks and closed this fine crossing with a splendid grand slam.

The Mongolia was not due at Bombay until the 22nd of October. But here she was arriving on the 20th. That made, since her departure from London, a gain of two days, which Phileas Fogg methodically entered on his itinerary, in the column of credits.
