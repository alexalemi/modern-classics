In Which Passepartout Does Not Need to Be Told Twice the Order His Master Gives Him

The next day, the people of Savile Row would have been very surprised to learn that Mr. Fogg had come home. Doors and windows were all shut. Nothing about the place had changed on the outside.

For, in fact, after leaving the station, Phileas Fogg had told Passepartout to buy a few provisions, and had gone into his house.

He had taken the blow with his usual composure. Ruined! And through the blunder of that clumsy police inspector! After marching so surely across that long road, after knocking down a thousand obstacles, braving a thousand dangers, even finding the time to do a little good along the way — to founder in sight of harbor, over some brute fact he could not have foreseen and against which he was helpless: it was a terrible thing. Of the considerable sum he had carried off at the start, only a trifling remnant was left. His whole fortune now came to the twenty thousand pounds deposited with Baring Brothers, and those twenty thousand pounds he owed to his colleagues at the Reform Club. After so much money spent, this wager, even won, would surely not have made him rich — and it is likely he had never sought to grow rich, being one of those men who bet for honor's sake — but this wager lost ruined him utterly. Still, the gentleman had made up his mind. He knew what remained for him to do.

One room of the house on Savile Row had been set aside for Aouda. The young woman was in despair. From certain words Mr. Fogg had let fall, she had understood that he was turning over some grim design.

For it is well known to what deplorable extremes these obsessive Englishmen will sometimes go under the pressure of a fixed idea. And so Passepartout, without seeming to, was keeping watch on his master.

But first of all, the honest fellow had gone up to his room and put out the gas jet that had been burning for eighty days. In the letterbox he had found a bill from the gas company, and he thought it was more than time to stop these charges, for which he was responsible.

The night passed. Mr. Fogg had gone to bed — but had he slept? As for Aouda, she could not find a single moment's rest. And Passepartout kept watch like a dog at his master's door.

The next morning Mr. Fogg sent for him and instructed him, in very few words, to see to Aouda's breakfast. For himself, he would make do with a cup of tea and a slice of toast. Aouda would be so good as to excuse him for breakfast and dinner both, since all his time was given over to putting his affairs in order. He would not be coming down. Only in the evening would he ask Aouda's leave to speak with her for a few minutes.

Passepartout, having his marching orders for the day, had nothing to do but follow them. He looked at his master, as unruffled as ever, and could not bring himself to leave the room. His heart was heavy, his conscience gnawed with remorse, for he blamed himself more than ever for this irreparable disaster. Yes! If only he had warned Mr. Fogg, if only he had told him what Fix was planning, Mr. Fogg would surely never have dragged the man all the way to Liverpool, and then —

Passepartout could hold it in no longer.

"Master! Mr. Fogg!" he cried. "Curse me! It's my fault that —"

"I blame no one," said Phileas Fogg, in his calmest tone. "Go."

Passepartout left the room and went to find the young woman, to whom he made his master's wishes known.

"Madame," he added, "I can do nothing on my own, nothing! I have no sway over my master's mind. But you, perhaps —"

"What sway could I have?" said Aouda. "Mr. Fogg yields to none! Has he ever once understood that my gratitude for him was ready to overflow? Has he ever once read my heart? My friend, we must not leave him — not for a single moment. You say he means to speak with me this evening?"

"Yes, madame. No doubt it's to see your position secured here in England."

"We shall wait, then," said the young woman, and she sank into thought.

And so, all through that Sunday, the house on Savile Row stood as though no one lived in it, and for the first time since he had made it his home, Phileas Fogg did not go to his club when half past eleven struck from the Parliament tower.

And why should the gentleman have shown himself at the Reform Club? His colleagues no longer expected him there. Since, the evening before — on that fatal date of Saturday, December 21, at eight forty-five — Phileas Fogg had failed to appear in the club's drawing room, his wager was lost. He need not even call on his banker to draw out that sum of twenty thousand pounds. His opponents held a check signed in his own hand, and it would take no more than a single stroke of the pen at Baring Brothers to carry the twenty thousand pounds over to their credit.

So Mr. Fogg had no reason to go out, and he did not go out. He stayed in his room and set his affairs in order. Passepartout never stopped going up and down the stairs of the house on Savile Row. For the poor fellow, the hours would not move. He listened at the door of his master's room, and in doing so felt he was committing no indiscretion at all! He peered through the keyhole, and imagined he had every right to! At every moment Passepartout dreaded some catastrophe. Now and then his thoughts turned to Fix, but a change had come over him. He no longer held a grudge against the police inspector. Fix had been mistaken about Phileas Fogg, like everyone else, and in shadowing him, in arresting him, he had only done his duty — whereas he himself... The thought crushed him, and he counted himself the lowest of wretches.

When at last Passepartout grew too miserable to be alone, he would knock at Aouda's door, slip into her room, sit down in a corner without a word, and watch the young woman, still lost in thought.

Toward half past seven in the evening, Mr. Fogg sent to ask Aouda whether she could receive him, and a few moments later the two of them were alone together in that room.

Phileas Fogg took a chair and sat down by the fireplace, facing Aouda. His face betrayed no emotion. The Fogg who had come home was exactly the Fogg who had set out. The same calm, the same composure.

He sat without speaking for five minutes. Then, raising his eyes to Aouda:

"Madame," he said, "will you forgive me for having brought you to England?"

"I, Mr. Fogg?" said Aouda, pressing down the beating of her heart.

"Allow me to finish," Mr. Fogg went on. "When I first thought of carrying you far from that country, which had become so dangerous for you, I was a rich man, and I meant to place part of my fortune at your disposal. Your life would have been happy and free. Now I am ruined."

"I know it, Mr. Fogg," the young woman answered, "and I must ask you in my turn: will you forgive me for having followed you, and — who knows? — for having perhaps, by delaying you, helped to bring about your ruin?"

"Madame, you could not remain in India, and your safety was assured only if you went far enough away that those fanatics could not seize you again."

"So, Mr. Fogg," Aouda went on, "not content with snatching me from a horrible death, you believed yourself bound to secure my position abroad as well?"

"Yes, madame, but events have turned against me. Still, of the little that remains to me, I ask your leave to make it over to you."

"But you, Mr. Fogg — what will become of you?" asked Aouda.

"I, madame," the gentleman answered coldly, "I have need of nothing."

"But how, sir, do you look upon the fate that awaits you?"

"As a man ought to," answered Mr. Fogg.

"At any rate," Aouda went on, "want could never touch a man like you. Your friends —"

"I have no friends, madame."

"Your relatives —"

"I have no relatives left."

"Then I pity you, Mr. Fogg, for to be alone is a sad thing. What! Not one heart to pour your sorrows into? And yet they say that with two, even misery itself can be borne."

"So they say, madame."

"Mr. Fogg," said Aouda then, rising and holding out her hand to the gentleman, "will you have both a kinswoman and a friend at once? Will you have me for your wife?"

At these words Mr. Fogg rose in his turn. There was something like an unaccustomed light in his eyes, something like a tremor on his lips. Aouda was looking at him. The sincerity, the uprightness, the firmness and gentleness of that beautiful gaze — the gaze of a noble woman who dares everything to save the man to whom she owes everything — astonished him first, then went straight through him. He closed his eyes for an instant, as if to keep that look from sinking any deeper. When he opened them again:

"I love you!" he said simply. "Yes, in truth, by all that is most sacred in the world, I love you, and I am wholly yours!"

"Ah!" cried Aouda, pressing her hand to her heart.

Passepartout was rung for. He came at once. Mr. Fogg still held Aouda's hand in his own. Passepartout understood, and his broad face blazed like the sun at its zenith over the tropics.

Mr. Fogg asked him whether it would be too late to go and notify the Reverend Samuel Wilson, of the parish of Marylebone.

Passepartout gave his very best smile.

"Never too late," he said.

It was only five past eight.

"It would be for tomorrow, Monday!" he said.

"For tomorrow, Monday?" asked Mr. Fogg, looking at the young woman.

"For tomorrow, Monday!" answered Aouda.

Passepartout dashed off at a run.
