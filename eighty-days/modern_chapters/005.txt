In Which Detective Fix Shows a Very Understandable Impatience

Here are the circumstances under which that telegram concerning Mr. Phileas Fogg had been sent.

On Wednesday, the 9th of October, the steamer Mongolia — of the Peninsular and Oriental Company, an iron screw-driven vessel with a spar deck, of two thousand eight hundred tons and a nominal five hundred horsepower — was expected at Suez at eleven o'clock in the morning. The Mongolia ran regularly between Brindisi and Bombay by way of the Suez Canal. She was one of the fastest ships in the company's fleet, and she had always beaten her regulation speeds — ten miles an hour between Brindisi and Suez, and nine and fifty-three hundredths between Suez and Bombay.

While the Mongolia was awaited, two men paced the quay amid the crowd of locals and foreigners who flock to this town — a mere village not long ago, but one to which M. de Lesseps's great work now promises a considerable future.

One of the two men was the consular agent of the United Kingdom, posted at Suez, who — despite the gloomy forecasts of the British government and the dire predictions of the engineer Stephenson — watched English ships cross the canal every day, cutting in half the old route from England to India by the Cape of Good Hope.

The other was a small, thin man with a fairly shrewd face, high-strung, who kept knitting his brows with remarkable persistence. Behind his long lashes shone a very bright eye — but one whose fire he could dim at will. Just now he was showing certain signs of impatience, pacing back and forth, unable to keep still.

This man's name was Fix, and he was one of those English "detectives," or police agents, who had been dispatched to the various ports after the robbery at the Bank of England. Fix's job was to keep the closest possible watch on every traveler taking the Suez route, and if any one of them struck him as suspicious, to "shadow" him while awaiting a warrant for his arrest.

As it happened, two days earlier Fix had received from the head of the metropolitan police a description of the man presumed to be the thief. It was the description of that distinguished, well-dressed figure who had been noticed in the paying room of the Bank.

The detective — plainly tempted by the handsome reward promised for success — was therefore waiting for the Mongolia with an impatience that was easy to understand.

"And you say, Mr. Consul," he asked for the tenth time, "that this ship can't be far off?"

"No, Mr. Fix," the consul answered. "She was sighted yesterday off Port Said, and the hundred and sixty kilometers of canal are nothing for a runner like her. I tell you again, the Mongolia has always earned the twenty-five-pound bonus the government pays for every twenty-four hours she comes in ahead of schedule."

"This steamer comes straight from Brindisi?" Fix asked.

"From Brindisi itself, where she took on the India mail — Brindisi, which she left Saturday at five in the evening. So be patient; she can't be long now. But I really don't see how, with the description you've received, you'll manage to recognize your man, if he's aboard the Mongolia."

"Mr. Consul," Fix replied, "men like that you sense rather than recognize. You need a nose for it, and the nose is a special sense, one that draws on hearing, sight, and smell all at once. I've arrested more than one of these gentlemen in my time, and provided my thief is aboard, I'll answer for it that he won't slip through my fingers."

"I hope so, Mr. Fix, for it's a serious robbery."

"A magnificent robbery," the agent said, warming to it. "Fifty-five thousand pounds! We don't often come by such windfalls! Thieves are turning small-time! The breed of Sheppard is dying out! Nowadays a man gets himself hanged for a few shillings!"

"Mr. Fix," said the consul, "you talk in such a way that I heartily wish you success; but I say again, in the circumstances you're in, I'm afraid it will be difficult. Do you realize that, by the description you've received, this thief looks exactly like an honest man?"

"Mr. Consul," the inspector answered sententiously, "the great thieves always look like honest folk. You'll appreciate that a man with the face of a scoundrel has only one course open to him, which is to stay upright — otherwise he'd get himself arrested. It's the honest faces you have to size up above all. Difficult work, I grant you, and no longer a trade but an art."

Clearly this Fix was not lacking in a certain measure of self-regard.

Meanwhile the quay was slowly coming to life. Sailors of every nationality, merchants, brokers, porters, fellahs, all thronging in. The steamer's arrival was plainly near.

The weather was fairly fine, but the air was cold, with this east wind. A few minarets stood out above the town under the pale rays of the sun. To the south, a jetty two thousand meters long stretched like an arm across the roadstead of Suez. On the surface of the Red Sea rolled several fishing and coasting boats, some of which still keep in their lines the elegant model of the ancient galley.

Moving through the crowd, Fix — out of professional habit — sized up the passers-by with a quick glance.

It was then half past ten.

"But this steamer's never coming!" he cried, hearing the harbor clock strike.

"She can't be far off," the consul replied.

"How long will she lie at Suez?" Fix asked.

"Four hours. Time enough to take on her coal. From Suez to Aden, at the far end of the Red Sea, is reckoned at thirteen hundred and ten miles, and she has to lay in fuel."

"And from Suez this ship goes straight to Bombay?" Fix asked.

"Straight there, without breaking cargo."

"Well, then," said Fix, "if the thief has taken this route and this ship, it must be part of his plan to land at Suez, so as to reach the Dutch or French possessions of Asia by some other way. He must know very well that he wouldn't be safe in India, which is English soil."

"Unless he's a very clever man," the consul replied. "As you know, an English criminal is always better hidden in London than he would be abroad."

On this remark, which gave the agent a good deal to think about, the consul went back to his offices, a short distance off. The inspector was left alone, gripped by a nervous impatience, and by the rather odd presentiment that his thief was bound to be aboard the Mongolia — and in truth, if the scoundrel had left England meaning to reach the New World, the route by India, less closely watched, or harder to watch, than the Atlantic route, would surely have been his choice.

Fix was not left to his reflections for long. Sharp whistle blasts announced the steamer's arrival. The whole horde of porters and fellahs rushed toward the quay in a tumult somewhat alarming for the limbs and clothes of the passengers. A dozen small boats put out from the shore and went to meet the Mongolia.

Soon the huge hull of the Mongolia was seen passing between the banks of the canal, and eleven o'clock was striking when the steamer came to anchor in the roadstead, her steam roaring loudly through the escape pipes.

There were a good many passengers aboard. Some stayed on the spar deck to take in the picturesque panorama of the town; but most went ashore in the boats that had come alongside the Mongolia.

Fix scrupulously examined everyone who set foot on land.

Just then one of them came up to him, after vigorously fending off the fellahs who were mobbing him with offers of service, and asked him very politely if he could point out the offices of the English consular agent. At the same time the passenger held out a passport on which he no doubt wished to have the British visa stamped.

Fix instinctively took the passport and, at a quick glance, read the description on it.

An involuntary start nearly escaped him. The sheet trembled in his hand. The description entered on the passport was identical to the one he had received from the head of the metropolitan police.

"This passport isn't yours?" he said to the passenger.

"No," the man answered, "it's my master's passport."

"And your master?"

"He's stayed aboard."

"But," the agent went on, "he'll have to present himself in person at the consulate to establish his identity."

"What! Is that necessary?"

"Indispensable."

"And where are these offices?"

"There, at the corner of the square," the inspector replied, pointing to a house some two hundred paces off.

"Then I'll go and fetch my master — though it won't much please him to be put out!"

With that, the passenger bowed to Fix and went back aboard the steamer.
