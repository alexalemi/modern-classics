In Which a New Security Appears on the London Exchange

When he left London, Phileas Fogg surely had no idea of the enormous stir his departure was about to create. The news of the wager spread first through the Reform Club, where it produced a genuine sensation among the members of that honorable circle. From the club the excitement passed, by way of the reporters, into the newspapers, and from the newspapers to the public of London and the whole of the United Kingdom.

This "question of the tour of the world" was commented on, debated, and dissected with as much passion and heat as if it had been some new Alabama affair. Some took Phileas Fogg's side; others — and they soon formed a considerable majority — came out against him. To make this trip around the world, other than in theory and on paper, in that minimum of time, with the means of communication currently in use — it was not merely impossible, it was insane!

The Times, the Standard, the Evening Star, the Morning Chronicle, and twenty other papers of wide circulation declared against Mr. Fogg. Only the Daily Telegraph stood by him, and only up to a point. Phileas Fogg was generally written off as a maniac and a madman, and his fellow members at the Reform Club were blamed for having taken on a bet that pointed to a softening of the wits in the man who had proposed it.

Articles appeared on the question — extremely heated, yet perfectly logical. Everyone knows the interest England takes in anything touching on geography. And so there was not a reader, of whatever class, who did not devour the columns devoted to the case of Phileas Fogg.

During the first few days, a few bold spirits — the women chiefly — were for him, especially after the Illustrated London News ran his portrait, copied from the photograph on file in the archives of the Reform Club. Certain gentlemen went so far as to say, "Well, well — why not, after all? Stranger things have happened!" These were mostly readers of the Daily Telegraph. But it soon became clear that even that paper was beginning to waver.

For a long article appeared on October 7 in the Bulletin of the Royal Geographical Society. It treated the question from every possible angle and demonstrated, clearly, the sheer folly of the enterprise. According to the article, everything was against the traveler — the obstacles of man and the obstacles of nature alike. To succeed in this scheme, one would have to assume a miraculous alignment of departure and arrival times, an alignment that did not exist and could not exist. In Europe, at a pinch — where the runs are of relatively modest length — one may count on the trains arriving on schedule; but when they take three days to cross India and seven to cross the United States, could anyone build the terms of such a problem on their punctuality? And the engine breakdowns, the derailments, the collisions, the bad season, the piling up of snow — was not all of it against Phileas Fogg? On the steamers, would he not find himself, in winter, at the mercy of gales and fogs? Is it really so rare for the best runners on the transoceanic lines to lose two or three days? And a single delay, just one, was all it would take to snap the chain of connections beyond repair. If Phileas Fogg missed a steamer's departure by so much as a few hours, he would be forced to wait for the next one, and by that alone his whole journey would be wrecked, irrevocably.

The article made a great noise. Nearly every paper reprinted it, and shares in Phileas Fogg fell sharply.

In the first days after the gentleman's departure, considerable business had been done on the "odds" of his venture. Everyone knows what the betting world in England is — a world more intelligent, more elevated than the world of common gamblers. Betting is in the English temperament. And so not only did the various members of the Reform Club lay heavy wagers for and against Phileas Fogg, but the mass of the public joined the movement. Phileas Fogg was entered, like a racehorse, in a kind of studbook. He was also turned into a security, quoted at once on the London Exchange. People bid for "Phileas Fogg" and offered him, firm or at a premium, and enormous business was done in him. But five days after his departure, following the article in the Bulletin of the Geographical Society, the offers began to pour in. Phileas Fogg fell. He was offered in bundles. Taken first at five, then at ten, he was soon taken only at twenty, at fifty, at a hundred!

He kept one lone supporter. This was the old paralytic, Lord Albermale. The honorable gentleman, nailed to his armchair, would have given his whole fortune to be able to make the tour of the world, even in ten years! — and he bet five thousand pounds in favor of Phileas Fogg. And whenever someone pointed out to him not only the stupidity of the scheme but its uselessness, he simply answered, "If the thing can be done, it's a good deal better that an Englishman should be the first to do it!"

Now matters stood there — Phileas Fogg's backers growing scarcer and scarcer, everyone, and not without reason, turning against him, so that he was taken only at a hundred and fifty, at two hundred to one — when, seven days after his departure, a completely unexpected incident meant that he was no longer taken at all.

For that day, at nine o'clock in the evening, the director of the metropolitan police received a telegram worded as follows:

"Suez to London.

"Rowan, Director of Police, Central Administration, Scotland Place.

"I am on the trail of the bank robber, Phileas Fogg. Send warrant of arrest without delay to Bombay (British India).

"Fix, Detective."

The effect of this telegram was immediate. The honorable gentleman vanished, and in his place stood the thief of banknotes. His photograph, kept at the Reform Club with those of all his colleagues, was studied. It reproduced, feature for feature, the man whose description the inquiry had supplied. People recalled how mysterious Phileas Fogg's whole existence had been — his solitude, his sudden departure — and it seemed obvious that this character, using a trip around the world as his pretext and propping it up on an insane wager, had had no other aim than to throw the agents of the English police off his scent.
