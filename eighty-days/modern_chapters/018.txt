In Which Passepartout Takes Too Keen an Interest in His Master, and What Comes of It

Hong Kong is only a little island, handed over to England by the Treaty of Nanking after the war of 1842. In a few short years, Britain's colonizing genius had raised an important city there and built a harbor, Victoria Harbour. The island sits at the mouth of the Canton River, and a mere sixty miles separate it from the Portuguese city of Macao, built on the opposite bank. Hong Kong was bound to beat Macao in the commercial contest, and by now most of the Chinese transit trade passed through the English town. Docks, hospitals, wharves, warehouses, a Gothic cathedral, a government house, macadamized streets — everything about it made you think that some trading town out of Kent or Surrey had bored clean through the terrestrial sphere and come out again at this spot in China, very nearly at its antipodes.

Passepartout, hands in his pockets, made his way down toward Victoria Harbour, taking in the sedan chairs, the sail-rigged wheelbarrows still in favor in the Celestial Empire, and the whole crowd of Chinese, Japanese, and Europeans jostling through the streets. Give or take a detail here and there, it was Bombay, Calcutta, or Singapore all over again that the good fellow kept finding along his route. There is, it seems, a sort of trail of English towns laid clear around the world.

Passepartout reached Victoria Harbour. There, at the mouth of the Canton River, was a swarming mass of ships of every nation — English, French, American, Dutch, warships and merchantmen, Japanese and Chinese craft, junks, sampans, tankas, and even flower-boats that made so many floating gardens on the water. Strolling along, Passepartout noticed a number of locals dressed in yellow, all of them well on in years. Stepping into a Chinese barber's to get a shave "in the Chinese style," he learned from the local Figaro, who spoke tolerably good English, that these old men were all at least eighty, and that at that age they had the privilege of wearing yellow, the imperial color. Passepartout found this very funny, without quite knowing why.

His beard trimmed, he went down to the Carnatic's boarding quay, and there he spotted Fix pacing back and forth — which did not surprise him in the least. But the police inspector's face wore the plain marks of a bitter disappointment.

"Good!" Passepartout said to himself. "This looks bad for the gentlemen of the Reform Club!"

And he greeted Fix with his cheerful smile, choosing not to notice his companion's sour expression.

Now, the detective had good reason to curse the infernal luck that dogged him. No warrant! It was clear the warrant was running along somewhere behind him and could only catch up if he stayed a few days in this city. And Hong Kong being the last stretch of English soil on the route, Fogg was going to slip away from him for good unless he could find some way to hold him there.

"Well, Mr. Fix, have you made up your mind to come along with us as far as America?" asked Passepartout.

"Yes," Fix answered through his teeth.

"There, you see!" cried Passepartout, letting out a ringing peal of laughter. "I knew you couldn't bear to part from us. Come sign up for your berth, come along!"

And the two of them stepped into the shipping office and booked cabins for four. But the clerk pointed out that, the repairs to the Carnatic being finished, the steamer would sail that very evening at eight o'clock, and not the next morning as had first been announced.

"Excellent!" said Passepartout. "That will suit my master perfectly. I'll go and let him know."

At that moment Fix made a desperate decision. He resolved to tell Passepartout everything. It was perhaps the only way left to him of keeping Phileas Fogg a few days in Hong Kong.

As they left the office, Fix offered to stand his companion a drink at a tavern. Passepartout had the time. He accepted.

A tavern opened onto the quay. It had an inviting look. The two of them went in. It was a large, well-appointed room, with a camp bed laid out at the far end, heaped with cushions. On this bed lay a number of sleepers.

Some thirty or so customers occupied little tables of woven cane in the main room. A few were draining pints of English beer, ale or porter; others jugs of gin or brandy. And most of them, besides, were smoking long pipes of red clay, packed with little pellets of opium blended with essence of rose. Now and then some smoker, his nerves gone, would slide under the table, and the waiters of the house would take him by the head and heels and carry him over to the camp bed to lie beside a fellow. A score of these drunkards were laid out that way, side by side, sunk to the last degree of stupor.

Fix and Passepartout understood that they had walked into one of those smoking dens haunted by the wretched, dull-eyed, wasted, witless men to whom mercantile England sells, every year, two hundred and sixty million francs' worth of that deadly drug called opium — a sad harvest of millions, drawn from one of the most ruinous of all human vices.

The Chinese government had certainly tried to check the abuse with strict laws, but in vain. From the wealthy class, to whom the use of opium had at first been strictly confined, the habit had worked its way down to the lower classes, and the ruin it caused could no longer be stopped. Opium is smoked everywhere, all the time, in the Middle Kingdom. Men and women alike give themselves over to the wretched craving, and once they are used to breathing it in, they cannot do without it, or else suffer horrible cramps of the stomach. A heavy smoker can get through as many as eight pipes a day — but he is dead within five years.

Well, it was into one of the many dens of this sort, which swarm even in Hong Kong, that Fix and Passepartout had walked with the idea of getting a drink. Passepartout had no money, but he gladly accepted his companion's "courtesy," meaning to return it in his own time and place.

They ordered two bottles of port, which the Frenchman did full justice to, while Fix, more sparing, watched him with the closest attention. They talked of this and that, and above all of the excellent notion Fix had had of booking passage on the Carnatic. And on the subject of that steamer, whose departure had been moved up a few hours, Passepartout — the bottles now empty — got to his feet to go and warn his master.

Fix held him back.

"One moment," he said.

"What is it, Mr. Fix?"

"I have something serious to talk to you about."

"Something serious!" cried Passepartout, tipping back the last few drops of wine in his glass. "Well, we'll talk about it tomorrow. I haven't the time today."

"Stay," Fix answered. "It concerns your master!"

At that word, Passepartout looked hard at the man across from him. There was something odd in Fix's expression. He sat back down.

"What is it you have to tell me?" he asked.

Fix laid a hand on his companion's arm and, dropping his voice, said:

"You've guessed who I am?"

"Of course I have!" said Passepartout, smiling.

"Then I'm going to confess everything to you—"

"Now that I already know the whole of it, my friend! Ah, that's not much of a secret! Still, go on. But first let me tell you those gentlemen have put themselves to a great deal of trouble for nothing!"

"For nothing!" said Fix. "You talk very easily! It's plain you don't know the size of the sum!"

"Of course I know it," Passepartout answered. "Twenty thousand pounds!"

"Fifty-five thousand!" Fix shot back, gripping the Frenchman's hand.

"What!" cried Passepartout. "Mr. Fogg would have dared!... Fifty-five thousand pounds!... Well then! All the more reason not to lose a single moment," he added, getting to his feet again.

"Fifty-five thousand pounds!" Fix repeated, forcing Passepartout back into his seat and calling for a flask of brandy to be brought. "And if I pull it off, I earn a bonus of two thousand pounds. Will you take five hundred of it — on condition you help me?"

"Help you?" cried Passepartout, his eyes stretched enormously wide.

"Yes — help me keep Fogg a few days in Hong Kong!"

"Eh!" said Passepartout. "What are you saying? What! Not content with having my master followed, with doubting his honesty, these gentlemen actually want to throw obstacles in his path! I'm ashamed of them!"

"Now then — what do you mean by that?" asked Fix.

"I mean it's downright shabby. You might as well strip Mr. Fogg bare and lift the money straight out of his pocket!"

"Ah — but that's exactly what we're counting on doing!"

"But this is an ambush!" cried Passepartout — who was warming up now under the brandy Fix kept pouring, and which he drank without noticing. "A regular ambush! And from gentlemen! From colleagues!"

Fix was beginning to lose the thread.

"Colleagues!" cried Passepartout. "Members of the Reform Club! I'll have you know, Mr. Fix, that my master is an honest man, and that when he has made a bet, he means to win it fair and square."

"But who do you think I am?" asked Fix, fixing his gaze on Passepartout.

"Why — an agent of the members of the Reform Club, sent to check up on my master's route, which is singularly humiliating! And that's why, though I guessed what you were some time ago now, I've taken good care not to breathe a word of it to Mr. Fogg!"

"He knows nothing?" Fix asked quickly.

"Nothing," Passepartout answered, draining his glass once more.

The police inspector passed a hand across his forehead. He hesitated before speaking again. What was he to do? Passepartout's mistake seemed genuine, but it made his scheme all the harder. It was plain the fellow was speaking in absolute good faith, and that he was no accomplice of his master's — which was what Fix might have feared.

"Well," he said to himself, "since he's not the accomplice, he'll help me."

For the second time, the detective made up his mind. Besides, he had no more time to wait. At any cost, Fogg had to be stopped in Hong Kong.

"Listen," said Fix in a clipped voice. "Listen to me carefully. I am not what you think — that is, an agent of the members of the Reform Club—"

"Bah!" said Passepartout, eyeing him mockingly.

"I am a police inspector, charged with a mission by the Metropolitan authorities—"

"You... a police inspector!"

"Yes, and I can prove it," Fix went on. "Here is my commission."

And the detective, drawing a paper from his wallet, showed his companion a commission signed by the director of the central police. Passepartout, dumbfounded, stared at Fix, unable to get out a single word.

"Fogg's bet," Fix continued, "is nothing but a pretext, and you and his fellow members of the Reform Club are the dupes of it, because he had an interest in securing your unwitting help."

"But why?" cried Passepartout.

"Listen. On the 29th of September last, a theft of fifty-five thousand pounds was committed at the Bank of England, by a man whose description was taken down. Well, here is that description, and it matches Fogg feature for feature."

"Nonsense!" cried Passepartout, bringing his sturdy fist down on the table. "My master is the most honest man in the world!"

"How would you know?" Fix returned. "You don't even know him! You entered his service the very day he left, and he set off in a mad rush on some senseless pretext, without so much as a trunk, carrying off a large sum in banknotes! And you still dare to maintain he's an honest man!"

"Yes! Yes!" the poor fellow kept repeating, mechanically.

"So you'd like to be arrested as his accomplice?"

Passepartout had taken his head in both hands. He was no longer himself. He did not dare look at the police inspector. Phileas Fogg a thief — he, the rescuer of Aouda, that generous and fearless man! And yet what a weight of presumptions stood against him! Passepartout tried to push away the suspicions creeping into his mind. He would not believe his master guilty.

"Well, what is it you want from me?" he said to the detective, holding himself in by one supreme effort.

"Here it is," Fix answered. "I've trailed Fogg this far, but I still haven't received the arrest warrant I asked London for. So you must help me hold him in Hong Kong—"

"Me! That I should—"

"And I'll split with you the two-thousand-pound bonus promised by the Bank of England!"

"Never!" answered Passepartout, who tried to rise and fell back again, feeling both his wits and his strength deserting him at once.

"Mr. Fix," he said, stammering, "even supposing everything you've told me were true... even if my master were the thief you're after... which I deny... I have been — I am — in his service... I have seen him good and generous... betray him... never... no, not for all the gold in the world... I come from a village where we don't eat that kind of bread!"

"You refuse?"

"I refuse."

"Then let's say I've said nothing," Fix answered, "and let's drink."

"Yes — let's drink!"

Passepartout felt the drunkenness taking hold of him more and more. Fix, seeing that he had to separate the man from his master at any cost, resolved to finish him off. On the table lay a few pipes charged with opium. Fix slipped one into Passepartout's hand. He took it, raised it to his lips, lit it, drew in a few puffs, and sank back, his head heavy under the drug's grip.

"Well," said Fix, watching Passepartout go under, "Fogg won't be warned in time about the Carnatic's departure, and if he does sail, at least he'll sail without this cursed Frenchman!"

Then he went out, after settling the bill.
