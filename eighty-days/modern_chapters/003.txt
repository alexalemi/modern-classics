In Which Phileas Fogg Astounds His Servant Passepartout

At twenty-five minutes past seven, Phileas Fogg — having won a score of guineas at whist — took his leave of his honorable colleagues and left the Reform Club. At ten minutes to eight he opened the door of his house and let himself in.

Passepartout, who had conscientiously studied his schedule, was more than a little surprised to see Mr. Fogg guilty of a lapse in punctuality, turning up at so unusual an hour. According to the notice, the tenant of Savile Row was not to be home until midnight precisely.

Phileas Fogg had first gone up to his room, then he called out:

"Passepartout."

Passepartout did not answer. The call could not be meant for him. It was not the hour.

"Passepartout," Mr. Fogg repeated, without raising his voice any further.

Passepartout appeared.

"That's the second time I've called you," said Mr. Fogg.

"But it isn't midnight," answered Passepartout, his watch in his hand.

"I know that," said Phileas Fogg, "and I'm not reproaching you. We leave in ten minutes for Dover and Calais."

A sort of grimace flickered across the round face of the Frenchman. Clearly he had misheard.

"Sir is going away?" he asked.

"Yes," answered Phileas Fogg. "We are going around the world."

Passepartout — eyes stretched impossibly wide, eyebrows and lids hoisted up, arms hanging slack, body sagging — displayed at that moment every symptom of astonishment pushed all the way to stupefaction.

"Around the world!" he murmured.

"In eighty days," answered Mr. Fogg. "So we haven't a moment to lose."

"But the trunks?..." said Passepartout, unconsciously wagging his head from side to side.

"No trunks. Just an overnight bag. Inside it, two wool shirts, three pairs of socks. The same for you. We'll buy along the way. Bring down my mackintosh and my traveling blanket. Have a good pair of shoes. Though we'll be doing little or no walking. Off you go."

Passepartout would have liked to answer. He couldn't. He left Mr. Fogg's room, went up to his own, dropped into a chair, and — falling back on a rather coarse phrase of his native country — said to himself:

"Well, now! That's a good one, that is! And me, who wanted a quiet life!..."

And, mechanically, he began his preparations for departure. Around the world in eighty days! Was he dealing with a madman? No... Was it a joke? They were off to Dover, fine. To Calais, all right. After all, that could hardly upset the good fellow much, seeing as he hadn't set foot on his native soil in five years. Perhaps they might even go as far as Paris, and — my word — he'd be glad to see the great capital again. But surely a gentleman so sparing of his steps would stop there... Yes, no doubt, but the fact remained that he was leaving, that he was going traveling — this gentleman who until now had been such a homebody!

By eight o'clock, Passepartout had packed the modest bag that held his own wardrobe and his master's; then, his mind still in a whirl, he left his room, carefully locked the door behind him, and rejoined Mr. Fogg.

Mr. Fogg was ready. He carried under his arm Bradshaw's Continental Railway Steam Transit and General Guide, which was to furnish him with every detail he needed for his journey. He took the bag from Passepartout's hands, opened it, and slipped inside a thick wad of those handsome banknotes that are good tender in every country.

"You've forgotten nothing?" he asked.

"Nothing, sir."

"My mackintosh and my blanket?"

"Here they are."

"Good. Take this bag."

Mr. Fogg handed the bag back to Passepartout.

"And take good care of it," he added. "There are twenty thousand pounds inside."

The bag nearly slipped from Passepartout's hands, as though the twenty thousand pounds had been in gold and weighed a considerable amount.

Master and servant then went downstairs, and the street door was double-locked behind them.

There was a cab stand at the end of Savile Row. Phileas Fogg and his servant climbed into a cab, which drove briskly toward Charing Cross station, the terminus of one of the branches of the South Eastern Railway.

At twenty minutes past eight the cab stopped in front of the station gate. Passepartout jumped down. His master followed and paid the driver.

At that moment a poor beggar woman, a child by the hand, barefoot in the mud, a battered hat on her head with a sorry feather dangling from it, a tattered shawl over her rags, came up to Mr. Fogg and begged him for alms.

Mr. Fogg drew from his pocket the twenty guineas he had just won at whist, and, holding them out to the beggar woman, said:

"Here you are, my good woman. I'm glad to have met you!"

Then he passed on.

Passepartout felt something like a dampness about his eyes. His master had taken a step into his heart.

He and Mr. Fogg went straight into the great hall of the station. There Phileas Fogg gave Passepartout the order to buy two first-class tickets to Paris. Then, turning around, he caught sight of his five colleagues from the Reform Club.

"Gentlemen, I'm off," he said, "and the various visas stamped on a passport I'm carrying for the purpose will allow you, on my return, to verify my itinerary."

"Oh, Mr. Fogg," Gauthier Ralph replied politely, "that's unnecessary. We'll rely on your honor as a gentleman!"

"Better this way," said Mr. Fogg.

"You won't forget that you're to be back?..." Andrew Stuart pointed out.

"In eighty days," answered Mr. Fogg. "On Saturday, the 21st of December, 1872, at a quarter to nine in the evening. Good-bye, gentlemen."

At twenty minutes to nine, Phileas Fogg and his servant took their seats in the same compartment. At a quarter to nine a whistle sounded, and the train began to move.

The night was pitch black. A fine rain was falling. Phileas Fogg, settled back in his corner, said nothing. Passepartout, still stunned, mechanically hugged the bag of banknotes against himself.

But the train had barely passed Sydenham when Passepartout let out a genuine cry of despair!

"What's the matter?" asked Mr. Fogg.

"It's... that... in my rush... my confusion... I forgot..."

"Forgot what?"

"To turn off the gas jet in my room!"

"Well, my boy," Mr. Fogg answered coldly, "it's burning on your account!"
