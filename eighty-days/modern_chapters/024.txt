In Which a Brief Glimpse Is Given of San Francisco on the Day of a Political Rally

It was seven o'clock in the morning when Phileas Fogg, Aouda, and Passepartout set foot on the American continent — if you can give that grand name to the floating wharf they landed on. These wharves rise and fall with the tide, which makes loading and unloading the ships easier. Clippers of every size berth there, steamers of every flag, and those many-decked steamboats that run the Sacramento River and its tributaries. There too are piled the goods of a trade that reaches to Mexico, Peru, Chile, Brazil, Europe, Asia, and every island of the Pacific Ocean.

In his joy at finally touching American soil, Passepartout had felt it only right to disembark by turning a somersault of the very finest style. But when he came down on the wharf, whose planking was worm-eaten, he nearly went straight through it. Utterly thrown off by the manner in which he had "set foot" on the new continent, the honest fellow let out a tremendous yell that sent an enormous flock of cormorants and pelicans — the usual tenants of these floating wharves — flapping into the air.

The moment he landed, Mr. Fogg asked when the first train left for New York. Six o'clock that evening. So Mr. Fogg had a whole day to spend in the capital of California. He called a carriage for Aouda and himself. Passepartout climbed up onto the box, and the vehicle — three dollars the ride — set off for the International Hotel.

From his high perch, Passepartout studied the great American city with curiosity: broad streets, low houses in neat rows, churches and chapels of an Anglo-Saxon Gothic, immense docks, warehouses like palaces, some of wood, some of brick; in the streets, no end of carriages, omnibuses, tramway "cars"; and on the crowded sidewalks, not only Americans and Europeans, but Chinese and Indians too — in short, everything needed to make up a population of more than two hundred thousand souls.

Passepartout was rather surprised by what he saw. In his mind he was still back in the legendary city of 1849 — the city of bandits, arsonists, and murderers who had come racing after the gold nuggets, one vast bedlam of every outcast on earth, where men gambled with gold dust, a revolver in one hand and a knife in the other. But "the good old days" were gone. San Francisco had the look of a great trading city now. The tall tower of the city hall, where the watchmen kept their lookout, loomed over the whole grid of streets and avenues, all crossing at right angles, with green squares blooming between them, and then a Chinese quarter that seemed to have been shipped over from the Celestial Empire in a toy box. No more sombreros, no more red shirts in the fashion of the gold-diggers, no more feathered Indians — only silk hats and black coats, worn by any number of gentlemen driven by a devouring energy. Some streets — Montgomery Street among them, the Regent Street of London, the Boulevard des Italiens of Paris, the Broadway of New York — were lined with splendid shops that displayed the goods of the entire world.

By the time Passepartout reached the International Hotel, it hardly seemed to him that he had ever left England.

The ground floor of the hotel was taken up by an enormous "bar," a kind of buffet open free of charge to every passerby. Dried meat, oyster soup, biscuit, and Cheshire cheese were served out without the customer's ever having to loosen his purse strings. He paid only for his drink — ale, port, or sherry — should the fancy take him to refresh himself. All this struck Passepartout as "very American."

The hotel restaurant was comfortable. Mr. Fogg and Aouda settled at a table and were lavishly served, in Lilliputian dishes, by waiters of the deepest black.

After breakfast, Phileas Fogg, with Aouda on his arm, left the hotel for the offices of the English consul, to have his passport stamped. On the sidewalk he found his servant, who asked whether, before taking the Pacific Railroad, it might not be wise to buy a few dozen Enfield rifles or Colt revolvers. Passepartout had heard tell of Sioux and Pawnees who stop trains the way ordinary Spanish bandits do. Mr. Fogg replied that this was a needless precaution, but he left him free to do as he saw fit. Then he headed for the offices of the consular agent.

Phileas Fogg had not gone two hundred paces when, "by the greatest of chances," he ran into Fix. The inspector looked extremely surprised. What! He and Mr. Fogg had crossed the Pacific together and never once met aboard! In any case, Fix could only be honored to see again the gentleman to whom he owed so much, and, as his business was calling him back to Europe, he would be delighted to continue his journey in such agreeable company.

Mr. Fogg replied that the honor would be his, and Fix — who was anxious not to lose sight of him — asked leave to explore this curious city of San Francisco in his company. This was granted.

And so here were Aouda, Phileas Fogg, and Fix strolling through the streets. They soon found themselves on Montgomery Street, where the crowd was enormous. On the sidewalks, in the middle of the roadway, on the tramway rails, despite the ceaseless passing of coaches and omnibuses, in the doorways of the shops, at the windows of every house, and even up on the rooftops — a countless multitude. Men wearing sandwich boards moved among the knots of people. Banners and streamers flapped in the wind. Shouts burst out on every side.

"Hurrah for Kamerfield!"

"Hurrah for Mandiboy!"

It was a rally. That, at least, was Fix's guess, and he shared the thought with Mr. Fogg, adding:

"We would do well, sir, not to mix ourselves up in this mob. There's nothing to come of it but a beating."

"Quite so," answered Phileas Fogg. "And a fist, for being political, is no less a fist."

Fix thought it right to smile at this remark, and, so as to watch without getting caught in the scuffle, he and Aouda and Phileas Fogg took up a position on the top landing of a staircase that led to a terrace, set above Montgomery Street. Before them, across the street, between a coal merchant's wharf and an oil dealer's warehouse, stood a large open-air office toward which the various currents of the crowd seemed to be converging.

And now, what was this rally about? What was the occasion for it? Phileas Fogg had absolutely no idea. Was it the appointment of some high military or civil official, a state governor, a member of Congress? It was fair to guess as much, judging by the extraordinary excitement that had the whole city in a fever.

At that moment a great heave ran through the crowd. Every hand was in the air. Some of them, tightly clenched, seemed to rise and fall quickly amid the shouting — a vigorous way, no doubt, of casting a vote. Eddies stirred the mass as it surged back. The banners swayed, vanished for an instant, and reappeared in tatters. The rolling of the swell spread all the way to the staircase, while every head bobbed on the surface like a sea suddenly whipped up by a squall. The number of black hats dropped before your eyes, and most of them seemed to have lost their usual height.

"It's plainly a rally," said Fix, "and the question behind it must be a burning one. I shouldn't be surprised if it were still that Alabama business, even though it's been settled."

"Perhaps," Mr. Fogg answered simply.

"In any case," Fix went on, "two champions are squaring off against each other — the honorable Kamerfield and the honorable Mandiboy."

Aouda, on Phileas Fogg's arm, watched this tumultuous scene in amazement, and Fix was just about to ask one of the bystanders the reason for all this popular ferment when a sharper surge broke out. The hurrahs, now spiced with insults, redoubled. Banner poles turned into weapons. No more open hands — fists everywhere. From the tops of the halted carriages and the stalled omnibuses, blows were traded thick and fast. Anything served as a missile. Boots and shoes went whistling through the air on long flat trajectories, and it even seemed that a few revolvers were adding their patriotic reports to the roar of the crowd.

The throng closed in on the staircase and spilled back onto the first steps. One of the parties had clearly been driven back, though the mere onlookers couldn't tell whether the advantage lay with Mandiboy or with Kamerfield.

"I think it would be wise to withdraw," said Fix, who had no wish to see "his man" take a nasty blow or land himself in a nasty fix. "If England comes into any of this and we're recognized, we'll be badly compromised in the brawl."

"An English citizen —" Phileas Fogg began.

But the gentleman never finished his sentence. Behind him, from the terrace above the staircase, came a burst of appalling howls. They were shouting: "Hurrah! Hip! Hip! for Mandiboy!" It was a band of voters arriving to the rescue, taking Kamerfield's supporters in the flank.

Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and Fix found themselves caught between two fires. It was too late to escape. This torrent of men, armed with loaded canes and blackjacks, was irresistible. Phileas Fogg and Fix, shielding the young woman, were horribly jostled. Mr. Fogg, no less unflappable than usual, tried to defend himself with those natural weapons that nature has fitted to the end of every Englishman's arms — but in vain. An enormous fellow with a red goatee, a florid complexion, and broad shoulders, who seemed to be the leader of the gang, raised his formidable fist at Mr. Fogg, and he would have done the gentleman serious damage had not Fix, out of devotion, taken the blow in his place. An enormous lump instantly rose under the detective's silk hat, which was flattened into a mere cap.

"Yankee!" said Mr. Fogg, flinging his adversary a look of deep contempt.

"Englishman!" the other retorted.

"We shall meet again!"

"Whenever you please. Your name?"

"Phileas Fogg. Yours?"

"Colonel Stamp W. Proctor."

That said, the tide swept on past. Fix was knocked down and got back up, his clothes torn but with no serious bruises. His traveling coat had come apart into two unequal halves, and his trousers looked like the sort of breeches certain Indians — a matter of fashion — put on only after first cutting the seat out of them. But on the whole Aouda had been spared, and Fix alone was the worse for it, with his one punch to show.

"Thank you," said Mr. Fogg to the inspector, as soon as they were clear of the crowd.

"Don't mention it," answered Fix, "but come along."

"Where?"

"To a clothier's."

The visit was indeed timely. The clothes of Phileas Fogg and Fix hung in shreds, as if the two gentlemen had been fighting on behalf of the honorable Kamerfield and the honorable Mandiboy.

An hour later, they were properly dressed and hatted again. Then they returned to the International Hotel.

There Passepartout was waiting for his master, armed with half a dozen six-shot dagger-revolvers with center-fire priming. When he caught sight of Fix in Mr. Fogg's company, his brow darkened. But once Aouda had told, in a few words, what had happened, Passepartout brightened up. Fix was plainly no longer an enemy — he was an ally. He was keeping his word.

Dinner over, a coach was brought around to carry the travelers and their luggage to the station. As they were about to step into it, Mr. Fogg said to Fix:

"You haven't seen that Colonel Proctor again?"

"No," Fix replied.

"I shall come back to America to find him," said Phileas Fogg coldly. "It would not be fitting for an English citizen to let himself be treated in such a way."

The inspector smiled and said nothing. But, as you can see, Mr. Fogg was of that breed of Englishman who, while they will not tolerate dueling at home, will fight abroad when it is a matter of defending their honor.

At a quarter to six, the travelers reached the station and found the train ready to leave. Just as Mr. Fogg was about to board, he spotted a railway employee and, catching up with him:

"My friend," he said, "wasn't there some trouble in San Francisco today?"

"It was a rally, sir," the man answered.

"Still, I thought I noticed a certain excitement in the streets."

"It was simply a rally organized for an election."

"The election of a commander in chief, no doubt?" asked Mr. Fogg.

"No, sir. Of a justice of the peace."

At this answer, Phileas Fogg climbed into the car, and the train pulled out at full steam.
