In Which Fix Comes Face to Face with Phileas Fogg

While this scene — which might yet do such grave damage to his future — was playing out, Mr. Fogg was strolling with Aouda through the streets of the English town. Ever since she had accepted his offer to see her safely to Europe, he had had to think through all the details that a journey of such length involves. That an Englishman like himself should circle the globe with a single bag in hand — well and good; but a woman could hardly undertake such a crossing on those terms. Hence the necessity of buying the clothing and other necessities for the trip. Mr. Fogg saw to the task with his usual calm, and to every apology or objection from the young widow, embarrassed by so much kindness, he replied without fail:

"It's in the interest of my journey. It's in my program."

The purchases made, Mr. Fogg and the young woman returned to the hotel and dined at the table d'hôte, which was laid out on a lavish scale. Then Aouda, a little tired, went back up to her room, but not before shaking hands "in the English fashion" with her imperturbable rescuer.

As for the honorable gentleman, he spent the whole evening buried in the Times and the Illustrated London News.

Had he been a man capable of being surprised by anything, it would have been by the failure of his servant to appear at bedtime. But knowing that the Yokohama steamer would not leave Hong Kong before the next morning, he thought no more of it. The following day, Passepartout did not answer Mr. Fogg's ring.

What the honorable gentleman thought on learning that his servant had not come back to the hotel, no one could have said. Mr. Fogg simply picked up his bag, sent word to Aouda, and called for a palanquin.

It was then eight o'clock, and high tide — which the Carnatic needed in order to clear the channel — was set for half past nine.

When the palanquin arrived at the hotel door, Mr. Fogg and Aouda climbed into the comfortable conveyance, and the luggage followed behind on a barrow.

Half an hour later, the travelers stepped down onto the embarkation quay, and there Mr. Fogg learned that the Carnatic had sailed the day before.

Mr. Fogg, who had counted on finding both the steamer and his servant, was now reduced to doing without either. But not a flicker of disappointment crossed his face, and when Aouda looked at him anxiously, he only replied:

"It's a small setback, madam, nothing more."

At that moment a man who had been watching him closely stepped up. It was Inspector Fix, who greeted him and said:

"Aren't you, like me, sir, one of the passengers off the Rangoon that came in yesterday?"

"Yes, sir," Mr. Fogg answered coldly, "but I haven't the honor of —"

"Forgive me. I thought I'd find your servant here."

"Do you know where he is, sir?" the young woman asked quickly.

"What!" said Fix, feigning surprise. "Isn't he with you?"

"No," said Aouda. "He hasn't reappeared since yesterday. Could he have gone aboard the Carnatic without us?"

"Without you, madam?" said the detective. "But — excuse the question — you were meaning to take that steamer yourselves?"

"Yes, sir."

"So was I, madam, and you see me thoroughly put out. The Carnatic finished her repairs and left Hong Kong twelve hours early without warning a soul, and now we'll have to wait eight days for the next departure!"

As he pronounced the words "eight days," Fix felt his heart leap for joy. Eight days! Fogg held at Hong Kong for eight days! That would be time enough for the warrant to arrive. At last luck was breaking the way of the law's representative.

Judge, then, of the blow that landed on him when he heard Phileas Fogg say, in that calm voice of his:

"But there are other ships besides the Carnatic in the port of Hong Kong, I imagine."

And Mr. Fogg, offering his arm to Aouda, headed off toward the docks in search of a ship about to sail.

Fix, thunderstruck, followed. You would have thought a thread tied him to the man.

Yet luck did seem, this time, to abandon the one it had served so well until now. For three hours Phileas Fogg tramped the harbor in every direction, resolved, if it came to that, to charter a vessel to carry him to Yokohama; but he saw only ships loading or unloading, and therefore unable to weigh anchor. Fix began to hope again.

Still, Mr. Fogg was not put off, and he was about to go on searching, even if it meant pushing on as far as Macao, when a sailor hailed him on the outer harbor.

"Is Your Honor looking for a boat?" said the sailor, taking off his cap.

"Have you a boat ready to sail?" asked Mr. Fogg.

"Yes, Your Honor, pilot-boat number 43, the best of the fleet."

"Is she fast?"

"Eight to nine miles, close-hauled. Would you care to see her?"

"Yes."

"Your Honor will be satisfied. Is it a pleasure trip at sea?"

"No. A voyage."

"A voyage?"

"Will you take it on to carry me to Yokohama?"

At these words the sailor's arms fell slack and his eyes went wide.

"Your Honor is joking?" he said.

"No! I've missed the Carnatic, and I have to be at Yokohama by the 14th at the latest to catch the San Francisco steamer."

"I'm sorry," the pilot answered, "but it's impossible."

"I'll offer you a hundred pounds a day, and a bonus of two hundred pounds if I arrive in time."

"You're serious?" asked the pilot.

"Perfectly serious," Mr. Fogg replied.

The pilot had drawn off to one side. He was studying the sea, plainly torn between the itch to earn an enormous sum and the fear of venturing so far. Fix was in mortal agonies.

Meanwhile Mr. Fogg had turned back to Aouda.

"You won't be afraid, madam?" he asked.

"Not with you, Mr. Fogg," the young woman answered.

The pilot had come forward again toward the gentleman, twisting his hat in his hands.

"Well, pilot?" said Mr. Fogg.

"Well, Your Honor," the pilot replied, "I can't risk my men, or myself, or you either, on so long a crossing in a boat of barely twenty tons, and at this time of year. Besides, we wouldn't get there in time, for it's sixteen hundred and fifty miles from Hong Kong to Yokohama."

"Sixteen hundred only," said Mr. Fogg.

"It's the same thing."

Fix breathed a good lungful of air.

"But," the pilot added, "there might be a way to manage it differently."

Fix stopped breathing.

"How?" asked Phileas Fogg.

"By going to Nagasaki, at the southern tip of Japan — eleven hundred miles — or only as far as Shanghai, eight hundred miles from Hong Kong. On that last run we'd keep close to the Chinese coast, which would be a great advantage, all the more since the currents there set to the north."

"Pilot," Phileas Fogg replied, "it's at Yokohama that I have to catch the American mail steamer, not at Shanghai or Nagasaki."

"Why not?" the pilot answered. "The San Francisco steamer doesn't start from Yokohama. She puts in at Yokohama and Nagasaki, but her port of departure is Shanghai."

"You're certain of what you say?"

"Certain."

"And when does the steamer leave Shanghai?"

"On the 11th, at seven in the evening. So we've got four days ahead of us. Four days makes ninety-six hours, and at an average of eight miles an hour, if we're well served, if the wind holds to the southeast, if the sea stays calm, we can knock off the eight hundred miles that lie between us and Shanghai."

"And you could leave —?"

"Within the hour. Just time enough to buy provisions and get under way."

"It's settled... You're the master of the boat?"

"Yes, John Bunsby, master of the Tankadère."

"Would you like something on account?"

"If it's no trouble to Your Honor."

"Here's two hundred pounds on account... Sir," Phileas Fogg added, turning to Fix, "if you'd care to take advantage —"

"Sir," Fix answered firmly, "I was about to ask you that very favor."

"Good. In half an hour we'll be aboard."

"But that poor fellow —" said Aouda, deeply troubled by Passepartout's disappearance.

"I'll do for him everything that can be done," Phileas Fogg replied.

And while Fix — nervous, feverish, seething — made his way to the pilot-boat, the two of them went off to the Hong Kong police offices. There Phileas Fogg gave a description of Passepartout and left a sum sufficient to send him home. The same formality was carried out at the office of the French consular agent, and the palanquin, after a stop at the hotel to collect the luggage, brought the travelers back to the outer harbor.

Three o'clock was striking. Pilot-boat number 43, her crew aboard and her provisions loaded, was ready to weigh anchor.

The Tankadère was a lovely little schooner of twenty tons, sharp in the bow, easy in her handling, long in her waterlines. She looked like a racing yacht. Her gleaming brasswork, her galvanized iron fittings, her deck white as ivory, all showed that her master, John Bunsby, knew how to keep her in trim. Her two masts raked a little aft. She carried a spanker, foresail, staysail, jibs, and topsails, and could rig out a squaresail for running before the wind. She was bound to sail beautifully, and in fact she had already won several prizes in the pilot-boat "matches."

The crew of the Tankadère was made up of master John Bunsby and four men. These were bold seamen of the kind who put out in all weathers in search of ships and know these waters wonderfully well. John Bunsby, a man of about forty-five, sturdy, tanned dark, quick-eyed, with a strong face, steady on his feet and thoroughly on top of his business, would have inspired confidence in the most fearful soul.

Phileas Fogg and Aouda came aboard. Fix was there already. Through the after hatch of the schooner one went down into a square cabin, its walls hollowed into frames above a circular divan. In the middle stood a table lit by a gimbal lamp. It was small, but clean.

"I'm sorry to have nothing better to offer you," said Mr. Fogg to Fix, who bowed without a word.

The police inspector felt something like humiliation at profiting this way from Mr. Fogg's courtesy.

"A polite rogue, to be sure," he thought, "but a rogue all the same!"

At ten minutes past three the sails were hoisted. The flag of England flew at the schooner's gaff. The passengers were seated on deck. Mr. Fogg and Aouda cast one last look at the quay, to see whether Passepartout might not appear.

Fix was not without his own misgivings, for chance might well have led to that very spot the wretched fellow he had treated so shamefully, and then there would have been an explosion of explanations from which the detective would not have come off well. But the Frenchman did not show himself; no doubt the stupefying narcotic still held him in its grip.

At last master John Bunsby stood out to sea, and the Tankadère, taking the wind under her spanker, foresail, and jibs, went bounding off across the waves.
