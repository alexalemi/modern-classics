In Which the Skipper of the Tankadère Risks Losing a Two-Hundred-Pound Reward

It was a bold undertaking, this eight-hundred-mile voyage, on a boat of twenty tons, and above all at that time of year. The seas off China are generally foul, exposed to terrible gales, especially around the equinoxes — and they were still in the first days of November.

Plainly it would have been to the skipper's advantage to carry his passengers all the way to Yokohama, since he was paid by the day. But it would have been reckless in the extreme to attempt such a crossing in these conditions, and it was already a piece of daring — of near foolhardiness — merely to push on as far as Shanghai. Still, John Bunsby had faith in his Tankadère, which rode the swell like a gull, and perhaps he was not wrong.

Through the last hours of that day the Tankadère worked her way out through the fickle channels of Hong Kong, and on every point of sailing — close-hauled or running before the wind — she behaved admirably.

"I don't need to tell you, skipper," said Phileas Fogg as the sloop stood out into open water, "to crowd on all the speed you can."

"Your Honor may leave it to me," answered John Bunsby. "As for sail, we're carrying everything the wind will let us carry. Our topsails would add nothing — they'd only bury her nose and spoil her way."

"That's your trade, skipper, not mine, and I trust you to it."

Phileas Fogg, body erect, legs braced, steady as a sailor, watched the heaving sea without flinching. The young woman, seated aft, felt a stir of emotion as she gazed at that ocean, already darkening in the dusk, which she was daring in so frail a craft. Overhead the white sails spread, bearing her through space like great wings. Lifted by the wind, the sloop seemed to fly through the air.

Night came on. The moon was entering its first quarter, and its feeble light would soon be snuffed out in the mists on the horizon. Clouds were driving up from the east and already overrunning part of the sky.

The skipper had set out his running lights — an indispensable precaution in those crowded waters near the coast. Ships were not seldom met with, and at the speed she was making, the sloop would have shattered at the slightest collision.

Fix brooded up forward. He kept apart, knowing Fogg to be a man of few words. Besides, it went against the grain to talk to this man whose hospitality he was accepting. He was thinking, too, about what came next. It seemed certain to him that Fogg would not stop at Yokohama, that he would take the San Francisco steamer at once in order to reach America, whose vast expanse would guarantee him both impunity and safety. Phileas Fogg's plan struck him as simplicity itself.

Instead of sailing straight from England to the United States like a common crook, this Fogg had gone the long way round and crossed three-quarters of the globe, the better to make sure of the American continent, where he could quietly spend the Bank's stolen fortune once he had thrown the police off the scent. But once on Union soil, what would Fix do? Give the man up? No, a hundred times no! And until he had secured a warrant of extradition, he would not budge an inch from his side. It was his duty, and he would carry it through to the end. In any case, one lucky thing had happened: Passepartout was no longer with his master — and above all, after Fix's confidences, it was vital that master and servant should never meet again.

Phileas Fogg, for his part, was not without a thought for his own servant, so strangely vanished. All things considered, it did not seem impossible to him that, through some misunderstanding, the poor fellow had gone aboard the Carnatic at the last moment. That was Aouda's opinion as well; she deeply missed that honest servant, to whom she owed so much. It might be, then, that they would find him again at Yokohama, and if the Carnatic had carried him there, it would be easy enough to learn.

Toward ten the breeze freshened. It might have been prudent to take in a reef, but the skipper, after carefully studying the state of the sky, left the sail as it stood. Besides, the Tankadère carried canvas beautifully, drawing a deep draught, and everything was ready to bring the sails down fast in case of a squall.

At midnight Phileas Fogg and Aouda went below to the cabin. Fix had gone down before them and stretched himself out on one of the bunks. As for the skipper and his men, they stayed on deck all night.

By the next morning, the 8th of November, at sunrise, the sloop had run more than a hundred miles. The log, cast again and again, showed her average speed to be between eight and nine miles an hour. The Tankadère had the wind on her quarter, all her canvas drawing, and under this point of sailing she was making her best speed. If the wind held on these terms, the odds were with her.

All that day the Tankadère kept close to the coast, where the currents ran in her favor. She held it at five miles off at most on her port quarter, and that coast, its outline ragged, showed itself now and then through the breaks in the weather. With the wind coming off the land, the sea was the less heavy for it — a lucky thing for the sloop, since small craft suffer above all from the swell, which breaks their way and, in the sailors' phrase, "kills them."

Toward noon the breeze slackened a little and hauled to the southeast. The skipper had the topsails set; but two hours later they had to be brought down again, for the wind was freshening once more.

Mr. Fogg and the young woman, luckily immune to seasickness, ate the tinned food and ship's biscuit with a hearty appetite. Fix was invited to share their meal and had to accept, knowing well that a stomach, like a boat, must be ballasted — but it galled him! To travel at this man's expense, to eat this man's own provisions, struck him as something less than honorable. He ate all the same — on the wing, it's true — but eat he did.

When the meal was over, however, he felt he ought to take Fogg aside, and he said to him:

"Sir…"

That word "sir" scraped his lips, and he had to hold himself back from taking this "sir" by the collar!

"Sir, you were very obliging to offer me passage aboard your boat. But though my means don't allow me to do things as handsomely as you, I mean to pay my share—"

"Let's not speak of it, sir," answered Mr. Fogg.

"But I insist—"

"No, sir," Fogg repeated, in a tone that brooked no reply. "It comes under general expenses!"

Fix bowed; he was choking; and going to stretch himself out in the bow of the sloop, he did not say another word the whole day.

Meanwhile they were making fast time. John Bunsby had good hopes. Several times he told Mr. Fogg that they would reach Shanghai in good time. Mr. Fogg answered simply that he counted on it. And the whole crew of the little sloop threw themselves into the work with a will. The reward tempted these good fellows. So not a sheet that was not conscientiously hauled taut! Not a sail that was not vigorously set up! Not a yaw the helmsman could be blamed for! They could not have been worked harder in a regatta of the Royal Yacht Club.

By evening the skipper's log gave a run of two hundred and twenty miles since Hong Kong, and Phileas Fogg could hope that on reaching Yokohama he would have no delay to enter against his schedule. So the first serious setback he had met since leaving London would, in all likelihood, do him no harm at all.

During the night, toward the small hours of the morning, the Tankadère stood boldly into the Strait of Fo-Kien, which divides the great island of Formosa from the Chinese coast, and cut across the Tropic of Cancer. The sea ran very hard in that strait, full of eddies stirred up by the cross-currents. The sloop labored heavily. The short seas broke her way. It grew very hard to keep one's feet on deck.

With the coming of day the wind freshened still more. There was the look of a gale in the sky. The barometer, moreover, foretold a coming change in the weather; its daily march was erratic, and the mercury swung about capriciously. The sea, too, could be seen heaving up toward the southeast in long swells "that smelled of the storm." The evening before, the sun had set in a red haze, amid the phosphorescent glittering of the ocean.

The skipper studied that ugly aspect of the sky for a long while and muttered something unintelligible between his teeth. At one point, finding himself near his passenger, he said in a low voice:

"May I tell Your Honor everything?"

"Everything," answered Phileas Fogg.

"Well then — we're going to get a gale."

"From the north or the south?" Mr. Fogg asked simply.

"From the south. Look. It's a typhoon brewing!"

"Then let it be the typhoon from the south, since it will push us the right way," answered Mr. Fogg.

"If you take it like that," the skipper replied, "I've nothing more to say!"

John Bunsby's forebodings did not deceive him. At a less advanced season of the year the typhoon, in the words of a famous meteorologist, would have spent itself like a luminous cascade of electric flame; but at the winter equinox there was reason to fear it would break loose with fury.

The skipper took his precautions in advance. He had all the sloop's sails furled and the yards brought down on deck. The topmasts were struck. The jib-boom was run in. The hatches were carefully battened down. Not a drop of water could get into the boat's hull now. A single triangular sail, a storm-jib of heavy canvas, was hoisted forward to keep the sloop running before the wind. And they waited.

John Bunsby had urged his passengers to go below into the cabin; but in a cramped space, nearly airless, and with the jolting of the swell, that confinement held no charm. Neither Mr. Fogg, nor Aouda, nor even Fix would agree to leave the deck.

Toward eight the squall of rain and blast came down on them. With no more than her scrap of canvas, the Tankadère was snatched up like a feather by a wind of which no exact idea can be given when it blows a full storm. To compare her speed to four times that of a locomotive going flat out would still be to fall short of the truth.

All day long the boat ran on this way toward the north, borne up by the monstrous seas, keeping, mercifully, a pace as swift as their own. Twenty times she was on the point of being pooped by one of those mountains of water that reared up astern; but a deft turn of the helm from the skipper warded off the disaster. The passengers were sometimes drenched from head to foot by the spray, which they took philosophically. Fix grumbled, no doubt, but the intrepid Aouda, her eyes fixed on her companion, whose coolness she could only admire, showed herself worthy of him and braved the tempest at his side. As for Phileas Fogg, it seemed as though this typhoon were part of his schedule.

Until now the Tankadère had always held her course to the north; but toward evening, as might have been feared, the wind veered three-quarters and hauled to the northwest. The sloop, now offering her flank to the seas, was shaken frightfully. The sea struck her with a violence well fitted to terrify anyone who does not know how solidly every part of a vessel is bound to the rest.

With the night the storm rose still higher. Seeing the darkness come on, and with the darkness the tempest grow, John Bunsby felt keen misgivings. He wondered whether it might not be time to put in somewhere, and he consulted his crew.

His men consulted, John Bunsby stepped up to Mr. Fogg and said:

"I think, Your Honor, that we'd do well to make one of the ports along the coast."

"I think so too," answered Phileas Fogg.

"Ah!" said the skipper. "But which one?"

"I know only one," Mr. Fogg answered calmly.

"And that is—?"

"Shanghai."

For a few moments the skipper could not grasp what this answer meant, all the stubbornness and tenacity it held. Then he cried out:

"Well — yes! Your Honor is right. To Shanghai!"

And the Tankadère's course was held imperturbably to the north.

A truly terrible night! It was a miracle the little sloop did not capsize. Twice she was swamped, and everything on board would have been swept away had the lashings given. Aouda was battered and spent, but she did not let a single complaint be heard. More than once Mr. Fogg had to rush to her side to shield her from the fury of the seas.

Day came again. The storm was still raging with the utmost violence. The wind, however, fell back into the southeast. This was a favorable shift, and the Tankadère once more made her way over that heaving sea, whose waves now clashed with the ones raised by the wind's new quarter. From this came a battering of cross-seas that would have crushed a less solidly built craft.

Now and again the coast could be glimpsed through the torn mists, but not a ship in sight. The Tankadère alone was keeping the sea.

At noon there were some signs of a lull, which grew more marked as the sun sank toward the horizon.

The storm's short life was owing to its very violence. The passengers, utterly worn out, were able to eat a little and get some rest.

The night was relatively quiet. The skipper had his sails set again, close-reefed. The boat's speed was considerable. The next day, the 11th, at daybreak, having taken his bearings from the coast, John Bunsby could declare that they were not a hundred miles from Shanghai.

A hundred miles, and only this one day left to make them! It was that very evening that Mr. Fogg must reach Shanghai, if he did not want to miss the departure of the Yokohama steamer. But for this storm, in which he had lost several hours, he would at this moment have been within thirty miles of the port.

The breeze slackened noticeably, but happily the sea fell with it. The sloop covered herself with canvas. Topsails, staysails, outer jib — everything drew, and the sea foamed under her stem.

At noon the Tankadère was no more than forty-five miles from Shanghai. She had six hours left to gain the port before the Yokohama steamer sailed.

Fears ran high on board. They meant to arrive at any cost. Every heart — save Phileas Fogg's, no doubt — was beating with impatience. The little sloop had to hold an average of nine miles an hour, and the wind was slackening still! It was a fitful breeze, capricious gusts coming off the coast. They would pass, and the sea smoothed over the moment they were gone.

Yet the boat was so light, her sails so high and of such fine cloth, that they gathered the wild little gusts so well that, with the current helping, by six o'clock John Bunsby reckoned no more than ten miles to the Shanghai river — for the city itself lies at least twelve miles above the river's mouth.

At seven they were still three miles from Shanghai. A tremendous oath burst from the skipper's lips… The two-hundred-pound reward was clearly about to slip through his fingers. He looked at Mr. Fogg. Mr. Fogg was impassive — and yet his whole fortune was riding on that moment…

At that moment, too, a long black spindle, crowned with a plume of smoke, appeared just above the water. It was the American steamer, standing out at her scheduled hour.

"Damnation!" cried John Bunsby, thrusting away the helm with a desperate arm.

"Signals!" said Phileas Fogg simply. A little bronze cannon lay along the bow of the Tankadère. It was used to make signals in foggy weather.

The cannon was loaded to the muzzle, but just as the skipper was about to touch a live coal to the priming:

"Colors at half-mast," said Mr. Fogg.

The flag was run down to half-mast. It was a signal of distress, and there was hope that the American steamer, on seeing it, would alter her course for a moment to close with the boat.

"Fire!" said Mr. Fogg.

And the report of the little bronze cannon burst upon the air.
