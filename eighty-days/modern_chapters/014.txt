In Which the Bag of Banknotes Grows Lighter by a Few Thousand Pounds More

The train pulled into the station. Passepartout stepped down from the car first, followed by Mr. Fogg, who handed his young companion onto the platform. Phileas Fogg meant to go straight to the Hong Kong steamer and settle Aouda comfortably aboard, for he did not intend to leave her side so long as she was in a country so dangerous for her.

Just as Mr. Fogg was about to leave the station, a policeman came up to him and said:

"Mr. Phileas Fogg?"

"That is my name."

"And this man is your servant?" the policeman added, pointing at Passepartout.

"He is."

"Then be so good as to come with me, both of you."

Mr. Fogg made not the slightest movement that might have betrayed any surprise. This officer was an agent of the law, and to any Englishman the law is sacred. Passepartout, with his French habits, was inclined to argue the point, but the policeman touched him with his little rod, and Phileas Fogg signaled him to obey.

"May this young lady come with us?" Mr. Fogg asked.

"She may," the policeman answered.

He led Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and Passepartout to a palki-ghari — a sort of four-wheeled, four-seated carriage drawn by two horses — and off they went. No one spoke during the drive, which lasted about twenty minutes.

The carriage passed first through the "black town," with its narrow streets lined with hovels where a cosmopolitan population, filthy and in rags, swarmed and jostled; then it crossed the European quarter, bright with brick houses, shaded by coconut palms, bristling with ships' masts, and already — despite the early hour — crossed by elegant riders and magnificent teams of horses.

The palki-ghari stopped before a plain-looking building that plainly was not meant for domestic use. The policeman handed his prisoners down — for that was truly the word for them now — and led them into a room with barred windows, telling them:

"At half past eight you will appear before Judge Obadiah."

Then he withdrew and shut the door.

"Well! We're caught!" cried Passepartout, dropping into a chair.

Aouda turned at once to Mr. Fogg and said, in a voice she tried and failed to steady:

"Sir, you must leave me! It is on my account that you are being pursued! It is for having saved me!"

Phileas Fogg replied simply that this was not possible. Prosecuted over that business of the suttee! Out of the question! How would the plaintiffs dare show their faces? There was some mistake. In any case, Mr. Fogg added, he would not abandon the young woman, and he would see her to Hong Kong.

"But the boat leaves at noon!" Passepartout pointed out.

"Before noon we shall be aboard," the imperturbable gentleman answered simply.

He said it so flatly that Passepartout could not help telling himself:

"By heaven! It's certain! Before noon we'll be aboard!" But he was not reassured in the least.

At half past eight the door of the room opened. The policeman reappeared and showed the prisoners into the next room. It was a courtroom, and a fair-sized public of Europeans and natives already filled the gallery.

Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and Passepartout took their seats on a bench facing the places reserved for the magistrate and the clerk.

This magistrate, Judge Obadiah, entered almost at once, followed by the clerk. He was a big, perfectly round man. He lifted a wig off a nail and clapped it briskly onto his head.

"The first case," he said.

Then, putting a hand to his head:

"Hey! This isn't my wig!"

"Quite right, Mr. Obadiah — it's mine," answered the clerk.

"My dear Mr. Oysterpuff, how do you expect a judge to hand down a sound sentence in a clerk's wig?"

The wigs were duly exchanged. Through all these preliminaries Passepartout was seething with impatience, for the hand of the big courtroom clock seemed to him to be racing around the dial at a terrifying rate.

"The first case," Judge Obadiah began again.

"Phileas Fogg?" said Oysterpuff the clerk.

"Here," answered Mr. Fogg.

"Passepartout?"

"Present!" answered Passepartout.

"Good!" said Judge Obadiah. "For two days now, prisoners, we have been watching for you on every train from Bombay."

"But what are we charged with?" cried Passepartout, out of patience.

"You are about to find out," said the judge.

"Sir," Mr. Fogg then said, "I am an English citizen, and I have the right —"

"Have you been treated with any disrespect?" asked Mr. Obadiah.

"None at all."

"Good. Bring in the plaintiffs."

At the judge's order a door opened, and an usher led in three Hindu priests.

"That's it exactly!" muttered Passepartout. "These are the rascals who wanted to burn our young lady!"

The priests stood before the judge, and the clerk read aloud a complaint of sacrilege lodged against one Phileas Fogg and his servant, accused of profaning a place consecrated by the Brahmin religion.

"You have heard the charge?" the judge asked Phileas Fogg.

"Yes, sir," answered Mr. Fogg, consulting his watch, "and I confess it."

"Ah! You confess?"

"I confess, and I wait for these three priests to confess in their turn what they meant to do at the pagoda of Pillaji."

The priests looked at one another. They seemed to understand nothing of the accused man's words.

"Of course!" cried Passepartout hotly. "At that pagoda of Pillaji, in front of which they were about to burn their victim!"

Fresh astonishment from the priests, and deep bewilderment from Judge Obadiah.

"What victim?" he asked. "Burn whom? In the middle of the city of Bombay?"

"Bombay?" cried Passepartout.

"Certainly. This has nothing to do with the pagoda of Pillaji, but with the pagoda of Malabar Hill, in Bombay."

"And here, as evidence, are the desecrator's shoes," the clerk added, setting a pair of shoes on his desk.

"My shoes!" cried Passepartout, who, caught utterly off guard, could not hold back the involuntary exclamation.

One can imagine the confusion that had taken hold of master and servant alike. That incident at the Bombay pagoda they had forgotten entirely — and it was that very thing that had now hauled them before the magistrate of Calcutta.

For the truth was that Detective Fix had grasped exactly how much use he could make of this unfortunate affair. Delaying his departure by twelve hours, he had made himself counsel to the priests of Malabar Hill; he had promised them substantial damages, knowing perfectly well that the English government came down hard on offenses of this kind; and then, by the next train, he had launched them on the trail of the sacrilege. But because so much time had been spent rescuing the young widow, Fix and the Hindus reached Calcutta ahead of Phileas Fogg and his servant, whom the magistrates, tipped off by telegram, were to arrest as they stepped off the train. Imagine Fix's dismay when he learned that Phileas Fogg had not yet arrived in the capital of India. He must have supposed that his thief, stopping at one of the stations along the Peninsular Railway, had gone to ground in the northern provinces. For twenty-four hours, in a fever of anxiety, Fix kept watch at the station. Imagine his joy, then, when that very morning he saw the man step down from the car — in the company, it is true, of a young woman whose presence he could not account for. He set a policeman on him at once, and that is how Mr. Fogg, Passepartout, and the widow of the rajah of Bundelkund came to be led before Judge Obadiah.

And had Passepartout been less absorbed in his own troubles, he would have spotted, in a corner of the courtroom, the detective following the proceedings with an interest easy enough to understand — for at Calcutta, as at Bombay, as at Suez, the warrant of arrest still had not reached him.

Meanwhile Judge Obadiah had taken formal note of the admission that had slipped out of Passepartout, who would have given all he owned to take back his rash words.

"The facts are admitted?" said the judge.

"Admitted," answered Mr. Fogg coldly.

"Whereas," the judge went on, "whereas English law means to protect equally and rigorously all the religions of the peoples of India; and the offense being admitted by the said Passepartout, convicted of having profaned with a sacrilegious foot the pavement of the pagoda of Malabar Hill, in Bombay, on the 20th of October — the court sentences the said Passepartout to fifteen days' imprisonment and a fine of three hundred pounds."

"Three hundred pounds?" cried Passepartout, who was genuinely stung by nothing but the fine.

"Silence!" the usher shrieked.

"And," Judge Obadiah added, "whereas it is not materially proven that there was no collusion between the servant and the master, and whereas the latter must in any case be held responsible for the acts of a servant in his pay, the court holds the said Phileas Fogg and sentences him to eight days' imprisonment and a fine of one hundred and fifty pounds. Clerk, call the next case!"

In his corner, Fix felt an unspeakable satisfaction. Phileas Fogg held eight days at Calcutta — that was more than enough time for the warrant to reach him.

Passepartout was thunderstruck. This sentence ruined his master. A wager of twenty thousand pounds lost, and all because he, like a gaping fool, had walked into that cursed pagoda!

Phileas Fogg, as much master of himself as if the sentence had nothing to do with him, had not so much as knit his brow. But at the very moment the clerk was calling the next case, he rose and said:

"I offer bail."

"That is your right," the judge answered.

Fix felt a chill go down his spine, but he recovered his confidence when he heard the judge — "in view of the foreign status of Phileas Fogg and his servant" — set bail for each of them at the enormous sum of one thousand pounds.

That would cost Mr. Fogg two thousand pounds if he did not serve out his sentence.

"I'll pay," said the gentleman.

And from the bag Passepartout carried, he drew a packet of banknotes and laid it on the clerk's desk.

"This sum will be returned to you on your release from prison," said the judge. "In the meantime, you are free on bail."

"Come," said Phileas Fogg to his servant.

"But at least make them give back the shoes!" cried Passepartout with a burst of rage.

They gave him back his shoes.

"An expensive pair, these!" he muttered. "More than a thousand pounds apiece! And they pinch, into the bargain!"

Passepartout, utterly crestfallen, followed Mr. Fogg, who had offered his arm to the young woman. Fix still hoped that his thief would never bring himself to give up that sum of two thousand pounds and would serve out his eight days in prison. So he set off once more on Fogg's trail.

Mr. Fogg took a carriage, into which Aouda, Passepartout, and he climbed at once. Fix ran along behind the carriage, which soon halted at one of the city's quays.

Half a mile out in the roadstead lay the Rangoon at anchor, its departure flag run up to the masthead. Eleven o'clock struck. Mr. Fogg was an hour ahead of schedule. Fix saw him step down from the carriage and get into a boat with Aouda and his servant. The detective stamped his foot on the ground.

"The scoundrel!" he cried. "He's leaving! Two thousand pounds thrown away! Spendthrift as any thief! Ah, I'll tail him to the ends of the earth if I have to; but at the rate he's going, every pound of the stolen money will be gone!"

The police inspector had good grounds for the thought. In fact, since leaving London — what with travel costs and tips, the elephant, the bail money and the fines — Phileas Fogg had already scattered more than five thousand pounds along his route, and the detective's percentage of the recovered sum was shrinking all the time.
