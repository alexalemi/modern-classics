In Which Passepartout Cannot Manage to Make Anyone Listen to Reason

Leaving Great Salt Lake and the station at Ogden, the train climbed northward for an hour, as far as the Weber River, having covered some nine hundred miles since San Francisco. From that point it swung east again through the rugged bulk of the Wahsatch Mountains. It was in this stretch of country, between these mountains and the Rocky Mountains proper, that American engineers ran up against their most serious difficulties. Here the government subsidy rose to forty-eight thousand dollars a mile, against a mere sixteen thousand out on the plains — but the engineers, as has been said, did not do violence to nature so much as outwit her, going around the obstacles rather than through them. To reach the great basin, a single tunnel, fourteen thousand feet long, was the only one bored along the whole run of the line.

It was at Salt Lake itself that the track had so far reached its highest point. From there its profile traced a long, drawn-out curve, dropping toward the valley of the Bitter Creek before climbing again to the divide between the waters of the Atlantic and the Pacific. Rivers were plentiful in this mountainous region. The Muddy, the Green, and others had to be crossed on little trestle bridges. Passepartout had grown more impatient the nearer they came to the goal. But Fix, for his part, would have given anything to be out of this awkward country already. He dreaded delays, he feared accidents, and he was in more of a hurry than Phileas Fogg himself to set foot on English soil.

At ten o'clock that night the train stopped at Fort Bridger station, which it left again almost at once, and twenty miles farther on it entered the state of Wyoming — the old Dakota — following the whole valley of the Bitter Creek, which feeds part of the waters that make up the river system of the Colorado.

The next day, the 7th of December, there was a quarter-hour halt at Green River station. Snow had fallen fairly heavily during the night, but mixed with rain and half melted, it was in no state to slow the train down. Still, the bad weather did nothing to ease Passepartout's mind, for a heavy build-up of snow, miring the wheels of the cars, would certainly have wrecked the journey.

"What an idea," he said to himself, "for my master to go traveling in winter! Couldn't he have waited for the fine weather to better his chances?"

But just now, while the honest fellow was fretting over nothing but the state of the sky and the falling temperature, Aouda was gripped by fears of a far keener kind, springing from an altogether different cause.

For a few of the passengers had stepped down from their car and were strolling along the platform of the Green River station, waiting for the train to leave. And there, through the window, the young woman recognized among them Colonel Stamp W. Proctor — the American who had behaved so grossly toward Phileas Fogg during the meeting at San Francisco. Not wishing to be seen, Aouda drew back from the glass.

The sight left her badly shaken. She had grown attached to this man who, coldly though he did it, gave her every day the marks of the most absolute devotion. She did not yet grasp, no doubt, the full depth of the feeling her rescuer stirred in her, and for the moment she gave that feeling no name but gratitude — though, without her knowing it, there was already more than that in it. And so her heart clenched when she recognized the coarse creature to whom Mr. Fogg meant, sooner or later, to call for an accounting. Plainly it was pure chance that had put Colonel Proctor on this train; but there he was, all the same, and Phileas Fogg had to be kept from catching sight of his adversary at any cost.

Once the train was moving again, Aouda seized on a moment when Mr. Fogg had dozed off to bring Fix and Passepartout up to speed.

"That Proctor is on the train!" cried Fix. "Well, set your mind at rest, madam. Before he has to deal with — with Mr. Fogg, he'll have to deal with me! It seems to me that in all this, I'm still the one who took the worst of the insults!"

"And what's more," Passepartout added, "I'll take him on myself, colonel or no colonel."

"Mr. Fix," Aouda went on, "Mr. Fogg will let no one else take on the job of avenging him. He is quite capable — he said so himself — of coming back to America to hunt down the man who insulted him. So if he catches sight of Colonel Proctor, we won't be able to prevent a confrontation, and it could lead to dreadful results. He simply must not see him."

"You're right, madam," Fix answered. "A run-in could ruin everything. Winner or loser, Mr. Fogg would be delayed, and —"

"And," Passepartout put in, "that would play right into the hands of the gentlemen of the Reform Club. In four days we'll be in New York! Well, if for four days my master doesn't leave his car, we can hope that chance won't bring him face to face with that cursed American — may God confound him! So we'll just have to keep him from —"

The conversation broke off. Mr. Fogg had woken, and was gazing out at the country through the snow-flecked window. But later on, without being overheard by his master or by Aouda, Passepartout said to the police inspector:

"Would you really fight for him?"

"I'll do anything to bring him back to Europe alive!" answered Fix simply, in a tone that spoke of an iron resolve.

Passepartout felt something like a shudder run through his whole body, but his convictions where his master was concerned did not weaken in the least.

And now — was there any way at all of keeping Mr. Fogg in this compartment, to head off any meeting between the colonel and himself? It ought not to be hard, the gentleman being of a naturally unrestless and incurious disposition. In any case, the police inspector believed he had hit on the means, for a few moments later he was saying to Phileas Fogg:

"They're long, slow hours, sir, that one spends this way on a railway."

"They are indeed," replied the gentleman, "but they pass."

"On board the steamers," Fix went on, "you were in the habit of playing your whist?"

"Yes," answered Phileas Fogg, "but here it would be difficult. I have neither cards nor partners."

"Oh, cards — we'll manage to buy some. They sell everything in the American cars. As for partners, if by chance, madam, you —"

"Certainly, sir," the young woman answered readily. "I know whist. It's part of an English education."

"And I," Fix resumed, "have some claim to playing the game well. So, the three of us and a dummy —"

"As you please, sir," said Phileas Fogg, delighted to take up his favorite pastime again — even on a railway.

Passepartout was dispatched to find the steward, and he came back before long with two full packs, counters, chips, and a small baize-covered board. Nothing was missing. The game began. Aouda knew whist quite well enough, and even earned a few compliments from the exacting Phileas Fogg. As for the inspector, he was simply first-rate, worthy of holding his own against the gentleman.

"Now," Passepartout said to himself, "we've got him. He won't budge!"

At eleven in the morning the train had reached the divide between the two oceans. This was at Bridger Pass, seven thousand five hundred and twenty-four English feet above sea level, one of the highest points touched by the line on its passage through the Rockies. Another two hundred miles or so, and the travelers would at last be out on those long plains that stretch all the way to the Atlantic — plains that nature had made so obliging for the laying of a railway.

On the slope of the Atlantic basin the first rivers were already unfolding, tributaries and sub-tributaries of the North Platte. The whole northern and eastern horizon was walled off by that immense semicircular curtain that forms the northern portion of the Rocky Mountains, crowned by Laramie Peak. Between this great curve and the railway line lay vast, well-watered plains. To the right of the track rose the first ramps of the mountain mass that swings around to the south as far as the sources of the Arkansas River, one of the great tributaries of the Missouri.

At half past twelve the travelers caught a glimpse, for a moment, of Fort Halleck, which commands that country. A few more hours, and the crossing of the Rockies would be done. There was reason to hope, then, that no accident would mark the train's passage through this difficult region. The snow had stopped falling. The weather was turning to a dry cold. Great birds, startled by the locomotive, fled off into the distance. No wild beast, bear or wolf, showed itself on the plain. It was the desert in its immense bareness.

After a fairly comfortable lunch, served in the car itself, Mr. Fogg and his partners had just resumed their endless whist when a series of violent whistle-blasts sounded. The train stopped.

Passepartout put his head out the window and saw nothing to explain the halt. No station was in sight.

For a moment Aouda and Fix could fear that Mr. Fogg meant to climb down onto the track. But the gentleman merely said to his servant:

"Go see what it is."

Passepartout bolted out of the car. Some forty passengers had already left their seats, and among them Colonel Stamp W. Proctor.

The train had halted before a signal turned to red, which closed the line. The engineer and the conductor had climbed down and were arguing rather heatedly with a flagman whom the stationmaster of Medicine Bow, the next station along, had sent out to meet the train. Passengers had drawn near and were joining in the discussion — among them the aforesaid Colonel Proctor, loud of voice and imperious of gesture.

Passepartout, having come up to the group, heard the flagman saying:

"No! There's no way through! The Medicine Bow bridge is shaky and wouldn't bear the weight of the train."

The bridge in question was a suspension bridge, thrown across a rapid a mile from the spot where the train had stopped. According to the flagman, it was in danger of collapse, several of the cables were broken, and it was impossible to risk crossing it. The flagman was in no way exaggerating when he insisted there was no getting through. And besides, given the happy-go-lucky habits of Americans, one may say that when they do take it into their heads to be careful, it would be madness not to be.

Passepartout, not daring to go and warn his master, listened with his teeth clenched, still as a statue.

"Well now!" cried Colonel Proctor. "We're not going to sit here, I suppose, taking root in the snow!"

"Colonel," answered the conductor, "we've telegraphed the Omaha station to ask for a train, but it isn't likely to reach Medicine Bow before six hours are out."

"Six hours!" cried Passepartout.

"No doubt," answered the conductor. "Besides, we'll need that long to reach the station on foot."

"On foot!" cried all the passengers.

"But how far off is this station?" one of them asked the conductor.

"Twelve miles, on the far side of the river."

"Twelve miles in the snow!" cried Stamp W. Proctor.

The colonel let fly a broadside of oaths, cursing the company, cursing the conductor, and Passepartout, furious, was not far from joining the chorus. Here was a hard, physical obstacle against which, this time, all his master's banknotes would come to nothing.

What was more, the disappointment was general among the passengers, who, quite apart from the delay, now saw themselves obliged to tramp some fifteen miles across a snow-covered plain. And so there rose a hubbub, a storm of exclamations and shouting, that would certainly have caught the attention of Phileas Fogg, had that gentleman not been absorbed in his game.

Meanwhile Passepartout found himself under the necessity of warning him, and, head down, he was heading toward the car when the train's engineer — a genuine Yankee named Forster — raised his voice and said:

"Gentlemen, there might just be a way through."

"Over the bridge?" asked a passenger.

"Over the bridge."

"With our train?" asked the colonel.

"With our train."

Passepartout had stopped short, drinking in the engineer's words.

"But the bridge is about to give way!" the conductor objected.

"Never mind," answered Forster. "I think that by launching the train at its top speed, we'd have some chance of getting across."

"The devil!" said Passepartout.

But a fair number of the passengers had been won over to the idea at once. It pleased Colonel Proctor in particular. This hothead found the thing entirely doable. He even reminded them that engineers had had the notion of crossing rivers "without a bridge" using rigid trains launched at full speed, and so on. And in the end, everyone with a stake in the question came around to the engineer's view.

"We've got fifty chances of making it," said one.

"Sixty," said another.

"Eighty! — ninety in a hundred!"

Passepartout was dumbfounded, though he was ready to try anything to get across the Medicine Creek — but the attempt struck him as a little too "American."

"Besides," he thought, "there's a much simpler thing to do, and these people don't even think of it!"

"Sir," he said to one of the passengers, "the way the engineer proposes seems to me a bit chancy, but —"

"Eighty chances!" answered the passenger, and turned his back on him.

"I know that," said Passepartout, turning to another gentleman, "but a simple thought —"

"No thinking, it's no use!" answered the American he had addressed, with a shrug, "since the engineer swears we'll get across!"

"No doubt," Passepartout went on, "we'll get across, but it might perhaps be more prudent —"

"What! Prudent!" cried Colonel Proctor, whom the word, caught by chance, made leap into the air. "At full speed, you're told! Do you understand? At full speed!"

"I know — I understand," Passepartout kept repeating, though no one would let him finish his sentence, "but it would be, if not more prudent — since the word offends you — at least more natural —"

"Who? what? which? What's the matter with this fellow and his natural?" came the cry from all sides.

The poor fellow no longer knew whose ear he might catch.

"Are you afraid?" Colonel Proctor demanded of him.

"Me, afraid!" cried Passepartout. "Well, so be it! I'll show these people that a Frenchman can be as American as they are!"

"All aboard! All aboard!" the conductor was shouting.

"Yes! All aboard," Passepartout kept repeating, "all aboard! And right away! But no one will stop me from thinking it would have been more natural to send us passengers across the bridge on foot first, and the train after!"

But no one heard this wise reflection, and no one would have cared to admit that it was right.

The passengers had climbed back into their cars. Passepartout took his seat again, saying nothing of what had passed. The players were wholly given over to their whist.

The locomotive whistled vigorously. The engineer, throwing the engine into reverse, ran the train backward for nearly a mile — drawing back like a jumper about to take his run-up.

Then, at a second whistle-blast, the forward march began again: it gathered pace; soon the speed became frightful; nothing could be heard but a single long whinny pouring out of the locomotive; the pistons beat twenty strokes to the second; the axles smoked in their grease-boxes. One felt, so to speak, that the whole train, traveling at a hundred miles an hour, no longer bore down on the rails. Speed was devouring weight.

And they got across! It was like a flash of lightning. No one saw a thing of the bridge. The train leapt, one might say, from one bank to the other, and the engineer only managed to stop his runaway machine five miles beyond the station.

But no sooner had the train cleared the river than the bridge, wrecked for good, came crashing down into the rapid of Medicine Bow.
