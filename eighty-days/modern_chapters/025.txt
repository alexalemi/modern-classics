In Which the Party Boards the Pacific Railroad Express

"Ocean to Ocean" — so the Americans put it — and those three words really ought to be the general name for the great trunk line that crosses the United States at their widest point. But in fact the Pacific Railroad divides into two distinct parts: the Central Pacific, between San Francisco and Ogden, and the Union Pacific, between Ogden and Omaha. There five separate lines meet, putting Omaha in frequent communication with New York.

New York and San Francisco are therefore now joined by an unbroken ribbon of metal measuring no less than three thousand seven hundred and eighty-six miles. Between Omaha and the Pacific, the railroad crosses country still frequented by Indians and wild beasts — a vast stretch of territory the Mormons began to settle around 1845, after they had been driven out of Illinois.

In earlier days, under the most favorable conditions, it took six months to travel from New York to San Francisco. Now it takes seven days.

It was in 1862 that, over the objections of the Southern representatives — who wanted a line farther south — the route of the railroad was fixed between the forty-first and forty-second parallels. President Lincoln, of such sadly missed memory, himself set the head of the new line in the State of Nebraska, at the city of Omaha. Work began at once and was pushed forward with that American energy that is neither red tape nor bureaucracy. Speed of labor was in no way allowed to harm the soundness of the work. Out on the prairie they advanced at the rate of a mile and a half a day. A locomotive, running on the rails laid the day before, would bring up the rails for the next day and roll along their surface as fast as they were set down.

The Pacific Railroad throws off several branches along its course, in the States of Iowa, Kansas, Colorado, and Oregon. Leaving Omaha, it follows the left bank of the Platte River as far as the mouth of the north fork, takes up the south fork, crosses the Laramie country and the Wahsatch Mountains, skirts the Salt Lake, reaches Salt Lake City, the Mormon capital, plunges into the Tuilla Valley, runs along the American desert, the Cedar and Humboldt mountains, the Humboldt River, and the Sierra Nevada, and comes down again by Sacramento to the Pacific — all without the grade of this route ever exceeding one hundred and twelve feet to the mile, even in crossing the Rocky Mountains.

Such was the long artery that the trains ran in seven days, and that was going to let the honorable Phileas Fogg — so he hoped, at least — catch the Liverpool steamer at New York on the 11th.

The car Phileas Fogg occupied was a kind of long omnibus resting on two four-wheeled trucks, whose play allowed it to take curves of small radius. Inside there were no compartments: just two rows of seats down each side, set crosswise to the axis, with an aisle between them leading to the washrooms and other conveniences each car is fitted with. All along the train, the cars connected to one another by gangways, and passengers could walk from one end of the train to the other, which put at their disposal parlor cars, observation cars, dining cars, and coffee cars. The only thing missing was a theater car. But that will come one day.

Along the gangways there passed a ceaseless traffic of booksellers and newsmen crying their wares, and hawkers of drink, food, and cigars, who never lacked for customers.

The travelers had left the Oakland station at six in the evening. Night had already fallen — a cold, dark night, the sky overcast with clouds that threatened to turn to snow. The train did not go very fast. Counting the stops, it made no more than twenty miles an hour, a pace that should nonetheless get it across the United States within the scheduled time.

There was little talk in the car. Besides, sleep would soon be creeping over the travelers. Passepartout found himself seated next to the police inspector, but he did not speak to him. Since the recent events, their relations had cooled noticeably. No more warmth, no more familiarity. Fix had changed nothing in his manner; Passepartout, on the contrary, kept himself severely on guard, ready at the least suspicion to throttle his old friend.

An hour after the train pulled out, snow began to fall — a fine snow that luckily could do nothing to slow the train down. Through the windows one could now see only a vast white sheet, against which the locomotive's steam, uncoiling its scrolls, looked grayish.

At eight o'clock a steward came into the car and announced to the travelers that the hour for bed had come. This was a sleeping car, and in a few minutes it was turned into a dormitory. The seat backs folded down, carefully bundled berths unrolled by some ingenious mechanism, cabins were improvised in an instant, and each traveler soon had a comfortable bed at his disposal, screened by thick curtains from any prying eye. The sheets were white, the pillows soft. There was nothing left to do but lie down and sleep — which everyone did, as though tucked into the snug cabin of a steamer — while the train raced along at full steam across the State of California.

In the stretch of country between San Francisco and Sacramento, the ground is not much broken up. This part of the line, under the name of the Central Pacific road, first took Sacramento as its starting point and pushed eastward to meet the one setting out from Omaha. From San Francisco to the Californian capital, the line ran straight to the northeast, following the American River, which empties into San Pablo Bay. The hundred and twenty miles between these two important cities were covered in six hours, and toward midnight, while they slept their first sleep, the travelers passed through Sacramento. So they saw nothing of that sizable city, seat of the California legislature — neither its fine quays, nor its wide streets, nor its splendid hotels, nor its squares, nor its temples.

Out of Sacramento, the train passed the stations of Junction, Roclin, Auburn, and Colfax, and struck into the mass of the Sierra Nevada. It was seven in the morning when it ran through Cisco. An hour later the dormitory had become an ordinary car again, and through the windows the travelers could catch the picturesque views of this mountain country. The line bent to every whim of the Sierra — here clinging to the mountainside, there hung over sheer drops, dodging sharp angles by bold curves, diving into narrow gorges that looked as though they had no way out. The locomotive, glittering like a reliquary, with its great headlamp throwing off tawny gleams, its silvery bell, and its cowcatcher jutting out like a spur, mixed its whistles and bellows with those of the torrents and waterfalls, and twined its smoke through the black branches of the firs.

Few tunnels or none, and no bridges along the way. The railroad rounded the flanks of the mountains, not looking to the straight line for the shortest way from one point to another, and doing no violence to nature.

Toward nine o'clock, by way of the Carson valley, the train crossed into the State of Nevada, still holding to the northeast. At noon it left Reno, where the travelers had twenty minutes for lunch.

From this point the railway, skirting the Humboldt River, climbed for some miles to the north, following the stream. Then it bent to the east, not to leave the watercourse again until it had reached the Humboldt Ranges, which give the river its source, almost at the eastern edge of the State of Nevada.

After lunch, Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and their companions took their seats again in the car. Phileas Fogg, the young woman, Fix, and Passepartout, comfortably settled, watched the changing landscape slide past their eyes — broad prairies, mountains ranged along the horizon, creeks rolling their foaming waters. Now and then a great herd of bison, massed in the distance, would appear like a moving dam. These endless armies of ruminants often throw up an insurmountable barrier to the passage of trains. Thousands of the animals have been known to file past for hours on end, in dense ranks, across the track. The locomotive is then forced to halt and wait until the way is clear again.

That was exactly what happened now. Toward three in the afternoon, a herd of ten to twelve thousand head blocked the line. The engine slowed and tried to work its spur into the side of the immense column, but had to stop before the impenetrable mass.

There they were — these "buffalo," as the Americans wrongly call them — plodding along at their leisurely pace, now and then letting out formidable bellows. They stood taller than the bulls of Europe, with short legs and tails, a jutting withers that formed a muscular hump, horns splayed wide at the base, and head, neck, and shoulders shrouded in a mane of long hair. There was no question of stopping this migration. Once the bison have settled on a direction, nothing can check or turn their march. It is a torrent of living flesh that no dam could hold.

The travelers, scattered along the gangways, watched the curious spectacle. But the one who had most reason to be in a hurry, Phileas Fogg, kept his seat and waited philosophically for the buffalo to see fit to let him pass. Passepartout was livid at the delay this mob of animals was causing. He would gladly have emptied his whole arsenal of revolvers into them.

"What a country!" he cried. "A pack of ordinary cattle that hold up trains, and go filing off in a procession, in no more of a hurry than if they weren't blocking the road at all! Good Lord — I'd dearly love to know whether Mr. Fogg had allowed for this little snag in his program! And that engineer who doesn't dare drive his machine straight through this pesky livestock!"

The engineer had not tried to bowl the obstacle over, and he had acted wisely. No doubt he would have crushed the first buffalo the locomotive's spur struck; but, powerful as the engine was, it would soon have been brought up short, a derailment would inevitably have followed, and the train would have been left stranded.

The best thing, then, was to wait patiently, and make up the lost time later by putting on speed. The parade of bison lasted three long hours, and the track did not clear until nightfall. By then the last ranks of the herd were crossing the rails while the first were vanishing below the southern horizon.

So it was eight o'clock when the train threaded the passes of the Humboldt Ranges, and half past nine when it entered the territory of Utah — the region of the Great Salt Lake, the curious country of the Mormons.
