In Which Are Told Various Incidents Found Only on the Railroads of the Union

That same evening the train rolled on without trouble, passed Fort Sanders, cleared Cheyenne Pass, and reached Evans Pass. Here the line touched the highest point of the whole route — eight thousand and ninety-one feet above the level of the ocean. From there the travelers had nothing to do but run downhill toward the Atlantic, across those boundless plains that nature herself had leveled flat.

Here, off the great trunk line, ran the branch to Denver City, the chief town of Colorado. The territory is rich in gold and silver mines, and already more than fifty thousand people have settled there.

By this point, thirteen hundred and eighty-two miles had been covered from San Francisco, in three days and three nights. Four more days and four more nights, by every reasonable forecast, should be enough to reach New York. Phileas Fogg was still comfortably inside his schedule.

During the night they left Camp Walbach on the left. Lodgepole Creek ran alongside the track, following the ruler-straight border shared by the states of Wyoming and Colorado. At eleven o'clock they entered Nebraska, passed near Sedgwick, and touched Julesburg, on the south branch of the Platte River.

It was here, on the 23rd of October, 1867, that the Union Pacific Road was inaugurated, its chief engineer being General J. M. Dodge. Here the two mighty locomotives had halted, towing the nine cars of invited guests — among them the vice-president, Mr. Thomas C. Durant; here the cheering had gone up; here the Sioux and the Pawnee had put on the show of a little Indian war; here the fireworks had burst; and here, at last, by means of a portable printing press, the first issue of the newspaper Railway Pioneer had come off the type. So was celebrated the opening of this great railroad — an instrument of progress and civilization flung across the desert, meant to link together towns and cities that did not yet exist. The whistle of the locomotive, more powerful than the lyre of Amphion, would soon call them up out of the American soil.

At eight in the morning Fort McPherson was left behind. Three hundred and fifty-seven miles separate that point from Omaha. The line followed, along its left bank, the capricious windings of the south branch of the Platte. At nine o'clock they reached the important town of North Platte, built between the two arms of the great river, which come together around it to form a single channel once more — a considerable stream whose waters merge with those of the Missouri a little above Omaha.

The hundred-and-first meridian was crossed.

Mr. Fogg and his partners had taken up their game again. None of them complained about the length of the road — not even the dummy hand. Fix had begun by winning a few guineas, which he was now busy losing back, but he showed himself no less caught up in it than Mr. Fogg. That morning luck singularly favored the gentleman. Trumps and honors rained into his hands. At one point, having worked out a bold play, he was about to lead a spade when, from behind the seat, a voice made itself heard, saying:

"Now me, I'd play a diamond."

Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and Fix looked up. Colonel Proctor was standing beside them.

Stamp W. Proctor and Phileas Fogg knew each other at once.

"Ah! So it's you, mister Englishman," cried the colonel. "You're the one who wants to play a spade!"

"And who is playing it," Phileas Fogg answered coldly, laying down a ten of that suit.

"Well, it suits me that it should be a diamond," the colonel retorted in an irritated voice.

And he made a move to snatch up the played card, adding:

"You don't understand the first thing about this game."

"Perhaps I'll prove abler at another," said Phileas Fogg, rising to his feet.

"You've only to try, son of John Bull!" the boor shot back.

Aouda had gone pale. The blood drained back to her heart. She had seized Phileas Fogg's arm, and he gently freed himself. Passepartout was ready to throw himself on the American, who was eyeing his adversary with the most insulting air imaginable. But Fix had risen, and, going up to Colonel Proctor, he said:

"You forget that it's me you have to deal with, sir — me, whom you not only insulted, but struck!"

"Mr. Fix," said Mr. Fogg, "I beg your pardon, but this concerns me alone. By claiming I was wrong to play a spade, the colonel has offered me a fresh insult, and he will answer to me for it."

"Whenever you like, wherever you like, and with whatever weapon you like!" answered the American.

Aouda tried in vain to hold Mr. Fogg back. The inspector tried, just as uselessly, to take the quarrel onto himself. Passepartout wanted to pitch the colonel out through the door, but a sign from his master stopped him. Phileas Fogg left the car, and the American followed him out onto the platform between the coaches.

"Sir," said Mr. Fogg to his opponent, "I am in a great hurry to get back to Europe, and any delay whatever would badly damage my interests."

"Well, and what's that to me?" answered Colonel Proctor.

"Sir," Mr. Fogg went on, very politely, "after our meeting in San Francisco, I had formed the plan of coming to find you again here in America, as soon as I had finished the business that calls me to the old continent."

"Had you, now!"

"Will you give me a rendezvous six months from now?"

"Why not six years?"

"I said six months," answered Mr. Fogg, "and I will keep the appointment to the minute."

"Excuses, all of it!" cried Stamp W. Proctor. "Now or never."

"Very well," answered Mr. Fogg. "You're going to New York?"

"No."

"To Chicago?"

"No."

"To Omaha?"

"None of your business! Do you know Plum Creek?"

"No," answered Mr. Fogg.

"It's the next station. The train will be there in an hour. It stops there ten minutes. In ten minutes a man can exchange a few revolver shots."

"Very well," answered Mr. Fogg. "I'll stop at Plum Creek."

"And I rather think you'll stay there!" the American added, with unmatched insolence.

"Who knows, sir?" answered Mr. Fogg, and he went back into his car, as cold as ever.

There the gentleman began by reassuring Aouda, telling her that braggarts were never anything to fear. Then he asked Fix to serve as his second in the encounter that was about to take place. Fix could not refuse, and Phileas Fogg calmly took up his interrupted game, leading a spade with perfect composure.

At eleven o'clock the locomotive's whistle announced that the Plum Creek station was near. Mr. Fogg rose and, followed by Fix, went out onto the platform. Passepartout came with him, carrying a pair of revolvers. Aouda had stayed behind in the car, pale as death.

Just then the door of the other coach opened, and Colonel Proctor appeared on the platform as well, followed by his second, a Yankee cut from the same cloth. But at the very moment when the two adversaries were about to step down onto the track, the conductor ran up and shouted to them:

"No one gets off, gentlemen."

"And why not?" demanded the colonel.

"We're twenty minutes late, and the train isn't stopping."

"But I have to fight this man."

"I'm sorry," answered the railwayman, "but we're leaving again at once. There's the bell now!"

The bell was indeed ringing, and the train pulled out.

"I'm truly sorry, gentlemen," the conductor said then. "In any other circumstance I'd have been glad to oblige you. But after all — since you didn't have time to fight it out here, who's to stop you fighting on the move?"

"That may not suit the gentleman!" said Colonel Proctor, with a mocking air.

"It suits me perfectly," answered Phileas Fogg.

"Well, there's no doubt about it — we really are in America!" thought Passepartout. "And the train conductor is a gentleman of the very finest sort!"

And so thinking, he followed his master.

The two adversaries and their seconds, led by the conductor, made their way from car to car toward the rear of the train. The last car held no more than a dozen passengers. The conductor asked whether they would be so kind as to leave the place free for a few minutes to two gentlemen who had an affair of honor to settle.

Would they! The passengers were only too happy to do the two gentlemen a good turn, and they withdrew onto the platforms.

This car, some fifty feet long, lent itself very nicely to the occasion. The two opponents could march at each other between the seats and blaze away to their hearts' content. Never was a duel easier to arrange. Mr. Fogg and Colonel Proctor, each armed with two six-shooters, stepped into the car. Their seconds, staying outside, shut them in. At the first blast of the locomotive's whistle they were to open fire. Then, after a lapse of two minutes, whatever remained of the two gentlemen would be taken out of the car.

Nothing could be simpler, in truth. It was so simple, in fact, that Fix and Passepartout could feel their hearts pounding fit to burst.

So they were all waiting for the agreed whistle when, suddenly, savage cries rang out. Gunshots came with them — but they did not come from the car set aside for the duelists. On the contrary, these reports carried all the way to the front and along the whole length of the train. Cries of terror could be heard inside the coaches.

Colonel Proctor and Mr. Fogg, revolvers in hand, came out of the car at once and rushed toward the front, where the shots and the shouting rang out loudest.

They had understood that the train was under attack by a band of Sioux.

These daring warriors were not on their first such venture; more than once already they had stopped trains. As was their habit, without waiting for the train to halt, a hundred of them had leaped onto the running boards and swarmed up the sides of the cars the way a circus rider vaults onto a galloping horse.

These Sioux were armed with rifles. Hence the gunfire, which the passengers — nearly all of them armed — answered with revolver shots. From the first, the attackers had rushed the engine. The engineer and the fireman had been half knocked senseless with blows from war clubs. One Sioux chief, meaning to stop the train but not knowing how to work the regulator lever, had thrown the steam wide open instead of shutting it off, and the locomotive, running away, tore along at a terrifying speed.

At the same time the Sioux had overrun the cars. They raced like maddened apes along the roofs, they battered in the doors, and they grappled hand to hand with the passengers. The baggage car was forced and looted, and its parcels were flung out onto the track. The cries and the gunfire never let up.

The passengers, though, defended themselves with courage. Some cars, barricaded, held out under siege like so many rolling forts, carried along at a hundred miles an hour.

From the very start of the attack, Aouda had borne herself bravely. Revolver in hand, she defended herself like a hero, firing through the shattered windows whenever some attacker showed himself at hers. A score of Sioux, hit fatally, had dropped onto the track, and the wheels of the cars crushed like worms those who slid onto the rails from the tops of the platforms.

Several passengers, gravely struck by bullets or war clubs, lay stretched across the seats.

But an end had to be made of it. The fight had already gone on ten minutes, and it could only finish in the Sioux's favor if the train did not stop. In fact, the Fort Kearney station was less than two miles off. There stood an American post; but once that post was passed, between Fort Kearney and the next station the Sioux would be masters of the train.

The conductor was fighting at Mr. Fogg's side when a bullet cut him down. As he fell, the man cried out:

"We're lost, if the train doesn't stop within five minutes!"

"It will stop!" said Phileas Fogg, and he made to spring out of the car.

"Stay, sir," Passepartout shouted at him. "This one's mine!"

Phileas Fogg had no time to stop the brave fellow, who, opening a door without being seen by the Indians, managed to slip beneath the car. And then, while the fight went on above and the bullets crossed over his head, finding again all his old agility, all his old circus suppleness, he wormed his way under the cars, catching hold of the chains, using the brake levers and the frame beams, crawling from one carriage to the next with wonderful skill, and so gained the front of the train. He had not been seen; he could not have been.

There, hanging by one hand between the baggage car and the tender, with the other he unhooked the safety chains; but on account of the pull of the moving train he could never have managed to unscrew the coupling bar, had not a jolt of the engine flung that bar loose of itself — and the train, cut free, gradually dropped back, while the locomotive fled ahead with fresh speed.

Carried by its own momentum, the train rolled on a few minutes more, but the brakes were worked from inside the cars, and the whole convoy at last came to a stop less than a hundred paces from the Kearney station.

There the soldiers of the fort, drawn by the gunfire, came running in haste. But the Sioux had not waited for them; before the train had fully stopped, the whole band had made off.

Yet when the passengers counted themselves on the station platform, they realized that several were missing from the roll — and among them the brave Frenchman whose devotion had just saved them all.
