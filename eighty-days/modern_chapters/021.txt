In Which Passepartout Sees That, Even at the Antipodes, It Is Wise to Keep Some Money in One's Pocket

The Carnatic had left Hong Kong on the 7th of November at half past six in the evening, and was steaming at full pressure toward the shores of Japan. She carried a full load of cargo and passengers. Two cabins at the stern stood empty — the two that had been booked in the name of Mr. Phileas Fogg.

The next morning, the men forward could see, not without some surprise, a passenger with a half-dazed eye, an unsteady step, and a wild head of hair, who came up through the second-class hatch and staggered over to sit down on a spare spar.

That passenger was Passepartout in person. Here is what had happened.

A few moments after Fix had left the smoking den, two attendants had lifted the deeply sleeping Passepartout and laid him on the bed kept for the smokers. But three hours later, hounded even into his nightmares by one fixed idea, Passepartout woke and fought against the stupefying grip of the drug. The thought of a duty left undone shook him out of his torpor. He left that bed of drunkards, and stumbling, catching at the walls, falling and picking himself up, but always and irresistibly driven by a kind of instinct, he lurched out of the den crying as though in a dream: "The Carnatic! The Carnatic!"

The steamer was right there, smoking, ready to cast off. Passepartout had only a few steps to go. He threw himself onto the gangway, cleared the ship's ladder, and dropped senseless at the bow just as the Carnatic let go her moorings.

A few sailors, men used to scenes of the kind, carried the poor fellow down to a second-class cabin, and Passepartout did not wake until the next morning, a hundred and fifty miles from the shores of China.

And that was how, on that particular morning, Passepartout came to be on the deck of the Carnatic, gulping down great lungfuls of the fresh sea breeze. The pure air sobered him. He began to gather his thoughts, and did not manage it without some difficulty. But at last he remembered the scenes of the night before — Fix's confidences, the smoking den, and the rest.

"It's plain enough," he said to himself, "that I got abominably drunk! What is Mr. Fogg going to say? Well, at any rate I haven't missed the boat, and that's the main thing."

Then, thinking of Fix:

"As for that one," he said to himself, "I certainly hope we've seen the last of him, and that after what he proposed to me, he hasn't dared follow us aboard the Carnatic. A police inspector, a detective on my master's heels, accused of that theft at the Bank of England! Come off it! Mr. Fogg is about as much a thief as I am a murderer!"

Should Passepartout tell his master all this? Would it be right to let him know the part Fix had played in the business? Wouldn't he do better to wait until they reached London, and then tell him that an agent of the metropolitan police had shadowed him around the world — and have a good laugh about it together? Yes, no doubt. In any case, a question to be examined later. The pressing thing was to rejoin Mr. Fogg and beg his pardon for his unspeakable behavior.

So Passepartout got up. The sea was heavy, and the steamer rolled hard. The worthy fellow, none too steady on his legs yet, worked his way aft as best he could.

On deck he saw no one who looked like either his master or Aouda.

"Right," he said, "Aouda is still in bed at this hour. As for Mr. Fogg, he'll have found himself some whist player, and, as is his habit —"

So saying, Passepartout went down to the saloon. Mr. Fogg was not there. There was only one thing left to do: ask the purser which cabin Mr. Fogg occupied. The purser answered that he knew no passenger of that name.

"Excuse me," said Passepartout, pressing him. "I mean a gentleman, tall, cold, not much of a talker, traveling with a young lady —"

"We have no young lady aboard," the purser replied. "But here — here is the passenger list. You can look for yourself."

Passepartout ran through the list. His master's name was nowhere on it.

Everything went dark before his eyes for a moment. Then an idea flashed through his brain.

"Wait — I am on the Carnatic, aren't I?" he cried.

"Yes," answered the purser.

"Bound for Yokohama?"

"Exactly."

For one instant Passepartout had been seized with the fear that he had boarded the wrong ship! But if he was on the Carnatic, then it was certain his master was not.

Passepartout dropped into an armchair. It was a thunderbolt. And all at once the light broke in on him. He remembered that the Carnatic's departure had been moved up, that he was supposed to have warned his master — and that he had not done it! So it was his fault, his own, that Mr. Fogg and Aouda had missed the sailing!

His fault, yes — but even more the fault of the traitor who, to separate him from his master, to keep Mr. Fogg pinned in Hong Kong, had gotten him drunk! For at last he understood the police inspector's maneuver. And now Mr. Fogg was surely ruined, his wager lost, arrested, imprisoned perhaps! At the thought, Passepartout tore at his hair. Ah, if ever Fix fell into his hands, what a settling of accounts there would be!

At last, after the first shock of dismay, Passepartout recovered his composure and studied the situation. It was not an enviable one. The Frenchman was on his way to Japan. Certain of getting there, how was he to get back? His pockets were empty. Not a shilling, not a penny! Still, his passage and his meals aboard were paid in advance. So he had five or six days ahead of him to decide on a course. As for how he ate and drank during that crossing — it defies description. He ate for his master, for Aouda, and for himself. He ate as though Japan, where he was about to land, were a desert country stripped of every edible substance.

On the 13th, on the morning tide, the Carnatic entered the port of Yokohama.

This is an important port of call in the Pacific, where all the steamers on the mail and passenger service between North America, China, Japan, and the Malay islands put in. Yokohama sits in the very bay of Yeddo, a short distance from that enormous city, the second capital of the Japanese empire — once the residence of the taikun, back when that civil emperor still existed, and the rival of Meako, the great city inhabited by the mikado, the ecclesiastical emperor, descended from the gods.

The Carnatic came alongside the quay at Yokohama, near the jetties of the port and the customs warehouses, in the midst of a crowd of ships belonging to every nation.

Passepartout set foot, without the slightest enthusiasm, on this most curious land of the Sons of the Sun. With nothing better to do, he took chance for a guide and set off at random through the streets of the city.

Passepartout found himself first in a wholly European quarter, with low-fronted houses trimmed with verandas beneath which ran elegant colonnades — a district whose streets, squares, docks, and warehouses covered every foot of ground from the promontory of the Treaty down to the river. There, as in Hong Kong, as in Calcutta, swarmed a jumble of people of every race — Americans, English, Chinese, Dutch — merchants ready to sell anything and buy anything, and among them the Frenchman felt as much a stranger as if he had been dropped into the land of the Hottentots.

Passepartout did have one resource left: he could go and present himself to the French or English consular agents stationed at Yokohama. But it went against the grain to tell his story, so closely bound up with his master's, and before he came to that he wanted to have exhausted every other chance.

So, having crossed the European part of the town without chance doing anything to help him, he entered the Japanese part, resolved, if he had to, to push on all the way to Yeddo.

This native portion of Yokohama is called Benten, after a goddess of the sea worshipped on the neighboring islands. There one saw splendid avenues of firs and cedars, sacred gates of strange architecture, bridges half buried among bamboo and reeds, temples sheltered under the vast, melancholy cover of ancient cedars, monasteries in whose depths the priests of Buddhism and the followers of the religion of Confucius quietly lived out their days, and endless streets where one might have gathered a whole harvest of rosy-skinned, red-cheeked children — little fellows who might have been cut out of some native folding screen — playing among short-legged spaniels and yellowish cats, tailless, very lazy, and very affectionate.

In the streets it was nothing but swarming, an endless coming and going: bonzes filing past in procession beating their monotonous tambourines; yakunins — customs or police officers — in pointed hats inlaid with lacquer, two sabers at the belt; soldiers in blue cotton with white stripes, armed with percussion muskets; the mikado's men-at-arms sheathed in their silk doublets, with hauberk and coat of mail, and any number of other soldiers of every rank — for in Japan the soldier's profession is held in as much esteem as it is despised in China. Then came begging friars, pilgrims in long robes, plain civilians with sleek hair black as ebony, big heads, long torsos, spindly legs, and short stature, their complexions ranging from the dark shades of copper to a dull white — but never the yellow of the Chinese, from whom the Japanese differ essentially. And finally, among the carriages, the palanquins, the horses, the porters, the sail-driven wheelbarrows, the lacquer-paneled "norimons," and the cushioned "cangos" — real bamboo litters — one saw circulating, at the little steps of their little feet, shod in cloth shoes, straw sandals, or carved wooden clogs, a few women, not very pretty, with slanted eyes, flat chests, and teeth blackened in the fashion of the day, but wearing with elegance the national dress, the "kirimon" — a sort of dressing gown crossed by a silk scarf whose broad sash blossomed behind into an extravagant knot, which the Parisiennes of today seem to have borrowed from the Japanese.

Passepartout strolled for several hours through this motley crowd, gazing at the curious, opulent shops, the bazaars heaped with all the glitter of Japanese goldsmithery, the "restaurations" hung with streamers and banners that he was forbidden to enter, and those teahouses where they drink cupfuls of hot fragrant water together with "saki," a liquor drawn from fermenting rice, and those comfortable smoking dens where they smoke a very fine tobacco — not opium, the use of which is nearly unknown in Japan.

Then Passepartout found himself out in the fields, among the vast rice paddies. There, along with flowers casting off their last colors and last scents, bloomed brilliant camellias — carried no longer on shrubs but on trees — and, inside bamboo enclosures, cherry, plum, and apple trees that the natives cultivate for their blossoms rather than their fruit, and that grinning scarecrows and screeching whirligigs guard against the beaks of sparrows, pigeons, crows, and other greedy birds. There was no majestic cedar that did not shelter some great eagle, no weeping willow that did not cover with its foliage some heron perched, melancholy, on one leg — and everywhere, rooks, ducks, hawks, wild geese, and great numbers of those cranes that the Japanese address as "Your Lordships," and that stand to them as symbols of long life and good fortune.

Wandering along this way, Passepartout spotted a few violets among the grasses.

"Good!" he said. "There's my supper."

But when he had smelled them, he found they had no scent at all.

"No such luck!" he thought.

To be sure, the honest fellow had — with an eye to the future — breakfasted as heartily as he possibly could before leaving the Carnatic; but after a day of walking, his stomach felt very hollow indeed. He had certainly noticed that mutton, goats, and pigs were entirely absent from the stalls of the native butchers, and since he knew it was a sacrilege to kill oxen — reserved solely for the needs of agriculture — he had concluded that meat was scarce in Japan. He was not mistaken; but failing butcher's meat, his stomach would have made itself very much at home with a haunch of wild boar or venison, with partridge or quail, with fowl or fish, on which the Japanese feed almost exclusively together with the produce of their rice fields. But he had to put a brave face on it, and left until tomorrow the business of seeing to his food.

Night came. Passepartout went back into the native town, and wandered the streets amid the many-colored lanterns, watching the troupes of tumblers perform their marvelous feats and the open-air astrologers who gathered a crowd around their telescopes. Then he saw the harbor again, spangled with the fires of fishermen luring the fish by the glow of burning resin.

At last the streets emptied. The crowd gave way to the rounds of the yakunins. These officers, in their magnificent costumes and surrounded by their retinues, looked like ambassadors, and Passepartout kept saying to himself in good humor, each time he met one of their dazzling patrols:

"There now! Another Japanese embassy setting off for Europe!"
