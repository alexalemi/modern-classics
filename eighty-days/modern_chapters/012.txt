In Which Passepartout Proves Once More That Fortune Favors the Bold

The plan was daring, bristling with difficulties, and possibly impossible. Mr. Fogg was about to gamble his life — or at the very least his liberty, and with it the success of the whole enterprise — but he did not hesitate. And in Sir Francis Cromarty he found a partner just as resolved.

As for Passepartout, he was ready; they could do with him as they pleased. His master's scheme thrilled him. He could feel a heart, a soul, beating beneath all that ice. He was beginning to love Phileas Fogg.

That left the guide. Whose side would he take in the affair? Mightn't he lean toward the Hindus? If they couldn't count on his help, they would at least have to be sure of his neutrality.

Sir Francis Cromarty put the question to him plainly.

"Sir," the guide answered, "I am a Parsee, and this woman is a Parsee. Do with me as you will."

"Good, guide," said Mr. Fogg.

"But understand this," the Parsee went on. "We risk not only our lives, but horrible tortures if we are caught. So be warned."

"Understood," said Mr. Fogg. "I take it we'll have to wait for nightfall to act?"

"I think so too," the guide replied.

Then the good fellow gave them a few particulars about the victim. She was an Indian woman of famous beauty, Parsee by race, the daughter of wealthy merchants of Bombay. In that city she had received a thoroughly English education, and from her manners and learning you would have taken her for a European. Her name was Aouda.

Left an orphan, she had been married against her will to this old rajah of Bundelkund. Three months later she was a widow. Knowing the fate that awaited her, she fled, was recaptured at once, and the rajah's relatives — who had an interest in her death — condemned her to this ordeal, from which there seemed no chance she could escape.

The story only rooted Mr. Fogg and his companions more firmly in their generous resolve. It was decided that the guide would steer the elephant toward the pagoda of Pillaji, drawing as near to it as he could.

Half an hour later they halted in a thicket, five hundred paces from the pagoda, which they could not see; but the howling of the fanatics came through clear and distinct.

They then talked over how to reach the victim. The guide knew this pagoda of Pillaji, and he was certain the young woman was imprisoned inside it. Could they slip in through one of the doors once the whole band had sunk into a drunken sleep, or would they have to cut a hole through a wall? That could only be decided on the spot, in the moment itself. But one thing was beyond doubt: the rescue had to be carried out that very night, and not once day came and the victim was led to her death. At that point no human power on earth could have saved her.

Mr. Fogg and his companions waited for night. As soon as the shadows fell, around six in the evening, they resolved to scout around the pagoda. The last cries of the fakirs were dying away. As was their habit, these men would be sunk in the heavy stupor of "hang" — liquid opium laced with an infusion of hemp — and it might just be possible to steal between them to the temple.

The Parsee, leading Mr. Fogg, Sir Francis Cromarty, and Passepartout, moved off soundlessly through the forest. After ten minutes of crawling under the branches, they reached the bank of a small river, and there, by the light of iron torches tipped with burning resin, they saw a heap of piled wood. It was the pyre, built of costly sandalwood and already soaked in perfumed oil. On top of it lay the embalmed body of the rajah, to be burned together with his widow. A hundred paces from the pyre rose the pagoda, its minarets piercing the dark above the tops of the trees.

"Come!" whispered the guide.

And, redoubling his caution, followed by his companions, he glided silently through the tall grass.

Now the only sound was the murmur of wind in the branches.

Soon the guide stopped at the edge of a clearing. A few resin torches lit the place. The ground was strewn with clusters of sleepers, heavy with drink. It looked like a battlefield covered with the dead. Men, women, children — all lay tangled together. Here and there a few drunkards still groaned.

In the background, among the mass of trees, the temple of Pillaji loomed dimly. But to the guide's great dismay, the rajah's guards, lit by smoking torches, were keeping watch at the doors, pacing back and forth with drawn swords. It was safe to assume that the priests inside were keeping watch as well.

The Parsee went no farther. He had seen that forcing the temple door was impossible, and he led his companions back.

Phileas Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty had understood, as he had, that nothing could be attempted on that side.

They stopped and talked in low voices.

"Let's wait," said the brigadier general. "It's only eight o'clock yet, and it's possible these guards will give in to sleep too."

"That is possible, yes," the Parsee replied.

So Phileas Fogg and his companions stretched out at the foot of a tree and waited.

The time dragged! Now and then the guide left them to go watch the edge of the wood. The rajah's guards were still on watch by their torches, and a faint light filtered through the windows of the pagoda.

They waited that way until midnight. Nothing changed. The same vigilance outside. Plainly there was no counting on the guards dozing off; the stupor of the "hang" had probably been spared them. They would have to try another way — break in through an opening cut in the pagoda's walls. That left the question of whether the priests were watching over their victim as closely as the soldiers watched the temple door.

After a last conference, the guide declared himself ready to go. Mr. Fogg, Sir Francis, and Passepartout followed. They made a fairly long detour to come at the pagoda from the rear.

Around half past midnight they reached the foot of the walls without meeting a soul. No watch had been posted on this side — though it must be said there were no windows or doors here at all.

The night was dark. The moon, then in its last quarter, was barely clearing a horizon crowded with heavy clouds. The height of the trees deepened the gloom further.

But reaching the foot of the walls was not enough; they still had to make an opening in them. For this the only tools Phileas Fogg and his companions had were their pocketknives. Very luckily, the temple walls were made of a mixture of brick and wood that could not be hard to breach. Once the first brick was out, the rest would come away easily.

They set to work, making as little noise as they could. The Parsee on one side, Passepartout on the other, worked at prying loose the bricks, trying to open a gap two feet wide.

The work was coming along when a cry rang out inside the temple, and almost at once other cries answered it from without.

Passepartout and the guide broke off. Had they been spotted? Was the alarm being raised? The plainest prudence told them to slip away — which they did, along with Phileas Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty. They crouched once more under cover of the wood, waiting for the alarm, if that was what it was, to die down, and ready in that case to take up their work again.

But — a disastrous stroke of luck — guards now appeared at the rear of the pagoda and posted themselves there so as to block any approach.

It would be hard to describe the dismay of these four men, stopped in their work. Now that they could no longer reach the victim, how were they to save her? Sir Francis Cromarty gnawed his fists. Passepartout was beside himself, and the guide had some trouble holding him back. The impassive Fogg waited, betraying nothing.

"Is there nothing left but to go?" the brigadier general asked under his breath.

"There is nothing left but to go," the guide answered.

"Wait," said Fogg. "I only need to be at Allahabad before noon tomorrow."

"But what do you hope for?" Sir Francis Cromarty replied. "In a few hours it will be light, and —"

"The chance that's slipping away may come back at the final moment."

The brigadier general would have given a great deal to read what was in Phileas Fogg's eyes.

What was this cold Englishman counting on? Did he mean, at the moment of execution, to rush at the young woman and tear her openly from her killers?

That would have been madness, and how could anyone believe the man was mad to that degree? Still, Sir Francis Cromarty agreed to wait for the outcome of this terrible scene. The guide, however, did not leave his companions where they had taken shelter; he led them back to the forward part of the clearing. There, screened by a clump of trees, they could watch the sleeping groups.

Meanwhile Passepartout, perched on the lower branches of a tree, was turning over an idea that had first flashed through his mind like lightning and had ended by lodging itself firmly in his brain.

He had begun by telling himself, "What madness!" — and now he kept repeating, "Why not, after all? It's a chance, maybe the only one, and with such brutes as these!"

At any rate Passepartout put his thought no more clearly than that, but he soon slid with the suppleness of a snake along the low branches of the tree, whose ends curved down toward the ground.

The hours slipped by, and before long a few paler streaks announced the coming of day. Yet the darkness was still deep.

This was the moment. A kind of resurrection ran through the drowsing crowd. The groups came to life. Tom-toms began to beat. Songs and shouts broke out anew. The hour had come when the poor woman was to die.

And indeed, the doors of the pagoda swung open. A brighter light spilled out from within. Mr. Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty could make out the victim, sharply lit, dragged out by two priests. It even seemed to them that, shaking off the numbness of the drug by a final instinct of self-preservation, the wretched woman was trying to escape her captors. Sir Francis Cromarty's heart leaped, and with a convulsive movement he seized Phileas Fogg's hand — and felt that the hand was holding an open knife.

At that moment the crowd surged forward. The young woman had sunk back into the torpor brought on by the fumes of the hemp. She passed among the fakirs, who escorted her with their religious howling.

Phileas Fogg and his companions, blending into the last ranks of the crowd, followed.

Two minutes later they reached the riverbank and stopped less than fifty paces from the pyre, on which lay the rajah's body. In the half-dark they saw the victim utterly still, stretched out beside her husband's corpse.

Then a torch was brought near, and the oil-soaked wood caught fire at once.

At that instant Sir Francis Cromarty and the guide held back Phileas Fogg, who in a burst of generous madness was flinging himself toward the pyre —

But Phileas Fogg had already thrown them off, when the whole scene changed in a heartbeat. A cry of terror went up. The entire crowd threw itself to the ground, appalled.

So the old rajah was not dead after all — for there he was, rising up all at once like a phantom, lifting the young woman in his arms, coming down from the pyre through swirling clouds of vapor that gave him the look of a specter.

Fakirs, guards, priests, seized with a sudden terror, lay face down, not daring to lift their eyes and look upon such a marvel.

The lifeless victim passed along in the strong arms that carried her, and seemed to weigh nothing at all. Mr. Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty had stayed on their feet. The Parsee had bowed his head, and Passepartout, no doubt, was no less astonished than the rest!

The resurrected man came up in this way to the spot where Mr. Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty stood, and there, in a curt voice, he said:

"Let's clear out!"

It was Passepartout himself who had slipped up to the pyre through the thick smoke! It was Passepartout who, taking advantage of the still-deep darkness, had snatched the young woman from death! It was Passepartout who, playing his part with an audacity blessed by luck, was walking straight through the general panic!

A moment later all four vanished into the wood, and the elephant bore them off at a swift trot. But shouts, an uproar, and even a bullet through the crown of Phileas Fogg's hat told them that the trick had been discovered.

For there on the blazing pyre now stood out the body of the old rajah. The priests, recovered from their fright, had understood that an abduction had just taken place.

At once they rushed into the forest. The guards ran after them. A volley was fired, but the rescuers were fleeing fast, and in a few moments they were beyond the reach of bullets and arrows.
