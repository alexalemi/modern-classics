In Which Phileas Fogg and Passepartout Accept Each Other, the One as Master, the Other as Servant

In the year 1872, the house at No. 7 Savile Row, Burlington Gardens — the very house in which Sheridan died in 1814 — was occupied by Phileas Fogg, Esq., one of the oddest and most talked-about members of the Reform Club of London, though he seemed to make it his business to do nothing whatever that might attract attention.

So the successor to one of the greatest orators England has ever honored was this Phileas Fogg — an enigma of a man, about whom nothing was known except that he was a thorough gentleman and one of the handsomest figures in English high society.

People said he looked like Byron — the head, at least, for his feet were beyond reproach — but a Byron with a mustache and side-whiskers, an unruffled Byron who might have lived a thousand years without growing old.

English beyond all doubt, Phileas Fogg was perhaps not a Londoner. He had never been seen on the Exchange, at the Bank, or in any of the counting-houses of the City. No dock or basin in London had ever taken in a ship of which Phileas Fogg was the owner. His name appeared on no board of directors. It had never sounded in any inn of court — not at the Temple, not at Lincoln's Inn, not at Gray's Inn. He had never argued a case before the Court of Chancery, the Queen's Bench, the Exchequer, or the Ecclesiastical Court. He was no manufacturer, no trader, no merchant, no farmer. He belonged to none of the learned bodies — not the Royal Institution of Great Britain, nor the London Institution, nor the Artisans' Institution, nor the Russell Institution, nor the Western Literary Institution, nor the Law Institution, nor that combined Institution of Arts and Sciences that enjoys the direct patronage of Her Gracious Majesty. In short, he belonged to none of the countless societies that swarm through the capital of England, from the Harmonica Society all the way down to the Entomological Society, founded chiefly for the extermination of harmful insects.

Phileas Fogg was a member of the Reform Club, and that was all.

To anyone astonished that so mysterious a gentleman should be counted among the members of that honorable association, the answer is that he came in on the recommendation of Baring Brothers, with whom he kept an open line of credit. Hence a certain standing, owing to the fact that his checks were paid regularly on sight, charged to a current account that was invariably in the black.

Was this Phileas Fogg rich? Beyond question. But how he had made his fortune, not even the best-informed could say, and Mr. Fogg was the last man on earth to ask. He was never wasteful, yet never a miser either, for wherever a good, useful, or generous cause fell short of funds, he made up the difference quietly — anonymously, even.

In a word, nothing could be less talkative than this gentleman. He spoke as little as possible, and seemed all the more mysterious for his silence. And yet his life was an open book; it was simply that everything he did was so exactly, mathematically the same, day after day, that the imagination, unsatisfied, went looking for something more.

Had he traveled? Very likely, for no one knew the map of the world better than he did. There was no spot so remote that he did not seem to have some special knowledge of it. Now and then, in a few brief and pointed words, he would set straight the thousand rumors that circulated through the club about lost or missing travelers; he would name the true odds, and his words had often proved so exactly right that it was as if he had second sight — the outcome always ended up bearing him out. Here was a man who must have traveled everywhere — in his mind, at the very least.

What was certain, all the same, was that for years Phileas Fogg had not left London. Those who had the honor of knowing him a shade better than the rest swore that no one could claim to have ever seen him anywhere but on the one straight route he walked each day from his house to the club. His only pastime was reading the newspapers and playing whist. At that silent game — so perfectly suited to his nature — he often won; but his winnings never went into his own pocket. They made up a considerable line in his charity budget. And it should be noted that Mr. Fogg plainly played for the sake of playing, not for the sake of winning. To him the game was a contest, a struggle against a difficulty — but a struggle without motion, without travel, without fatigue, and that suited him perfectly.

Phileas Fogg had, as far as anyone knew, no wife and no children — which can happen to the most honest of men — and no relatives and no friends, which is rarer, in truth. Phileas Fogg lived alone in his house on Savile Row, where no one ever set foot. Of his home life there was never a word. A single servant was enough to wait on him. He breakfasted and dined at the club at hours fixed to the second, in the same room, at the same table, never treating his fellow members, never inviting a guest, and he came home only to sleep — at midnight exactly — never once making use of the comfortable rooms the Reform Club keeps ready for its members. Of every twenty-four hours he spent ten at home, whether sleeping or seeing to his grooming. When he walked, it was always at an even pace, up and down the entrance hall with its inlaid parquet floor, or along the circular gallery beneath the dome of blue stained glass, held up by twenty Ionic columns of red porphyry. When he dined or breakfasted, it was the club's kitchens, its larder, its pantry, its fishmonger and its dairy that stocked his table with their succulent stores; it was the club's servants — grave figures in black coats, shod in soft felt-soled shoes — who waited on him, serving him from a special china on splendid Saxony linen; it was the club's own antique crystal that held his sherry, his port, or his claret laced with cinnamon, maidenhair, and cassia; and it was the club's ice — ice brought at great expense from the lakes of America — that kept his drinks at a satisfying coolness.

If living in such conditions makes a man an eccentric, then it must be admitted that eccentricity has its charms!

The house on Savile Row, without being lavish, was a model of comfort. And given the fixed habits of the tenant, there was little enough for the staff to do. Even so, Phileas Fogg required of his one servant a punctuality, a regularity, that were quite out of the ordinary. That very day — the 2nd of October — Phileas Fogg had dismissed James Forster, the young man having committed the crime of bringing him shaving water at eighty-four degrees Fahrenheit instead of eighty-six; and now he sat waiting for the successor, who was to present himself between eleven and half past.

Phileas Fogg sat squarely in his armchair, his two feet together like a soldier on parade, his hands resting on his knees, his body straight, his head high, watching the hand of the clock move — an elaborate contraption that showed the hours, the minutes, the seconds, the days of the week, the days of the month, and the year. At the stroke of half past eleven Mr. Fogg would, by his daily custom, leave the house and set off for the Reform Club.

At that moment there came a knock at the door of the little parlor where Phileas Fogg was sitting.

James Forster, the dismissed man, appeared.

"The new servant," he announced.

A fellow of about thirty came in and bowed.

"You are French, and your name is John?" Phileas Fogg asked him.

"Jean, if it please you, sir," the newcomer replied. "Jean Passepartout — a nickname that stuck to me, and one my knack for getting out of scrapes has earned. I believe I'm an honest fellow, sir, but to be frank, I've turned my hand to a good many trades. I've been a traveling singer; a bareback rider in a circus, doing trapeze work like Léotard and walking the tightrope like Blondin; then I became a gymnastics instructor, to put my talents to better use, and last of all I was a sergeant in the fire brigade in Paris. I've even got a few notable blazes in my record. But it's now five years since I left France, and, wanting a taste of family life, I've been working as a valet in England. Finding myself out of a place, and having heard that Mr. Phileas Fogg was the most precise and most stay-at-home man in the United Kingdom, I've come to your door, sir, in the hope of a quiet life here — and of forgetting even that name of Passepartout."

"Passepartout suits me," the gentleman replied. "You come recommended. I have good reports of you. You know my terms?"

"Yes, sir."

"Good. What time do you have?"

"Twenty-two minutes past eleven," answered Passepartout, hauling an enormous silver watch out of the depths of his waistcoat pocket.

"You are slow," said Mr. Fogg.

"Begging your pardon, sir, but that's impossible."

"You are four minutes slow. No matter. It's enough to note the difference. So then — from this moment, eleven twenty-nine in the morning, this Wednesday the 2nd of October, 1872, you are in my service."

That said, Phileas Fogg rose, took his hat in his left hand, set it on his head with the motion of an automaton, and vanished without another word.

Passepartout heard the street door shut once: that was his new master, going out. Then a second time: that was his predecessor, James Forster, taking his own leave.

Passepartout was left alone in the house on Savile Row.
