In Which Phileas Fogg Is Once Again at a Premium on the Market

It is time to record here the great swing of public opinion that ran through the United Kingdom when word came of the arrest of the real bank robber — one James Strand — which had taken place on the 17th of December, at Edinburgh.

Three days earlier, Phileas Fogg had been a criminal hunted relentlessly by the police; now he was the most honest gentleman alive, carrying out his eccentric journey around the world with mathematical precision.

What a stir it made, what an uproar in the papers! Every bettor, for him or against, who had long since forgotten the whole affair, sprang back to life as if by magic. Every transaction was good again. Every wager revived, and — it must be said — the betting started up with fresh energy. The name of Phileas Fogg was once again at a premium on the market.

The gentleman's five colleagues at the Reform Club spent those three days in no small anxiety. This Phileas Fogg they had all but forgotten was suddenly before their eyes again! Where was he at this moment? On the 17th of December — the day James Strand was arrested — seventy-six days had passed since Phileas Fogg set out, and not a word from him! Had he come to grief? Had he given up the struggle, or was he still pressing on along the agreed route? And on Saturday, the 21st of December, at eight forty-five in the evening, would he appear, like the very god of punctuality, on the threshold of the Reform Club's drawing room?

There is no use trying to paint the suspense in which, for three days, all this English society lived. Telegrams were fired off to America and to Asia for news of Phileas Fogg! Morning and evening, men were sent to watch the house on Savile Row... Nothing. The police themselves no longer knew what had become of Detective Fix, who had so unluckily thrown himself onto a false trail. None of which kept the betting from starting up again on a still grander scale. Phileas Fogg, like a racehorse, was rounding the final turn. He was no longer quoted at a hundred to one, but at twenty, at ten, at five; and the palsied old Lord Albermale was taking him at even money.

And so, on the Saturday evening, there was a crowd in Pall Mall and the neighboring streets. You might have taken it for a vast, permanent gathering of brokers camped around the Reform Club. Traffic was at a standstill. People argued, disputed, shouted the going price of "Phileas Fogg" as they would the price of English government stock. The policemen had all they could do to hold back the crowd, and as the hour drew on at which Phileas Fogg was due to arrive, the excitement swelled to unbelievable proportions.

That evening the gentleman's five colleagues had been gathered since eight o'clock in the great drawing room of the Reform Club. The two bankers, John Sullivan and Samuel Fallentin; the engineer Andrew Stuart; Gauthier Ralph, a director of the Bank of England; and the brewer Thomas Flanagan — all waited in anxious suspense.

The moment the drawing-room clock marked eight twenty-five, Andrew Stuart rose to his feet and said:

"Gentlemen, in twenty minutes the deadline agreed between Mr. Phileas Fogg and ourselves will have expired."

"What time did the last train from Liverpool get in?" asked Thomas Flanagan.

"At seven twenty-three," answered Gauthier Ralph, "and the next one doesn't arrive until ten past midnight."

"Well then, gentlemen," Andrew Stuart went on, "if Phileas Fogg had come in on the seven twenty-three, he'd already be here. So we may consider the wager won."

"Let's wait — let's not commit ourselves," replied Samuel Fallentin. "You know our colleague is an eccentric of the first order. His exactness in everything is well known. He's never too late nor too early, and if he turned up here at the very last minute, I shouldn't be the least bit surprised."

"For my part," said Andrew Stuart, as jumpy as ever, "if I saw him I wouldn't believe it."

"Quite," Thomas Flanagan put in. "Phileas Fogg's whole scheme was madness. However exact he might be, he couldn't keep unavoidable delays from piling up, and a delay of only two or three days was enough to wreck the whole journey."

"You'll notice, too," added John Sullivan, "that we've had no word whatever from our colleague — and there was no shortage of telegraph wires along his route."

"He's lost, gentlemen," Andrew Stuart resumed, "lost a hundred times over! You know, besides, that the China — the only New York steamer he could have taken to reach Liverpool in time — got in yesterday. Well, here is the passenger list, printed in the Shipping Gazette, and the name of Phileas Fogg is nowhere on it. Granting him even the best of luck, our colleague is barely in America by now! I put the delay he'll run up past the agreed date at twenty days at the very least — and old Lord Albermale will be out his five thousand pounds into the bargain!"

"It's plain enough," answered Gauthier Ralph. "Tomorrow we'll have nothing to do but present Mr. Fogg's check at Baring Brothers."

At that moment the drawing-room clock struck eight forty.

"Five minutes more," said Andrew Stuart.

The five colleagues looked at one another. One may suppose that the beating of their hearts had quickened a little, for even for cool-headed players the stakes were high! But they let nothing show, and at Samuel Fallentin's suggestion they took their places at a card table.

"I wouldn't give up my four-thousand-pound share in the wager," said Andrew Stuart as he sat down, "even if I were offered three thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine for it!"

At that instant the hand stood at eight forty-two.

The players had picked up their cards, but every moment their eyes went back to the clock. However sure of themselves they were, never — one may safely say — had minutes seemed so long!

"Eight forty-three," said Thomas Flanagan, cutting the deck that Gauthier Ralph held out to him.

Then came a moment of silence. The club's great room was still. But outside, one could hear the din of the crowd, pierced now and then by shrill cries. The pendulum of the clock beat off the seconds with mathematical regularity. Each player could count the sixty beats of the minute as they struck his ear.

"Eight forty-four!" said John Sullivan, in a voice that betrayed an emotion he could not help.

One minute more, and the wager was won. Andrew Stuart and his colleagues had stopped playing. They had dropped the cards! They were counting the seconds!

At the fortieth second, nothing. At the fiftieth, still nothing!

At the fifty-fifth, a sound like thunder was heard outside — applause, hurrahs, even curses — rolling on in one unbroken roar.

The players rose to their feet.

At the fifty-seventh second, the drawing-room door opened; and before the pendulum had beaten the sixtieth second, Phileas Fogg appeared, followed by a delirious crowd that had forced its way into the club, and in his calm voice said:

"Here I am, gentlemen."
