[Day the Fourth]

THREE YOUNG MEN LOVE THREE SISTERS AND FLEE WITH THEM TO CRETE, WHERE THE ELDEST SISTER KILLS HER LOVER OUT OF JEALOUSY. THE SECOND SISTER SAVES HER SISTER FROM DEATH BY YIELDING HERSELF TO THE DUKE OF CRETE, BUT THEN HER OWN LOVER KILLS HER AND FLEES WITH THE ELDEST SISTER. MEANWHILE, THE THIRD LOVER AND THE YOUNGEST SISTER ARE ACCUSED OF THE NEW MURDER, CONFESS TO IT, AND THEN BRIBE THEIR GUARDS TO FLEE TO RHODES, WHERE THEY DIE IN POVERTY.

Filostrato, having heard the end of Pampinea's story, paused for a moment and then said to her, "There was some good and pleasing aspects to the ending of your story, but there was too much before that which gave cause for laughter, and I would not have had that there." Then, turning to Lauretta, he said, "Lady, tell a better story, if you can." She laughed and replied, "You are too harsh towards lovers, if you desire only an ill end for them. But, to obey you, I will tell a story of three who all met an equally unhappy fate, having barely tasted the joys of their love."

Lauretta began: "Young ladies, as you should know, every vice can turn to the grave harm of those who practice it, and often of others as well. But of all vices, the one that most recklessly carries us to our peril is anger - a sudden and uncontrolled emotion, provoked by an offense suffered, which banishes all reason and darkens the eyes of understanding with blindness, kindling the soul to the fiercest fury. 

"While anger often afflicts men, and more in some than in others, it has been seen to cause greater mischiefs in women, for it is more easily inflamed in us and burns with a fiercer flame, urging us with less restraint. This is not to be wondered at, for just as fire more readily catches to light and delicate things than to those that are denser and heavier, so we women - let men not take offense - are of a more delicate nature and far more volatile. Seeing then, that we are naturally inclined to anger, and considering how our gentleness and loving-kindness bring peace and pleasure to the men we are with, while anger and fury bring harm and peril, I will tell you a tale to show how the loves of three young men and three ladies came, as I said, to an unhappy end through the rage of one of the women.

"Marseilles, as you know, is an ancient and noble city in Provence, on the seashore, and was once richer and more prosperous in great and wealthy merchants than it is today. Among these was a man named Narnald Cluada, of humble origins but renowned for his honesty and loyalty as a merchant. He was exceedingly wealthy in lands and monies, and had several children by his wife, the three eldest of whom were daughters. Two of these, born as twins, were fifteen years old, and the third was fourteen. Their family was only waiting for Narnald's return from a trip to Spain to arrange their marriages.

"The two elder sisters were named Ninetta and Maddalena, while the third was called Bertella. A young man of gentle birth, though poor, named Restagnone, was deeply in love with Ninetta, and she with him. They had managed to secretly enjoy the fruits of their love.
