The Second Story
[Day the Tenth]

Ghino di Tacco Treats the Abbot of Cluny, Curing His Stomach Ailment and Then Letting Him Go; The Abbot Then Returns to the Court of Rome and Reconciles Ghino with Pope Boniface, Making Him a Prior of the Hospitallers

The generosity shown by King Alfonso to the Florentine knight had been properly praised. The king, who had greatly enjoyed it, instructed Elisa to continue the storytelling. She began immediately: 

"Dear ladies, it cannot be denied that for a king to be generous and show that generosity to someone who had served him is a great and admirable thing. But what shall we say if a churchman is reported to have practiced remarkable magnanimity towards someone, whom, if he had treated as an enemy, he would not have been blamed? Truly, we can say nothing other than that the king's generosity was a virtue, while the churchman's was a miracle. This is because the clergy are exceedingly stingy, even more so than women, and sworn enemies of all forms of liberality. And although all people naturally yearn for vengeance against affronts received, we see churchmen, for all their preaching of patience and especially the forgiveness of offenses, pursue it more eagerly than others. 

This, then, how a churchman showed magnanimity, you may clearly learn from the following story of mine.

Ghino di Tacco, a man very famous for his cruelty and robberies, having been expelled from Siena and at odds with the Counts of Santa Fiore, took control of Radicofani against the Church of Rome and, making that his base, had his men plunder all who passed through the surrounding countryside. Now, when Boniface the Eighth was pope in Rome, the Abbot of Cluny, believed to be one of the wealthiest prelates in the world, came to the papal court. There, he ruined his stomach, and the doctors advised him to go to the baths of Siena to be cured. Having obtained the pope's leave, he set out in great pomp with his gear, baggage, horses, and servants, unaware of Ghino's reputation. 

Ghino, hearing of his coming, set his traps and surrounded the abbot and all his household and possessions in a tight spot, not letting a single footboy escape. He then sent one of his most capable men, well-accompanied, to kindly request that the abbot be pleased to alight and sojourn with the aforementioned Ghino in his castle. The abbot, hearing this, answered furiously that he would not do it, having nothing to do with Ghino, and that he would continue on his way, daring anyone to stop him. The messenger humbly replied, "Sir, you have come to a place where, unless God himself intervenes, there is nothing to fear for us, and where excommunications and interdicts are all excommunicated. Therefore, it would please you to comply with Ghino in this matter."

During this exchange, the entire area had been surrounded by armed men. Seeing himself and his party captured, the abbot, greatly against his will, went with the messenger to the castle, along with his whole household and possessions. There, by Ghino's orders, he was lodged all alone in a very dark and modest little room in one of the pavilions, while everyone else was accommodated according to their status around the castle. The horses and all the gear were secured without anything being touched. 

Ghino then went to the abbot and said, "Sir, Ghino, whose guest you are, sends word to you, asking that you tell him where you are going and on what business." The abbot, having by now set aside his pride, told him where he was headed and why. Hearing this, Ghino took his leave and set about curing the abbot without baths. He kept a large fire constantly burning in the little room, had the place well-guarded, and did not return to the abbot until the next morning. Then he brought the abbot, in a very white napkin, two slices of toasted bread and a large beaker of his own Corniglia vernage wine, saying, "Sir, when Ghino was young, he studied medicine, and he says there is no better remedy for a stomach ailment than what he intends to apply to you, of which these things I bring you are the beginning. So take them and refresh yourself."

The abbot, whose hunger was greater than his desire to argue, ate the bread and drank the wine, though he did so reluctantly. Afterward, he made many proud speeches, asking and advising about many things, and in particular demanding to see Ghino. Ghino, hearing this talk, let some of it pass as idle and answered the rest very courteously, assuring the abbot that Ghino would visit him as soon as possible. He then took his leave and did not return until the next day, when he again brought the abbot toasted bread and malmsey wine. He continued this for several days, until he noticed the abbot had eaten some dried beans he had secretly left there on purpose. At that point, Ghino asked the abbot, on Ghino's behalf, how he felt about his stomach. The abbot answered, "It seems I would fare well if I were just out of his hands, and after that, I have no greater desire than to eat, so well have his remedies cured me."

Thereupon, Ghino had the abbot's own people set up a fine chamber for him with his own furnishings and prepare a magnificent banquet, to which he invited the prelate's whole household, along with many of the townspeople. The next morning, he went to the abbot and said, "Sir, since you feel yourself well, it is time to leave the infirmary." Then, taking him by the hand, he brought him to the prepared chamber and, leaving him there with his own people, occupied himself with ensuring the banquet was magnificent.

The abbot spent some time with his men, telling them of his life since his capture, while they in turn assured him they had all been wonderfully treated by Ghino. When the meal hour arrived, the abbot and the others were well and properly served with fine foods and wines, but Ghino still did not make himself known to the prelate. After several days of this, the outlaw, having brought all his possessions into one hall and all his horses, down to the most humble nag, into a courtyard visible from the windows, went to the abbot and asked how he felt and if he deemed himself strong enough to take horse. The abbot answered that he was strong enough and completely recovered from his stomach ailment, and that he would fare perfectly well once out of Ghino's hands.

Ghino then brought him into the hall where all his possessions were, and, taking him to a window overlooking the courtyard where the horses were, said, "My lord abbot, you must know that it was being a gentleman expelled from his home and poor, with many powerful enemies, and not an evil nature, that brought Ghino di Tacco (who is none other than myself) to become, in defense of his life and nobility, a highwayman and an enemy of the court of Rome. Nevertheless, since you seem to me a worthy gentleman, I do not intend to treat you as I would another, from whom, being in my hands as you are, I would take as much of his goods as seemed fit to me. Rather, it is my intent that you, having regard to my need, shall appoint to me such part of your goods as you yourself will. All is here before you, and your horses you may see in the courtyard; take, therefore, both part and all, as it pleases you, and from this time forth be it at your pleasure to go or to stay."

The abbot marveled to hear such generous words from a highwayman and was exceedingly pleased by them, so that his anger and displeasure suddenly turned to goodwill, and he became Ghino's hearty friend, embracing him and saying, "I vow to God that, to gain the friendship of a man such as I now judge you to be, I would gladly consent to suffer a far greater affront than that which I deemed you had done me. Cursed be fortune that constrained you to so damnable a trade!" Then, taking only a very few necessary things from his many goods and horses, he left the rest to Ghino and returned to Rome. 

The pope had received news of the abbot's capture and, although it had greatly troubled him, when he saw the abbot, he asked how the baths had benefited him. The abbot replied, smiling, "Holy Father, I found a worthy physician nearer than the baths, who has excellently cured me," and told him how. At this, the pope laughed, and the abbot, moved by a generous spirit, asked a boon of him. The pope, thinking he would demand something else, freely offered to grant whatever he asked, and the abbot said, "Holy Father, what I mean to ask of you is that you restore your favor to Ghino di Tacco, my physician, for of all the men of worth and high account whom I have ever known, he is certainly one of the most deserving. And for this wrong he does, I hold it much more fortune's fault than his. If you change this by bestowing on him something whereby he may live according to his condition, I doubt not at all but that you will, in a short time, think of him even as I do." 

The pope, who was great of soul and a lover of worthy men, hearing this, replied that he would gladly do it, if Ghino was indeed of such account as the abbot avowed, and bade the latter cause him to come there in all security. Accordingly, Ghino, at the abbot's instance, came to the court, upon that assurance, and had not been long about the pope's person ere the latter reputed him a man of worth and, taking him into favor, bestowed on him a grand priory of the Hospitallers, having first had him made a knight of that order. This office Ghino held for the rest of his life, still approving himself a loyal friend and servant of Holy Church and of the Abbot of Cluny.
