THE SECOND STORY

[Day the Second]

RINALDO D'ASTI, HAVING BEEN ROBBED, MAKES HIS WAY TO CASTEL GUGLIELMO, WHERE HE IS HOSPITABLY ENTERTAINED BY A WIDOW LADY, AND HAVING MADE GOOD HIS LOSS, RETURNS TO HIS OWN HOUSE, SAFE AND SOUND

The ladies laughed heartily at Martellino's misfortunes as narrated by Neifile, as did the young men, especially Filostrato, whom the queen instructed to tell the next story. Without delay, he began, "Dear ladies, I must tell you a tale that involves religious matters, mixed with misadventures and love, which will likely prove useful, especially to those who travel the perilous lands of love, for even those with the finest accommodations are often ill-lodged if they have not said Saint Julian's prayer.

In the days of Marquis Azzo of Ferrara, a merchant named Rinaldo d'Asti came to Bologna on business. Returning home, as he left Ferrara and rode towards Verona, he encountered a group of men who appeared to be merchants but were in fact highway robbers and scoundrels. Unsuspecting, Rinaldo joined their company and engaged in conversation. Seeing him as a wealthy merchant, the thieves plotted to rob him at the first opportunity.

As they traveled, the conversation turned to the prayers people say on a journey. One of the robbers asked Rinaldo what prayer he used. Rinaldo explained that he was a simple man, with few prayers, but always said a Pater Noster and Ave Maria for the souls of Saint Julian's parents before setting out, believing this prayer had protected him from many perils and ensured his safe lodging. The robber, hearing this, thought to himself, "May it do you little good! For unless we are prevented, I believe you shall find poor lodging tonight."

As night fell, the thieves attacked Rinaldo, robbing him of his money, clothes, and horse, then left him barefoot and in his shirt, saying, "Go see if your Saint Julian will give you good lodging tonight, as ours surely will for us." Rinaldo's cowardly servant fled to Castel Guglielmo, leaving his master behind.

Shivering in the cold and snow, Rinaldo hurried towards Castel Guglielmo, hoping for shelter, but arrived too late, finding the gates closed for the night. Despairing, he took refuge under the projection of a house just outside the town walls. 

Meanwhile, in that very house lived a beautiful widow lady whom the Marquis Azzo kept at his disposal. The Marquis had planned to visit her that night, but was called away at the last moment. The disappointed lady decided to enjoy the prepared bath and supper herself. As she bathed, she heard Rinaldo's shivering and weeping. Her maid investigated and brought Rinaldo inside, where the lady had him bathe and then provided him with her late husband's clothes. After supper, the lady, struck by Rinaldo's handsome appearance and engaging manner, made advances towards him. Rinaldo, sensing her desires, gladly responded, and they spent the night together in her bed, satisfying their mutual passion.

In the morning, the lady helped Rinaldo re-enter the town, giving him money and modest clothes. As he was preparing to leave, by a stroke of luck, the thieves who had robbed him were brought into the town and made to confess, allowing Rinaldo to recover his belongings. Thanking God and Saint Julian, Rinaldo returned home safely.
