The First Story

[Day the First]

MASTER CIAPPELLETTO DECEIVES A HOLY FRIAR WITH A FALSE CONFESSION AND DIES; AND HAVING BEEN IN HIS LIFETIME THE WORST OF MEN, HE IS, AFTER HIS DEATH, REPUTED A SAINT AND CALLED SAINT CIAPPELLETTO.

It is proper, dear ladies, that we begin our storytelling by invoking the holy and admirable name of He who is the maker of all things. As the first to commence our tales, I intend to recount one of His marvels, so that by hearing it, our hope in Him, as in something unchanging, may be strengthened, and His name ever praised by us.

It is clear that, just as earthly things are all transitory and mortal, both within and without, they are full of annoyance, anguish, and peril, against which we, who live intermingled with them and are indeed part of them, could neither endure nor defend ourselves, were it not for the special grace of God that lends us strength and foresight. This grace, we must believe, does not descend upon us due to our own merit, but rather from the proper motion of His own benignity and the efficacy of the prayers of those who, having diligently followed His commandments while on earth, are now with Him, eternal and blessed, and to whom we, not daring to address ourselves to the proper presence of so august a judge, offer our petitions for the things we deem needful to ourselves, as to advocates informed by experience of our frailty.

And this we further discern in Him, full as He is of compassionate liberality towards us, that, whereas it sometimes happens (the keenness of mortal eyes not availing in any wise to penetrate the secrets of the Divine intent) that we, beguiled by report, make such an one our advocate unto His majesty, who is outcast from His presence with an eternal banishment, nevertheless He, from whom nothing is hidden, having regard rather to the purity of the suppliant's intent than to his ignorance or to the reprobate estate of him whose intercession be invoketh, giveth ear unto those who pray unto the latter, as if he were in very deed blessed in His aspect. This will manifestly appear from the story which I purpose to relate.

It is told, then, that Musciatto Franzesi, being from a very rich and considerable merchant in France become a knight and it behoving him thereupon go into Tuscany with Messire Charles Sansterre, brother to the king of France, who had been required and bidden thither by Pope Boniface, found his affairs in one part and another sore embroiled, (as those of merchants most times are,) and was unable lightly or promptly to disentangle them...
