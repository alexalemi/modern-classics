THE FIRST STORY

[Day the Fourth]

TANCRED, PRINCE OF SALERNO, KILLS HIS DAUGHTER'S LOVER AND SENDS HIS HEART TO HER IN A GOLDEN BOWL; WHEREUPON, POURING POISONED WATER OVER IT, SHE DRINKS AND DIES

"Our king has given us a sorrowful subject to discuss today, as we must speak of others' tears, which cannot be recounted without evoking compassion in both the teller and the listener. Perhaps he has done this to temper the mirth of the previous days; but whatever his reasons, since it is not my place to change his wishes, I will recount a pitiful and ill-fated event, worthy of your tears.

Tancred, the Lord of Salerno, was a kind and benign prince, (had he not in his old age stained his hands with the blood of a lover,) who in all his life had but one daughter, and he would have been happier had he none. She was as tenderly loved by him as any daughter by a father, and he, knowing not how to part with her due to this deep love, did not marry her until she had long passed the age when she should have had a husband. Finally, he gave her in marriage to the son of the Duke of Capua, with whom she abided a while before becoming a widow and returning to her father. Now she was the fairest woman, both in form and in beauty, and young and lively and perhaps more learned than is required of a lady. Residing then with her father in great ease and luxury, like the great lady she was, and seeing that, for the love he bore her, he cared little to remarry her, nor did it seem a proper thing for her to require it of him, she determined to seek, if she could, to secretly acquire a worthy lover. She observed many men, noble and common, frequenting her father's court, and considering the manners and ways of many, a young servant of her father's, named Guiscardo, a man of humble enough origins, but nobler in worth and manners than any other, pleased her above all, and of him, seeing him often, she became secretly ardently enamored, approving more and more his ways with each passing hour; while the young man, who was no fool, perceiving her fondness for him, received her into his heart, so that his mind was diverted from nearly everything else but the love of her.

Each, then, thus secretly cherishing the other, the young lady, who desired nothing so much as to meet with him, but had no mind to make any one a confidant of her passion, devised a clever scheme to apprise him of the means; that is, she wrote him a letter, wherein she showed him how he should do to meet with her on the following day, and placing it in the hollow of a cane, gave the cane jestingly to Guiscardo, saying, 'Make yourself a bellows thereof for your serving-maid, wherewith she may blow up the fire tonight.' Guiscardo took the cane and, thinking that she would not have given it to him nor spoken thus, without some reason, took his leave and returned therewith to his lodging. There he examined the cane and, seeing it was split, opened it and found the letter, which, having read and well understood that which he had to do, he was the happiest man alive and set about taking the necessary steps to go to her, as she had directed.

There was, beside the prince's palace, a grotto hewn out of the rock and made in days long ago, and to this grotto some little light was given by an airshaft wrought in the mountain, which latter, for that the grotto was abandoned, was well nigh blocked at its mouth with briers and weeds that had overgrown it. Into this grotto one might go by a secret stair which was in one of the ground floor rooms of the lady's apartment in the palace and which was shut in by a very strong door. This stair was so out of all folk's minds, for that it had been unused from time immemorial, that well nigh none remembered it to be there; but Love, to whose eyes there is nothing so secret but it winneth, had recalled it to the memory of the enamored lady, who, that none should get wind of the matter, had labored sore many days with such tools as she might command, ere she could make shift to open the door; then, going down alone thereby into the grotto and seeing the airshaft, she sent to bid Guiscardo study to come to her thereby and acquainted him with the height which she thought should be from the mouth thereof to the ground.

To this end Guiscardo promptly made ready a rope with certain knots and loops, whereby he might avail to descend and ascend, and donning a leather suit, that might defend him from the briers, he on the following night repaired, without letting any know aught of the matter, to the mouth of the airshaft. There making one end of the rope fast to a sturdy tree-stump that had grown up in the mouth, he let himself down thereby into the grotto and there awaited the lady, who, on the morrow, pretending a desire to sleep, dismissed her women and shut herself up alone in her chamber; then, opening the secret door, she descended into the grotto, where she found Guiscardo. They greeted one another with great joy and betook themselves to her chamber, where they abode a good part of the day in the utmost delight; and after they had made arrangements together for the discreet conduct of their love, so it might remain secret, Guiscardo returned to the grotto, whilst she shut the secret door and went forth to her women. When night came, Guiscardo climbed up by his rope to the mouth of the airshaft and issuing forth whence he had entered in, returned to his lodging; and having learned this road, he in time returned many times thereafter.

But fortune, jealous of so long and great a joy, with a woeful chance changed the gladness of the two lovers into mourning and sorrow; and it befell on this wise. Tancred was wont to come at times all alone into his daughter's chamber and there abide with her and converse awhile and after go away. Accordingly, one day, after dinner, he came thither, what time the lady (whose name was Ghismonda) was in a garden of hers with all her women, and willing not to take her from her diversion, he entered her chamber, without being seen or heard of any. Finding the windows closed and the curtains drawn over the bed, he sat down in a corner on a hassock at the bedfoot and leant his head against the bed; then, drawing the curtain over himself, as if he had studied to hide himself there, he fell asleep. As he slept thus, Ghismonda, who, as ill chance would have it, had appointed her lover to come thither that day, softly entered the chamber, leaving her women in the garden, and having shut herself in, without perceiving that there was some one there, opened the secret door to Guiscardo, who awaited her. They straightway betook themselves to bed, as of their wont, and what while they sported and solaced themselves together, it befell that Tancred awoke and heard and saw that which Guiscardo and his daughter did; whereat beyond measure grieved, at first he would have cried out at them, but after bethought himself to keep silence and abide, if he might, hidden, so with more secrecy and less shame to himself he might avail to do that which had already occurred to his mind.

The two lovers abode a great while together, according to their custom, without observing Tancred, and coming down from the bed, whenas it seemed to them time, Guiscardo returned to the grotto and she departed the chamber; whereupon Tancred, for all he was an old man, let himself down into the garden by a window and returned, unseen of any, to his own chamber, sorrowful unto death. That same night, at the time of the first sleep, Guiscardo, by Tancred's orders, was seized by two men, as he came forth of the airshaft, and carried secretly, trussed as he was in his suit of leather, to Tancred, who, whenas he saw him, said, well nigh weeping, 'Guiscardo, my kindness to thee merited not the outrage and the shame thou hast done me in mine own flesh and blood, as I have this day seen with my very eyes.' Whereto Guiscardo answered nothing but this, 'Love can far more than either you or I.'

On the morrow, having meanwhile revolved in himself many and diverse plans, Tancred betook himself, after eating, as of his wont, to his daughter's chamber and sending for the lady, who as yet knew nothing of these things, shut himself up with her and proceeded, with tears in his eyes, to address her thus: 'Ghismonda, I thought I knew thy virtue and honesty, nor might it ever have occurred to my mind, though it were told me, had I not seen it with mine own eyes, that thou wouldst, even so much as in thought, have abandoned thyself to any man, except he were thy husband; wherefore in this scant remnant of life that my old age reserveth unto me, I shall still abide sorrowful, remembering me of this. Would God, an thou must needs stoop to such wantonness, thou hadst taken a man sortable to thy quality! But, amongst so many who frequent my court, thou hast chosen Guiscardo, a youth of the meanest condition, reared in our court, well nigh of charity, from a little child up to this day; wherefore thou hast put me in sore travail of mind, for that I know not what course to take with thee.'

Ghismonda, hearing her father's words and knowing that not only was her secret love discovered, but Guiscardo taken, felt an inexpressible chagrin, but with her haughty spirit overmastering any outward show of weakness, with marvellous fortitude she composed her countenance and, rather than plead for herself, determined inwardly to end her life, doubting not but her Guiscardo was already dead. Wherefore, not as a woman rebuked and sorrowful for her fault, but as one undaunted and valiant, with dry eyes and face open and nowise troubled, she thus replied to her father: 'Tancred, I purpose neither to deny nor to entreat, for that the one would profit me nothing nor would I have the other avail me; more by token that I am nowise minded to seek to render thy kindness and affection favourable to me, but rather, confessing the truth, first with true arguments to vindicate mine honor and after with deeds right resolutely to follow the greatness of my soul. True is it I have loved and love Guiscardo, and what while I live, which will be little, I shall love him, nor, if folk live after death, shall I ever cease loving him; but unto this it was not so much my feminine frailty that moved me as thy little solicitude to remarry me and his own worth.

'I am, then, as being of thee begotten, of flesh and blood and have lived so little that I am yet young and (for the one and the other reason) full of carnal desire, whereunto the having aforetime, by reason of marriage, known what pleasure it is to give accomplishment to such desire hath added marvellous strength. Unable, therefore, to withstand the strength of my desires, I addressed myself, being young and a woman, to ensue that whereto they prompted me and became enamored. And certes in this I set my every faculty to the endeavoring that, so far as in me lay, no shame should ensue either to thee or to me through this to which natural frailty moved me.'

Tancred, knowing the greatness of his daughter's spirit, yet believed her not altogether so firmly resolved as she professed. Wherefore, having laid aside all intent of using rigor against her person, he thought to cool her ardent love with another's suffering and accordingly bade Guiscardo's two guardians strangle him without noise that same night and taking out his heart, bring it to him. They did even as it was commanded them, and on the morrow the prince let bring a great and goodly bowl of gold and setting therein Guiscardo's heart, despatched it to his daughter by the hands of a very privy servant of his, bidding him say, whenas he gave it her, 'Thy father sendeth thee this, to solace thee of the thing thou most lovest, even as thou hast solaced him of that which he loved most.'

Now Ghismonda, unmoved from her stern purpose, had, after her father's departure, let bring poisonous herbs and roots and distilled and reduced them in water, so she might have it at hand, an that she feared should come to pass. The serving-man coming to her with the prince's present and message, she took the cup with a steadfast countenance and uncovered it. Whenas she saw the heart and apprehended the words of the message, she was throughly certified that this was Guiscardo's heart and turning her eyes upon the messenger, said to him, 'No sepulchre less of worth than one of gold had beseemed a heart such as this.' So saying, she set the heart to her lips and kissing it, said, 'Still in everything and even to this extreme limit of my life have I found my father's love most tender towards me; but now more than ever.'

Then, bending down over the cup, which she held fast, she said, looking upon the heart, 'Alack, sweetest harbourage of all my pleasures, accursed be his cruelty who maketh me now to see thee with the eyes of the body!' Then, without making any womanly outcry, she began, lamenting, to shed so many and such tears that they were a marvel to behold, kissing the dead heart the while an infinite number of times. Her women, who stood about her, understood not what this heart was nor what her words meant, but, overcome with compassion, wept all and in vain questioned her affectionately of the cause of her lament. The lady, having wept as much as she thought fit, raised her head and drying her eyes, said, 'O much-loved heart, I have accomplished mine every office towards thee, nor is there left me aught else to do save to come with my soul and bear thine company.' So saying, she called for the vial wherein was the poisoned water she had made the day before and poured the latter into the bowl where was the heart bathed with so many of her tears; then, setting her mouth thereto without any fear, she drank it all off and having drunken, mounted, with the cup in her hand, upon the bed, where composing her body as most decently she might, she pressed her dead lover's heart to her own and without saying aught, awaited death.

Tancred, fearing that which came to pass, came quickly down into his daughter's chamber, where he arrived what time she laid herself on her bed; but, seeing the extremity wherein she was, he fell a-weeping grievously. Quoth the lady to him, 'Tancred, keep these tears against a less desired fate than this of mine and give them not to me, who desire them not.' The agony of his grief suffered not the prince to reply; whereupon the young lady, feeling herself come to her end, strained the dead heart to her breast and said, 'Abide ye with God, for I go hence.' Then, closing her eyes and losing every sense, she departed this life of woe. Such, then, as you have heard, was the sorrowful ending of the loves of Guiscardo and Ghismonda, whose bodies Tancred, after much lamentation, too late repenting him of his cruelty, caused honourably bury in one same sepulchre, amid the general mourning of all the people of Salerno."
