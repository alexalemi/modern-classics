The Seventh Story

[Day the Second]

The Sultan of Babylon Sends His Daughter to Marry the King of Algarve, and Through Various Misfortunes She Comes into the Hands of Nine Men in Different Places. In the End, Being Restored to Her Father as a Virgin, She Goes to the King of Algarve as His Wife, as Was Originally Intended

If Emilia's story had gone on much longer, the young ladies would likely have been moved to tears by the misfortunes of Lady Beritola. But with the tale now concluded, the queen wished for Pamfilo to continue with his own story. Being very obedient, he began thus:

"Alas, gracious ladies, it is not easy for us to know what is best for us, for as can often be seen, many who imagined that if they were but rich, they could live without worry or care, have not only prayed to God for riches, but have diligently strived to acquire them, sparing no toil or peril in the quest. And whereas, before they became enriched, they cherished their lives, once they had obtained their desire, they have found people to slay them, out of greed for so ample an inheritance. Others of lowly estate, having through a thousand perilous battles and the blood of their kin and friends, risen to the summit of kingdoms, thinking in the royal estate to enjoy supreme felicity, without the innumerable cares and alarms whereof they see and feel it full, have learned, at the cost of their lives, that poison is drunk at royal tables in cups of gold. Many there be who have with most ardent appetite desired bodily strength and beauty and divers personal adornments and perceived not that they had desired ill till they found these very gifts a cause to them of death or dolorous life. In short, not to speak particularly of all the objects of human desire, I dare say that there is not one which can, with entire assurance, be chosen by mortal men as secure from the vicissitudes of fortune; wherefore, if we would do aright, we must resign ourselves to take and possess that which is appointed us of Him who alone knoweth that which behoveth unto us and is able to give it to us. 

But whereas men sin in desiring various things, you, gracious ladies, sin, above all, in one, to wit, in wishing to be fair—insomuch that, not content with the charms vouchsafed you by nature, you still with marvellous art study to augment them. It therefore pleases me to recount to you how ill-fortunedly fair was a Saracen lady, whom it befell, for her beauty, to be in some four years' space nine times wedded anew.

It is now a while since there was a certain Sultan of Babylon, by name Berminedab, to whom in his day many things happened according to his pleasure. Amongst many other children, both male and female, he had a daughter called Alatiel, who, by report of all who saw her, was the fairest woman to be seen in the world in those days. After the Sultan had inflicted a great defeat upon a vast multitude of Arabs who had come upon him, the King of Algarve, who had greatly aided him in this battle, requested the Sultan's daughter's hand in marriage. The Sultan, acceding to this request, embarked her aboard a well-armed and equipped ship, with an honorable company of men and ladies and a great deal of rich and sumptuous gear and furnishings, and dispatched her to the King of Algarve, commending her to God.

The sailors, seeing the weather favorable, set their sails to the wind and departed the port of Alexandria, faring on prosperously for many days.
