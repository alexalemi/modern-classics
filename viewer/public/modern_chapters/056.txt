THE NINTH STORY

[Day the Fifth]

FEDERIGO DEGLI ALBERIGHI LOVES BUT IS NOT LOVED. HE SPENDS ALL HIS WEALTH ON EXTRAVAGANT HOSPITALITY UNTIL HE HAS ONLY ONE FALCON LEFT, WHICH HE GIVES TO HIS MISTRESS TO EAT WHEN SHE VISITS HIS HOME; AND SHE, LEARNING OF THIS, CHANGES HER MIND AND TAKES HIM AS HER HUSBAND, MAKING HIM WEALTHY AGAIN

After Filomena finished speaking, the queen, seeing that only she and Dioneo remained to tell a story, said with a cheerful expression, "It is now my turn to speak, and I, dear ladies, will gladly do so, telling a tale similar in part to the previous one, so that not only may you know how much the love for you can sway gentle hearts, but also that you may learn to be bestowers of your own rewards, without always letting fortune be your guide, which most often, as it happens, gives not discreetly, but immoderately.

You must know, then, that Coppo di Borghese Domenichi, a man of great respect and authority in our city, who was alive in our time and may still be so, was much more admired for his manners and merits than for the nobility of his blood. As he grew older, he delighted in recounting to his neighbors and others the events of the past, which he knew how to do better, more orderly, and with greater memory and eloquence than any other man. Among his many fine tales, he would often tell of a young man in Florence named Federigo, the son of Messer Filippo Alberighi, renowned for his deeds of arms and courtesy above all other bachelors in Tuscany. As often happens with gentlemen, Federigo became enamored of a lady named Madam Giovanna, who was held to be one of the fairest and most lively ladies in Florence. To win her love, he held jousts and tournaments, made entertainments, and gave gifts, spending his wealth without restraint. But she, being no less virtuous than beautiful, cared nothing for these things done for her or for the one who did them. As Federigo spent far beyond his means and gained nothing, his wealth, as often happens, eventually came to an end, and he was left with only a small farm, on whose income he lived very modestly, and his beloved falcon, one of the best in the world. 

Federigo, now poorer than ever and still deeply in love, withdrew to his farm in Campi, where he lived patiently, hunting with his falcon whenever he could, and asking nothing of anyone. It happened one day that Madam Giovanna's husband fell ill and, realizing he was near death, made his will, leaving his well-grown son as his heir. Afterward, having greatly loved Madam Giovanna, he substituted her as his heir should his son die without legitimate heirs. The husband then passed away, and Madam Giovanna, now a widow, went that summer, as is the custom of our ladies, to a country estate of hers near Federigo's. It so happened that her son made acquaintance with Federigo and took delight in his hawks and hounds, and having often seen the falcon flown, he became greatly enamored of it and longed to have it, but did not dare ask Federigo for it, knowing how dear it was to him. The situation being thus, the boy fell ill, and his mother, who had no other child and loved him dearly, was deeply concerned. She spent all day comforting him and repeatedly asked if there was anything he desired, promising to get it for him if possible.

The boy, hearing these offers many times, said, "Mother, if you could get me Federigo's falcon, I believe I would soon recover." The lady, upon hearing this, paused to consider. She knew that Federigo had long loved her, though she had never granted him so much as a glance. She thought to herself, "How can I go to him and ask for the falcon, which is, by all accounts, the best that ever flew and is his only pleasure left in the world? And how can I be so thoughtless as to take this from a gentleman who has nothing else?" Perplexed by this thought and uncertain what to say, though certain she could get the bird if she asked, she remained silent. However, her love for her son eventually prevailed, and she resolved to satisfy him, come what may, and not to send, but to go herself to get the falcon and bring it to him. She then said to the boy, "My son, take heart and focus on getting well, for I promise you that the first thing I do tomorrow, I will go and fetch the falcon for you."

The next morning, the lady, taking another lady with her for company, went to Federigo's modest home and asked for him. As it was no weather for hawking and had not been for some days, Federigo was in his garden, tending to some small matters, and hearing that Madam Giovanna was at his door, hurried there, rejoicing and marveling greatly. Seeing him approach, she rose and, with gracious womanly manner, greeted him and said, "Federigo, I have come to make amends for the harm you have suffered on my account in loving me more than you should. That amends is that I intend to dine with you informally this morning, with this lady companion of mine." Federigo humbly replied, "Madam, I cannot recall having ever received any ill from you, but rather so much good that, if I was ever worth anything, it came through your worth and the love I bore you. And although you have come to a poor host, your gracious visit is far more precious to me than if I were once again able to spend as much as I have in the past." He then led her into his garden, saying that as he had no one else to accompany her, the good wife of a nearby farmer would keep her company while he saw to the table.

Never before, in his extreme poverty, had Federigo been so painfully aware of the straits to which he had brought himself by his past extravagance. But that morning, finding he had nothing worthy with which to entertain the lady he had once entertained so many, he ran about, greatly perplexed, inwardly cursing his ill fortune, but found neither money nor anything he could pawn. As the hour grew late and he still wished to honor the gentle lady, his eyes fell upon his prized falcon perched in his small hall. Deciding the bird would make a worthy dish, he took it, found it to be in fine condition, and had it plucked and roasted. Then, setting the table with the cleanest linens he had, he returned to the garden and told the lady that their meal was ready.

The lady and her companion went to the table and dined, unknowingly, on Federigo's beloved falcon. After the meal, and having conversed awhile with Federigo in a pleasant manner, the lady felt it was time to state her reason for coming. She turned to Federigo and said courteously, "Federigo, I do not doubt that when you hear why I have come here, you will marvel at my presumption, remembering your past life and my virtue, which you may have taken for cruelty and hardness of heart. But if you had or have children, by whom you could know the power of a mother's love, I believe you would partly excuse me. Although you have none, I, who have one child, cannot escape the common laws that govern other mothers, and I must, against my will and contrary to all propriety, ask of you a boon that I know is most dear to you - your falcon, for which my boy is so enamored that, if I do not bring it to him, I fear his present illness will be so aggravated that he may not survive. Therefore, I implore you, not by the love you bear me, to which you are in no way beholden, but by your own noble spirit, which in acts of generosity has proven greater than in any other way - that you would be pleased to give me this falcon, so that I may say I have kept my son alive and made him forever in your debt."

Federigo, hearing the lady's request and knowing he could not grant it, for he had given her the falcon to eat, began to weep in her presence before he could reply. The lady at first thought his tears were from grief at having to part with his beloved falcon, and was about to say she would not take it. But she restrained herself and waited for Federigo to speak, which he did after some weeping, saying, "Madam, since it pleased God that I should set my love on you, in many things have I found fortune against me, and I have complained of her; but all the ills she has done me are as nothing compared to what she now does in preventing me from being able to grant your request. The reason I cannot is that this morning, when I heard you were graciously coming to dine with me, wishing to honor you, as far as I was able, with something more choice than is commonly set before others, I bethought me of the falcon, which you now ask for, and judging him a worthy dish for you, I had him killed and served to you. Thus, you have now unwittingly had him on your plate. Seeing that I cannot now provide him to you in the manner you desire, it is such a grief to me that I believe I shall never be able to forgive myself." With that, he showed her the falcon's feathers, feet, and beak.

The lady, seeing and hearing this, first blamed him for having killed such a falcon to give a woman to eat, and then greatly commended the greatness of his soul, which poverty had not been able to diminish. Losing all hope of getting the falcon and in doubt of her son's recovery, she took her leave and, deeply saddened, returned to him. Before long, whether from grief at not getting the bird or because his illness was fated to do so, the boy passed away, to his mother's great sorrow. After a time, though still distraught, she was urged by her brothers to remarry. While she would have preferred not to, she remembered Federigo's worth and his final magnificent act of sacrificing his precious falcon for her, and she said to her brothers, "I would gladly remain as I am, if it were your wish, but since you desire me to take another husband, I will have no one other than Federigo degli Alberighi." Her brothers, mocking her, said, "Silly woman, what are you saying? How can you choose him, since he has nothing in the world?" But she replied, "Brothers, I know well that it is as you say, but I would rather have a man without riches than riches without a man." Hearing her mind and knowing Federigo to be a man of great merit, even in his poverty, they gave her, with all her wealth, to him, and he, finding himself married to such a worthy lady and one he had so deeply loved, became a better steward of his resources and lived the rest of his days with her in joy and comfort.
