THE SECOND STORY

[Day the Third]

A STABLEHAND SLEEPS WITH THE WIFE OF KING AGILULF, WHO, BECOMING AWARE OF IT, FINDS HIM OUT AND CUTS HIS HAIR; BUT THE STABLEHAND CUTS THE HAIR OF ALL HIS FELLOWS IN THE SAME WAY AND SO ESCAPES HARM

After Filostrato's story, which sometimes made the ladies blush and sometimes made them laugh, the queen decided that Pampinea should follow with a story. Pampinea began with a smile, saying, "Some people are so indiscreet in their efforts to show that they know and understand things that do not concern them, that while rebuking unseen flaws in others, they think to lessen their own shame, when in fact they only increase it infinitely. I intend to prove this to you, dear ladies, by showing you the cleverness of one who, in the judgment of a worthy and valiant king, was perhaps held in even less regard than Masetto himself.

Agilulf, King of the Lombards, as his predecessors had done, made the city of Pavia in Lombardy the seat of his kingdom, and took as his wife Theodolinda, the widow of Autari, another Lombard king. She was a very beautiful and exceedingly discreet and virtuous lady, but unfortunate in having a lover.

With the kingdom of the Lombards prospering thanks to King Agilulf's valor and wisdom, it came to pass that one of the queen's stable hands, a man of very low birth but with worth far exceeding his humble station, and handsome and tall as though he were the king himself, became deeply enamored of his mistress. His lowly status did not prevent him from realizing that this love of his was completely unreasonable, so, like a prudent man, he told no one of it, nor dared he even let it show in his eyes. But although he lived without any hope of ever winning her favor, he took great inner pride in having bestowed his affections on such a lofty lady. Burning with amorous fire, he worked harder than any of his fellows to do whatever he thought might please the queen. As a result, whenever she had occasion to ride abroad, she preferred to mount the palfrey cared for by this stable hand rather than any other. And when this happened, he counted it a great privilege, never leaving her stirrup, and considering himself fortunate whenever he might but touch her clothes.

But as it often happens that, even as hope grows less, so love grows greater, so it befell with this poor groom. His great desire was so hard for him to bear, hidden as it was and upheld by no hope, that many times he determined within himself to die. And considering the manner of it, he resolved to seek his death in such a way that it would be clear he died for the love he bore the queen. To this end, he decided to try his fortune in an enterprise that might give him either all or part of his desire. He did not set himself to speak to the queen or make her aware of his love by letters, knowing he would speak and write in vain, but rather chose to see if he might, in the guise of the king who, he knew, did not lie with her continually, contrive to make his way to her bedchamber and lie with her.

So one night, he hid himself in a great hall of the palace, which lay between the king's bedchamber and the queen's, and watched as the king came forth, wrapped in a large cloak, holding a lighted candle in one hand and a little wand in the other, and struck the door of the queen's chamber two or three times with the wand, without saying a word, whereupon the door was immediately opened and the candle taken from his hand. Noting this, and having seen the king return in the same manner, the stable hand set about doing likewise. He obtained a cloak like the one he had seen the king wear, along with a candle and a wand, and after first thoroughly bathing in a bathhouse lest the smell of the stables might offend the queen and give away the deception, he hid himself in the great hall as usual.

When he knew that all were asleep and it seemed to him time either to accomplish his desire or to meet the death he craved, he struck a light with the flint and steel he had brought, kindled the candle, wrapped himself tightly in the cloak, and went to the chamber door, striking it twice with the wand. The door was opened by a drowsy chambermaid, who took the light and covered it; whereupon, without a word, the stable hand passed within the curtain, removed his cloak, and entered the bed where the queen slept. Then, taking her desirefully in his arms and feigning distress (for he knew the king's custom, that when he was troubled, he did not wish to be spoken to), without speaking or being spoken to, he several times carnally knew the queen. Grievous as it was to him to depart, fearing lest his too long stay should turn the gained delight into sorrow, he rose, took up the cloak and the light, and withdrew, without a word, returning as quickly as he could to his own bed.

He had scarcely reached it when the king arose and went to the queen's chamber, at which she marveled exceedingly. And as he entered the bed and greeted her cheerfully, she took courage from his good humor and said, "My lord, what new custom is this of yours tonight? You left me but now, after having taken your pleasure of me more than usual, and do you return so soon? Take care what you do." The king, hearing these words, at once concluded that the queen had been deceived by similarity of manner and person, but, like a wise man, he bethought himself forthwith, seeing that neither she nor any else had perceived the deception, not to make her aware of it. Many simpletons would not have done so, but would have said, "I have not been here, I. Who is it that has been here? How did it happen? Who came hither?" From which many things might have arisen, whereby he would needlessly have afflicted the lady and given her cause to desire again that which she had already tasted; more by token that, by keeping silent on the matter, no shame might revert to him, whereas, by speaking, he would have brought dishonor upon himself. The king, then, more troubled at heart than in looks or speech, answered, saying, "Wife, seem I not man enough to have been here a first time and to come yet again after that?" "Ay, my lord," answered she. "Nevertheless, I beseech you have regard to your health." Quoth Agilulf, "And it pleaseth me to follow your counsel, wherefore for the nonce I will get me gone again, without giving you more annoy." This said, taking up his cloak, he departed the chamber, with a heart full of wrath and despite for the affront that he saw had been done him, and bethought himself quietly to seek to discover the culprit, concluding that he must be of the household and could not, whoever he might be, have issued forth of the palace. Accordingly, taking a very small light in a little lantern, he betook himself to a very long gallery that was over the stables of his palace and where all his household slept in different beds, and judging that, whoever he might be that had done what the queen said, his pulse and the beating of his heart for the exertion endured could not yet have had time to abate, he silently, beginning at one end of the gallery, fell to feeling each one's breast, to know if his heart beat high. Although every other slept fast, he who had been with the queen was not yet asleep, but, seeing the king come and guessing what he went seeking, fell into such a fright that to the beating of the heart caused by the recent activity, fear added yet a greater, and he doubted not but the king, if he became aware of this, would put him to death without delay. However, seeing him unarmed, he resolved to feign sleep and await what he should do. Agilulf, then, having examined many and found none whom he judged to be he of whom he was in quest, came presently to the stable hand and feeling his heart beat high, said in himself, "This is the man." Nevertheless, not wishing anything to be known of what he purposed to do, he did nought to him but cut, with a pair of scissors he had brought with him, somewhat on one side of his hair, which they then wore very long, so by that token he might know him again on the morrow; and this done, he withdrew and returned to his own chamber. The culprit, who had felt all this, like a shrewd fellow as he was, understood plainly enough why he had been thus marked; wherefore he arose without delay and, finding a pair of shears, which by chance there were several of about the stables for the service of the horses, went softly up to all who lay in the gallery and clipped each one's hair on like wise over the ear; which having done without being observed, he returned to sleep. When the king arose in the morning, he commanded that all his household should present themselves before him, or ever the palace-doors were opened; and it was done as he said. Then, as they all stood before him with uncovered heads, he began to look that he might know him whom he had polled; but, seeing the most part of them with their hair clipped after one and the same fashion, he marveled and said in himself, "He whom I seek, for all he may be of mean estate, showeth right well he is of no mean wit." Then, seeing that he could not, without making a stir, avail to have him whom he sought, and having no mind to incur a great shame for the sake of a paltry revenge, it pleased him with one sole word to admonish the culprit and show him that he was aware of the matter; wherefore, turning to all who were present, he said, "Let him who did it do it no more and get you gone in peace." Another would have been for giving them the strappado, for torturing, examining and questioning, and doing this, would have published that which every one should go about to conceal; and having thus discovered himself, though he should have taken entire revenge for the affront suffered, his shame had not been minished, nay, were rather much enhanced therefor and his lady's honour sullied. Those who heard the king's words marveled and long debated amongst themselves what he meant by this speech; but none understood it, save he whom it concerned, and he, like a wise man, never, during Agilulf's lifetime, discovered the matter nor ever again committed his life to the hazard of such a venture.
