THE FIRST STORY

[Day the Second]

MARTELLINO PRETENDS TO BE CRIPPLED AND CLAIMS TO BE HEALED BY THE BODY OF ST. ARRIGO. WHEN HIS DECEPTION IS DISCOVERED, HE IS BEATEN AND THEN NEARLY HANGED AS A THIEF, BUT ULTIMATELY ESCAPES

It often happens, dear ladies, that one who tries to deceive others, especially in matters of faith, finds himself with nothing to show for his efforts but ridicule and misfortune. Therefore, to obey the queen's command and begin the appointed theme with a story of my own, I shall relate to you what befell a fellow townsman of ours, first disastrously and then, beyond all expectation, fortunately.

Not long ago, there was a German named Arrigo in Treviso who, being a poor man, would carry burdens for hire as needed. He was held by all to be a man of very holy and good life. So it came to pass, whether true or not, that when he died, the bells of the great church in Treviso began to ring of their own accord, according to what the people of the city claimed. Considering this a miracle, the citizens proclaimed Arrigo a saint and carried his body, as that of a holy man, to the Cathedral, where they brought the lame, the infirm, and the blind, as well as those afflicted with any other ailment or infirmity, in the belief that they would be healed by touching the body.

Amidst this great commotion and throng of people, it happened that three of our fellow townsmen arrived in Treviso - one called Stecchi, another Martellino, and the third Marchese. These were men who visited the courts of princes and lords, amusing onlookers by disguising themselves and mimicking other people with remarkable motions and expressions. Having never been there before and seeing everyone rush by, they marveled and, learning the reason, wished to go observe the saint. However, Marchese said, "I do not see how we can get there, for I understand the Cathedral square is full of German and other soldiers, put there by the lord of this city to prevent any disturbance. Moreover, they say the church is so crowded that almost no one else can enter."

"Let that not stop you," replied Martellino, eager to witness the spectacle. "I will find a way for us to get in." "How so?" asked Marchese, and Martellino answered, "I will tell you. I will pretend to be a cripple, and you and Stecchi will support me on either side, as if I could not walk on my own, and we will try to bring me to the holy body so that he may heal me. Seeing us, no one will fail to make way and let us pass."

Marchese and Stecchi approved the plan, and they immediately set out from the inn. When they reached a secluded place, Martellino began to twist and contort his hands, arms, legs, mouth, and eyes in a most frightening manner, so that anyone seeing him would have sworn he was truly crippled and paralyzed. Marchese and Stecchi, supporting the apparently disabled Martellino, made their way toward the church, humbly beseeching all who crossed their path, for the love of God, to clear a path for them, which they readily did. Finally, they reached the place where Saint Arrigo's body lay, and Martellino was promptly taken up by some gentlemen standing around and laid upon the body, so that he might be healed.

After lying there a while, as the people eagerly awaited what would happen, Martellino began, as he knew very well how to do, to slowly open his fingers, then a hand, then an arm, and finally to stretch out completely. When the people saw this, they erupted in such praise of Saint Arrigo that it would have drowned out the very thunder.

However, by chance, there was a Florentine nearby who knew Martellino very well but had not recognized him in his disguise. But when he saw him fully restored, he knew him and immediately burst out laughing, saying, "God confound him! Who that saw him come would not have thought him truly crippled?" Some Trevisans overheard this and asked him, "What? Was he not crippled?" "God forbid!" answered the Florentine. "He has always been as straight as any of us, but he knows better than anyone how to play such tricks and counterfeit any shape he wishes."

Upon hearing this, the others needed no further proof. They pushed their way forward, crying out, "Seize that traitor and mocker of God and the saints, who, being sound in body, has come here disguised as a cripple to make a fool of us and our saint!" They seized Martellino and dragged him down from where he lay, pulling his hair, tearing off his clothes, and raining blows and kicks upon him. Martellino cried out, "Mercy, for God's sake!" and defended himself as best he could, but to no avail, as the crowd only intensified their attack.

Stecchi and Marchese, seeing this, began to say to each other that the situation was dire, but, fearing for themselves, dared not come to his aid; indeed, they joined the others in calling for him to be put to death. However, Marchese quickly devised a plan to extricate him. Knowing that all the officers of the authorities were outside the church, he hastened to the commander and said, "Help, for God's sake! There is a scoundrel inside who has cut my purse, with a good hundred gold florins. I pray you, have him arrested so I may recover my money." 

Hearing this, a dozen sergeants rushed to where the wretched Martellino was being beaten and, with great difficulty, extracted him from the mob, bruised and disheveled, and took him to the palace, where many followed, claiming he had stolen their purses as well. The judge, a crabby and ill-tempered man, immediately interrogated Martellino, who at first jested about his arrest, angering the judge, who had him strung up and given a few good turns of the rack, intent on forcing him to confess the thefts of which he was accused, so that he might then have him hanged.

When Martellino was let down, the judge asked him again if the accusations were true. Seeing that denying it was useless, Martellino replied, "My lord, I am ready to confess the truth to you, but first have each of my accusers say when and where I stole their purses, and I will tell you what I did and did not do." The judge agreed, and upon questioning the accusers, they gave conflicting accounts, some claiming he had stolen from them days earlier, others that very day. Martellino then said, "My lord, these all lie, for I swear to you that I have only just arrived in this city and, as misfortune would have it, went straight to see the holy body in the church, where I was so mistreated, as you can see. If you inquire of the Prince's officer who keeps the register of strangers, and of my host, they will confirm what I say is true. I beg you, therefore, not to torture or kill me at the behest of these wicked men."

While this was happening, Marchese and Stecchi, hearing that the judge was proceeding harshly against Martellino and had already had him racked, grew greatly afraid, saying to themselves, "We have gone about this the wrong way - we have brought him out of the frying pan and into the fire." They quickly went in search of their host and, finding him, recounted the situation. He laughed and took them to one Sandro Agolanti, a man of great influence with the Prince in Treviso, and, telling him everything, joined them in pleading for him to intervene on Martellino's behalf.

Sandro, after much laughter, went to the Prince and persuaded him to summon Martellino. The Prince's messengers found Martellino still in his shirt before the judge, utterly confounded and terrified, for the judge would hear nothing in his defense, being, by chance, somewhat spiteful toward the Florentines and determined to have him hanged, despite the Prince's orders. Martellino, brought before the lord of the city, told him everything in detail and begged, as a special favor, to be allowed to go on his way, for until he was back in Florence, he would still feel the noose around his neck. The Prince laughed heartily at his misfortune and had each of the three men given a suit of clothes, whereupon they returned home safely, having, beyond all hope, escaped so great a peril.
