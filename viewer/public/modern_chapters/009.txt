THE SEVENTH STORY

[Day the First]

BERGAMINO COURTEOUSLY REBUKES MESSER CANE DELLA SCALA'S SUDDEN STINGINESS WITH A STORY ABOUT PRIMASSO AND THE ABBOT OF CLUNY

Emilia's witty story caused the queen and everyone else to laugh and applaud the clever scheme of this new crusader. After the laughter had subsided, and all were silent again, Filostrato, whose turn it was to speak, began in this manner:

"Noble ladies, it is a fine thing to strike a target that never moves; but it is nearly miraculous if, when some unusual event suddenly appears, an archer can promptly hit the mark. The immoral and unclean lives of the clergy, in many ways a constant target for malice, provide ample opportunity for anyone who wishes to speak of, mock, and rebuke them. Therefore, while the worthy man who sharply criticized the hypocritical charity of the friars, who give to the poor what they should instead throw to the pigs or cast away, did well, I consider the one of whom I am about to speak even more praiseworthy. He used a clever story to reprimand Messer Cane della Scala, a magnificent nobleman, for a sudden and unaccustomed stinginess that had recently appeared in him, conveying through another person what he meant to say about Messer Cane himself. The story was as follows:

As is well known throughout the world, Messer Cane della Scala, who was favored by fortune in many ways, was one of the most notable and magnificent gentlemen ever seen in Italy since the time of Emperor Frederick the Second. Wishing to host a grand and impressive celebration in Verona, where many people from various places, especially skilled performers of all kinds, were expected to attend, he suddenly (for whatever reason) withdrew from the event. After partially compensating those who had come, he dismissed them all, except for one man named Bergamino, an eloquent and accomplished speaker beyond what anyone could believe who had not heard him. Bergamino had received neither a reward nor a dismissal and remained, hoping his stay would eventually prove to his advantage. But Messer Cane had decided that anything he might give to Bergamino would be worse spent than if it were thrown in the fire, and he did not speak to Bergamino or let him know this.

After some days, Bergamino, finding himself neither called upon nor required for any task related to his craft and wasting his resources at the inn with his horses and servants, began to be greatly concerned, but still waited, thinking it unwise to leave. He had brought with him three fine and valuable sets of clothing, given to him by other noblemen so he could appear impressive at the festival. As his host pressed him for payment, he gave one of the outfits to him. Staying even longer, he had to give the host the second set of clothing if he wished to remain, and he began living off the third, determined to wait as long as that lasted before departing.

While subsisting on the third set of clothes, Bergamino happened one day, while Messer Cane was dining, to present himself before him with a sorrowful expression. Seeing this, Messer Cane, more to tease him than to truly entertain himself with Bergamino's speech, said to him, "What troubles you, Bergamino, to stand there so disconsolate? Tell us something."

Bergamino, without hesitation, immediately recounted the following story relevant to his own situation:

"My lord, you should know that Primasso was a very learned grammarian and an exceptionally skilled and quick-witted poet above all others, making him so renowned and famous that, although he might not be recognized by sight everywhere, there was scarcely anyone who did not know his name and reputation. It happened that, finding himself once in Paris in a destitute state, as he often was, since merit is little valued by those who have the most power and means, he heard talk of the Abbot of Cluny, who is believed to be, next to the Pope, the richest prelate in the Church of God in terms of revenue. Primasso heard marvelous and magnificent things about the Abbot, how he maintained an open house and never denied food and drink to anyone who came to where he might be during mealtimes.

Primasso, who delighted in observing worthy and noble men, decided to go see the magnificence of this Abbot and inquired how close he was to Paris at the time. He was told the Abbot was then at one of his residences maybe six miles away, so Primasso thought to arrive there by dinnertime if he set out early in the morning.

Fearing he might get lost on the way and end up somewhere without readily available food, Primasso wisely brought with him three loaves of bread, reasoning that, while water (which he did not much enjoy) could be found anywhere, the bread would ensure he did not go hungry. Arriving at the Abbot's residence before the meal, he went about observing everything and saw the great number of tables set and the tremendous preparations in the kitchen and elsewhere for the dinner. He said to himself, 'Truly, this Abbot is as magnificent as they say.'

After waiting a while, when it was time to eat, the Abbot's steward called for water to wash the hands, then seated everyone at the tables. It happened that Primasso was placed directly across from the door of the chamber where the Abbot would emerge into the dining hall.

The custom in that household was that neither wine, bread, nor any other food or drink would be set on the tables until the Abbot had first come to sit at his own table. So, when the steward had set the tables, he informed the Abbot that the meal was ready whenever he pleased to come out. The Abbot opened the chamber door to enter the hall, and the first person his eyes fell upon was Primasso, who was very poorly dressed and unknown to him. At this sight, an uncharitable thought immediately came into the Abbot's mind, one that had never occurred to him before, and he said to himself, 'See to whom I give my wealth to eat!'

Turning back, he had the chamber door closed and asked those around him if anyone knew the scoundrel sitting at the table across from his door, but they all answered no. Meanwhile, Primasso, eager to eat after his journey and unaccustomed to fasting, waited a while and then, seeing the Abbot did not come, pulled one of the three loaves of bread from his cloak and began to eat.

The Abbot, after waiting some time, had one of his servants check if Primasso had left, and the servant reported that no, Primasso was eating the bread he had brought with him. The Abbot said, 'Well, let him eat his own, since he has it; he shall not eat of mine today.' He would have preferred for Primasso to leave of his own accord, thinking it improper to have him turned away, but Primasso, having eaten one loaf, began on the second. This was also reported to the Abbot, who continued pondering.

Finally, with the Abbot still delaying, Primasso, having eaten the second loaf, started on the third, and this was likewise reported to the Abbot. He then fell to reflecting and said to himself, 'Alas, what new miserly idea has entered my head today? What avarice! What disdain! And for whom? For all these years I have freely given my wealth to eat to whomever wished it, without regard to whether they were noble or common, poor or rich, merchant or peddler, and I have seen it squandered by a multitude of scoundrels; yet never until now has the thought occurred to me that has come to me about this man. Surely avarice cannot have assailed me for a person of little account; this man who seems a scoundrel to me must be of some great importance, since my soul has so revolted against honoring him.'

Realizing it was Primasso, a man of merit whom he had long known by reputation, come there to see with his own eyes the magnificence he had heard about, the Abbot was ashamed and eager to make amends. He studied many ways to honor Primasso, had him sumptuously clothed as befitted his status, gave him money and a palfrey, and left it to Primasso's own choice whether to stay or go. Primasso, well pleased with his treatment, expressed the greatest gratitude and returned on horseback to Paris, from whence he had set out on foot.

Messer Cane, being a gentleman of understanding, fully grasped Bergamino's meaning without further explanation and said to him, smiling, 'Bergamino, you have very aptly conveyed to me your wrongs and merits and my stinginess, as well as what you would have of me. And truly, never before, except now on your account, have I been accused of parsimony; but I will drive it away with the same stick you yourself have shown me.' Then, having Bergamino's host paid and dressing himself most sumptuously in one of his own suits, he gave Bergamino money and a palfrey and left it to him to choose, for the moment, whether to go or stay."
