The Ninth Story
(Day the Eighth)

Master Simone the Physician, Lured by Bruno and Buffalmacco to a Certain Place at Night to be Made a Member of a Roving Company, is Cast by Buffalmacco into a Trench Full of Filth and Left There

After the ladies had discussed the shared wives of the two Sienese, the queen, who alone had the duty of telling the next story, began in this manner so as not to wrong Dioneo:

"The trick played on Spinelloccio was well-deserved, my lovely ladies, and so I do not believe he should be harshly criticized, as Pampinea tried to argue earlier. Spinelloccio deserved it, and now I will tell you about someone who sought trickery for himself. Those who deceived him are not blameworthy, in my opinion, but rather praiseworthy, and the one deceived was a physician who, having left for Bologna a simpleton, returned to Florence cloaked in fine furs.

As we see every day, our fellow citizens return from Bologna - one a judge, another a physician, and a third a notary - decked out in long, large robes of scarlet and fur, making a grand display, though their actions often fall far short of their appearance. Among these was a certain Master Simone da Villa, richer in inherited goods than in learning, who returned here recently, calling himself a doctor of medicine, dressed in scarlet with a large fur hood, and took a house on the street we now call the Via del Cocomero. 

This Master Simone, newly returned as described, had among his other notable habits the tendency to ask whoever was with him about any person he saw passing by in the street, as if he were compounding medicines based on people's behaviors and fashions. He took particular notice of two painters we have already discussed twice today - Bruno and Buffalmacco - who were his neighbors and still went about together. Seeing that they seemed to care less for the world and live more merrily than others, which was indeed the case, he inquired of various people about their condition. Hearing from all that they were poor painters, he came to the conclusion that their merry living did not stem from their poverty, but rather, having heard they were clever fellows, that they must be deriving great profits from some unknown source. As a result, he became desirous of striking up a friendship with them both, or at least one of them, and succeeded in making friends with Bruno.

The latter, perceiving after a few visits that the physician was a complete simpleton, began to thoroughly enjoy himself at the man's expense and to be hugely entertained by his extraordinary mannerisms, while Master Simone, in turn, took marvelous delight in Bruno's company.

After a while, having invited him to dinner several times and feeling that he had earned the right to speak openly with him, the physician revealed the wonder he felt about how he and Buffalmacco, being poor men, could live so merrily, and begged him to explain how they did it. Bruno, hearing this from the physician and seeing that it was one of his usual senseless impertinences, stifled a laugh and, thinking to answer him as his foolishness deserved, said, "Doctor, there are not many to whom I would reveal how we live, but I shall not hesitate to tell you, since you are a friend and I know you will not repeat it to anyone. It is true that my friend and I live as merrily and as well as you see, indeed even more so, although neither from our craft nor from any income from possessions do we have enough to pay for the water we consume. Yet I would not have you think that we steal; no, we go roving, and from that, without harming anyone, we obtain all that we need or desire. And that is what we call going roving. But how strictly this must be kept secret, you can see for yourself, and therefore I say no more to you nor ask you of it."

The physician, whose knowledge likely extended no further than curing children of scald-head, gave as much credence to Bruno's story as the most evident truth, and was inflamed with as strong a desire to be admitted into that company as any could be kindled for the most desirable thing in the world. He therefore replied that it was certainly no marvel if they lived so merrily, and could scarcely restrain himself from asking Bruno to bring him there immediately. But reserving this request for a more favorable time, he proceeded to keep closer company with Bruno, having him eat with him morning and evening and showing him excessive affection. Indeed, their association was so great and constant that it seemed the physician could not or did not know how to live without the painter.

The latter, finding himself in good circumstances, so as not to appear ungrateful for the hospitality shown him, had painted a picture of Lent in the physician's hall, as well as an Agnus Dei at the entrance to his chamber and a chamber pot over the street door, so that those who had need of his advice might know how to recognize him. Moreover, in a small gallery he had, he had depicted the battle of the rats and the cats, which appeared to the physician a very fine thing. And at times, when he had not supped with him the night before, he would say to him, "I was at the society last night, and being a little weary of the Queen of England, I had them fetch me the Dolladoxy of the Grand Khan of Tartary." "What does 'Dolladoxy' mean?" asked Master Simone. "I do not understand these names." "Indeed, doctor mine," replied Bruno, "I marvel not at that, for I have heard that Porcograsso and Vannacena say nothing of them." The physician said, "Thou meanest Hippocrates and Avicenna." "In truth," answered Bruno, "I know not; I understand your names as poorly as you do mine. But 'Dolladoxy' in the Grand Khan's language means the same as 'Empress' in our tongue. By my faith, you would think her a fine woman! I dare say she would make you forget your drugs and your enemas and all your plasters."

In this manner he addressed him at one time and another, to inflame him the more, until one night, as it chanced that the doctor was holding the light for Bruno, who was painting the battle of the rats and the cats, the latter, believing he had now well secured him with his hospitality, decided to open his mind to him. And so, they being alone together, he said to him, "God knows, Bruno, there is no one alive for whom I would do everything as I would for thee; indeed, shouldst thou bid me go hence to Peretola, methinketh it would take little to make me go thither. Wherefore I would not have thee marvel if I require thee of somewhat familiarly and with confidence. As thou knowest, it is no great while since thou bespokest me of the fashions of your merry company, wherefore so great a longing hath taken me to be one of you that never did I desire aught so much. Nor is this my desire without cause, as thou shalt see, if ever it chance that I be of your company; for I give thee leave to make mock of me an I cause not come thither the finest serving-wench thou ever setst eyes on. I saw her but last year at Cacavincigli and wish her all my weal; and by the body of Christ, I had e'en given her half a score Bolognese groats, so she would but have consented to me; but she would not. Wherefore, as most I may, I prithee teach me what I must do to avail to be of your company and do thou also do and contrive so I may be thereof. Indeed, you will have in me a good and loyal comrade, ay, and a worshipful. Thou seest, to begin with, what a fine man I am and how well I am set up on my legs. Ay, and I have a face as it were a rose, more by token that I am a doctor of medicine, such as I believe you have none among you. Moreover, I know many fine things and goodly canzonets; marry, I will sing you one." And immediately he began to sing.

Bruno had such a strong desire to laugh that he was nearly bursting, but he restrained himself. The physician, having finished his song, asked, "How did you like that?" "Truly," answered Bruno, "there is no Jew's harp that would not lose against you, so grotesquely do you caterwail it." The physician said, "I tell thee, thou wouldst never have believed it, hadst thou not heard me." "Truly," replied Bruno, "you speak the truth!" And the physician went on, "I know a store of others; but let that be for the present. Such as thou seest me, my father was a gentleman, albeit he abode in the country, and I myself come by my mother of the Vallecchio family. Moreover, as thou mayst have seen, I have the finest books and gowns of any physician in Florence. Cock's faith, I have a gown that stood me, all reckoned, in nigh upon an hundred pounds of doits, more than half a score years ago; wherefore I pray thee as most I may, to bring me to be of your company, and by Cock's faith, an thou do it, thou mayst be as ill as thou wilt, for I will never take a farthing of thee for my services."

Bruno, hearing this and the physician seeming to him a greater simpleton than ever, said, "Doctor, hold the light a thought more this way and take patience till I have made these rats their tails, and after I will answer you." Once the tails were finished, Bruno pretended that the physician's request was extremely troublesome to him and said, "Doctor mine, these be great things you would do for me and I acknowledge it; nevertheless, that which you ask of me, little as it may be for the greatness of your brain, is yet to me a very grave matter, nor know I any one in the world for whom, it being in my power, I would do it, an I did it not for you, both because I love you as it behoveth and on account of your words, which are seasoned with so much wit that they would draw the straps out of a pair of boots, much more me from my purpose; for the more I consort with you, the wiser you appear to me. And I may tell you this, to boot, that, though I had none other reason, yet do I wish you well, for that I see you enamoured of so fair a creature as is she of whom you speak. But this much I will say to you; I have no such power in this matter as you suppose and cannot therefore do for you that which were behoving; however, an you will promise me, upon your solemn and surbated faith, to keep it me secret, I will tell you the means you must use and meseemeth certain that, with such fine books and other gear as you tell me you have, you will gain your end."

The doctor replied, "Say on in all assurance; I see thou art not yet well acquainted with me and knowest not how I can keep a secret. There be few things indeed that Messer Guasparruolo da Saliceto did, whenas he was judge of the Provostry at Forlimpopoli, but he sent to tell me, for that he found me so good a secret-keeper. And wilt thou judge an I say sooth? I was the first man whom he told that he was to marry Bergamina: seest thou now?" "Marry, then," rejoined Bruno, "all is well; if such a man trusted in you, I may well do so. The course you must take is on this wise. You must know that we still have to this our company a captain and two counsellors, who are changed from six months to six months, and without fail, at the first of the month, Buffalmacco will be captain and I shall be counsellor; for so it is settled. Now whoso is captain can do much by way of procuring whomsoever he will to be admitted into the company; wherefore meseemeth you should seek, inasmuch as you may, to gain Buffalmacco's friendship and do him honour. He is a man, seeing you so wise, to fall in love with you incontinent, and whenas with your wit and with these fine things you have you shall have somedele ingratiated yourself with him, you can make your request to him; he will not know how to say you nay. I have already bespoken him of you and he wisheth you all the weal in the world; and whenas you shall have done this, leave me do with him." The physician said, "That which thou counsellest liketh me well. Indeed, an he be a man who delighteth in men of learning and talketh but with me a little, I will engage to make him go still seeking my company, for that, as for wit, I have so much thereof that I could stock a city withal and yet abide exceeding wise."
