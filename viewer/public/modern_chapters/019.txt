THE SIXTH STORY

[Day the Second]

Lady Beritola, Having Lost Her Two Sons, Is Found on a Desert Island With Two Kids and Goes Thence Into Lunigiana, Where One of Her Sons, Taking Service With the Lord of the Country, Lies With His Daughter and Is Cast Into Prison. Sicily After Revolting Against King Charles and the Youth Being Recognized by His Mother, He Weds His Lord's Daughter, and His Brother Being Likewise Found, They Are All Three Restored to High Estate

Ladies and young men alike laughed heartily at Andreuccio's adventures, as told by Fiammetta, and when the story ended, Emilia, by the queen's command, began to speak thus: "The many changes of Fortune are grievous and sorrowful things, and whenever we discuss them, it awakens our minds, which easily fall asleep under her blandishments. I believe it should never be tiresome for either the happy or the unhappy to hear them told, as it makes the former wary and comforts the latter. Therefore, although great things have already been recounted on this subject, I intend to tell you a story that is no less true than it is pitiful. Although it had a joyful ending, the bitterness was so great and long-lasting that I can scarcely believe it was ever fully assuaged by any subsequent gladness.

You must know, dear ladies, that after the death of Emperor Frederick the Second, Manfred was crowned King of Sicily, in very high estate. With him was a gentleman from Naples called Arrighetto Capece, who had as his wife a fair and noble lady, also from Naples, named Lady Beritola Caracciola. This Arrighetto, who had the governance of the island in his hands, hearing that King Charles the First had overcome and slain Manfred at Benevento and that all the realm had revolted to him, and having little confidence in the fickle loyalty of the Sicilians, prepared for flight, disliking to become a subject of his lord's enemy. But his intent being known to the Sicilians, he and many other friends and servants of King Manfred were suddenly made prisoners and delivered to King Charles, together with possession of the island.

Lady Beritola, in this grievous change of affairs, knowing not what had become of Arrighetto and greatly afraid of what had befallen, abandoned all her possessions for fear of shame. Poor and pregnant as she was, she embarked, with a son of hers about eight years old, named Giusfredi, in a small boat and fled to Lipari, where she gave birth to another son, whom she named Scacciato. There she hired a nurse and took ship with all three to return to her family in Naples. But it happened otherwise than as she purposed; for the ship, which should have gone to Naples, was carried by a storm to the island of Ponza, where they entered a small cove and there awaited an opportunity to continue their voyage. 

Lady Beritola, going up, like the rest, onto the island and finding a remote and solitary place, began to lament for her Arrighetto, all alone there. While she was thus occupied in bewailing herself, a pirate galley came upon them unobserved and took them all, except for Lady Beritola, who had gone off from the shore. The galley made off with its prize, leaving Lady Beritola poor, desolate, and forsaken, not knowing where she might ever again find any of them. Seeing herself there alone, she fell down fainting upon the strand, calling upon her husband and children. 

After a time, her lost senses returning to her wretched body, accompanied by tears and laments, she called long upon her children and went seeking them in every cave. At last, finding all her labor in vain and seeing the night coming on, she began, hoping and knowing not what, to care for herself and returned to the cave where she was wont to weep. There she passed the night in great fear and unspeakable grief. The new day being come, she was compelled by hunger to feed upon herbs; and having done so, she gave herself, weeping, to various thoughts of her future life. 

As she pondered, she saw a she-goat enter a nearby cave and presently issue thence and go into the wood. Entering where the goat had come forth, she found there two little kids, which seemed to her the most charming and pretty things in the world. Her milk being yet undried from her recent delivery, she tenderly took up the kids and set them to her breast. They accepted her service, sucking her as if she had been their own mother, and thenceforth made no distinction between her and the old goat. Thus, deeming she had found some company in that desert place, and growing familiar with the goats, she resigned herself to live and die there, eating of herbs and drinking water and weeping as often as she remembered her husband and children and her past life.

It happened after some months that a small vessel from Pisa came to the place where Lady Beritola had been driven by the storm. On board was a gentleman named Currado, of the Marquises of Malespina, who, with his wife, a lady of worth and piety, was returning home from a pilgrimage. To pass the time, Currado and his party went about the island, and the dogs started the two kids, which had now grown quite big, as they grazed. The kids, chased by the dogs, fled into the cave where Lady Beritola was, and seeing this, she started up and beat off the dogs with a staff. Currado and his wife, marveling at the wild-looking lady, prevailed upon her, with much entreaty, to tell them who she was and what she did there. 

Currado, who had known Arrighetto Capece very well, hearing her tale, wept for pity and did his utmost to persuade her to leave her barbarous purpose, offering to take her back to her own home or to keep her with himself, holding her in such honor as his sister, until God should send her happier fortune. Though the lady did not yield to these offers, Currado left his wife with her, bidding the latter see that she was clothed and fed. Ultimately, after much persuasion, Lady Beritola agreed to go with Currado's wife to Lunigiana, together with the two kids and their mother.

Meanwhile, the corsairs who had taken the ship wherein Lady Beritola came to Ponza, but had left herself, as being unseen of them, betook themselves with all the other folk to Genoa, where the booty was divided, and the nurse and the two children fell to the lot of a certain Messer Guasparrino d'Oria. He sent them all three to his mansion, to be there employed as slaves about the service of the house. The nurse, though poor, was discreet, and when she saw that tears availed nothing, she resolved to conceal from everyone the true identity of the two children, telling all who asked that they were her sons. She named the elder, not Giusfredi, but Giannotto di Procida, and explained to him why she had changed his name, showing him the danger he might be in if he were known.

Years passed, and the two boys and their nurse abode patiently in Messer Guasparrino's house, ill-clad and employed about the meanest offices. But Giannotto, now sixteen years old and having more spirit than befitted a slave, embarked on board certain galleys bound for Alexandria, taking leave of Messer Guasparrino's service. After some years, hearing that his father, whom he thought dead, was yet alive but kept by King Charles in prison, he went wandering until he came to Lunigiana and there took service with Currado Malespina.

Giannotto, being in Currado's service, chanced to catch the eye of Currado's daughter, Spina, a widow of little more than sixteen years. They became passionately enamored of each other, and their love eventually led to an intimate encounter in a wood, where they were discovered by Currado and Spina's mother. Enraged, Currado had them both seized and imprisoned, intending to put them to a shameful death. However, the girl's mother persuaded him to imprison them instead, where they languished for a year.

During this time, Giannotto learned of the Sicilian revolt against King Charles, and realizing his father was likely alive and in a position of power, he revealed his true identity to his jailers. Word of this reached Currado, who then recognized Giannotto as the long-lost son of Arrighetto Capece. Seeing an opportunity, Currado arranged for Giannotto, now known as Giusfredi, to marry Spina, thus restoring the family's honor.

Meanwhile, Messer Guasparrino in Genoa also came to recognize the true identities of the two boys, and sent the younger son, Scacciato, along with Giusfredi's new wife, to reunite with the family in Lunigiana. There, the joyful reunion of mother, sons, and the recovered father, Arrighetto Capece, who had been freed in the Sicilian rebellion, was celebrated with great feasting and festivities.
