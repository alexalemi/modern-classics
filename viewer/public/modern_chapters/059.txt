A Noble Lady Silences a Clumsy Storyteller

Not long ago, in our city, there lived a noble, well-mannered, and eloquent gentlewoman whose worth deserved to be remembered. She was called Madame Oretta and was the wife of Messer Geri Spina. She happened to be in the countryside, along with a group of ladies and gentlemen, whom she had entertained for dinner at her home that day. The journey from their starting point to their intended destination on foot was quite lengthy, and one of the gentlemen said to her, "Madame Oretta, if you're willing, I'll carry you part of the way on horseback while telling you one of the finest stories in the world." "Indeed, sir," the lady replied, "I would be most pleased if you did."

The gentleman, who perhaps fared better with his sword than his storytelling, began his tale, which was genuinely quite good. However, he repeatedly stumbled, now pausing to correct himself, now mixing up the names, and often delivering the narrative in a disorderly fashion, much to the distress of Madame Oretta, who listened with growing unease, as if she were becoming ill. Finally, unable to bear it any longer and seeing the gentleman hopelessly tangled in his own story, she said to him pleasantly, "Sir, this horse of yours has too rough a gait; I beg you, please set me down." The gentleman, who fortunately understood a hint better than he told a story, took the jest in good humor, changed the subject, and abandoned the tale he had begun and so poorly told.
