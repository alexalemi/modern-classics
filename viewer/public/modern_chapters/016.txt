The Third Story

[Day the Second]

Three Young Men Squander Their Wealth and Become Poor; But a Nephew of Theirs, Returning Home in Desperation, Meets an Abbot Who Is Revealed to Be the King's Daughter of England, Who Takes Him as Her Husband and Restores His Uncles to Good Standing

The tales of Rinaldo d'Asti were heard with admiration, and the ladies commended his piety, thanking God and St. Julian for assisting him in his hour of greatest need. They also subtly joked about the woman who had welcomed the "good fortune" that came to her own home. As they discussed the pleasant night she had experienced, Pampinea, seeing that her turn was next, began to collect her thoughts and consider what she would say. After receiving the queen's command, she spoke boldly and cheerfully:

"Noble ladies, the more we discuss the workings of Fortune, the more there is to be said about them, for anyone who observes her actions carefully will see that all the things we foolishly call our own are in her hands and are constantly being transformed by her in ways we cannot comprehend. Although this truth is clearly demonstrated every day and has already been shown in the previous stories, since it is the queen's wish that we discourse on this theme, I will add a tale of my own that I believe will be pleasing to the listeners.

There was once a wealthy gentleman in our city named Messer Tedaldo, who some say was of the Lamberti family, while others claim he was of the Agolanti, arguing more from the trade his sons followed - which was similar to that practiced by the Agolanti - than from anything else. But regardless of which family he belonged to, I will say that he was a very rich gentleman in his time and had three sons: the eldest was named Lamberto, the second Tedaldo, and the third Agolante. They were all handsome and lively youths, with the eldest not yet having reached his eighteenth year when Messer Tedaldo died, leaving all his possessions, both movable and immovable, to them as his rightful heirs.

The young men, seeing themselves left very wealthy in both lands and money, began to spend without restraint or prudence, following only their own pleasure. They maintained a vast household, with many fine horses, dogs, and hawks, always entertaining guests and being generous, engaging in tournaments and other pursuits befitting gentlemen, as well as anything else that occurred to their youthful desires.

They had not long lived this way before the fortune left by their father was exhausted, and their revenues alone were insufficient to cover their current expenses. They proceeded to sell and mortgage their estates, selling one today and another tomorrow, until they found themselves with almost nothing, their wealth having blinded them to their decline into poverty. One day, Lamberto called the other two together, reminding them of their father's and their own former magnificence, laying out the wealth they had possessed and the poverty to which they had come through their excessive spending. He exhorted them, as best he could, to sell what little was left and depart with him before their distress became more apparent.

They did as he counseled, leaving Florence without fanfare or ceremony, and went to England, where they took a small house in London and, spending very little, diligently lent money at interest. Fortune favored them so much that in a few years, they had amassed a vast sum of money. One by one, they returned to Florence, buying back much of their former estates and acquiring others, taking wives for themselves.

However, they continued to lend money in England, sending a young nephew of theirs named Alessandro to look after their affairs there. The three brothers, now fathers of families, forgot the ruin that excessive spending had previously brought them and began to spend even more extravagantly than before, enjoying great credit with all the merchants, who trusted them for any sum of money, however large. The remittances sent by Alessandro, who was profitably lending to barons upon their castles and other possessions, allowed the brothers to maintain these expenses for some years. But eventually, as the three brothers spent freely and borrowed more, lacking their own money, while still counting on England, a war unexpectedly broke out there between the king and his son, dividing the whole island into two factions. As a result, Alessandro lost all the barons' castles, and there was no other source of revenue to repay them. Alessandro remained in England, hoping for peace between father and son to be restored and for everything to be returned to him, both interest and capital. But the three brothers in Florence did not reduce their extravagant spending, borrowing more and more every day.

When, after several years, their expectations were not fulfilled, the three brothers not only lost their credit, but their creditors, seeking payment, had them suddenly arrested. Their possessions were insufficient to pay their debts, so they remained in prison, while their wives and children went to the countryside or elsewhere, in great distress, not knowing what to expect but misery for the rest of their lives.

Meanwhile, Alessandro, after waiting several years in England for peace and seeing that it did not come, and fearing not only that his stay there was in vain but also for his life, decided to return to Italy. As he was leaving Bruges, he encountered an abbot of white friars, accompanied by many monks and with a large retinue and baggage train. Behind the abbot came two old knights, kinsmen of the King, whom Alessandro greeted as acquaintances and was gladly admitted into their company. As they traveled, he softly asked them who the monks were riding ahead with such a large train and where they were bound. One of the knights replied that the man riding ahead was a young gentleman of their family, newly elected abbot of one of the most important abbeys in England, and that they were going to Rome with him to obtain from the Holy Father a dispensation for his youth, as he was younger than the law allowed for such a position, but this was not to be spoken of to anyone.

The new abbot, sometimes riding ahead of his retinue and sometimes behind, as is common for noblemen on a journey, happened to see the handsome, well-mannered, and agreeable Alessandro near him and was immediately captivated, more than by anything else he had ever seen. Calling him to his side, he began to converse pleasantly with him, asking who he was, where he came from, and where he was bound. Alessandro frankly disclosed his whole situation and satisfied the abbot's questions, offering himself in whatever little way he could be of service. Hearing Alessandro's refined and well-ordered speech, the abbot took closer note of his manners and, judging him to be a man of gentle breeding despite his lowly circumstances, grew even more enamored of his pleasantness and full of compassion for his misfortunes. He comforted him in a very friendly manner, telling him to have good hope, for if he was a worthy man, God would yet restore him to the estate from which fortune had cast him down, or even a higher one. Moreover, he invited Alessandro, since he was bound for Tuscany, to accompany him, as he himself was also heading that way; Alessandro gratefully accepted the invitation and declared himself ready to serve the abbot's every command.

As the abbot's journey continued, it happened that they came to a village not well-equipped with inns, and the abbot, wanting to spend the night there, Alessandro arranged for him to stay at the house of an innkeeper who was his acquaintance, and had the abbot's sleeping chamber prepared in the least inconvenient part of the house. Being now, like an experienced man, almost a master of the abbot's household, Alessandro lodged all the abbot's company around the village as best he could. After the abbot had supped, with the night well advanced and everyone gone to bed, Alessandro asked the host where he himself could sleep. The host replied that he did not know, since every place was full and he and his household would have to sleep on the benches. However, he said, in the abbot's chamber there were some grain sacks, and he could make a small bed for Alessandro there, if that pleased him. Alessandro was concerned, knowing the chamber was small and none of the monks could have slept there, but the host assured him the matter was settled, and if Alessandro wished, he could sleep there quite comfortably.

Seeing that this could be done without disturbing the abbot, Alessandro agreed and settled himself on the grain sacks as softly as he could. The abbot, who was not sleeping but whose thoughts were ardently occupied with new desires, had heard what passed between Alessandro and the host and noted where the former laid himself to sleep. Pleased with this, he began to say to himself, "God has sent an occasion to my desires; if I do not take it, it may be long before the like recurs to me." Accordingly, fully resolved to seize the opportunity, and sensing that all was quiet in the inn, he called to Alessandro in a low voice and bade him come lie with him. After many excuses, Alessandro put off his clothes and laid himself beside the abbot, who put his hand on his breast and began to caress him in the manner amorous maidens use with their lovers. Alessandro marveled greatly at this and suspected the abbot was moved by unnatural love to handle him so, but the latter quickly divined his suspicions, whether by presumption or some gesture of his, and smiled. Then, suddenly removing a shirt he wore, he took Alessandro's hand and, laying it on his own breast, said, "Alessandro, put away your foolish thought and, searching here, know that which I conceal."

Alessandro accordingly put his hand to the abbot's bosom and found there two small, round, firm, and delicate breasts, no different than those of ivory, whereby perceiving that the supposed prelate was a woman, without further ado, he took her in his arms and would have kissed her. But she said to him, "Before you draw nearer to me, hear what I have to say to you. As you can see, I am a woman, not a man, and having left home a maiden, I was on my way to the Pope, that he might marry me. Whether by your good fortune or my misfortune, no sooner did I see you the other day than love so inflamed me for you that never was a woman who so loved a man. Therefore, I am resolved to take you, before any other, as my husband; but if you will not have me as your wife, depart from here at once and return to your place."

Alessandro, although he did not know her, considering her company and retinue, judged her to be necessarily noble and rich, and seeing that she was very beautiful, without much thought, replied that, if this pleased her, it was most agreeable to him. Accordingly, sitting up with him in bed, she put a ring into his hand and made him espouse her before a picture in which our Lord was portrayed, after which they embraced each other and delighted in amorous caresses, to the exceeding pleasure of both parties, for as much of the night as remained.

When day came, after they had made arrangements concerning their affairs together, Alessandro arose and departed the chamber by the way he had entered, without anyone knowing where he had passed the night. Then, overjoyed, he resumed the road with the abbot and his company and, after many days, arrived in Rome. There they remained some days, after which the abbot, with the two knights and Alessandro, went in to the Pope and, having done him due reverence, spoke thus..."
