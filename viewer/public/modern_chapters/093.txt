This being settled, Bruno told Buffalmacco the whole matter, and the latter was eager to do what this schemer was seeking. The physician, who longed greatly to go wandering, wasted no time in befriending Buffalmacco, which he easily accomplished, and he then began lavishing the finest suppers and dinners in the world on the two painters. The two artists, being the accommodating gentlemen they were, gladly engaged with him and, having once tasted the excellent wines, fat chickens, and other abundant good things he provided, stuck very close to him, uninvited, declaring they would not do this for anyone else.

When the time seemed right, the physician made the same request to Buffalmacco that he had previously made to Bruno. Buffalmacco then pretended to be deeply upset and loudly accused Bruno of betraying their secrets to the doctor. 

Master Simone did his best to excuse Bruno, saying and swearing he had learned of it from elsewhere, and after many of his wise words, he succeeded in calming Buffalmacco. The latter then turned to him and said, "Doctor, it's clear you've been to Bologna and brought back a gift for keeping secrets here. And I'll tell you that you didn't learn your ABCs on an apple like many fools, but rather on a pumpkin, which is so long. And if I'm not mistaken, you were baptized on a Sunday." He went on to say that although Bruno had said the doctor had told him he studied medicine in Bologna, it seemed he had really studied the art of catching men, which he knew better than anyone.

The doctor interrupted, saying to Bruno, "What a thing it is to talk and associate with learned men! Who would have so quickly understood every detail of my intelligence as this worthy man has? You didn't grasp my value half as quickly as he did. But at least, what I told you, when you said that Buffalmacco delights in learned men, have I not done it?" "Yes, you have," replied Bruno, "and even better."

The doctor then said to Buffalmacco, "You would have told a different tale if you had seen me in Bologna, where there was no one, great or small, doctor or scholar, who did not wish me every good thing, so well did I know how to entertain them all with my speech and wit. And what's more, I never said a word there that did not make everyone laugh, so much did I please them. And when I departed, they all lamented greatly and would have had me stay. In fact, they offered to let me lecture on medicine to all the students there, but I would not, for I was determined to come here to certain very great inheritances I have here in my family."

Bruno said to Buffalmacco, "What do you think? You didn't believe me when I told you. By the Gospels, there is not a doctor in these parts who is as skilled in urine analysis as this one, and you certainly wouldn't find another like him from here to the gates of Paris. Truly, you must refrain from doing what he wishes."

The doctor said, "Bruno speaks the truth, but I am not understood here. You Florentines are rather dull-witted, but you shall see me among the doctors, as I am accustomed to be." Buffalmacco replied, "Truly, doctor, you are far wiser than I could have believed. Therefore, to speak to you as one should speak to scholars like you, I tell you plainly, I will without fail have you admitted to our company."

After this promise, the physician redoubled his hospitality to the two rascals, who enjoyed themselves at his expense, filling him with the greatest fancies in the world and fooling him to the utmost, promising to give him as mistress the Countess of Sewers, who was the fairest creature to be found in all the back alleys of humanity. The physician inquired who this countess was, and Buffalmacco replied, "Good pumpkin-head, she is a very great lady and there are few houses in the world where she doesn't have some jurisdiction. Even the Franciscan friars pay her tribute, to the sound of trumpets. And I can assure you that when she goes abroad, she makes her presence known, even though she mostly stays shut up. It's not long since she passed by your door one night when she went to the Arno to wash her feet and get some air, but her most constant abode is in Latrine-Land. There are often her sergeants going about, all bearing the staff and the plumb-line in token of her supremacy, and many of her barons are to be seen everywhere, such as Sir-Reverence-of-the-Gate, Goodman-Turd, Hard-Cake, Loose-Bowels, and others who I think you're familiar with, even if you don't call them to mind right now. In the soft arms of this great lady, leaving aside her of Cacavincigli, we will, if our expectations are not disappointed, place you."

The physician, who had been born and raised in Bologna, did not understand their canting terms and accordingly declared himself well pleased with the lady in question. Not long after this talk, the painters brought him word that he was accepted into the company, and when the day came before the night appointed for their assembly, he invited them both to dinner. After they had dined, he asked them what he should do to get there. Buffalmacco said, "Look, doctor, you must have plenty of courage, for if you are not very resolute, you may encounter hindrance and do us great harm. As for what you must prove yourself very stout-hearted in, you shall hear. You must find a way to be this evening, at the time of the first sleep, on one of the new tombs outside Santa Maria Novella, wearing one of your finest gowns, so you may make an honorable appearance for your first meeting with the company. Also, it was supposedly said to us (though we weren't there) that the Countess intends to make you a Knight of the Bath at her own expense, since you are a gentleman. And there you must wait until one whom we shall send comes for you. 

"To apprise you of everything, a black horned beast, not too large, will come prancing about the piazza before you, whistling and bounding to terrify you. But when he sees you are not to be daunted, he will come up to you quietly. Then you must, without any fear, come down from the tomb and mount the beast, naming neither God nor the Saints. As soon as you are settled on his back, you must cross your hands on your breast, in an attitude of obedience, and touch him no more. He will then set off gently and bring you to us. But if you call on God or the Saints or show fear, I must tell you that he may chance to throw you off or deposit you where you are like to stink for it. So if your heart fails you and you cannot be very resolute, do not come, for you would only do us harm, not yourself any good."

The physician replied, "You don't know me yet. Maybe you judge me by my gloves and long gown. If you knew what I used to do at night in Bologna when I went wenching with my friends, you would marvel. By God, there was such and such a night when, one of them refusing to come with us (more by token that she was a nasty little thing no higher than my fist), I first gave her a good beating, then picked her up bodily and carried her a crossbow-shot and made her come with us. Another time I remember that, with only a servant of mine for company, I went past the cemetery of the Franciscans shortly after the Ave Maria, even though a woman had been buried there that very day, and felt no fear at all. So have no doubts about my courage and hardiness. Moreover, I tell you that to do you credit at my coming, I will wear my scarlet gown in which I was admitted as a doctor, and you shall see if the company does not rejoice at my sight and make me their captain straightaway. You'll see how things will go once I'm there, since, without even having seen me yet, this countess has fallen so in love with me that she means to make me a Knight of the Bath. Knighthood may not sit so ill on me, nor shall I be at a loss to carry it off with honor! Just let me do it."

"You speak very well," answered Buffalmacco. "But see that you do not fail us or not be found at the meeting place when we send for you, and I say this because the weather is cold and you gentlemen doctors are very careful of yourselves in that regard." "God forbid!" cried Master Simone. "I'm not one of your chilly ones. I don't care about the cold; seldom or never, when I get up at night to take care of my bodily needs, as a man will sometimes, do I put on more than my fur gown over my doublet. So I will certainly be there."

Thereupon they took their leave, and when night began to fall, Master Simone contrived some excuse or other to his wife and secretly got out his fine gown. Then, when he thought it the right time, he put it on and went to Santa Maria Novella, where he climbed up onto one of the tombs and, huddling on the marble because the cold was great, began to wait for the beast's arrival. Meanwhile, Buffalmacco, who was tall and sturdy, managed to get hold of one of the masks used for certain games no longer held these days. He donned it, arraying himself in a black fur cloak worn inside-out so that he seemed a very bear, save that his mask had a devil's face and horns. Thus accoutered, he went to the new piazza of Santa Maria, with Bruno following to see how things would go. As soon as he saw the physician was there, he began prancing and caracoling, making a terrible blustering about the piazza, whistling and howling and bellowing as if possessed by the devil.

When Master Simone, who was more fearful than a woman, heard and saw this, every hair on his body stood on end and he began trembling all over. He would have much rather been at home than there. Nevertheless, since he was already there, he forced himself to take heart, so overcome was he with the desire to see the marvels the painters had told him of.

After Buffalmacco had raged about as described, he pretended to grow calmer and came up to the tomb where the physician was. Master Simone, who was all a-tremble with fear, did not know what to do, whether to mount or stay where he was. However, at last, fearing the beast might do him harm if he did not mount, he overcame his first fear with the second and came down from the tomb, getting on the beast's back, saying softly, "God help me!" Then he settled himself as best he could, still trembling in every limb, crossing his hands on his breast as he had been instructed. Whereupon Buffalmacco set off at a slow pace towards Santa Maria della Scala, going on all fours and bringing him close to the Convent of Ripoli.

In those days there were ditches in that area where the nearby farmers emptied their cesspits to fertilize their fields. As they approached one of these ditches, Buffalmacco went up to the edge and, seizing the opportunity, grabbed one of the physician's legs and jerked him off his back, pitching him headfirst into the filth. Then he fell to snorting and snarling and prancing about for a while before making off alongside Santa Maria della Scala to the Fields of All Saints, where he found Bruno, who had fled because he could not restrain his laughter. Together they enjoyed the spectacle of the soiled physician.

The poor doctor, finding himself in that abominable place, struggled to get up and tried his best to get out, falling in again and again, now here and now there, and swallowing some of the filth, until at last he succeeded in making his way out, in the most woeful state, covered from head to foot and having lost his cap. Then, having wiped himself as best he could with his hands and not knowing what other course to take, he returned home and knocked till the door was opened to him. Hardly had he entered, stinking as he did, and the door shut again, than up came Bruno and Buffalmacco, eager to hear how he would be received by his wife. Standing and listening, they heard the lady give him the foulest scolding ever given to a poor soul, saying, "Good heavens, what a mess you're in! You've been gadding about with some other woman and trying to cut a figure in your scarlet gown! What, wasn't I enough for you? Why, man alive, I could satisfy a whole people, let alone you. I wish they'd choked you, like they threw you where you deserved to be thrown! Here's a fine doctor for you, with a wife of his own and going out at night after other people's womenfolk!" And with these and many other such words, she tormented him until midnight, while the physician was forced to wash himself from head to foot.

The next morning, Bruno and Buffalmacco came, having painted their flesh under their clothes with livid blotches as if from beatings, and entering the physician's house, found him already up. They went to him and found the whole place full of stench, for they had not yet been able to clean everything so that it no longer stank. Master Simone, seeing them enter, greeted them, and the two rascals, as they had agreed beforehand, replied angrily, saying, "We don't give you good day; rather, we pray God give you so many ill years that you may die a dog's death, you being the most disloyal man and the vilest traitor alive. For it was no thanks to you that, whereas we sought to do you honor and worship, we were not slain like dogs. As it is, thanks to your disloyalty, we have gotten so many blows this past night that an ass would go to Rome for less, not to mention that we have been in danger of expulsion from the company into which we had arranged to have you admitted. If you don't believe us, look at our bodies and see how they have fared." Then, opening their clothes in front, they showed him, by an uncertain light, their breasts all painted and covered them up again in haste.

The physician would have excused himself and told of his misadventures and how and where he had been thrown in, but Buffalmacco said, "I wish he had thrown you off the bridge into the Arno! Why did you call on God and the Saints? Were you not forewarned of this?" "By God's faith," replied the physician, "I did not do it." "How?" cried Buffalmacco. "You did not call on them? Egad, you did it again and again, for our messenger told us that you were shaking like a reed and didn't know where you were. Well, for now you have fooled us finely; but never again shall any one serve us thus, and we will yet do you such honor thereof as you merit." The physician began begging pardon and imploring them, for God's sake, not to dishonor him, and he tried to appease them with the best words he could command. And if before he had treated them with honor, from that time on he honored them even more and made much of them, entertaining them with banquets and the like, for fear they should publish his shame. Thus, then, as you have heard, sense is taught to one who has learned no great store thereof at Bologna.
