The Fourth Story
[Day the Fourth]

Gerbino, Defying the Promise Made by His Grandfather, King William of Sicily, Attacks a Ship of the King of Tunis to Capture His Daughter, Who Is Then Put to Death by Those on Board, and He in Turn Is Later Beheaded

After Lauretta finished her story, the group mourned the tragic fate of the young lovers. Some blamed Ninetta's anger, while others offered various perspectives. The king then lifted his head, as if emerging from deep thought, and signaled for Elisa to continue. She began modestly:

"Charming ladies, there are many who believe that Love only strikes when kindled by the eyes and mock those who hold that one may fall in love by hearsay. But you will see that this is mistaken, for in the story I will relate, you will witness that mere report, without the lovers ever having seen each other, not only brought this about but also led both of them to a miserable end.

King William the Second of Sicily had, as the Sicilians claim, two children - a son named Roger and a daughter named Constance. The son died before his father, leaving a grandson named Gerbino, who was carefully raised by his grandfather and became a very handsome youth, renowned for his valor and courtesy. His fame spread far beyond Sicily, and was particularly glorious in Barbary, which was then tributary to the King of Sicily.

Among those who heard of Gerbino's great reputation for valor and courtesy was the daughter of the King of Tunis, who, according to all who had seen her, was one of the most beautiful and well-bred women ever created. Delighting in tales of brave men, she received the accounts of Gerbino's courageous deeds with such goodwill that, picturing the prince's appearance, she became ardently enamored of him and spoke of him more willingly than anyone else, eagerly listening to any who spoke of him.

Similarly, the great renown of her beauty and worth had reached Sicily, as elsewhere, and had not in vain inflamed Gerbino with love for her, no less than the love she herself had conceived for him. Desiring greatly to see her, Gerbino waited for a suitable opportunity to ask his grandfather's permission to travel to Tunis. In the meantime, he charged every friend of his who went there to make known to her, as best they could, his secret and great love, and bring him news of her. This was skillfully done by one of them, who, under the pretense of showing the princess some women's trinkets as merchants do, thoroughly conveyed Gerbino's passion to her and affirmed that the prince and all he possessed were at her command. The princess received the messenger and the message with pleasure and, declaring that she burned with the same love for the prince, sent him back one of her most precious jewels as a token.

Gerbino received this with the utmost joy, and wrote to her again and again through the same messenger, sending her the most costly gifts and entering into certain negotiations with her, whereby they might have seen and touched one another, had fortune allowed it. But as things progressed further than was prudent, the young lady on one side and Gerbino on the other burning with desire, it happened that the King of Tunis gave her in marriage to the King of Granada, at which she was greatly distressed, realizing that not only would she be separated from her lover by a great distance, but was likely to be entirely parted from him. Had she seen a way to do so, she would gladly have fled her father and gone to Gerbino, if this might not come to pass. Gerbino, likewise, upon hearing of this marriage, was deeply saddened and often considered seizing her by force, should she travel to her husband by sea.

The King of Tunis, getting wind of Gerbino's love and intentions and fearing his valor and prowess, informed King William when the time came to send his daughter to Granada, advising him that he planned to do so and requesting assurance that he would not be hindered by Gerbino or anyone else. The aging King William, having heard nothing of Gerbino's passion and consequently unsuspecting of the reason for this request, freely granted the assurance and sent the King of Tunis a glove as a token. The latter, having obtained the desired guarantee, equipped a very large and fine ship in the port of Carthage, provisioning it for the voyage to Granada, and awaited only favorable weather to set sail with his daughter.

The young lady, aware of all these preparations, secretly sent one of her servants to Palermo to greet the gallant Gerbino on her behalf and tell him that she was to sail in a few days for Granada, so that he might now show if he was as valiant a man as was said and if he loved her as much as he had declared. Her messenger carried out the errand excellently and returned to Tunis, while Gerbino, upon hearing this and knowing that his grandfather had given the King of Tunis assurance, knew not what to do. However, urged by love and unwilling to appear a coward, he went to Messina, where he hastily armed two light galleys and, manning them with men of proven valor, set sail for the coast of Sardinia, expecting the lady's ship to pass there.

Nor was he mistaken, for he had been there but a few days when the ship came into view, becalmed not far from where he lay in wait. Gerbino, seeing this, said to his companions, 'Gentlemen, if you are the men of valor I take you for, I believe there is none of you who has not either felt or feels the power of love, without which, as I see it, no mortal can possess any virtue or worth. And if you have been or are enamored, it will be easy for you to understand my desire. I love, and love has moved me to bring you here; and she whom I love is in the ship you see becalmed yonder, which, besides the thing I most desire, is full of great riches. These latter, if you are men of valor, we may easily acquire by fighting manfully; of the victory, I desire nothing for myself save one lady, for whose love I have taken up arms - the rest shall freely be yours. Come, then, and let us boldly assail the ship; God favors our enterprise and holds it here, denying it a breeze.'

The gallant Gerbino had no need of many words, for the Messinese with him, eager for plunder, were already prepared to do what he exhorted them to. Therefore, with a great cry at the end of his speech, declaring that it should be so, they sounded the trumpets and, taking up their weapons, drove their oars into the water and made for the Tunisian ship. Those aboard, seeing the galleys approaching from afar and unable to flee, readied themselves for defense. Gerbino, approaching the ship, commanded that the masters thereof be sent to the galleys if they had no mind to fight; but the Saracens, having ascertained who they were and what they sought, declared that they were attacked despite the faith pledged to them by King William, in token whereof they showed the king's glove, and altogether refused to surrender or give them anything from the ship, save by force of battle.

Gerbino, who saw the lady upon the poop, far fairer than he had pictured her, and was more inflamed than ever, replied to the showing of the glove that there were no falcons there at the moment and consequently no need for gloves; therefore, if they chose not to surrender the lady, they must prepare to receive battle. Accordingly, without further parley, they began exchanging arrows and stones, and fought for a long while, with loss on both sides. At last, Gerbino, seeing he was making little progress, took a small vessel he had brought from Sardinia, set it afire, and thrust it, along with the two galleys, against the ship. The Saracens, seeing they must either surrender or die, brought the king's daughter, who was weeping below, up on deck and, calling to Gerbino, slaughtered her before his eyes as she begged for mercy and aid, then cast her body into the sea, saying, 'Take her; we give her to you, such as we may and as your faithlessness has merited.'

Gerbino, witnessing their barbarous deed, pulled alongside the ship, reckless of arrows or stones, and boarded it, as if courting death, in spite of those aboard; then, like a hungry lion among a herd of oxen, slaughtering first one, then another, and sating his fury rather than his hunger with teeth and claws, he cruelly killed many of the Saracens, sword in hand. After this, as the fire grew in the kindled ship, he had the sailors fetch what they could, as payment for their pains, and descended, having gained but a sorry victory over his adversaries. Then, having the fair lady's body retrieved from the sea, he long and bitterly lamented her, and steering for Sicily, buried her honorably on the little island of Ustica, opposite Trapani; following which, he returned home, the most wretched man alive.

The King of Tunis, hearing the grievous news, sent his ambassadors, clad all in black, to King William, complaining of the breach of the faith he had pledged. They recounted to him how the events had unfolded, at which King William was greatly incensed. Seeing no way to deny them the justice they sought, he had Gerbino arrested, and despite the pleas of his barons to sway him, condemned him to death himself and had his head struck off in his presence, choosing to be without an heir rather than be held a faithless king. Thus, as I have told you, these two lovers met a violent and miserable death within a few days, without having tasted any fruit of their loves."
