PROEM

A kind and compassionate act is to have pity on the afflicted, and though this befits everyone, it is particularly required of those who have themselves needed comfort and found it in others. Among these, if anyone has ever been in need of it or held it dear or taken pleasure in it before, I am certainly one of them.

From my earliest youth up until now, I have been inflamed with a very high and noble passion - higher and nobler, perhaps, than might appear if I were to recount it, given my humble station. Although I was commended for it by discreet individuals who were aware of it and considered all the more worthy for it, it was nonetheless a great trial for me to bear, not due to the cruelty of the lady I loved, but because of the intense ardor kindled in my breast by an unrestrained desire. This often caused me more distress than I had reason for.

In my affliction, the pleasant discourse and excellent consolations of a certain friend of mine provided me such comfort that I firmly believe I would have died without them. But as it pleased Him who, being infinite, has ordained that all earthly things must have an end, my love - fervent beyond any other and which neither reasoned arguments nor counsel, nor even manifest shame or peril that might ensue, had been able to break or bend - of its own accord, in time, has so abated that it has now left me only that pleasure which it is accustomed to afford to those who venture not too far into the depths of its oceans. Thus, all distress being dispelled, I find it now delightful where it was once grievous.

Yet, though the pain has ceased, the memory of the benefits and kindnesses bestowed on me by those to whom, out of the goodwill they bore me, my troubles were distressing, has not fled, nor, I believe, will it ever pass away save with my death. And since gratitude, in my opinion, is especially praiseworthy among the virtues, and its contrary blameworthy, I have, now that I can call myself free, bethought myself to endeavor, in what little way is possible to me, to provide some relief in return for what I received before - if not to those who succored me, who, by their good sense or their fortune, likely have no need thereof, then at least to those who do.

And though my support, or rather my comfort, may be and indeed is of little enough avail to the afflicted, it still seems to me that it should be offered where the need appears greater, both because it will there be of more service and because it will there be the more welcome. And who will deny that this [comfort], whatever its worth, it behooves much more to give to lovesick ladies than to men? For these, within their tender bosoms, fearful and shamefaced, hold hidden the fires of love (which those who have experienced them know to have far greater power than those which are manifest), and, constrained by the wishes, the pleasures, the commands of fathers, mothers, brothers and husbands, abide most often confined within the narrow bounds of their chambers, and, sitting, as it were, idle, willing and unwilling in the same breath, revolve within themselves diverse thoughts which it is not possible should ever be merry. Wherefore, if any melancholy, bred of ardent desire, arise in their minds, it must of necessity abide there with grievous distress, unless it be dispelled by new discourse; and the more so for that they are far less strong than men to endure.

With men in love it happens not on this wise, as we may manifestly see. If any melancholy or heaviness of thought oppress them, they have many means of easing it or doing it away, for that to them, an they have a mind thereto, there lacketh not opportunity of going about, hearing and seeing many things, fowling, hunting, fishing, riding, gaming and trafficking; each of which hath, altogether or in part, power to draw the mind unto itself and to divert it from troublous thought, at least for some space of time, whereafter, one way or another, either solace supervenes or else the distress grows less. Wherefore, to the end that the injustice of Fortune may in part be amended by me, which, where there is the less strength to endure, as we see it in delicate ladies, hath there been the more niggard of support, I purpose, for the relief and comfort of ladies in love (unto others the needle and the spindle and the reel suffice), to recount a hundred stories or fables or parables or histories or whatever you like to style them, in ten days' time, related by an honorable company of seven ladies and three young men, together with sundry songs sung by the aforesaid ladies for their diversion. In these stories will be found love-chances, both joyful and grievous, and other adventures of fortune befallen as well in present times as in days of old, whereby the ladies aforesaid, who shall read them, may at once take solace from the delectable things therein displayed and useful counsel, inasmuch as they may learn thereby what is to be eschewed and what is on like wise to be ensued - the which methinks cannot betide without cessation of distress. If it happen thus (as God grant it may) let them render thanks therefor to Love, who, by loosing me from his bonds, hath vouchsafed me the power of applying myself to the service of their pleasures.
