THE TENTH STORY

[Day the Eighth]

A WOMAN IN SICILY TRICKS A MERCHANT OUT OF HIS GOODS; BUT HE, PRETENDING TO HAVE RETURNED WITH EVEN MORE MERCHANDISE THAN BEFORE, BORROWS MONEY FROM HER AND LEAVES HER WITH ONLY WATER AND TOW IN PAYMENT

It's no surprise that the queen's story made the ladies laugh in many places - there was not one whose eyes hadn't filled with tears of laughter at least a dozen times. But when the story ended, Dioneo, knowing it was his turn to speak, said, "Dear ladies, it's clear that tricks and schemes are more enjoyable the more skillfully the deceiver is outwitted. Although you've told some excellent stories, I'm going to share one that I think you'll enjoy even more, since the one who was tricked was a greater master of deception than any of the men or women you've mentioned.

In all the port cities, there is a custom that when merchants arrive with goods, they must unload everything and carry it all to a warehouse, which is often called a customs house, run by the local government or lord. There, the merchants provide a written list of all their merchandise and its value, and the officers assign each merchant a storeroom where they can lock up their goods. The customs officers then record all the merchants' goods in the customs ledger, and collect any fees due when the merchants withdraw their goods.

These customs ledgers are closely consulted by brokers, who use them to learn about the quality and quantity of goods held in the warehouses, as well as the identities of the merchants who own them. The brokers then make deals with these merchants, trading, selling, and exchanging as opportunities arise. This practice was common in Palermo, Sicily, where there were many beautiful women who were sworn enemies of honesty. Considered great ladies and paragons of virtue by those who didn't know them, these women were in fact devoted to fleecing men. As soon as they spotted a merchant, they would consult the customs ledger to learn about his goods and his wealth. Then, using their charming and alluring ways, they would try to entrap the merchant in their snares of false love. Many a man has found himself stripped of a great portion, or even all, of his merchandise by these smooth-tongued "barbers" who knew so well how to wield their razors.

It happened not long ago that one of our young Florentines, named Niccolo da Cignano but more commonly called Salabaetto, was sent by his masters to Palermo with woollen cloths worth about five hundred gold florins that he had left over from the Salerno fair. After declaring the goods to the customs officers, he stored them in a warehouse and began leisurely strolling around the city, taking his pleasure.

Salabaetto was fair-skinned, blond, and very handsome and lively. One of these "barbers," who called herself Madam Biancofiore, heard about him and set her sights on him. Thinking she must be some great lady who was attracted to his good looks, Salabaetto decided to pursue this love affair in the utmost secrecy. Without telling anyone, he started passing by her house frequently.

Noticing this, Madam Biancofiore, after a few days of drawing him in with her eyes, secretly sent one of her most skilled procuresses to him. The woman, speaking almost with tears in her eyes, told Salabaetto that her mistress was so smitten with his charm and good looks that she could find no rest, day or night. She said that Madam Biancofiore longed to meet him privately in a bathhouse, and she gave Salabaetto a ring from her mistress.

Hearing this, Salabaetto was overjoyed. He took the ring, rubbed it on his eyes, and kissed it, then put it on his finger and told the woman that if Madam Biancofiore loved him, her love was well returned, for he loved her more than his own life and was ready to go to her wherever and whenever she wished. The messenger returned to her mistress with this answer, and they arranged for Salabaetto to meet Madam Biancofiore at a certain bathhouse the next day after vespers.

Without telling anyone, Salabaetto went to the bathhouse at the appointed time and found it already occupied by the lady. Soon, two slave girls arrived carrying a fine large cotton mattress, sheets trimmed with silk, a snow-white Cyprus buckram coverlet, and two exquisitely embroidered pillows. They set up the bed in one of the chambers, then stripped off their clothes and thoroughly cleaned and washed the bath.

Shortly after, Madam Biancofiore herself arrived with two more slave girls. She greeted Salabaetto joyfully, then, as soon as she had the chance, embraced and kissed him repeatedly, sighing deeply, and called him her "little Tuscan dog." They then entered the bath together, along with two of the slave girls. Without allowing anyone else to touch him, Madam Biancofiore herself washed Salabaetto all over with musk and clove-scented soap, then had the slave girls wash her.

Afterwards, the slave girls brought two very white and fragrant rose-scented sheets, in one of which they wrapped Salabaetto and in the other the lady, then carried them both to the prepared bed. After they had rested, the slave girls unwrapped them and brought out silver bottles filled with sweet-smelling rose, jasmine, orange, and lemon-flower waters, with which they sprinkled them. Then they produced boxes of candied fruits and fine wines, and they refreshed themselves.

To Salabaetto, it seemed he was in paradise. He could not take his eyes off the beautiful lady, and every hour felt like a hundred years until the slave girls left and he found himself in her arms. When it seemed the right time to get up, the lady called the girls back, and they got dressed. After a second serving of wine and sweets, and washing their hands and faces with fragrant waters, they were about to depart. But the lady said to Salabaetto, "If it pleases you, I would be greatly honored if you would come sup with me tonight and spend the night."

Salabaetto, now completely captivated by the lady's beauty and the skillful charm of her ways, and firmly believing that she loved him as deeply as he loved her, replied, "Madam, your every wish is supremely agreeable to me, so tonight and always I intend to do what pleases you and what you command."

The lady then returned to her house, where she had her bedchamber beautifully decorated with her dresses and possessions. She prepared a splendid supper and awaited Salabaetto, who, as soon as it was a bit dark, went there and was received with open arms. He supped with her in great comfort and joy, then they went to the lavishly decorated bedchamber, where Salabaetto was struck by the wonderful fragrance of aloes-wood and saw the bed richly adorned with singing birds from Cyprus and many fine dresses hanging on the pegs - all of which made him conclude that this must be a great and wealthy lady. Though he had heard some whispers about her way of life, he would not believe it, thinking that even if she had deceived others, she could not possibly do the same to him. He spent the night with her in the utmost delight, growing ever more enamored. In the morning, she gave him a beautiful silver belt with a fine purse, saying, "My sweet Salabaetto, I commend myself to your remembrance, and just as my person is at your pleasure, so too are all that is here and all that depends on me at your service and command." Overjoyed, Salabaetto embraced and kissed her, then left and went to join the other merchants.

In this way, he continued visiting her from time to time, without it costing him anything, and becoming more and more entangled. Eventually, he sold his goods for cash and made a good profit. The lady immediately heard about this, not from Salabaetto himself but from others. One night when Salabaetto came to visit her, she began chatting and flirting with him, kissing and embracing him and pretending to be so in love that it seemed she would die in his arms. She even wanted to give him two very fine silver cups, but he wouldn't take them, since he had already received from her, at various times, the equivalent of about thirty gold florins, without her ever accepting a single penny from him. 

Finally, when she had thoroughly inflamed his desire by showing herself so enamored and generous, one of her slave girls called her out, as she had arranged in advance. The lady then returned, weeping bitterly and throwing herself facedown on the bed, wailing the most pitiful lamentations. Salabaetto, astonished, took her in his arms, weeping with her and asking what had suddenly distressed her so. After much entreaty, the lady replied, "Alas, my dear lord, I scarcely know what to say or do. I have just received a letter from Messina, and my brother writes that unless I sell or pawn everything I have, I must send him a thousand gold florins within eight days, or his head will be cut off. And I don't know how I can get that sum so quickly. If I had fifteen days' grace, I could find a way to get it from somewhere I'm owed much more, or I could sell one of our farms. But since that's not possible, I'd rather be dead than have this bad news come to me."

Saying this, she pretended to be deeply distressed and kept weeping. Salabaetto, whose love had deprived him of much of his usual good sense, believed her tears were true and her words truer still. So he said, "Madam, I can't give you a thousand florins, but I can certainly lend you five hundred, since you believe you'll be able to pay me back within a fortnight. I'm fortunate that I just happened to sell my goods yesterday; otherwise, I couldn't have given you a penny."

"Alas," cried the lady, "have you then been short of money? Why didn't you ask me for it? Though I don't have a thousand, I certainly have a hundred or even two hundred I could have given you. You have deprived me of all heart to accept the service you offer me." Salabaetto was more captivated than ever by these words and said, "Madam, don't refrain on that account, for if I had such an occasion as you do now, I would certainly have asked you." 

"Alas, my Salabaetto," the lady replied, "now I truly know that your love for me is true and perfect, since, without being asked, you freely help me in my distress with so great a sum of money. Truly, I was already wholly yours, but with this I shall be far more so. I shall never forget that you have saved my brother's life. But God knows I take it most unwillingly, since you are a merchant and with money merchants conduct all their affairs. However, since necessity compels me, and I have certain assurance of quickly restoring it to you, I will indeed take it." With that, she let herself fall, weeping, into Salabaetto's arms.

He comforted her, and the next morning, to show himself her most generous servant, he brought her the five hundred florins without even being asked, which she received with tears in her eyes but laughter in her heart. Salabaetto was content with her simple promise.

As soon as the lady had the money, her behaviour began to change. Whereas before he had been freely admitted to see her whenever he pleased, now reasons started arising that prevented him from gaining entry even once out of seven times, and he was not received with the same warmth and affection as before. And the deadline by which he was to have his money back, not just arrived but passed by a month or two, and when he demanded it, he was given excuses instead. At that point, Salabaetto's eyes were opened to the wicked woman's tricks and his own foolishness. Since he had no written agreement or other evidence, and was ashamed to complain to anyone, both because he had been forewarned and for fear of the ridicule he might reasonably expect for his folly, he was deeply distressed and inwardly lamented his gullibility.
