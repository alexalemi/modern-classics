THE FIRST STORY 

[Day the Fifth]

CIMON, LOVING, BECOMES WISE AND KIDNAPS HIS MISTRESS IPHIGENIA. BEING IMPRISONED IN RHODES, HE IS FREED BY LYSIMACHUS AND TOGETHER THEY ABDUCT IPHIGENIA AND CASSANDRA ON THEIR WEDDING DAY, WITH WHOM THE TWO MEN FLEE TO CRETE, WHERE THE WOMEN BECOME THEIR WIVES, AND WHENCE THEY ARE LATER RECALLED HOME

Many delightful stories, dear ladies, suitable to begin this joyous day, present themselves to me to be told. One in particular pleases me the most, for it will show you not only the happy outcome of today's tales, but also how holy, powerful, and full of goodness is the force of Love, which many, not knowing what they say, condemn and vilify most unjustly. And this, if I am not mistaken, must be extremely pleasing to you, for I believe you are all in love.

There was, then, on the island of Cyprus, as we have read in the ancient histories of the Cypriots, a very noble gentleman named Aristippus, who was richer than any other in the country in worldly goods and could have considered himself the happiest of men, had not Fortune made him unhappy in one thing - that among his other children, he had a son who surpassed all the other youths of his age in stature and physical beauty, but was a hopeless dunce and nearly an idiot. His true name was Galesus, but since neither the toil of teachers, nor the persuasion or beating of his father, nor any effort had been able to instill in his head even a glimmer of learning or good breeding, and he had a rough voice and manners more befitting a beast than a man, he was mockingly called Cimon by almost everyone, which in their language meant as much as "brute beast" in ours. His father endured his wasteful life with the deepest distress, and having finally given up all hope for him, ordered him to go to his country estate and stay there with the farmhands, so he would no longer have to see the cause of his grief; which was very agreeable to Cimon, for the manners and customs of peasants and boors were much more to his liking than those of townspeople.

So Cimon, going to the countryside and occupying himself with the things pertaining to it, it happened one day, a little after noon, as he was passing from one farm to another with his staff on his shoulder, that he entered a very beautiful copse that was there, all in leaf, for it was the month of May. Passing through it, he happened, as his fate guided him there, upon a small meadow surrounded by very tall trees, in one corner of which was a very clear and cool spring, beside which he saw a very beautiful young woman asleep on the green grass, with such a thin garment on her body that it hid almost nothing of her snowy flesh. She was covered only from the waist down with a very white and light coverlet; and at her feet slept likewise two women and a man, her servants. When Cimon saw the young lady, he stopped and, leaning on his staff, began to gaze at her with the utmost admiration, no otherwise than as if he had never yet seen a woman's form, while in his rude breast, wherein no least impression of civil pleasantness had ever penetrated, he felt a thought awaken which intimated to his gross and material spirit that this maiden was the fairest thing that any living soul had ever seen. From there he proceeded to admire her various parts - praising her hair, which he considered golden, her brow, her nose, her mouth, her throat, and her arms, and above all her bosom, as yet but little raised - and, having suddenly become from a boor a judge of beauty, he ardently desired to see her eyes, which, weighed down by deep sleep, she kept closed. To this end, he had a mind several times to awaken her; but, since she seemed to him beyond measure fairer than any other women he had seen before, he doubted whether she might not be some goddess. Now he had wit enough to account divine things worthy of more reverence than mundane ones; wherefore he refrained, waiting for her to awake of herself; and although the delay seemed overlong to him, yet, being taken with an unfamiliar pleasure, he knew not how to tear himself away.

It happened, then, that after a long while, the young lady, whose name was Iphigenia, came to herself, before any of her people, and opening her eyes, saw Cimon (who, because of his appearance and uncouthness and his father's wealth and nobility, was known in a manner to everyone in the country) standing before her, leaning on his staff, and marveled exceedingly, saying, "Cimon, what are you seeking in this wood at this hour?" He made her no answer, but, seeing her eyes open, began to gaze steadfastly upon them, seeming to himself that there proceeded from them a sweetness which filled him with a pleasure such as he had never before felt. The young lady, seeing this, began to fear that his so fixed looking upon her might move his rusticity to something that might turn to her shame; wherefore, calling her women, she rose up, saying, "Cimon, farewell." To which he replied, "I will go with you"; and although the young lady, who was still in fear of him, would have declined his company, she could not rid herself of him until he had accompanied her to her own house.

From there he went to his father's house in the city and declared to him that he would on no wise consent to return to the country; which was irksome enough to Aristippus and his kinsmen, nevertheless they let him be, awaiting to see what might be the cause of his change of mind. Love's arrow having, then, through Iphigenia's beauty, penetrated into Cimon's heart, wherein no teaching had ever availed to win an entrance, in a very brief time, proceeding from one idea to another, he made his father marvel and all his kinfolk and every other that knew him. In the first place he besought his father that he would cause him to be dressed in fine clothes and every other thing, even as his brothers, which Aristippus right gladly did. Then, consorting with young men of condition and learning the manners and bearing that befit gentlemen and especially lovers, he first, to the utmost wonderment of everyone, in a very brief space of time, not only learned the first elements of letters, but became very eminent among the students of philosophy, and after (the love which he bore Iphigenia being the cause of all this) he not only improved his rude and rustic manner of speech to elegance and civility, but became a master of song and music, and exceedingly skilled and brave in riding and martial exercises, both by land and by sea. In short, not to go recounting every particular of his merits, the fourth year was not accomplished from the day of his first falling in love, ere he was grown the most lively and accomplished gentleman of all the young men in the island of Cyprus, yes, and the best endowed with every particular excellence. What, then, charming ladies, shall we say of Cimon? Certainly, nothing other than that the lofty virtues implanted by heaven in his generous soul had been bound with exceeding strong bonds of jealous Fortune and shut in some straitest corner of his heart, all which bonds Love, as a mightier than Fortune, broke and burst asunder and in its quality of awakener and quickener of drowsed and sluggish wits, urged forth into broad daylight the virtues aforesaid, which had till then been overdarkened with a barbarous obscurity, thus manifestly discovering from how mean a room it can avail to uplift those souls that are subject unto it and to what an eminence it can conduct them with its beams.

Although Cimon, loving Iphigenia as he did, might exceed in certain things, as young men in love very often do, nevertheless Aristippus, considering that Love had turned him from a dunce into a man, not only patiently bore with the extravagances into which it might whiles lead him, but encouraged him to indulge its every pleasure. But Cimon, seeking to put an honorable end to his desire, caused essay Cipseus, Iphigenia's father, several times, so he should give him his daughter to wife; but Cipseus still answered that he had promised her to Pasimondas, a young nobleman of Rhodes, to whom he had no mind to fail of his word. The time coming for the promised nuptials of Iphigenia and the bridegroom, having sent for her, Cimon said to himself, "Now, O Iphigenia, is the time to prove how much you are beloved by me. By you am I become a man and so I may but have you, I doubt not to become more glorious than any god; and for certain I will either have you or die."

Accordingly, having secretly recruited certain young noblemen who were his friends and let privily equip a ship with everything apt for naval battle, he put out to sea and awaited the vessel wherein Iphigenia was to be transported to her husband in Rhodes. The bride, after much honor done of her father to the bridegroom's friends, took ship with the latter, who turned their prow towards Rhodes and departed. On the following day, Cimon, who slept not, came out upon them with his ship and cried out, in a loud voice, from the prow, to those who were on board Iphigenia's vessel, saying, "Stay, strike your sails or look to be beaten and sunken in the sea." Cimon's adversaries had gotten up their arms on deck and made ready to defend themselves; whereupon he, after speaking the words aforesaid, took a grappling-iron and casting it upon the poop of the Rhodians, who were making off at the top of their speed, made it fast by main force to the prow of his own ship. Then, bold as a lion, he leapt on board their ship, without waiting for any to follow him, as if he held them all for nought, and Love spurring him, he fell upon his enemies with marvellous might, cutlass in hand, striking now this one and now that and hewing them down like sheep.

The Rhodians, seeing this, cast down their arms and all as with one voice confessed themselves prisoners; whereupon Cimon said to them, "Young men, it was neither lust of plunder nor hate that made me depart Cyprus to assail you, arms in hand, in mid sea. That which moved me thereunto was the desire of a thing which to have gotten is a very grave matter to me and to you a very light one to yield me in peace; it is, to wit, Iphigenia, whom I love above all else and whom, availing not to have of her father on friendly and peaceful wise, Love hath constrained me to win from you as an enemy and by force of arms. Wherefore I mean to be to her that which your friend Pasimondas should have been. Give her to me, then, and begone and God's grace go with you."

The Rhodians, more by force constrained than of free will, surrendered Iphigenia, weeping, to Cimon, who, seeing her in tears, said to her, "Noble Lady, be not disconsolate; I am your Cimon, who by long love have far better deserved to have you than Pasimondas by plighted faith." Thereupon he had her carried aboard his own ship and returning to his companions, let the Rhodians go, without touching aught else of theirs. Then, glad beyond any man alive to have gotten so dear a prize, after devoting some time to comforting the weeping lady, he took counsel with his comrades not to return to Cyprus at that present; wherefore, of one accord, they turned the ship's head towards Crete, where almost everyone, and especially Cimon, had kinfolk, old and new, and friends in plenty and where they doubted not to be in safety with Iphigenia. But Fortune the unstable, which had cheerfully enough vouchsafed unto Cimon the acquisition of the lady, suddenly changed the inexpressible joyance of the enamored youth into sad and bitter mourning; for it was not four full told hours since he had left the Rhodians when the night (which Cimon looked to be more delightsome than any he had ever known) came on and with it a very troublous and tempestuous shift of weather, which filled all the sky with clouds and the sea with ravening winds, by reason whereof none could see what to do or whither to steer, nor could any even keep the deck to do any office.

How sorely concerned was Cimon for this it needeth not to ask; it seemed to him the gods had vouchsafed him his desire but to make death the more grievous to him, whereof, without that, he had before recked little. His comrades lamented on like wise, but Iphigenia bewailed herself over all, weeping sore and fearing every stroke of the waves; and in her chagrin she bitterly cursed Cimon's love and blamed his presumption, avouching that the tempest had arisen for none other thing but that the gods chose not that he, who would fain against their will have her to wife, should avail to enjoy his presumptuous desire, but, seeing her first die, should after himself perish miserably.
