The First Day

HERE BEGINS THE FIRST DAY OF THE DECAMERON, IN WHICH (AFTER THE AUTHOR HAS EXPLAINED HOW THE PEOPLE WHO WILL BE PRESENTED HEREINAFTER CAME TOGETHER) UNDER THE LEADERSHIP OF PAMPINEA, THEY DISCUSS WHAT IS MOST PLEASING TO EACH

My dear ladies, as I reflect on your inherent compassion, I realize that this present work will seem to you to have a sorrowful and wearisome beginning. This is because the painful memory of the recent pestilence, which it bears on its forefront, is universally unpleasant to all who witnessed or otherwise knew of it. But I would not have this frighten you away from reading further, as if you were to continue encountering sighs and tears. Let this grim beginning be no more to you than a rugged and steep mountain, beyond which lies a most fair and delightful plain, which appears all the more pleasant the greater the hardship of the ascent and descent. For just as pain occupies the extreme of joy, so are miseries determined by imminent delight.

This brief unpleasantness (I call it brief, since it occupies but a few pages) is immediately succeeded by the pleasure and delight which I have already promised you and which, had I not mentioned it beforehand, might not be expected from such a beginning. And truly, had I been able to lead you to my desired end by a less rugged path, I would have gladly done so; but being in a manner constrained thereto, for that, without this recollection of our past miseries, it might not be shown what was the occasion of the coming about of the things that will hereafter be read, I have brought myself to write them.

I say, then, that in the year of the fruitful Incarnation of the Son of God one thousand three hundred and forty-eight, the deadly pestilence entered the notable city of Florence, fairest of all the cities of Italy. This scourge, whether by the operation of the heavenly bodies or by our own wicked deeds, sent down upon mankind for our correction by the just wrath of God, had some years before appeared in the East and, after depriving those regions of an innumerable number of inhabitants, had now unhappily spread towards the West. 

Against this plague, neither the wisdom of officials who purged the city of many impurities, nor the banning of the sick from entering, nor the many prayers made by the devout, could avail. About the coming of Spring that year, it began to show its dolorous effects in a horrible and miraculous way. However, it did not manifest itself as it had done in the East, where bleeding from the nose was a certain sign of inevitable death. Rather, in both men and women, there appeared at the beginning of the illness certain swellings, either on the groin or under the armpits, which the common people called plague-boils. From these two parts, the deadly plague-boils soon spread to every part of the body. Afterward, black or livid spots began to show on the arms and thighs and other areas, and just as the plague-boils had been a sure sign of coming death, so too were these discolorations for all who developed them.

Physicians could offer no counsel or cure that availed against these ailments. Whether it was that the nature of the infection did not allow it, or the ignorance of the doctors - for their numbers, both men and women with no medical training, had grown exceedingly great - prevented them from understanding its origins and therefore taking proper measures, few recovered, and almost all died within three days of the appearance of the aforementioned signs, some sooner and some later, often without fever or other symptoms.

Moreover, the pestilence was more virulent because it spread from the sick to the healthy, no differently than fire spreads through dry or oily things brought near it. Even worse, not only did contact and association with the sick give the healthy the cause of their common death, but the mere touching of the clothes or other objects used by the sick appeared to communicate the disease to the toucher. It was a marvelous and terrible thing to witness - which, had I not seen it with my own eyes and heard it from those worthy of belief, I would scarcely have dared to write, much less believe. The nature of this pestilence was such that it passed not only from man to man, but also, many times visibly, from an animal of a foreign species to a human, quickly killing the animal as well.

From these and many other such strange occurrences, diverse fears and conceits arose in those who remained alive, which nearly all tended to a very barbarous conclusion: to shun and flee the sick and all that pertained to them, thinking thus to secure immunity for themselves. Some conceived that moderation in living and abstinence from excess was the best defense, so they made up their company and lived removed from others, using the most delicate foods and finest wines temperately and eschewing all incontinence. Others, inclining to the contrary opinion, held that to carouse and make merry, singing and frolicking and satisfying every appetite, was the surest remedy. They went about day and night, drinking without limit, and made free use of the abandoned possessions of others.

In this great affliction and misery of our city, the authority of the laws, both human and divine, was almost entirely dissolved and fallen into decay, for lack of ministers and executors, who, like other men, were all either dead or sick or left so destitute of followers that they could not perform their duties. As a result, everyone was free to do as they pleased.

Some others held a middle course, not restricting themselves so exactly in diet as the first group, nor allowing themselves such license in drinking and other debauchery as the second, but using things in moderation according to their appetites. Nor did they seclude themselves, but went about carrying flowers, fragrant herbs, and various spices, which they frequently held to their noses, considering it an excellent thing to fortify the brain with such odors, especially since the air seemed heavy and corrupted by the stench of the dead and the sick.

There were also some who, moved by a more barbarous but perhaps surer reasoning, held that the best - indeed the only - remedy against pestilences was to flee before them. Accordingly, many men and women abandoned their own city, their own houses and homes, their kinsfolk and possessions, and sought the country estates of others, or at least their own, as if the wrath of God, being moved to punish the iniquity of mankind, would not proceed to do so wherever they might be, but would content itself with afflicting those only who were found within the walls of their city.

Indeed, leaving aside that townsman avoided townsman and that scarcely any neighbor took thought for another, and that kinsfolk seldom or never visited one another, this tribulation had struck such terror to the hearts of all, men and women alike, that brother forsook brother, uncle nephew, and sister brother, and oftentimes wife husband. Nay, what is yet more extraordinary and well-nigh incredible, fathers and mothers refused to visit or tend their very children, as if they were not their own. As a result, those who fell sick - and their number, both male and female, was incalculable - had no other succor than what they owed either to the charity of friends (and these were few) or the greed of servants, who tended them, allured by high and extravagant wages.
