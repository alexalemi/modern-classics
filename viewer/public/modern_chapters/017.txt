THE FOURTH STORY

[Day the Second]

LANDOLFO RUFFOLO, GROWN POOR, BECOMES A PIRATE AND IS CAPTURED BY THE GENOESE. HIS SHIP IS WRECKED, BUT HE SAVES HIMSELF ON A CHEST FULL OF VALUABLE JEWELS. HE IS TAKEN IN BY A WOMAN IN CORFU AND RETURNS HOME RICH

Lauretta, sitting next to Pampinea, wasted no time in starting her own story after Pampinea had finished her glorious tale. "Dear ladies," she began, "there can be no greater feat of fortune than seeing someone raised from the depths of misery to the heights of success, as Pampinea's story has shown with Alessandro. And since from now on, each storyteller must work within those limits, I will not be ashamed to tell a tale that, though containing even greater misfortunes, does not end as splendidly.

I know well that, in comparison, my story will be heard with less enthusiasm. But I have no choice, so I hope you will excuse me.

The coastline from Reggio to Gaeta is considered one of the most delightful parts of Italy. Near Salerno, there is a hillside overlooking the sea that the locals call the Amalfi Coast, filled with small towns, gardens, springs, and people as rich and engaged in trade as any in the world. One of these towns is called Ravello, and although it is home to many wealthy men today, there was once a man there named Landolfo Ruffolo who was extremely rich. But his wealth was not enough, and in trying to double it, he nearly lost it all, as well as his own life.

This man, following the usual practice of merchants, made plans, bought a large ship, and loaded it entirely with his own goods to sail to Cyprus. There he found many other ships carrying the same kind of merchandise as he had brought, which forced him to sell his goods at a steep discount just to get rid of them. This misfortune nearly ruined him, as he went from being very rich to almost poor in a short time.

Distressed by this setback and unsure of what to do, Landolfo decided that he would either die or make up for his losses through piracy, rather than return home poor after having left rich. So he found a buyer for his large ship, and with the money from that and what he had made on his goods, he bought a small, fast ship that was well-suited for cruising, arming, and equipping for raiding. He then set out to plunder other ships, especially those belonging to the Turks.

Fortune was much kinder to him as a pirate than as a merchant. Within a year, he had plundered and captured so many Turkish vessels that he not only regained what he had lost in trade but more than doubled his former wealth. Having learned from the pain of his previous loss, and deeming that he now had enough, Landolfo resolved to return home and avoid further risk, rather than seek to gain more. So he set sail for home in the small ship he had used for his piracy.

He had reached the Aegean Sea when a powerful southeast wind arose, which was not only contrary to his course but also caused such great waves that his small vessel could not withstand it. He took refuge in a cove formed by a small island, intending to wait there for better weather. He had not been there long when two large Genoese ships, returning from Constantinople, sought shelter from the same storm in the little harbor. They spotted Landolfo's small ship and, knowing him by reputation to be very wealthy, blocked the way out and set about seizing it, as greedy Genoese are wont to do.

They landed some of their well-armed and equipped men and positioned them to prevent anyone from leaving the ship, while the rest rowed over in small boats and easily captured Landolfo's vessel, crew and all, without losing a single man. They took Landolfo aboard one of their ships, leaving him only a meager doublet, then stripped the small ship of everything and sank it.

The next day, the wind having shifted, the Genoese ships set sail westward and continued their journey prosperously throughout the day. But towards evening, a violent storm arose that separated the two ships. The one carrying the unfortunate Landolfo crashed violently against a shoal off the island of Cephalonia and broke apart, just as a glass shatters against a wall. The sea was suddenly filled with bales of merchandise, chests, and planks floating on the surface, as is usual in such cases. The poor souls on board, those who could swim, began grasping at whatever came within their reach, including the wretched Landolfo, who, though he had earlier wished for death rather than return home poor, now feared it and clung to a plank, hoping that God might provide some means of escape.
