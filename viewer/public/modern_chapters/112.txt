The Fifth Story
[Day the Tenth]

Lady Dianora Requests a Garden as Fair in January as in May from Messer Ansaldo, and He, by Binding Himself to a Necromancer, Gives It to Her. Her Husband Grants Her Permission to Fulfill Messer Ansaldo's Wishes, But Hearing of the Former's Generosity, He Absolves Her from Her Promise, Whereupon the Necromancer, in Turn, Releases Messer Ansaldo from His Bond Without Demanding Anything in Return

After Messer Gentile's actions had been praised to the heavens by the entire merry group, the king instructed Emilia to continue, and she confidently began her tale as follows:

"My lovely ladies, no one can reasonably deny that Messer Gentile acted magnificently. However, if one were to claim that his generosity could not be surpassed, it would not be difficult to show that more is possible, as I intend to demonstrate to you in my own little story.

In the region of Friuli, a land made pleasant by its lovely mountains and abundance of rivers and clear springs, there is a city called Udine. In this city, there lived a beautiful and noble lady named Lady Dianora, the wife of a wealthy and amiable gentleman named Gilberto. Lady Dianora's charm had captivated a noble and renowned baron named Messer Ansaldo Gradense, a man of high standing and widely acclaimed for his valor and courtesy. Messer Ansaldo loved her deeply and did all in his power to win her affection, frequently sending her messages, but to no avail.

Finally, tired of his persistent advances and seeing that, despite her constant refusals, he continued to love and pursue her, Lady Dianora decided to try to rid herself of him through an extraordinary and, in her judgment, impossible demand. One day, she said to a woman who often came to her on Messer Ansaldo's behalf, 'Good woman, you have many times assured me that Messer Ansaldo loves me above all else and have offered me wondrous gifts on his part, which I have chosen to keep for himself, as they have never persuaded me to love him or comply with his wishes. However, if I could be certain that he loves me as deeply as you say, I might well bring myself to love him and do as he wishes. Therefore, if he is willing to fulfill the request I shall make of him, I will be ready to do his bidding.'

The woman asked, 'And what is it, my lady, that you would have him do?'

Lady Dianora replied, 'What I desire is this: I want him to create, for the coming month of January, a garden near this city filled with lush grass, flowers, and fully leafed trees, just as it would be in May. If he fails to accomplish this, let him never again send you or anyone else to me, for if he continues to importune me, I shall no longer keep his pursuit hidden from my husband and my relatives, and I will find a way to rid myself of him.'

Hearing the lady's request, which seemed to him a difficult and nearly impossible task, yet knowing it was demanded of him solely to deprive him of all hope, Messer Ansaldo nevertheless determined to try whatever could be done. He sent inquiries to various parts of the world, seeking anyone who could assist and advise him in this matter. Finally, he found one who offered, for a substantial sum of money, to accomplish the feat through necromantic arts. Having agreed to the high price, Messer Ansaldo eagerly awaited the appointed time.

When the appointed time arrived, and the cold was extreme with snow and ice everywhere, the learned man, on the night before the first of January, so worked his magic in a lovely meadow adjoining the city that, in the morning, according to eyewitness accounts, one of the most magnificent gardens ever seen had appeared, with grass, trees, and fruits of every kind. Messer Ansaldo, overjoyed at the sight, had the finest fruits and flowers gathered and secretly presented to his mistress, bidding her come and behold the garden she had requested, so that she might know the depth of his love for her and, remembering her promise made and sworn to him, act as a loyal lady in fulfilling it.

The lady, seeing the fruits and flowers and having already heard many speak of the miraculous garden, began to regret her promise. Nevertheless, curious to witness this strange sight, she went with several other ladies of the city to view the garden, and after expressing great wonder and admiration, she returned home the most sorrowful woman alive, realizing the commitment she had made. Her distress was so evident that her husband, Gilberto, noticed it and pressed her to reveal the reason.

After much reluctance, the lady finally confessed everything to him. Gilberto was initially enraged, but then, considering the purity of the lady's intent and replacing his anger with wiser counsel, he said, 'Dianora, it is neither prudent nor virtuous for a woman to heed any such message or to bargain for her chastity under any condition. Words that enter the heart through the ears have more power than many realize, and nearly anything becomes possible for lovers. You erred in first listening and then entering into an agreement. However, knowing the purity of your intentions, I will, to absolve you of your promise, grant you that which perhaps no other would, being further moved by fear of the necromancer, whom Messer Ansaldo might make us suffer if you were to deceive him. I will, then, that you go to him and endeavor to be released from this promise, preserving your virtue if you can; but if that is not possible, you shall, for this once, yield him your body, but not your soul.'

The lady, hearing her husband's speech, wept and refused to accept such a favor from him, but he insisted. Accordingly, the next morning at dawn, the lady, without excessive adornment, went to Messer Ansaldo's house, accompanied by two of her servants and a chambermaid. Hearing that his mistress had arrived, Ansaldo was greatly astonished and, summoning the necromancer, said to him, 'You must see what a treasure your skill has gained me.'

Then, going to greet the lady, he received her with decency and reverence, without pursuing any disorderly passion, and they all entered a fine chamber with a great fire. There, he had her seated and said, 'Madam, I beseech you, if the long love I have borne you deserves any reward, do not be troubled to reveal to me the true reason that has brought you here at such an hour and in such company.'

The lady, ashamed and with tears in her eyes, replied, 'Sir, neither love for you nor a promise binds me to come here, but the command of my husband, who, more concerned for the torments of your disordered passion than for his own honor and mine, has sent me here; and by his order, I am disposed, for this once, to do your every pleasure.'

If Messer Ansaldo had marveled at the sight of the lady, he marveled much more when he heard her words, and, moved by Gilberto's generosity, his ardor began to change to compassion. He said, 'God forbid, madam, if what you say is true, that I should be the one to mar the honor of him who has shown pity for my love. Therefore, for as long as it pleases you to remain here, you shall be treated no differently than as my own sister; and when it is agreeable to you, you are free to depart, but first render to your husband, on my part, the thanks that you deem fitting for a courtesy such as his.'

The lady, hearing these words, was the most joyful woman in the world and replied, 'Nothing, considering your character, could ever make me believe that anything other than this would befall me from my coming here; for this, I shall remain ever grateful to you.' Then, taking her leave, she returned, under honorable escort, to Messer Gilberto and told him what had transpired, which led to a very close and loyal friendship between him and Messer Ansaldo.

Moreover, the necromancer, to whom the gentleman was to have paid the promised reward, seeing Gilberto's generosity towards his wife's lover and Ansaldo's towards the lady, said, 'God forbid that, since I have seen Gilberto liberal with his honor and you with your love, I should not likewise be liberal with my fee. Knowing it will serve you well, I intend for it to be yours.' At this, the gentleman was ashamed and tried to make the necromancer accept all or part of the payment, but, seeing that his efforts were in vain and that it pleased the necromancer (who had, after three days, caused his garden to disappear) to depart, he commended him to God and, having extinguished the lustful love he had harbored for the lady, he remained filled with honorable affection for her.

Now, my lovely ladies, shall we prefer [Gentile's resignation of] the already dead lady and his love that had cooled with the loss of hope, over the generosity of Messer Ansaldo, whose love was more ardent than ever and who was, in a sense, kindled with new hope, holding in his hands the prey he had so long pursued? It would be folly to claim that this generosity can be matched by the other."
