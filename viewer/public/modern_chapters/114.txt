THE SEVENTH STORY

[Day the Tenth]

KING PEDRO OF ARAGON, LEARNING OF THE DEEP LOVE HELD FOR HIM BY LISA, COMFORTS THE LOVELORN GIRL AND THEN MARRIES HER TO A NOBLE YOUNG MAN; AFTERWARD, HE KISSES HER ON THE FOREHEAD AND DECLARES HIMSELF HER KNIGHT FOR LIFE

Fiammetta had finished her tale, and the brave and noble acts of King Charles had been greatly praised, though one lady there, being a Ghibelline, was reluctant to praise him. On the king's command, Pampinea then began, "There is no one of sound mind, dear ladies, who would not agree with what you have said about good King Charles, unless they harbor ill will toward him for some other reason. But since a story has come to my mind, one perhaps no less commendable than this, done by one of his adversaries to one of our Florentine maidens, I am pleased to relate it to you.

At the time of the expulsion of the French from Sicily, one of our Florentines, a very wealthy man named Bernardo Puccini who was an apothecary in Palermo, had an only daughter by his wife, a very beautiful young woman already of marriageable age. Now King Pedro of Aragon, having become the ruler of the island, held a grand festival with his barons in Palermo, and in the jousting contest in the Catalan style, it happened that Bernardo's daughter, whose name was Lisa, saw him riding from a window where she was with other ladies. He so delighted her that, looking upon him once and again, she fell passionately in love with him. When the festival ended and she remained in her father's house, she could think of nothing but this illustrious and exalted love of hers. What troubled her most was the awareness of her own lowly status, which scarcely allowed her to cherish any hope of a happy outcome. Yet she could not cease loving the king, though, out of fear of greater anguish, she dared not reveal her feelings. The king had not noticed this and cared nothing for her, which caused her intolerable distress beyond all that can be imagined. So it happened that, with love continually growing in her and melancholy piling upon melancholy, the fair maiden, unable to bear it any longer, fell ill and visibly wasted away day by day, like snow in the sun. Her father and mother, deeply concerned by what had befallen her, tended to her with assiduous affection and supported her as best they could with physicians and medicines, but to no avail, for, despairing of her love, she had resolved to live no longer.

It happened one day that, when her father offered to grant her any wish, she thought it fitting to seek, before she died, to make the king aware of her love and her intent. Accordingly, she asked him to bring her Minuccio d'Arezzo. Now this Minuccio was in those days considered a very skilled and subtle singer and player, and was gladly received by the king. Bernardo concluded that Lisa wished to hear him sing and play for a while. So he sent for him, and Minuccio, a man of pleasant humor, immediately came to her and, after somewhat comforting her with kind words, softly played a piece or two on a viol he had with him and then sang her several songs, which were fire and flame to the maiden's passion, though he thought to console her. Presently she told him that she wished to speak some words with him alone. When all others had withdrawn, she said to him, 'Minuccio, I have chosen you to keep very faithfully a secret of mine, hoping first that you will never reveal it to anyone except to the one of whom I shall tell you, and then that you will help me in what lies within your power. I must tell you, Minuccio mine, that on the day when our lord King Pedro held the great festival in honor of his ascension to the throne, it happened that as he tilted, I caught sight of him at such a critical moment that a fire was kindled in my heart for love of him, which has brought me to the state you see me in. And knowing how ill my love befits a king, yet being unable, let alone to drive it away, but even to diminish it, and it being beyond measure grievous to me to bear, I have chosen, as the lesser evil, to die, as I shall do. It is true that I would depart hence in dire distress, were he not first to know of it. Therefore, not knowing by whom I could more fittingly acquaint him with this my resolve than by yourself, I wish to entrust it to you and beg you not to refuse to do it, and when you shall have done it, to let me know thereof, so that, dying comforted, I may be freed from these my pains.' And with that, she fell silent, weeping.

Minuccio marveled at the greatness of the maiden's soul and her cruel resolve, and he was deeply concerned for her. Then, as it occurred to his mind how he might honorably oblige her, he said to her, 'Lisa, I give you my word, of which you may be fully assured that you will never find yourself deceived, and further, commending you for having set your mind upon so great a king, I offer you my aid, by means of which I hope, if you will but take comfort, to bring you news within three days that will be exceedingly welcome to you. And to lose no time, I mean to go about it straightaway.' Lisa, having again urgently besought him thereof and promised to take comfort, bade him God speed. Thereupon Minuccio, taking his leave, went to one Mico da Siena, a highly skilled poet of those days, and with entreaties compelled him to make the following canzonet:

     Bestir thee, Love, and get thee to my Sire
       And tell him all the torments I undergo;
       Tell him I'm like to die,
     For fearfulness concealing my desire.

     Love, with clasped hands I cry thee mercy, so
       Thou mayst betake thee where my lord doth dwell.
     Say that I love and long for him, for lo,
       My heart he hath inflamed so sadly well;
     Yea, for the fire wherewith I'm all aglow,
       I fear to die nor yet the hour can tell
       When I shall part from pain so fierce and fell
         As that which, longing, for his sake I dree
         In shame and fear; ah me,
     For God's sake, cause him know my torment dire.

     Since first enamoured, Love, of him I grew,
       Thou hast not given me the heart to dare
     So much as one poor once my lord unto
       My love and longing plainly to declare,
     My lord who maketh me so sore to rue;
       Death, dying thus, were hard to me to bear.
       Belike, indeed, for he is debonair,
         'Twould not displease him, did he know what pain
         I feel and didst thou deign
     Me daring to make known to him my fire.

Minuccio immediately set these words to a soft and plaintive melody, as the matter required, and on the third day, he went to the court, where King Pedro was still at table, and was summoned by him to sing something with his viol. Thereupon, he began singing the song so sweetly that all who were in the royal hall appeared entranced, so still and attentive were they in listening, and the king perhaps more than the others. When Minuccio had finished his singing, the king inquired whence came this song that he seemed never to have heard before. 'My lord,' replied the minstrel, 'it is not yet three days since the words were made and the melody.' The king asked for whom it had been made, and Minuccio answered, 'I dare not reveal it except to you alone.' The king, desiring to hear it, as soon as the tables were cleared, sent for Minuccio into his chamber, and the latter recounted to him in order all that he had heard from Lisa. Whereat Don Pedro was exceedingly pleased and greatly commended the maiden, declaring himself resolved to take pity on so worthy a young lady. He therefore bade Minuccio go comfort her on his behalf and tell her that he would without fail come to visit her that day towards vespers. Minuccio, overjoyed to be the bearer of such pleasing news, went straightaway, viol and all, to the maiden and, speaking to her in private, recounted to her all that had passed, and then sang her the song to his viol. At this, she was so joyful and content that she immediately showed clear signs of great recovery and eagerly awaited the hour of vespers, when her lord should come, without any of the household knowing or guessing how the matter stood.

Meanwhile, the king, who was a gracious and generous prince, having pondered several times on what he had heard from Minuccio and being very familiar with the maiden and her beauty, grew even more compassionate toward her. Toward vespers, under the pretense of going abroad for his diversion, he mounted his horse and went to the apothecary's house, where, having requested that a very fine garden he had be opened to him, he alighted therein and then asked Bernardo what had become of his daughter and if he had yet married her. 'My lord,' replied the apothecary, 'she is not married; in truth, she has been and is yet very ill, although it is true that since then she has marvellously improved.' The king readily understood what this improvement meant and said, 'In truth, it would be a pity if so fair a creature were to be taken from the world. We would be pleased to visit her.' Accordingly, a little later, he went with Bernardo and two companions only to her chamber, and going up to the bed where the maiden, somewhat uplifted, awaited him with impatience, he took her by the hand and said to her, 'What does this mean, my mistress? You are young and should comfort other women, yet you allow yourself to be ill. We would beseech you, for the love of us, to take heart in such a way that you may speedily be whole again.' The maiden, feeling herself touched by the hands of him whom she loved above all else, though she was somewhat ashamed, yet felt such joy in her heart as if she were in Paradise, and answered him as best she could, saying, 'My lord, my having willed to subject my little strength to very grievous burdens has been the cause to me of this my infirmity, from which, thanks to your goodness, you shall soon see me freed.' The king alone understood the maiden's veiled speech and held her in even higher esteem; indeed, he several times inwardly cursed fortune, who had made her the daughter of such a man. Then, after he had stayed with her awhile and comforted her further, he took his leave.

This act of humanity by the king was greatly praised and seen as a great honor to the apothecary and his daughter, who remained as well pleased as any woman ever was with her lover, and, sustained by better hope, recovered in a few days and became fairer than ever. When she was well again, the king, having taken counsel with the queen on what return he should make her for so much love, one day mounted his horse with many of his barons and went to the apothecary's house and, entering the garden, had Master Bernardo and his daughter summoned. Then, the queen presently coming thither with many ladies and having received Lisa among them, they all began to make merry. After a while, the king and queen called Lisa to them, and the former said to her, 'Noble maiden, the great love you have borne us has earned you a great honor from us, with which we would have you, for the love of us, be content: that is, since you are fit for marriage, we would have you take as your husband the one whom we shall bestow on you, purposing, notwithstanding this, to call ourselves still your knight, without desiring anything from you of so much love but one single kiss.' The maiden, growing all rosy-cheeked from shyness, making the king's pleasure her own, replied in a low voice in this wise, 'My lord, I am well assured that, were it known that I had fallen in love with you, most folk would account me mad therefor, thinking belike that I had forgotten myself and knew not my own condition nor yet yours. But God, who alone sees the hearts of mortals, knows that, in that same hour when you first pleased me, I knew you for a king and myself for the daughter of Bernardo the apothecary, and that it ill beseemed me to address the ardor of my soul to so high a place. But, as you know far better than I, none here below falls in love according to fitness of election, but according to appetite and inclination, against which law I once and again strove with all my might, till, availing no farther, I loved and love and shall ever love you. But, since first I felt myself taken with love of you, I determined still to make your will mine; wherefore, not only will I gladly obey you in this matter of taking a husband at your hands and holding dear him whom it shall please you to bestow on me, since that will be my honor and estate, but, should you bid me abide in the fire, it were a delight to me, an I thought thereby to pleasure you. To have you, a king, for my knight, you know how far it befits me, wherefore to that I make no farther answer; nor shall the kiss be vouchsafed you, which alone of my love you would have, without leave of my lady the queen. Natheless, of such graciousness as hath been yours towards me and that of our lady the queen here God render you for me both thanks and recompense, for I have not the wherewithal.' And with that she was silent.

Her answer greatly pleased the queen, who found her as discreet as the king had reported. Don Pedro then summoned the girl's father and mother and, finding that they were well pleased with what he purposed to do, called a young man named Perdicone, who was of gentle birth but poor, and, giving certain rings into his hand, married him, nothing loath, to Lisa. This done, he then and there, in addition to many precious jewels bestowed by the queen and himself upon the maiden, gave him the fiefs of Cefalù and Calatafimi, saying, 'These we give thee as the lady's dowry. What we purpose to do for thyself, thou shalt see in time to come.' Having said this, he turned to the maiden and, saying, 'Now will we take that fruit which we are to have of your love,' took her head in his hands and kissed her on the brow. Perdicone and Lisa's father and mother, well pleased, (as indeed was she herself,) held high festival and joyous nuptials. And according as many avouch, the king very faithfully kept his covenant with the maiden, for that, whilst she lived, he still styled himself her knight nor ever went about any deed of arms but he wore none other favour than that which was sent him of her. It is by doing on this wise that subjects' hearts are gained, that others are incited to do well and that eternal renown is acquired; but this is a mark at which few or none nowadays bend the bow of their understanding, most princes being presently grown cruel and tyrannical.
