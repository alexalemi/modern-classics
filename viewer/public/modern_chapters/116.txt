Titus was pleased with the plan, so Gisippus welcomed the lady into his home as his own (since Titus had recovered and was doing well). After a great celebration, the ladies left the newly-married wife in her husband's bed and departed. Titus' chamber was next to Gisippus', and one could go from one room to the other. With the lights extinguished in his chamber, Gisippus stealthily went to his friend and told him to go lie with his mistress. Titus, overcome with shame, wanted to refuse and reconsider, but Gisippus, who wholeheartedly desired to please his friend, insisted that he go. When Titus came to the bed, he took the young woman in his arms and playfully asked if she would be his wife. Thinking he was Gisippus, she answered "Yes," and Titus placed a fine, rich ring on her finger, saying, "And I choose you to be my wife." Then, after consummating the marriage, he enjoyed her lovingly, without her or anyone else realizing that it was not Gisippus who lay with her.

With Titus and Sophronia now married, Titus' father Publius passed away, and Titus was summoned to return to Rome without delay to attend to his affairs. He discussed this with Gisippus, deciding to take Sophronia with him, but they would need to reveal the truth to her. One day, they called her into the chamber and thoroughly explained what had happened between the two friends. Sophronia, regarding them both with some disdain, began to weep bitterly, complaining of Gisippus' deception. Rather than make a scene in his home, she went to her father's house and informed him and her mother of the trick Gisippus had played on them, declaring herself to be Titus' wife, not Gisippus'. This was deeply distressing to Sophronia's father, who made long and severe complaints to her relatives and Gisippus' family. There was much discussion and uproar about the matter. Gisippus was despised by both his own relatives and Sophronia's, and everyone declared him deserving not only of blame but severe punishment. However, Gisippus maintained that he had done an honorable thing, for which Sophronia's family should be grateful, as he had married her to a better man than himself.

Titus heard and endured everything with no small annoyance, knowing it was the custom of the Greeks to clamor and threaten until they found someone to answer them, and then become humble and submissive. He decided their clamor could no longer be tolerated without response.
