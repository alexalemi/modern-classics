Chapter 13: The Natural Condition of Mankind — Their Happiness and Their Misery

Nature has made men so equal in the faculties of body and mind that, although you can sometimes find one man who is obviously stronger in body or quicker in mind than another, still, when you add everything up, the difference between man and man is not so great that one of them can claim any advantage for himself that another cannot claim just as well. Consider strength of body: the weakest man has strength enough to kill the strongest, either by a secret plot or by joining forces with others who are in the same danger he is.

As for the faculties of the mind, I find an even greater equality among men than the equality of strength. (I am setting aside the arts built on words, and especially the skill of proceeding by general and infallible rules called science, which very few people have, and only in a few subjects — because it is not a natural faculty we are born with, nor is it picked up, the way prudence is, while we are busy attending to something else.) For prudence is nothing but experience, and equal time bestows experience equally on all men in whatever things they equally apply themselves to.

What may make such equality hard to believe is simply each man's vain conceit of his own wisdom. Nearly every man thinks he has more of it than the common crowd — that is, more than everyone except himself and a few others whom he approves of, either because they are famous or because they agree with him. For such is the nature of men: however readily they admit that many others are more clever, more eloquent, or more learned, they will hardly believe that many are as wise as they are. Each man sees his own wit up close and other men's at a distance. But this proves that men are equal on this point, not unequal. For ordinarily there is no greater sign that a thing is equally distributed than that every man is contented with his share.

Equality Breeds Mistrust

From this equality of ability arises an equality of hope in attaining our ends. And therefore, if any two men desire the same thing, which nevertheless they cannot both enjoy, they become enemies; and on the way to their end — which is chiefly their own survival, and sometimes only their pleasure — they try to destroy or subdue one another. This is how it comes to pass that where an invader has nothing more to fear than another man's individual strength, then if one man plants, sows, builds, or holds a good piece of ground, others can be expected to come prepared with united forces to dispossess him — to strip him not only of the fruit of his labor, but of his life or liberty as well. And the invader, in turn, stands in the same danger from someone else.

Mistrust Breeds War

Given this mutual mistrust, there is no way for any man to secure himself so reasonable as striking first: that is, using force or cunning to master the persons of all the men he can, for as long as it takes until he sees no other power big enough to endanger him. This is no more than his own survival requires, and it is generally allowed.

There is more. Some men take pleasure in contemplating their own power through acts of conquest, and they push those conquests further than their security requires. And because of them, others — who would otherwise be glad to live at ease within modest bounds — would not be able to survive for long by standing only on the defensive. They too must increase their power by invading. And so, since enlarging one's dominion over other men is necessary to a man's survival, it has to be allowed him.

Again, men get no pleasure (but on the contrary, a great deal of grief) from keeping company where there is no power able to overawe them all. For every man expects his companion to value him at the same rate he sets on himself; and at every sign of contempt or undervaluing, he naturally tries — as far as he dares, which among men with no common power to keep them quiet is far enough to make them destroy each other — to extort a higher value from those who slight him, by harming them; and from everyone else, by the example.

So in the nature of man we find three principal causes of quarrel: first, competition; second, distrust; third, glory.

The first makes men invade for gain; the second, for safety; and the third, for reputation. The first use violence to make themselves masters of other men's persons, wives, children, and cattle; the second, to defend them; the third, over trifles — a word, a smile, a different opinion, or any other sign of being undervalued, whether aimed directly at them in person or reflected onto their relatives, their friends, their nation, their profession, or their name.

Outside Civil States, There Is Always a War of Every One Against Every One

This makes one thing clear: whenever men live without a common power to keep them all in check, they are in the condition called war — and not just any war, but a war of every man against every man. For WAR consists not in battle only, or in the act of fighting, but in a stretch of time in which the will to fight is sufficiently known. The notion of time, then, belongs to the nature of war just as it belongs to the nature of weather. Foul weather does not lie in a shower or two of rain, but in a tendency toward rain over many days together. In the same way, the nature of war consists not in actual fighting, but in the known disposition to fight, during all the time there is no assurance to the contrary. All other time is PEACE.

The Miseries of Such a War

Whatever follows from a time of war, where every man is enemy to every man, also follows from a time when men live with no other security than what their own strength and their own ingenuity can supply. In such a condition there is no place for industry, because the fruit of it is uncertain — and consequently no farming of the earth; no navigation, and no use of the goods that can be imported by sea; no comfortable building; no machines for moving and hauling things that require much force; no knowledge of the face of the earth; no reckoning of time; no arts; no letters; no society; and, worst of all, continual fear and danger of violent death — and the life of man: solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.

To someone who has not weighed these things carefully, it may seem strange that nature should pull men apart this way and make them apt to invade and destroy one another. Such a person, not trusting this inference drawn from the passions, may want to see it confirmed by experience. Then let him consider his own behavior. When he takes a journey, he arms himself and tries to travel in good company. When he goes to sleep, he locks his doors. Even inside his own house, he locks his chests. And he does all this knowing that there are laws and armed public officers ready to avenge any wrong done to him. What opinion does he have of his fellow subjects, when he rides armed? Of his fellow citizens, when he locks his doors? Of his own children and servants, when he locks his chests? Doesn't he accuse mankind by his actions just as much as I do by my words?

But neither of us is accusing man's nature in this. The desires and other passions of man are in themselves no sin. Neither are the actions that proceed from those passions — until men know a law that forbids them. And they cannot know such laws until laws are made; and no law can be made until men have agreed on the person who shall make it.

It may perhaps be thought that no such time or condition of war ever existed, and I believe it was never universal over the whole world. But there are many places where people live that way now. The indigenous peoples in many parts of America — at least as the reports that reach us describe them — have, apart from the government of small families whose harmony depends on natural appetite, no government at all, and live to this day in the brutish manner I described above. In any case, we can see what kind of life there would be where there was no common power to fear, from the kind of life that men who have previously lived under a peaceful government sink into during a civil war.

But even if there had never been a time when particular men were in a condition of war against one another, still, in all times, kings and persons of sovereign authority — because of their independence — are in continual jealousies, in the state and posture of gladiators: weapons pointed, eyes fixed on one another. That is, their forts, garrisons, and guns stand on the frontiers of their kingdoms, and their spies work continually among their neighbors. That is a posture of war. But because by this posture they uphold the industry of their subjects, it does not produce the misery that goes along with the liberty of individual men.

In Such a War, Nothing Is Unjust

From this war of every man against every man, something else follows: that nothing can be unjust. The notions of right and wrong, justice and injustice, have no place there. Where there is no common power, there is no law; where there is no law, there is no injustice. Force and fraud are in war the two cardinal virtues. Justice and injustice are not faculties of either the body or the mind. If they were, they could exist in a man who was alone in the world, just as his senses and passions can. They are qualities that relate to men in society, not in solitude.

It also follows from the same condition that there is no ownership, no dominion, no mine and thine distinct — but only that to be every man's that he can get, and for as long as he can keep it.

And this is the bad condition that man is actually placed in by mere nature — though with a possibility of climbing out of it, a possibility that lies partly in the passions, partly in his reason.

The Passions That Incline Men to Peace

The passions that incline men to peace are fear of death; desire of the things necessary for comfortable living; and a hope of obtaining them by their industry. And reason suggests convenient articles of peace on which men may be drawn to agreement. These articles are the ones otherwise called the laws of nature — of which I shall speak more particularly in the two following chapters.
