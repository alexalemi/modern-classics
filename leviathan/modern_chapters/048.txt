Chapter 43: What Is Necessary for a Person to Be Received into the Kingdom of Heaven

The Difficulty of Obeying Both God and Man at Once

The most frequent pretext for sedition and civil war in Christian commonwealths has long come from a single difficulty, never yet sufficiently resolved: how to obey both God and man at the same time, when their commands contradict each other. One point is obvious enough. When a man receives two contrary commands, and knows that one of them is God's, he ought to obey that one and not the other — even if the other is the command of his lawful sovereign (whether a monarch or a sovereign assembly), or the command of his own father. The real difficulty is this: when men are commanded in the name of God, they often cannot tell, in many cases, whether the command actually comes from God, or whether the man giving it is merely abusing God's name for private ends of his own. For just as the Church of the Jews had many false prophets who built reputations with the people on invented dreams and visions, so the Church of Christ has had, in every age, false teachers who seek reputation with the people through fantastic and false doctrines — and who use that reputation (as is the nature of ambition) to govern them for their own private benefit.

The Difficulty Vanishes for Those Who Can Distinguish What Is, and What Is Not, Necessary to Salvation

But this difficulty of obeying both God and the civil sovereign on earth carries no weight at all for those who can distinguish between what is necessary and what is not necessary for their reception into the kingdom of God. If the civil sovereign's command is one that can be obeyed without forfeiting eternal life, then it is unjust not to obey it, and the apostle's precept applies: "Servants, obey your masters in all things," and "Children, obey your parents in all things" — along with our Savior's precept: "The scribes and Pharisees sit in Moses' chair; all therefore that they tell you to observe, observe and do." But if the command is one that cannot be obeyed without being condemned to eternal death, then it would be madness to obey it, and our Savior's counsel applies (Matthew 10:28): "Fear not those that kill the body, but cannot kill the soul." Everyone, therefore, who wants to avoid both kinds of punishment — the punishments inflicted in this world for disobedience to an earthly sovereign, and those to be inflicted in the world to come for disobedience to God — needs to be taught to distinguish well between what is, and what is not, necessary to eternal salvation.

All That Is Necessary to Salvation Is Contained in Faith and Obedience

All that is necessary to salvation is contained in two virtues: faith in Christ, and obedience to laws. The second of these, if it were perfect, would be enough by itself. But because we are all guilty of disobedience to God's law — not only originally, in Adam, but also actually, by our own transgressions — something more is now required of us than obedience for the rest of our days. We also need remission of sins for the time past; and that remission is the reward of our faith in Christ. That nothing else is necessarily required for salvation is plain from this: the kingdom of heaven is shut to no one but sinners — that is, to the disobedient, the transgressors of the law — and not even to them, if they repent and believe all the articles of Christian faith that are necessary to salvation.

What Obedience Is Necessary

The obedience God requires of us — God, who in all our actions accepts the will for the deed — is a serious endeavor to obey him; and it goes by every name that signifies that endeavor. So obedience is sometimes called charity and love, because those imply a will to obey, and our Savior himself makes our love of God and of one another a fulfilling of the whole law. Sometimes it is called righteousness, for righteousness is nothing but the will to give everyone his own — that is to say, the will to obey the laws. And sometimes it is called repentance, because to repent implies a turning away from sin, which is the same thing as the will returning to obedience. Whoever sincerely desires to fulfill the commandments of God, or truly repents of his transgressions, or loves God with all his heart and his neighbor as himself, has all the obedience necessary for his reception into the kingdom of God. For if God required perfect innocence, no flesh could be saved.

And Obedience to What Laws

But which commandments are the ones God has given us? Are all the laws given to the Jews by the hand of Moses the commandments of God? If they are, why aren't Christians taught to obey them? If they are not, what laws are God's, besides the law of nature? For our Savior Christ did not give us new laws, but counsel to observe the laws we are already subject to — that is, the laws of nature and the laws of our several sovereigns. Even in the Sermon on the Mount he made no new law for the Jews; he only expounded the laws of Moses, to which they were subject already. The laws of God, therefore, are none other than the laws of nature — chief among them the law that we must not violate our faith: that is, a commandment to obey the civil sovereigns we set up over ourselves by mutual pact with one another. And this law of God, which commands obedience to the civil law, commands by consequence obedience to all the precepts of the Bible — which (as I proved in the previous chapter) is law only where the civil sovereign has made it law, and everywhere else is only counsel, which a man may refuse to obey at his own peril, without injustice.

In a Christian's Faith, Who Is the Person Believed?

Knowing now what obedience is necessary to salvation, and to whom it is owed, we must consider faith next: whom we believe, and why; and what articles, or points, must necessarily be believed by those who are to be saved. First, the person whom we believe. Since it is impossible to believe any person before we know what he says, the person believed must be someone we have heard speak. The person whom Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, and the prophets believed, then, was God himself, who spoke to them supernaturally. And the person whom the apostles and disciples who lived alongside Christ believed was our Savior himself. But of those to whom neither God the Father nor our Savior ever spoke, it cannot be said that the person they believed was God. They believed the apostles, and after them the pastors and doctors of the Church, who recommended to their faith the history of the Old and New Testaments. So the faith of Christians, ever since our Savior's time, has had for its foundation, first, the reputation of their pastors, and afterward the authority of those who made the Old and New Testaments be received as the rule of faith — and none could do that but Christian sovereigns, who are therefore the supreme pastors, and the only persons whom Christians now hear speak from God; except for any to whom God still speaks supernaturally in these days. But because many false prophets "are gone out into the world," other men must examine such spirits (as St. John advises us, 1 John 4:1) "whether they be of God, or not." And so, since the examination of doctrines belongs to the supreme pastor, the person whom everyone without special revelation is to believe is, in every commonwealth, the supreme pastor — that is to say, the civil sovereign.

The Causes of Christian Faith

The causes that lead men to believe any Christian doctrine are various; for faith is the gift of God, and he works it in each man by whatever means seem good to himself. The most ordinary immediate cause of our belief in any point of Christian faith is that we believe the Bible to be the word of God. But why we believe the Bible to be the word of God is much disputed — as every question must be that is badly framed. For people frame the question not as "Why do we believe it?" but as "How do we know it?" — as if believing and knowing were the same thing. And so one side grounds its supposed knowledge on the infallibility of the Church, and the other side on the testimony of the private spirit, and neither side proves what it claims. For how shall a man know the infallibility of the Church, except by first knowing the infallibility of the Scripture? Or how shall a man know that his own private spirit is anything more than a belief grounded on the authority and arguments of his teachers — or on a presumption about his own gifts? Besides, there is nothing in Scripture from which the infallibility of the Church can be inferred; much less the infallibility of any particular church; and least of all the infallibility of any particular man.

Faith Comes by Hearing

It is plain, therefore, that Christian men do not know, but only believe, the Scripture to be the word of God; and the means God is ordinarily pleased to use to make men believe it follows the way of nature — that is to say, it comes from their teachers. This is St. Paul's doctrine concerning Christian faith in general (Romans 10:17): "Faith cometh by hearing" — that is, by hearing our lawful pastors. He also says (Romans 10:14-15): "How shall they believe in him of whom they have not heard? And how shall they hear without a preacher? And how shall they preach, except they be sent?" From this it is evident that the ordinary cause of believing that the Scriptures are the word of God is the same as the cause of believing every other article of our faith: namely, hearing those whom the law allows and appoints to teach us — our parents in their houses, and our pastors in the churches. Experience makes this even plainer. What other reason can be given why, in Christian commonwealths, everyone believes — or at least professes — the Scripture to be the word of God, while in other commonwealths scarcely anyone does, except that in Christian commonwealths people are taught it from infancy, and in other places they are taught otherwise?

But if teaching is the cause of faith, why doesn't everyone believe? It is certain, then, that faith is the gift of God, and he gives it to whom he will. Nevertheless, because he gives it — to those he gives it to — by means of teachers, the immediate cause of faith is hearing. In a school where many are taught, some profit and others do not; the cause of learning in those who profit is the master — yet no one can infer from this that learning is not the gift of God. All good things proceed from God; yet not everyone who has them can say he is inspired, for that implies a supernatural gift and the immediate hand of God — and whoever claims that, claims to be a prophet, and is subject to the examination of the Church.

But whether men know, or believe, or merely grant the Scriptures to be the word of God: if, from passages of Scripture that contain no obscurity, I can show which articles of faith are necessary for salvation — and the only ones necessary — then those men must of necessity know, believe, or grant the same.

The Only Necessary Article of Christian Faith — the Unum Necessarium

The only article of faith that Scripture makes simply necessary to salvation — the unum necessarium, the one thing needed — is this: that JESUS IS THE CHRIST. By the name "Christ" is understood the king whom God had promised, through the prophets of the Old Testament, to send into the world: to reign under him eternally over the Jews, and over those of other nations who would believe in him; and to give them that eternal life which was lost by the sin of Adam. When I have proved this out of Scripture, I will go on to show when, and in what sense, certain other articles may also be called necessary.

Proved from the Aim of the Evangelists

For proof that belief in this one article — Jesus is the Christ — is all the faith required for salvation, my first argument is from the aim of the evangelists, which was, by describing the life of our Savior, to establish that one article. The sum of St. Matthew's gospel is this: that Jesus was of the line of David, and born of a virgin — which are the marks of the true Christ; that the Magi came to worship him as King of the Jews; that Herod, for the same reason, sought to kill him; that John the Baptist proclaimed him; that he preached, by himself and through his apostles, that he was that king; that he taught the law not as a scribe, but as a man with authority; that he cured diseases by his word alone, and did many other miracles that the Christ was foretold to do; that he was hailed as king when he entered Jerusalem; that he warned his followers to beware of all others who would claim to be Christ; that he was arrested, accused, and put to death for saying that he was king; and that the charge of his condemnation, written on the cross, was JESUS OF NAZARETH, THE KING OF THE JEWS. All of this points to one end and no other: that men should believe that Jesus is the Christ. Such, then, was the aim of St. Matthew's gospel. And the aim of all the evangelists, as anyone may see by reading them, was the same. Therefore the aim of the whole gospel was the establishing of that one article. And St. John expressly makes it his conclusion (John 20:31): "These things are written, that you may know that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God."

From the Sermons of the Apostles

My second argument is taken from the subject of the apostles' sermons, both while our Savior lived on earth and after his ascension. In our Savior's time, the apostles were sent (Luke 9:2) to preach the kingdom of God; for neither there, nor in Matthew 10:7, does he give them any commission other than this: "As ye go, preach, saying, the kingdom of heaven is at hand" — that is, that Jesus is the Messiah, the Christ, the king who was to come. That their preaching after his ascension was the same is plain from Acts 17:6: "They drew Jason and certain brethren unto the rulers of the city" (says St. Luke), "crying, These that have turned the world upside down are come hither also, whom Jason hath received. And these all do contrary to the decrees of Caesar, saying that there is another king, one Jesus." And from verses 2 and 3 of the same chapter, where it is said that St. Paul, "as his manner was, went in unto them, and three sabbath days reasoned with them out of the Scriptures, opening and alleging that Christ must needs have suffered, and risen again from the dead, and that this Jesus, whom he preached, is Christ."

From the Easiness of the Doctrine

The third argument is from those places of Scripture that declare all the faith required for salvation to be easy. For if an inward assent of the mind to all the doctrines of Christian faith now taught — most of which are disputed — were necessary to salvation, there would be nothing in the world so hard as being a Christian. The thief on the cross, though repentant, could not have been saved for saying, "Lord, remember me when thou comest into thy kingdom" — words that testify to no other article than this: that Jesus was the king. Nor could it be said (Matthew 11:30) that "Christ's yoke is easy, and his burden light." Nor that "little children believe in him," as in Matthew 18:6. Nor could St. Paul have said (1 Corinthians 1:21), "It pleased God by the foolishness of preaching to save them that believe." Nor could St. Paul himself have been saved — much less become so great a doctor of the Church so suddenly — when he had perhaps never once thought of transubstantiation, or purgatory, or many of the other articles now forced on believers.

From Plain and Clear Texts

The fourth argument is taken from texts that are express, and admit no controversy of interpretation. First, John 5:39: "Search the Scriptures, for in them ye think ye have eternal life; and they are they that testify of me." Our Savior here speaks only of the Scriptures of the Old Testament, for the Jews at that time could not search the Scriptures of the New Testament, which were not yet written. But the Old Testament contains nothing of Christ except the marks by which men might know him when he came: that he would descend from David, be born at Bethlehem, and of a virgin; that he would do great miracles, and the like. Therefore to believe that this Jesus was he was sufficient for eternal life. But more than sufficient is not necessary — and consequently no other article is required.

Second (John 11:26): "Whosoever liveth and believeth in me shall not die eternally." Therefore to believe in Christ is faith sufficient for eternal life, and consequently no more faith than that is necessary. And to believe in Jesus, and to believe that Jesus is the Christ, are one and the same thing, as the verses immediately following make clear. For when our Savior asks Martha (verse 26), "Believest thou this?" she answers (verse 27), "Yea, Lord: I believe that thou art the Christ, the Son of God, which should come into the world." Therefore this article alone is faith sufficient for eternal life, and more than sufficient is not necessary.

Third, John 20:31: "These things are written that ye might believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that believing ye might have life through his name." There, to believe that Jesus is the Christ is faith sufficient for obtaining life; and therefore no other article is necessary.

Fourth, 1 John 4:2: "Every spirit that confesseth that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh is of God." And 1 John 5:1: "Whosoever believeth that Jesus is the Christ is born of God." And verse 5: "Who is he that overcometh the world, but he that believeth that Jesus is the Son of God?"

Fifth, Acts 8:36-37: "See," says the eunuch, "here is water; what doth hinder me to be baptized? And Philip said, If thou believest with all thy heart, thou mayest. And he answered and said, I believe that Jesus Christ is the Son of God." Therefore this article believed — Jesus is the Christ — is sufficient for baptism, that is to say, for our reception into the kingdom of God; and by consequence, it is the only article necessary. And generally, wherever our Savior says to anyone, "Thy faith hath saved thee," the occasion for saying it is some confession that directly, or by consequence, implies a belief that Jesus is the Christ.

From Its Being the Foundation of All Other Articles

The last argument is from the places where this article is made the foundation of faith — for he that holds the foundation shall be saved. The first such place is Matthew 24:23-24: "If any man shall say unto you, Lo, here is Christ, or there, believe it not; for there shall arise false Christs and false prophets, and shall show great signs and wonders," and so on. Here we see that this article — Jesus is the Christ — must be held even if the man who teaches the contrary works great miracles.

The second place is Galatians 1:8: "Though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that we have preached unto you, let him be accursed." But the gospel which Paul and the other apostles preached was only this article, that Jesus is the Christ. Therefore, for the belief of this article, we are to reject the authority of an angel from heaven — much more of any mortal man — if he teaches the contrary. This, then, is the fundamental article of Christian faith.

A third place is 1 John 4:1-2: "Beloved, believe not every spirit. Hereby ye shall know the Spirit of God: every spirit that confesseth that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh is of God." From this it is evident that this article is the measure and rule by which all other articles are to be weighed and examined — and is therefore the only fundamental one.

A fourth is Matthew 16:18, where, after St. Peter had professed this article, saying to our Savior, "Thou art Christ, the Son of the living God," our Savior answered, "Thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church." From which I infer that this article is the rock on which all the other doctrines of the Church are built, as on their foundation.

A fifth is 1 Corinthians 3:11-15: "Other foundation can no man lay than that which is laid: Jesus is the Christ. Now if any man build upon this foundation gold, silver, precious stones, wood, hay, stubble; every man's work shall be made manifest; for the Day shall declare it, because it shall be revealed by fire, and the fire shall try every man's work, of what sort it is. If any man's work abide which he hath built thereupon, he shall receive a reward; if any man's work shall be burnt, he shall suffer loss — but he himself shall be saved, yet so as by fire." These words are partly plain and easy to understand, and partly allegorical and difficult. From the plain part this much may be inferred: pastors who teach this foundation — that Jesus is the Christ — may still be saved even if they draw false consequences from it (something all men are sometimes prone to). And much more may those be saved who are not pastors but hearers, and who believe what their lawful pastors teach them. Therefore belief in this article is sufficient; and by consequence, no other article of faith is necessarily required for salvation.

Now for the allegorical part — that "the fire shall try every man's work," and that "they shall be saved, but so as by fire," or "through fire" (the original Greek is dia puros) — it takes nothing away from the conclusion I have drawn from the plain words. Nevertheless, because this passage has been used as an argument to prove the fire of purgatory, I will offer here my own conjecture about the meaning of this trial of doctrines, and this saving of men as if by fire. The apostle here seems to allude to the words of the prophet Zechariah (Zechariah 13:8-9), who, speaking of the restoration of the kingdom of God, says: "Two parts therein shall be cut off, and die, but the third shall be left therein; and I will bring the third part through the fire, and will refine them as silver is refined, and will try them as gold is tried; they shall call on the name of the Lord, and I will hear them." The day of judgment is the day of the restoration of the kingdom of God; and on that day, St. Peter tells us (2 Peter 3:7, 10, 12), will come the conflagration of the world, in which the wicked shall perish — but the remnant whom God will save shall pass through that fire unhurt, and in it be tried and refined of their idolatry (as silver and gold are refined by fire from their dross), and be brought to call on the name of the true God. Alluding to this, St. Paul says here that the Day — that is, the day of judgment, the great day of our Savior's coming to restore the kingdom of God in Israel — shall try every man's doctrine, judging which are gold, silver, precious stones, and which are wood, hay, stubble. And then those who have built false consequences on the true foundation shall see their doctrines condemned; nevertheless they themselves shall be saved, pass unhurt through this universal fire, and live eternally, calling on the name of the true and only God. Taken in this sense, there is nothing here that does not agree with the rest of Holy Scripture — and not so much as a glimpse of the fire of purgatory.

In What Sense Other Articles May Be Called Necessary

But here a man may ask whether it is not just as necessary to salvation to believe that God is omnipotent; that he is creator of the world; that Jesus Christ is risen; and that all other men shall rise again from the dead at the last day — as it is to believe that Jesus is the Christ. To which I answer: it is; and so are many more articles. But they are articles contained in this one, and they can be deduced from it with more or less difficulty. For who does not see that those who believe Jesus to be the Son of the God of Israel, and that the Israelites had for their God the omnipotent creator of all things, thereby also believe that God is the omnipotent creator of all things? Or how can a man believe that Jesus is the king who shall reign eternally, unless he also believes that Jesus rose again from the dead? A dead man cannot exercise the office of a king. In sum: he that holds this foundation — Jesus is the Christ — holds expressly all that he sees rightly deduced from it, and implicitly all that follows from it, even if he lacks the skill to work out the consequences himself. And so it still stands good that belief in this one article is faith sufficient to obtain remission of sins for the penitent, and consequently to bring them into the kingdom of heaven.

Faith and Obedience Are Both Necessary to Salvation

Now I have shown that all the obedience required for salvation consists in the will to obey the law of God — that is to say, in repentance — and that all the faith required for it is comprehended in the belief of this article: Jesus is the Christ. I will now cite the places in the gospel that prove that all that is necessary to salvation is contained in these two joined together.

The men to whom St. Peter preached on the day of Pentecost, just after our Savior's ascension, asked him and the rest of the apostles (Acts 2:37), "Men and brethren, what shall we do?" St. Peter answered, in the next verse, "Repent, and be baptized every one of you, for the remission of sins, and ye shall receive the gift of the Holy Ghost." Therefore repentance and baptism — that is, believing that Jesus is the Christ — is all that is necessary to salvation.

Again, when a certain ruler asked our Savior (Luke 18:18), "What shall I do to inherit eternal life?" he answered (verse 20), "Thou knowest the commandments: Do not commit adultery, Do not kill, Do not steal, Do not bear false witness, Honor thy father and thy mother." And when the ruler said he had kept all these, our Savior added, "Sell all thou hast, give it to the poor, and come and follow me" — which was as much as to say: rely on me, who am the king. Therefore to fulfill the law, and to believe that Jesus is the king, is all that is required to bring a man to eternal life.

Third, St. Paul says (Romans 1:17), "The just shall live by faith" — not everyone, but the just. Therefore faith and justice (that is, the will to be just, or repentance) are all that is necessary to eternal life.

And in Mark 1:15 our Savior preached, saying, "The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand: repent, and believe the gospel" — that is, the good news that the Christ had come. Therefore to repent, and to believe that Jesus is the Christ, is all that is required for salvation.

What Each of Them Contributes to Salvation

Since, then, it is necessary that faith and obedience (implied in the word "repentance") both work together for our salvation, the question of which of the two justifies us is pointlessly disputed. Still, it will not be pointless to make clear in what manner each of them contributes, and in what sense it is said that we are justified by the one and by the other.

First: if by "righteousness" is understood the justice of the works themselves, then no man can be saved, for there is no one who has not transgressed the law of God. So when we are said to be justified by works, it must be understood of the will — which God always accepts for the work itself, in good men and in evil men alike. In this sense, and this sense only, a man is called just or unjust; and his justice justifies him in this sense: it gives him, in God's acceptance, the title of "just," and makes him capable of living by his faith, which before he was not. So justice justifies in the sense in which "to justify" means to declare a man just — not in the sense of discharging the law, which would make the punishment of his sins unjust.

But a man is also said to be justified when his plea, though insufficient in itself, is accepted — as when we plead our will, our endeavor to fulfill the law, and our repentance for our failings, and God accepts the plea in place of the performance itself. And because God accepts the will for the deed only in the faithful, it is faith that makes our plea good; and in this sense it is that faith alone justifies. So faith and obedience are both necessary to salvation; yet each of them, in a different sense, is said to justify.

Obedience to God and to the Civil Sovereign Do Not Conflict — When the Sovereign Is a Christian

Having now shown what is necessary to salvation, it is not hard to reconcile our obedience to God with our obedience to the civil sovereign — who is either a Christian or an infidel. If he is a Christian, he allows the belief of this article, that Jesus is the Christ, and of all the articles contained in it, or evidently deduced from it — which is all the faith necessary to salvation. And because he is a sovereign, he requires obedience to all his own laws — that is, to all the civil laws — in which are also contained all the laws of nature, that is, all the laws of God. For besides the laws of nature and the laws of the Church, which are part of the civil law (since the church that can make laws is the commonwealth), there are no other divine laws. Whoever obeys his Christian sovereign, therefore, is hindered neither from believing God nor from obeying him.

But suppose a Christian king, starting from this foundation — Jesus is the Christ — should draw some false consequences from it; that is, build some superstructure of hay or stubble; and command it to be taught. Since St. Paul says that such a builder shall be saved, much more shall the man be saved who teaches it by the king's command; and much more still the man who does not teach it at all, but only believes his lawful teacher. And if a subject is forbidden by the civil sovereign to profess some of his own opinions — on what grounds can he disobey? Christian kings may err in deducing a consequence; but who shall judge? Shall a private man judge, when the question is his own obedience? Or shall anyone judge but the one appointed to it by the Church — that is, by the civil sovereign who represents it? And if the Pope, or an apostle, judges — may not he too err in deducing a consequence? Did not one of the two, St. Peter or St. Paul, err in a superstructure, when St. Paul withstood St. Peter to his face? There can therefore be no contradiction between the laws of God and the laws of a Christian commonwealth.

Or When the Sovereign Is an Infidel

And when the civil sovereign is an infidel, any one of his own subjects who resists him sins against the laws of God (for the laws of nature are God's laws), and rejects the counsel of the apostles, who admonish all Christians to obey their princes, and all children and servants to obey their parents and masters in all things. As for their faith, it is internal and invisible. They have the license that Naaman had — the Syrian who bowed in the house of Rimmon at his master's side — and they need not put themselves in danger for it. But if they do, they ought to expect their reward in heaven, not complain of their lawful sovereign — much less make war upon him. For a man who is not glad of any just occasion of martyrdom does not have the faith he professes; he only pretends it, to put some color on his own defiance. And besides, what infidel king is so unreasonable that — knowing he has a subject who waits for the second coming of Christ after the present world is burned, and who intends then to obey him (which is what believing that Jesus is the Christ amounts to), and who in the meantime thinks himself bound to obey the laws of that infidel king (as all Christians are obliged in conscience to do) — he would put such a subject to death, or persecute him?

And this much shall suffice concerning the kingdom of God and church government. In all of it I claim to advance no position of my own, but only to show the consequences that seem to me deducible from the principles of Christian politics — which are the holy Scriptures — in confirmation of the power of civil sovereigns and the duty of their subjects. In citing Scripture, I have tried to avoid texts of obscure or disputed interpretation, and to cite none except in the sense that is plainest and most agreeable to the harmony and aim of the whole Bible, which was written for the re-establishment of the kingdom of God in Christ. For it is not the bare words, but the aim of the writer, that gives the true light by which any writing is to be interpreted. Those who insist on single texts, without considering the main design, can derive nothing from them clearly. Instead, by throwing atoms of Scripture like dust into men's eyes, they make everything more obscure than it is — the usual trick of those who seek not the truth, but their own advantage.
