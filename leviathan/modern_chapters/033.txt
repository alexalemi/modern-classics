Part III: Of a Christian Commonwealth

Chapter 32: The Principles of Christian Politics

The Word of God, Delivered by Prophets, Is the Main Principle of Christian Politics

Up to this point, I have derived the rights of sovereign power and the duty of subjects from the principles of nature alone — principles that experience has shown to be true, or that agreement about the use of words has made true. That is, I have argued from the nature of human beings as we know it by experience, and from definitions of the words essential to all political reasoning, definitions everyone accepts.

But the subject I take up next is the nature and rights of a CHRISTIAN COMMONWEALTH, and much of that depends on supernatural revelations of the will of God. So the foundation of my argument from here on must be not only the natural word of God, but also the prophetic word.

This does not mean we are to renounce our senses and our experience — or natural reason, which is itself the undoubted word of God. These are the talents God has placed in our hands to trade with until our blessed Savior comes again. They are not to be folded up in the napkin of an unquestioning faith, like the servant in the parable who buried his master's money; they are to be invested — spent in the pursuit of justice, peace, and true religion.

It is true that many things in God's word are above reason. That is, they can neither be demonstrated nor refuted by natural reason. But nothing in it is contrary to reason. And when something seems to be, the fault lies either in our clumsy interpretation or in our faulty reasoning.

So when something written there is too hard for our examination, we are told to take our understanding captive to the words, and not to wear ourselves out trying to extract a philosophical truth, by logic, from mysteries that cannot be comprehended and fall under no rule of natural science. The mysteries of our religion are like medicine for the sick: pills that cure when swallowed whole, but when chewed, are mostly thrown back up without doing any good.

What It Means to Take the Understanding Captive

But this captivity of our understanding does not mean submitting our intellect to the opinion of some other man. It means submitting our will to obedience, where obedience is due.

Here is why. Sense, memory, understanding, reason, and opinion are not in our power to change. They are always, and necessarily, whatever the things we see, hear, and consider suggest to us. They are not the effects of our will — our will is the effect of them. We take our understanding and reason captive, then, when we refrain from contradiction; when we speak as we are commanded to speak by lawful authority; and when we live accordingly. In short, it is trust, and faith placed in the one who speaks — even when the mind can form no idea at all from the words spoken.

How God Speaks to Men

When God speaks to a man, it must be either immediately — directly — or through another man, to whom God had previously spoken directly himself.

How God speaks to a man directly may be well enough understood by those he has spoken to. But how that should be understood by anyone else is hard, if not impossible, to know. If a man claims to me that God has spoken to him supernaturally and directly, and I doubt it, I cannot easily see what argument he could produce that would oblige me to believe him. It is true that if he is my sovereign, he can oblige me to obedience — meaning I may not declare, by act or word, that I do not believe him. But he cannot oblige me to think anything other than what my reason persuades me of. And if someone with no such authority over me makes the same claim, there is nothing that compels either belief or obedience.

Consider what such claims actually amount to. To say God has spoken to him in the Holy Scripture is not to say God has spoken to him directly, but through the prophets, or the apostles, or the church — the same way God speaks to every other Christian. To say God has spoken to him in a dream is no more than to say he dreamed that God spoke to him — which carries no force with anyone who knows that dreams are mostly natural, and can spring from earlier thoughts. And dreams of that particular kind tend to spring from self-conceit, foolish arrogance, and a false opinion of one's own godliness or virtue — the belief that one has earned the favor of an extraordinary revelation. To say he has seen a vision, or heard a voice, is to say he dreamed somewhere between sleeping and waking; that is exactly how a man often mistakes his dream for a vision, having failed to notice that he had dozed off. To say he speaks by supernatural inspiration is to say he feels a burning desire to speak, or holds some strong opinion of himself, for which he can give no natural and sufficient reason.

So although God Almighty certainly can speak to a man by dreams, visions, voices, and inspiration, he obliges no one to believe that he has done so to the man who claims it. That man, being a man, may err — and, what is more, may lie.

By What Marks Prophets Are Known

How, then, can a person to whom God has never revealed his will directly (except by way of natural reason) know when to obey, and when not to obey, the word of God delivered by someone who says he is a prophet?

Scripture itself shows the problem. Of the 400 prophets the king of Israel consulted about his war against Ramoth Gilead, only Micaiah was a true one (1 Kings 22). And the prophet sent to prophesy against the altar Jeroboam had set up — a true prophet, shown to be sent from God by two miracles performed in his presence — was nevertheless deceived by another, older prophet, who persuaded him, as if speaking from the mouth of God, to eat and drink with him (1 Kings 13). If one prophet can deceive another, what certainty is there of knowing the will of God by any way other than reason?

To this I answer, out of the Holy Scripture itself, that there are two marks by which — together, not separately — a true prophet is to be known. One is the working of miracles. The other is teaching no other religion than the one already established. Separately, I say, neither one is sufficient.

"If a prophet rise among you, or a dreamer of dreams, and shall claim to do a miracle, and the miracle comes to pass; if he says, Let us follow strange gods, which you have not known, you shall not listen to him... But that prophet and dreamer of dreams shall be put to death, because he has spoken to make you revolt from the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 13:1-5).

Two things should be observed in those words. First: God will not have miracles alone serve as proof of a prophet's calling. As the third verse says, they serve instead as a test of the constancy of our adherence to God himself. After all, the works of the Egyptian sorcerers, though not as great as those of Moses, were still great miracles. Second: however great the miracle may be, if it tends to stir up revolt against the king, or against whoever governs by the king's authority, the man who performs it is to be regarded as nothing other than someone sent to test their allegiance. For in this passage, the words "revolt from the Lord your God" are equivalent to "revolt from your king" — because Israel had made God their king by pact at the foot of Mount Sinai, and God ruled them through Moses alone; only Moses spoke with God, and from time to time he declared God's commandments to the people.

Our Savior Christ did the same. After he had brought his disciples to acknowledge him as the Messiah — that is, as God's anointed, the king the Jewish nation expected daily but refused when he came — he did not fail to warn them of the danger of miracles. "There shall arise," he says, "false Christs, and false prophets, and they shall do great wonders and miracles, even to the seducing (if it were possible) of the very elect" (Matthew 24:24). Which shows that false prophets may have the power of miracles — and yet we are not to take their doctrine for God's word. St. Paul goes further, telling the Galatians that "if he himself, or an angel from heaven, preach another gospel to them than the one he had preached, let him be accursed" (Galatians 1:8). That gospel was that Christ was king. So by these words, all preaching against the power of the established king stands accursed by St. Paul — for his speech was addressed to people who, through his preaching, had already received Jesus as the Christ, that is, as king of the Jews.

The Marks of a Prophet Under the Old Law: Miracles, and Doctrine That Conforms to the Law

Just as miracles without preaching the doctrine God has established prove nothing, so preaching the true doctrine without working miracles is an insufficient argument for direct revelation. If a man who teaches no false doctrine claims to be a prophet without showing any miracle, his claim deserves no special regard. This is clear from Deuteronomy: "If you say in your heart, How shall we know that the word of the prophet is not what the Lord has spoken? When the prophet has spoken in the name of the Lord something that does not come to pass, that is the word the Lord has not spoken; the prophet has spoken it out of the pride of his own heart. Do not fear him" (Deuteronomy 18:21-22).

But here a man may ask again: when the prophet has foretold a thing, how shall we know whether it will come to pass or not? He may foretell it as something to arrive after a long stretch of time — longer than a human life — or indefinitely, as something that will happen sooner or later. In that case, this mark of a prophet is useless. Therefore the miracles that oblige us to believe a prophet must be confirmed by an immediate event, or one not long delayed.

So it is plain: teaching the religion God has established, and showing a present miracle, joined together, were the only marks by which Scripture would have a true prophet — that is, direct revelation — acknowledged. Neither of them alone is sufficient to oblige any other man to heed what he says.

When Miracles Cease, Prophets Cease, and Scripture Takes Their Place

Since miracles have now ceased, we have no sign left by which to recognize the claimed revelations or inspirations of any private man, and no obligation to give ear to any doctrine beyond what conforms to the Holy Scriptures. Since the time of our Savior, the Scriptures supply the place of all other prophecy. From them, by wise and careful reckoning, all the rules and precepts necessary to know our duty to God and man can easily be deduced — without enthusiasm, and without supernatural inspiration.

And this Scripture is the source from which I will take the principles of my argument concerning the rights of those who are the supreme governors of Christian commonwealths on earth, and the duty of Christian subjects toward their sovereigns. To that end, I shall speak in the next chapter of the books, the writers, the scope, and the authority of the Bible.
