Chapter 26: Of Civil Laws
(Part 1 of 2)

What Civil Law Is

By CIVIL LAWS I mean the laws that men are bound to observe not because they are members of this or that commonwealth in particular, but because they are members of a commonwealth at all. Knowledge of particular laws belongs to those who make the study of their own countries' laws their profession; knowledge of civil law in general belongs to anyone. The ancient law of Rome was called their civil law, from the word civitas — which means a commonwealth. The countries that once lived under the Roman Empire and were governed by that law still keep whatever parts of it they see fit, and they call that portion "the Civil Law" to distinguish it from the rest of their own civil laws. But that is not what I intend to discuss here. My design is not to show what is law here or there, but what law is — as Plato, Aristotle, Cicero, and various others have done without ever taking up the legal profession.

First, it is obvious that law in general is not counsel but command. And not the command of just any man to just any man, but only the command of one whose command is addressed to someone formerly obliged to obey him. Civil law adds only one thing: the name of the person commanding, which is the persona civitatis — the person of the commonwealth.

With that in mind, I define civil law in this manner: CIVIL LAW is, to every subject, those rules which the commonwealth has commanded him — by word, writing, or other sufficient sign of its will — to use for the distinction of right and wrong; that is to say, of what is contrary to the rule and what is not.

There is nothing in this definition that is not evident at first sight. Everyone can see that some laws are addressed to all subjects in general, some to particular provinces, some to particular occupations, and some to particular men; each is law to everyone the command is directed at, and to no one else. Everyone can see that laws are the rules of just and unjust — nothing being counted unjust that is not contrary to some law. Likewise, that none can make laws but the commonwealth, because our subjection is to the commonwealth alone; and that commands must be signified by sufficient signs, because otherwise a man has no way of knowing how to obey them. Whatever can be deduced from this definition by necessary consequence, then, ought to be acknowledged as truth. Here is what I deduce from it.

The Sovereign Is the Legislator

1. In every commonwealth, the legislator is the sovereign and no one else — whether the sovereign is one man, as in a monarchy, or one assembly of men, as in a democracy or aristocracy. For the legislator is the one who makes the law, and only the commonwealth prescribes and commands the observance of the rules we call law. Therefore the commonwealth is the legislator. But the commonwealth is not a person, and has no capacity to do anything except through its representative — that is, the sovereign. Therefore the sovereign is the sole legislator. For the same reason, no one can repeal a law once made except the sovereign, because a law is repealed only by another law that forbids it to be put into execution.

And Is Not Subject to the Civil Laws

2. The sovereign of a commonwealth, whether an assembly or one man, is not subject to the civil laws. Since he has the power to make and repeal laws, he can free himself from any subjection whenever he pleases — by repealing the laws that trouble him and making new ones — and consequently he was free all along. The man who can be free whenever he wants to be is free already. Nor is it possible for any person to be bound to himself; whoever can bind can release, and so whoever is bound only to himself is not bound at all.

Custom Becomes Law by the Sovereign's Consent, Not by Length of Time

3. When long use acquires the authority of a law, it is not the length of time that makes the authority but the will of the sovereign, signified by his silence — for silence is sometimes a sign of consent. And custom remains law only so long as the sovereign stays silent about it. So if the sovereign has a question of right grounded not on his present will but on laws made long ago, the passage of time takes nothing away from his right, and the question must be judged by equity. After all, plenty of unjust actions and unjust sentences go unchallenged for longer than any man can remember. Our own lawyers agree that no customs count as law except reasonable ones, and that evil customs ought to be abolished. But the judgment of what is reasonable, and of what is to be abolished, belongs to the one who makes the law — the sovereign assembly or monarch.

The Law of Nature and the Civil Law Contain Each Other

4. The law of nature and the civil law contain each other, and they are of equal extent. Here is why. The laws of nature — which consist of equity, justice, gratitude, and the other moral virtues that depend on these — are not, in the condition of mere nature, properly laws at all, but qualities that dispose men to peace and obedience: conclusions, or theorems, as I said at the end of Chapter 15. Once a commonwealth is established, then they are actually laws — and not before — because then they are the commands of the commonwealth, and therefore civil laws as well; for it is the sovereign power that obliges men to obey them. When private men fall into disputes, someone must declare what equity is, what justice is, and what moral virtue is, and make those declarations binding; and that requires the ordinances of sovereign power, with punishments ordained for those who break them. Those ordinances are therefore part of the civil law. The law of nature, then, is part of the civil law in every commonwealth in the world.

The reverse is also true: the civil law is part of the dictates of nature. For justice — that is, performance of covenant, and giving every man his own — is a dictate of the law of nature. But every subject in a commonwealth has covenanted to obey the civil law: either with his fellow subjects, as when men assemble to make a common representative, or with the representative himself, one by one, as when men subdued by the sword promise obedience so that they may receive their lives. Therefore obedience to the civil law is part of the law of nature too. Civil law and natural law are not different kinds of law but different parts of law: one part, being written, is called civil; the other, unwritten, natural. The right of nature, however — man's natural liberty — may be cut back and restrained by the civil law. Indeed, the whole purpose of making laws is nothing but such restraint, without which there cannot possibly be any peace. Law was brought into the world for one thing only: to limit the natural liberty of particular men in such a way that they do not hurt one another but assist one another, and join together against a common enemy. These are the artificial chains, called civil laws; and the greatest liberty of subjects still depends on the silence of the law.

Provincial Laws Are Made by the Sovereign Power, Not by Custom

5. If the sovereign of one commonwealth subdues a people who have lived under other written laws, and afterward governs them by those same laws, those laws are still the civil laws of the victor, not of the vanquished commonwealth. For the legislator is not the one by whose authority the laws were first made, but the one by whose authority they continue to be laws now. So where the dominion of a commonwealth contains diverse provinces, and in those provinces a diversity of laws — commonly called the customs of each province — we are not to suppose that such customs have their force merely from length of time. They were anciently laws, written or otherwise made known, as the constitutions and statutes of the sovereigns of those days; and they are laws now, not by virtue of the prescription of time, but by the constitutions of their present sovereigns. But if an unwritten law is generally observed in all the provinces of a dominion, and no iniquity appears in its use, that law can be nothing but a law of nature, obliging all mankind equally.

Some Foolish Opinions of Lawyers About the Making of Laws

6. Since all laws, written and unwritten, receive their authority and force from the will of the commonwealth — that is, from the will of the representative, which in a monarchy is the monarch and in other commonwealths the sovereign assembly — a man may well wonder where certain opinions come from, found in the books of eminent lawyers in several commonwealths, which directly or by consequence make the legislative power depend on private men or subordinate judges.

For example: "The common law has no controller but Parliament." That is true only where a Parliament holds the sovereign power and can be assembled and dissolved by nothing but its own discretion. For if anyone else has the right to dissolve it, that person also has the right to control it — and consequently to control its controllings. And if no one has that right, then the controller of laws is not Parlamentum — the Parliament — but Rex in Parlamento — the King in Parliament. And where a Parliament is sovereign: even if it should assemble ever so many men, and ever so wise, from the countries subject to it, for whatever cause, no one will believe that such a gathering has thereby acquired legislative power for itself.

Another example: "The two arms of a commonwealth are force and justice; the first is in the King, the other deposited in the hands of Parliament." As if a commonwealth could hold together where the force is in any hand that justice has no authority to command and govern.

7. That law can never be against reason, our lawyers all agree; and they agree that the law is not the letter — that is, not every construction that can be put on the words — but what accords with the intention of the legislator. All true. But the question is: whose reason shall be received as law? It cannot be any private reason, for then there would be as much contradiction in the laws as there is among the Scholastics. Nor can it be — as Sir Edward Coke would have it (in his commentary on Littleton, Book 2, chapter 6, folio 97b) — an "artificial perfection of reason, gotten by long study, observation, and experience" (such as his own, he means). For long study may just as well increase and entrench erroneous opinions; and when men build on false foundations, the more they build, the greater the ruin. Of those who study and observe with equal time and equal diligence, the reasonings and resolutions are, and must remain, discordant. So it is not that juris prudentia — the wisdom of subordinate judges — that makes law, but the reason of this our artificial man, the commonwealth, and his command. And since the commonwealth, in its representative, is but one person, contradiction cannot easily arise in the laws; and when it does, the same reason is able to remove it, by interpretation or by alteration. In all courts of justice it is the sovereign — the person of the commonwealth — who judges. The subordinate judge ought to keep in view the reason that moved his sovereign to make the law, so that his sentence conforms to it; then his sentence is his sovereign's sentence. Otherwise it is his own, and an unjust one.

A Law Made but Not Made Known Is No Law

8. From the fact that law is a command, and that a command consists in a declaration or manifestation — by voice, writing, or some other sufficient sign — of the will of the one who commands, we may understand this: the command of the commonwealth is law only to those who have the means to take notice of it. Over born fools, children, and madmen there is no law, any more than over brute beasts; nor are they capable of the title of just or unjust, because they never had the power to make any covenant or to understand its consequences, and so they never took it upon themselves to authorize the actions of any sovereign, as those who make themselves a commonwealth must do. And just as nature or accident has taken from some men the notice of all laws in general, so too any man from whom some accident — not arising from his own fault — has taken away the means of taking notice of a particular law is excused if he does not observe it. Properly speaking, that law is no law to him. It is therefore necessary to consider here what arguments and signs are sufficient for knowing what the law is — that is to say, what the will of the sovereign is — in monarchies and in other forms of government alike.

All Unwritten Laws Are Laws of Nature

First: if a law obliges all subjects without exception, and is not written or otherwise published in places where they can take notice of it, it is a law of nature. For whatever men are to recognize as law not on other men's word but each from his own reason must be agreeable to the reason of all men — and no law fits that description but the law of nature. The laws of nature therefore need no publishing and no proclamation, since they are contained in this one sentence, approved by all the world: Do not that to another, which thou thinkest unreasonable to be done by another to thyself.

Second: if a law obliges only some category of men, or one particular man, and is not written or published by word, it is likewise a law of nature, known by the same arguments and signs that distinguish those in that condition from other subjects. For any law that is not written, or somehow published by the one who makes it law, can be known in no way except by the reason of the one who is to obey it — and such a law is therefore not only civil but natural. For example, if the sovereign employs a public minister without written instructions about what to do, that minister is obliged to take the dictates of reason as his instructions. If the sovereign makes him a judge, the judge is to take notice that his sentence ought to follow the reason of his sovereign — and since that reason is always understood to be equity, he is bound to it by the law of nature. If an ambassador, then in everything not contained in his written instructions, he is to take as his instruction whatever reason dictates to be most conducive to his sovereign's interest. And so it goes for all other ministers of the sovereignty, public and private. All these instructions of natural reason may be gathered under one name: fidelity, which is a branch of natural justice.

The law of nature excepted, it belongs to the essence of all other laws to be made known to every man who shall be obliged to obey them — by word, by writing, or by some other act known to proceed from the sovereign authority. For the will of another cannot be understood except by his own word or act, or by conjecture drawn from his aim and purpose; and in the person of the commonwealth, that purpose must always be presumed consonant with equity and reason. In ancient times, before letters were in common use, laws were often put into verse, so that uneducated people, taking pleasure in singing or reciting them, could keep them more easily in memory. For the same reason Solomon advises a man to bind the ten commandments upon his ten fingers (Proverbs 7:3). And concerning the law that Moses gave the people of Israel at the renewing of the covenant, he tells them to teach it to their children, talking of it both at home and on the road, at lying down and at rising up (Deuteronomy 11:19); to write it on the posts and doors of their houses; and to assemble the people — man, woman, and child — to hear it read (Deuteronomy 31:12).

Nothing Is Law Where the Legislator Cannot Be Known

Nor is it enough that the law be written and published; there must also be clear signs that it proceeds from the will of the sovereign. For private men, when they have force enough — or think they have — to secure their unjust designs and carry them safely to their ambitious ends, may publish whatever they please as law, without the legislative authority or against it. What is required, then, is not only a declaration of the law, but also sufficient signs of its author and its authority. The author, or legislator, is presumed in every commonwealth to be evident, because he is the sovereign — constituted by the consent of everyone, and so presumed by everyone to be sufficiently known. It is true that men are generally so heedless, and so secure, that once the memory of the first constitution of their commonwealth wears out, they no longer consider by whose power they are defended against their enemies, by whose power their industry is protected, and by whose power they are righted when injury is done to them. But since no man who actually considers the matter can question it, no excuse can be drawn from ignorance of where the sovereignty is placed. And it is a dictate of natural reason — and consequently an evident law of nature — that no man ought to weaken the very power whose protection he has himself demanded, or knowingly accepted, against others. Therefore no man can have any doubt about who is sovereign except by his own fault, whatever evil men may suggest. The real difficulty lies in the evidence of the authority derived from him; and removing that difficulty depends on knowledge of the public registers, public counsels, public ministers, and public seals, by which all laws are sufficiently verified.

The Difference Between Verifying and Authorizing

Verified, I say — not authorized. For the verification is only the testimony and record, not the authority of the law, which consists in the command of the sovereign alone.

The Law Verified by the Subordinate Judge

If, then, a man has a question of injury that depends on the law of nature — that is to say, on common equity — the sentence of the judge who is commissioned with authority to hear such causes is a sufficient verification of the law of nature in that individual case. The advice of someone who makes the study of the law his profession is useful for avoiding disputes; but it is only advice. It is the judge who must tell men what is law, upon the hearing of the controversy.

By the Public Registers

But when the question is of an injury or a crime under a written law, every man can — by recourse to the registers, himself or through others — be sufficiently informed, before he does the injury or commits the crime, whether it is an injury or not. Indeed, he ought to do so: when a man doubts whether the act he is about to do is just or unjust, and can inform himself if he wishes, going ahead anyway is unlawful. Likewise, a man who supposes himself injured in a case settled by the written law — which he can see and consider, himself or through others — does unjustly if he complains before he consults the law; he reveals a disposition to harass other men rather than to demand his own right.

By Letters Patent and the Public Seal

If the question is of obedience to a public officer, then to have seen his commission with the public seal and heard it read — or to have had the means of being informed of it, had one wished — is a sufficient verification of his authority. For every man is obliged to do his best endeavor to inform himself of all written laws that may concern his own future actions.

The Interpretation of the Law Depends on the Sovereign Power

The legislator known, and the laws sufficiently published — whether in writing or by the light of nature — there is still one very material circumstance wanting to make them obligatory. For the nature of a law lies not in its letter but in its intent, or meaning — that is to say, in the authentic interpretation of the law, which is the sense of the legislator. Therefore the interpretation of all laws depends on the sovereign authority; and the interpreters can be none but those whom the sovereign — to whom alone the subject owes obedience — shall appoint. Otherwise, by the craft of an interpreter, the law may be made to bear a sense contrary to that of the sovereign, and by that means the interpreter becomes the legislator.

All Laws Need Interpretation

All laws, written and unwritten, need interpretation. The unwritten law of nature is easy enough for those who use their natural reason without partiality or passion, and it therefore leaves its violators without excuse. But considering that there are very few men — perhaps none — who are not in some cases blinded by self-love or some other passion, it has become of all laws the most obscure, and consequently it has the greatest need of able interpreters. Written laws, if short, are easily misinterpreted through the various meanings of a word or two; if long, they are all the more obscure through the various meanings of many words. So no written law, delivered in few words or many, can be well understood without a perfect understanding of the final causes for which the law was made — and the knowledge of those final causes is in the legislator. To him, therefore, no knot in the law can be insoluble: either he finds the ends of the thread and unties it that way, or he makes whatever ends he pleases by the legislative power — as Alexander did with his sword and the Gordian knot — which no other interpreter can do.

The Authentic Interpretation of Law Is Not That of Writers

The interpretation of the laws of nature, in a commonwealth, does not depend on books of moral philosophy. The authority of writers, without the authority of the commonwealth, does not make their opinions law, however true those opinions may be. What I have written in this very treatise concerning the moral virtues, and their necessity for procuring and maintaining peace, though it is evident truth, is not law on that account; it is law because in every commonwealth in the world it is part of the civil law. Naturally reasonable it may be — but it is by the sovereign power that it is law. Otherwise it would be a great error to call the laws of nature "unwritten law" at all, when we see so many volumes published about them, and in those volumes so many contradictions of one another, and of themselves.

The Interpreter of the Law Is the Judge, Giving Sentence Viva Voce — Aloud, in Court — in Every Particular Case

The interpretation of the law of nature is the sentence of the judge who is constituted by the sovereign authority to hear and determine the controversies that depend on it; and it consists in applying the law to the case at hand. For in the act of judging, the judge does no more than consider whether the demand of the party is consonant with natural reason and equity; and the sentence he gives is therefore the interpretation of the law of nature. That interpretation is authentic — not because it is his private sentence, but because he gives it by the authority of the sovereign, by which it becomes the sovereign's sentence, and law, for that time, to the parties pleading.
