Chapter 33: The Number, Antiquity, Scope, Authority, and Interpreters of the Books of Holy Scripture

The Books of Holy Scripture

By the books of Holy Scripture we mean those books that ought to be the CANON — that is to say, the rules of Christian life. And because every rule of life that men are bound in conscience to observe is a law, the question of Scripture is really the question of what counts as law throughout all Christendom, both natural and civil. Scripture does not spell out which laws every Christian king shall set up in his own dominions; but it does determine which laws he shall not set up. And since I have already proved that sovereigns in their own dominions are the sole legislators, it follows that the only books that are canonical — that is, the only books that are law — in any nation are the ones established as such by the sovereign authority.

It is true that God is the sovereign of all sovereigns. So when he speaks to any subject, that subject ought to obey him, whatever any earthly power commands to the contrary. But the question is not whether to obey God. The question is when and what God has said. And subjects who have received no supernatural revelation cannot know that except by natural reason — the same reason that guided them, for the sake of peace and justice, to obey the authority of their commonwealths, that is, of their lawful sovereigns.

Following that obligation, I can acknowledge no books of the Old Testament as Holy Scripture except those that the authority of the Church of England has commanded us to acknowledge. Which books these are is well enough known without a catalogue here. They are the same ones acknowledged by St. Jerome, who counts the rest as Apocrypha — namely the Wisdom of Solomon, Ecclesiasticus, Judith, Tobias, the first and second books of Maccabees (though he had seen the first in Hebrew), and the third and fourth books of Esdras.

Of the canonical books, Josephus — a learned Jew who wrote in the time of the Emperor Domitian — counts twenty-two, making the number match the letters of the Hebrew alphabet. St. Jerome arrives at the same count, though the two of them divide the books differently. Josephus numbers five books of Moses, thirteen of prophets who wrote the history of their own times (how that squares with the prophets' writings contained in the Bible, we shall see later), and four of hymns and moral precepts. St. Jerome counts five books of Moses, eight of prophets, and nine of other holy writings, which he calls the Hagiographa — the "sacred writings." The Septuagint — the seventy learned Jewish scholars sent for by Ptolemy, King of Egypt, to translate the Jewish law from Hebrew into Greek — have left us no other books as Holy Scripture in the Greek language than the very ones received by the Church of England.

As for the books of the New Testament, they are acknowledged as canon equally by all Christian churches, and by every sect of Christians that admits any books at all as canonical.

Their Antiquity

Who originally wrote the various books of Holy Scripture has never been established by sufficient testimony from other history — and testimony is the only proof there is for a matter of fact. Nor can it be settled by arguments of natural reason, because reason serves only to establish truths of consequence, not truths of fact. So the light that must guide us in this question has to be the light held out to us by the books themselves. And though that light does not show us the writer of every book, it is still useful: it can tell us the time in which the books were written.

The Pentateuch Was Not Written by Moses

Start with the Pentateuch. The fact that these are called the five Books of Moses is not enough to prove that Moses wrote them — no more than the titles "the Book of Joshua," "the Book of Judges," "the Book of Ruth," and "the Books of the Kings" prove that those books were written by Joshua, by the judges, by Ruth, and by the kings. In book titles, the subject is named as often as the writer. "The History of Livy" names the writer; but "the History of Scanderbeg" is named for its subject.

Now consider what we read in the last chapter of Deuteronomy, verse 6, concerning the tomb of Moses: "no man knoweth of his sepulcher to this day" — that is, to the day those words were written. Plainly, then, those words were written after his burial. It would be a strange reading indeed to say that Moses spoke of his own tomb — even by prophecy — declaring that it had not been found up to a day on which he was still alive.

Perhaps someone will object that only the last chapter was written by another hand, not the whole Pentateuch. So consider what we find in the Book of Genesis (Genesis 12:6): "And Abraham passed through the land to the place of Sichem, unto the plain of Moreh, and the Canaanite was then in the land." These must be the words of someone writing at a time when the Canaanite was no longer in the land — and therefore not the words of Moses, who died before he ever entered it. Likewise, in Numbers 21:14, the writer cites another, more ancient book, titled The Book of the Wars of the Lord, in which were recorded the acts of Moses at the Red Sea and at the brook of Arnon. It is sufficiently evident, then, that the five Books of Moses were written after his time — though how long after is not so clear.

But even though Moses did not compile those books entirely, and not in the form we now have them, he did write everything that he is there said to have written. For example: the Volume of the Law, which is contained, it seems, in chapter 11 of Deuteronomy and the chapters following, up to chapter 27 — the same law that was commanded to be written on stones at their entry into the land of Canaan (Deuteronomy 31:9). This Moses himself wrote and delivered to the priests and elders of Israel, to be read every seventh year to all Israel when they assembled at the Feast of Tabernacles. This is the law God commanded their kings to copy from the priests and Levites — once they had established that form of government — and to lay beside the Ark (Deuteronomy 31:26). And it is the same law that, after being lost, was found again long afterward by Hilkiah and sent to King Josiah, who had it read to the people and so renewed the covenant between God and them (2 Kings 22:8; 23:1-3).

The Book of Joshua Was Written After His Time

That the Book of Joshua was also written long after the time of Joshua can be gathered from many passages in the book itself. Joshua had set up twelve stones in the middle of the Jordan as a monument of their crossing (Joshua 4:9), and of these the writer says, "They are there unto this day" (Joshua 5:9) — and "unto this day" is a phrase that signifies a time past, beyond living memory. In the same way, when the Lord said that he had rolled the reproach of Egypt off the people, the writer says, "The place is called Gilgal unto this day" — which would have been an odd thing to say in Joshua's own lifetime. So also with the name of the Valley of Achor, named for the trouble that Achan stirred up in the camp (Joshua 7:26): the writer says the name "remaineth unto this day," which must therefore be long after the time of Joshua. There are many other arguments of this kind, such as Joshua 8:29, 13:13, 14:14, and 15:63.

The Books of Judges and Ruth Were Written Long After — Indeed After the Captivity

The same is plain from similar arguments in the Book of Judges — chapters 1:21, 1:26, 6:24, 10:4, 15:19, 17:6 — and in Ruth 1:1. But the clearest of all is Judges 18:30, where it is said that Jonathan "and his sonnes were Priests to the Tribe of Dan, untill the day of the captivity of the land."

The Same for the Books of Samuel

That the Books of Samuel were likewise written after his own time, there are arguments of the same kind: 1 Samuel 5:5, 7:13, 7:15, 27:6, and 30:25, where, after David had ruled that those who guarded the supplies should share the spoils equally with those who fought, the writer says, "He made it a Statute and an Ordinance to Israel to this day." Again (2 Samuel 6:4), when David — displeased that the Lord had struck down Uzzah for putting out his hand to steady the Ark — called the place Perez-Uzzah, the writer says it is called so "to this day." The writing of that book, then, must come long after the time of the events; that is, long after the time of David.

The Books of the Kings and the Chronicles

As for the two Books of the Kings and the two Books of the Chronicles: besides the passages mentioning monuments that, as the writer says, remained until his own days — such as 1 Kings 9:13, 9:21, 10:12, 12:19; 2 Kings 2:22, 8:22, 10:27, 14:7, 16:6, 17:23, 17:34, 17:41; 1 Chronicles 4:41, 5:26 — there is a sufficient argument that they were written after the captivity in Babylon: their history is carried right up to that time. For the deeds recorded in a register are always more ancient than any book that mentions and quotes that register — and these books do exactly that in many places, referring the reader to the Chronicles of the Kings of Judah, to the Chronicles of the Kings of Israel, to the books of the prophet Samuel, of the prophet Nathan, of the prophet Ahijah, to the Vision of Jehdo, and to the books of the prophet Serveiah and of the prophet Addo.

Ezra and Nehemiah

The Books of Esdras — that is, Ezra — and Nehemiah were certainly written after the return from captivity, because they contain the return itself, the rebuilding of the walls and houses of Jerusalem, the renewal of the covenant, and the reordering of their government.

Esther

The history of Queen Esther belongs to the time of the captivity; the writer, therefore, must have lived at that time or after it.

Job

The Book of Job carries no mark of the time it was written. And though it is clear enough (Ezekiel 14:14; James 5:11) that Job was no invented person, the book itself seems to be not a history but a treatise on a question much disputed in ancient times: why wicked men have often prospered in this world while good men have suffered. The strongest evidence is this: from the beginning of the book to the third verse of the third chapter, where Job's complaint begins, the Hebrew is in prose (as St. Jerome testifies); from there to the sixth verse of the last chapter, it is in hexameter verse; and the rest of that final chapter is prose again. So the whole dispute is in verse, and the prose is added only as a preface at the beginning and an epilogue at the end. And verse is not the usual style of people in great pain, like Job, or of people who come to comfort them, like his friends — but it was common in philosophy, especially moral philosophy, in ancient times.

The Psalter

The Psalms were written for the most part by David, for the use of the choir. To these were added some songs of Moses and other holy men, and some written after the return from the captivity — such as Psalm 137 and Psalm 126. From this it is plain that the Psalter was compiled, and put into the form it now has, after the return of the Jews from Babylon.

The Proverbs

The Proverbs are a collection of wise and godly sayings — partly Solomon's, partly those of Agur the son of Jakeh, and partly those of the mother of King Lemuel. It is hardly likely that Solomon collected them rather than Agur or Lemuel's mother. The sayings are theirs; but the collecting and compiling of them into this one book was the work of some other godly man who lived after them all.

Ecclesiastes and the Song of Songs

The Books of Ecclesiastes and the Canticles — the Song of Songs — contain nothing that was not Solomon's, except the titles or inscriptions. For "The Words of the Preacher, the Son of David, King in Jerusalem," and "the Song of Songs, which is Solomon's," seem to have been added for the sake of distinguishing the books later, when the books of Scripture were gathered into one body of the law — so that not only the doctrine but the authors too would be on record.

The Prophets

Of the prophets, the most ancient are Zephaniah, Jonah, Amos, Hosea, Isaiah, and Micah, who lived in the time of Amaziah and Azariah — also called Uzziah — kings of Judah. But the Book of Jonah is not properly a record of his prophecy — his whole prophecy comes down to these few words: "Fourty dayes and Ninivy shall be destroyed" — but rather a history, a narrative, of his stubbornness in disputing God's commands. So it is hardly likely that Jonah is its author, since he is its subject. The Book of Amos, on the other hand, is his prophecy.

Jeremiah, Obadiah, Nahum, and Habakkuk prophesied in the time of Josiah.

Ezekiel, Daniel, Haggai, and Zechariah prophesied during the captivity.

When Joel and Malachi prophesied is not evident from their writings. But considering the inscriptions, or titles, of their books, this much is plain enough: the whole Scripture of the Old Testament was set forth in the form we now have it after the return of the Jews from their captivity in Babylon, and before the time of Ptolemy Philadelphus, who had it translated into Greek by the seventy men sent to him out of Judea for that purpose.

And if the books of the Apocrypha — which the Church commends to us not as canonical, but as profitable for our instruction — may be believed on this point, then Scripture was set forth in its present form by Esdras, that is, Ezra. This appears from what he himself says in the second book (2 Esdras 14:21-22 and following), where, speaking to God, he says: "Thy law is burnt; therefore no man knoweth the things which thou hast done, or the works that are to begin. But if I have found Grace before thee, send down the holy Spirit into me, and I shall write all that hath been done in the world, since the beginning, which were written in thy Law, that men may find thy path, and that they which will live in the later days, may live." And at verse 45: "And it came to passe when the forty dayes were fulfilled, that the Highest spake, saying, 'The first that thou hast written, publish openly, that the worthy and unworthy may read it; but keep the seventy last, that thou mayst deliver them onely to such as be wise among the people.'" And so much for the time when the books of the Old Testament were written.

The New Testament

The writers of the New Testament all lived within less than a generation after Christ's ascension, and all of them had seen our Savior or been his disciples — except St. Paul and St. Luke. Consequently, whatever they wrote is as ancient as the time of the apostles themselves. But the time when the books of the New Testament were received and acknowledged by the Church as their writing is not nearly so ancient. For just as the books of the Old Testament come down to us from no earlier a time than that of Ezra — who, by the direction of God's Spirit, restored them when they were lost — so the books of the New Testament, of which the copies were few and could not easily all be in any one private man's hands, cannot be traced back to any earlier time than when the governors of the Church collected, approved, and recommended them to us as the writings of those apostles and disciples whose names they bear.

The first complete list of all the books of both the Old and New Testaments appears in the Canons of the Apostles, supposedly collected by Clement, the first Bishop of Rome after St. Peter. But because that attribution is only supposed, and questioned by many, the Council of Laodicea is the first we know of that recommended the Bible to the Christian churches of the day as the writings of the prophets and apostles — and that council was held in the year 364 after Christ.

By that time, ambition had so far gotten the better of the great doctors of the Church that they no longer regarded emperors, even Christian ones, as the shepherds of the people, but as sheep — and non-Christian emperors as wolves. They worked to pass off their doctrine not as counsel and information, the way preachers should, but as laws, the way absolute governors do; and they thought pious any fraud that tended to make people more obedient to Christian doctrine. Yet even so, I am persuaded they did not falsify the Scriptures. The copies of the New Testament books were in the hands of the churchmen alone — and if they had intended to corrupt them, they would surely have made them more favorable to their own power over Christian princes and civil sovereignty than they actually are. I see no reason, therefore, to doubt that the Old and New Testaments, as we now have them, are the true registers of the things done and said by the prophets and apostles.

And the same may be true of some of the books called Apocrypha — if they were left out of the canon not because their doctrine conflicts with the rest, but only because they are not found in Hebrew. For after the conquest of Asia by Alexander the Great, there were few learned Jews who were not fluent in Greek. The seventy interpreters who turned the Bible into Greek were all Hebrews; and we still have the works of Philo and Josephus, both Jews, written by them eloquently in Greek. But in the end, it is not the writer that makes a book canonical — it is the authority of the Church.

Their Scope

Although these books were written by many different men, it is plain that the writers were all filled with one and the same Spirit, because they all work together toward one and the same end: setting forth the rights of the kingdom of God — Father, Son, and Holy Ghost.

The Book of Genesis traces the genealogy of God's people from the creation of the world to the going into Egypt. The other four books of Moses contain the election of God as their king and the laws he prescribed for their government. The books of Joshua, Judges, Ruth, and Samuel, up to the time of Saul, describe the acts of God's people until the time they cast off God's yoke and demanded a king, after the manner of their neighbor nations. The rest of the Old Testament history traces the succession of the line of David down to the captivity — the line from which would spring the restorer of the kingdom of God, our blessed Savior, God the Son, whose coming was foretold in the books of the prophets. After him, the evangelists wrote his life and actions and his claim to the kingdom during the time he lived on earth. And lastly, the Acts and the Epistles of the apostles declare the coming of God the Holy Ghost, and the authority he left with the apostles and their successors, for the direction of the Jews and the invitation of the Gentiles.

In sum: the histories and prophecies of the Old Testament, and the Gospels and Epistles of the New, have all had one and the same scope — to convert men to the obedience of God: first, in Moses and the priests; second, in the man Christ; and third, in the apostles and the successors to apostolic power. For these three, at different times, represented the person of God: Moses and his successors, the high priests and kings of Judah, in the Old Testament; Christ himself, during the time he lived on earth; and the apostles and their successors, from the day of Pentecost (when the Holy Ghost descended on them) to this day.

The Question of the Authority of the Scriptures, Properly Stated

There is a question much disputed among the various sects of the Christian religion: from where do the Scriptures derive their authority? The question is sometimes put in other terms — how do we know them to be the word of God? or, why do we believe them to be so? And the difficulty of answering it comes mainly from the sloppy wording of the question itself.

For it is believed on all hands that the first and original author of the Scriptures is God; so that is not the point in dispute. Again, it is obvious that no one can know they are God's word — though all true Christians believe it — except those to whom God himself has revealed it supernaturally; so the question is wrongly framed as a question of our knowledge. Lastly, if the question is framed as one of our belief, then — since some are moved to believe for one reason and others for other reasons — no single general answer can serve for them all.

The question, truly stated, is this: by what authority are they made law?

Their Authority and Interpretation

So far as the Scriptures do not differ from the laws of nature, there is no doubt that they are the law of God, and they carry their authority with them, legible to every man who has the use of natural reason. But this is no other authority than that of all other moral doctrine consonant with reason — whose dictates are laws not made, but eternal.

If they are made law by God himself, then they are of the nature of written law, and written laws are laws only to those to whom God has published them sufficiently — so sufficiently that no man can excuse himself by saying he did not know they were his.

Therefore, a man to whom God has not supernaturally revealed that these writings are his, nor that those who published them were sent by him, is not obliged to obey them by any authority except the authority of him whose commands already have the force of laws — that is to say, by no other authority than that of the commonwealth, residing in the sovereign, who alone has the legislative power.

Consider the alternative. If it is not the legislative authority of the commonwealth that gives the Scriptures the force of laws, it must be some other authority derived from God, either private or public.

If private, it obliges only the particular man to whom God has been pleased to reveal it. For suppose every man were obliged to accept as God's law whatever particular men, claiming private inspiration or revelation, chose to press upon him — and consider how many men there are who, out of pride and ignorance, take their own dreams, wild fancies, and madness for testimonies of God's Spirit, or who, out of ambition, claim such divine testimony falsely and against their own consciences. It would be impossible for any divine law to be acknowledged at all.

If public, then it is the authority either of the commonwealth or of the Church. But the Church, if it is one person, is the same thing as a commonwealth of Christians — called a commonwealth because it consists of men united in one person, their sovereign, and called a Church because it consists of Christian men united in one Christian sovereign. And if the Church is not one person, then it has no authority at all. It can neither command nor perform any action whatsoever; it is incapable of having any power or right to anything; it has no will, no reason, no voice — for all these qualities belong to a person. So if the whole number of Christians is not contained in one commonwealth, they are not one person, and there is no universal Church with any authority over them; and therefore the Scriptures are not made laws by a universal Church. And if Christendom is one commonwealth, then all Christian monarchs and states are private persons, subject to being judged, deposed, and punished by a universal sovereign of all Christendom.

So the question of the authority of the Scriptures comes down to this: whether Christian kings, and the sovereign assemblies in Christian commonwealths, are absolute in their own territories, immediately under God — or subject to one Vicar of Christ, set over the universal Church, to be judged, condemned, deposed, and put to death as he judges expedient or necessary for the common good.

This question cannot be resolved without a more particular consideration of the kingdom of God — and from the kingdom of God we must also judge the authority to interpret Scripture. For whoever has a lawful power over any writing, to make it law, has also the power to approve or disapprove any interpretation of it.
