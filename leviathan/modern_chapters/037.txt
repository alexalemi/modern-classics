Chapter 36: The Word of God, and Prophets

What "Word" Means

When Scripture speaks of the word of God, or the word of man, it does not mean a part of speech — what grammarians call a noun or a verb, or any single sound that signifies nothing until it is strung together with other words. It means a complete speech or discourse, by which the speaker affirms, denies, commands, promises, threatens, wishes, or asks. In this sense the right Latin term is not vocabulum — a single word — but sermo (in Greek, logos): a speech, a discourse, a saying.

Both the Words Spoken by God and the Words Concerning God Are Called God's Word in Scripture

Notice, too, that "the word of God" (or of man) can point in two different directions. Sometimes it points to the speaker — the words that God has spoken, or that a man has spoken. In this sense, when we say "the Gospel of St. Matthew," we mean that Matthew wrote it. But sometimes it points to the subject. When we read in the Bible of "the words of the days of the kings of Israel, or Judah," the meaning is that the acts done in those days were the subject of those words.

And in the Greek of the Scriptures, which keeps many Hebrew turns of phrase, "the word of God" very often means not what is spoken by God, but what is spoken concerning God and his government — that is, the doctrine of religion. So much so that logos Theou — the word of God — and theologia — theology — come to the same thing: the doctrine we usually call divinity. The following passages make this plain.

In Acts 13:46, "Then Paul and Barnabas waxed bold, and said, It was necessary that the word of God should first have been spoken to you, but seeing you put it from you, and judge yourselves unworthy of everlasting life, lo, we turn to the Gentiles." What is here called the word of God was the doctrine of the Christian religion, as the context before it makes evident. And in Acts 5:20, an angel tells the apostles, "Go stand and speak in the Temple, all the words of this life." By "the words of this life" is meant the doctrine of the Gospel — as is clear from what they actually did in the Temple, expressed in the last verse of that same chapter: "Daily in the Temple, and in every house, they ceased not to teach and preach Christ Jesus." Plainly, Jesus Christ was the subject of this "word of life" — or, what is the same thing, the subject of the words of that eternal life which our Savior offered them.

Likewise in Acts 15:7 the word of God is called the word of the Gospel, because it contains the doctrine of the kingdom of Christ. The same word is called in Romans 10:8-9 the word of faith — that is, as the passage itself spells out, the doctrine of Christ come, and raised from the dead. Again, Matthew 13:19: "When anyone heareth the word of the kingdom" — that is, the doctrine of the kingdom taught by Christ. And the same word is said in Acts 12:24 "to grow and to be multiplied" — which is easy to understand of the gospel doctrine, but hard and strange to understand of the voice or speech of God. In exactly the same sense, "the doctrine of devils" (1 Timothy 4:1) does not mean words spoken by any devil, but the doctrine of the pagans concerning demons, and those phantasms which they worshipped as gods.

Considering these two senses of the WORD OF GOD as Scripture uses it, one thing is clear: in the second sense — where it means the doctrine of the Christian religion — the whole of Scripture is the word of God. In the first sense, it is not. For example, the words "I am the Lord thy God," and so on to the end of the Ten Commandments, were spoken by God to Moses; but the preface — "God spake these words and said" — must be understood as the words of the man who wrote the holy history.

The word of God taken as that which God has spoken is itself understood sometimes properly, sometimes metaphorically. Properly: the words he has spoken to his prophets. Metaphorically: his wisdom, power, and eternal decree in making the world. In this sense those fiats of Genesis 1 — "Let there be light," "Let there be a firmament," "Let us make man" — are the word of God. In the same sense it is said in John 1:3, "All things were made by it, and without it was nothing made that was made." And Hebrews 1:3, "He upholdeth all things by the word of his power" — that is, by the power of his word; that is, by his power. And Hebrews 11:3, "The worlds were framed by the word of God" — and many other places to the same effect. The Romans used the word the same way: their name Fate properly signifies "the word spoken," and carries this very sense.

Second, for the Effect of His Word

Secondly, "the word of God" is sometimes put for the effect of his word — that is, for the thing itself which his word affirmed, commanded, threatened, or promised. So in Psalm 105:19, Joseph is said to have been kept in prison "till his word was come": that is, until the thing came to pass which he had foretold to Pharaoh's butler (Genesis 40:13) concerning his restoration to office. "His word was come" means the thing itself had come to pass. So also in 1 Kings 18:36, Elijah says to God, "I have done all these thy words," instead of "I have done all these things at thy word," or command. And Jeremiah 17:15, "Where is the word of the Lord?" is put for "Where is the evil he threatened?" And Ezekiel 12:28, "There shall none of my words be prolonged any more" — where by "words" are understood those things which God promised to his people. And in the New Testament, Matthew 24:35: "Heaven and earth shall pass away, but my words shall not pass away" — that is, there is nothing I have promised or foretold that shall not come to pass.

It is in this sense that St. John the Evangelist — and, I think, St. John only — calls our Savior himself, as he was in the flesh, the word of God. As in John 1:14, "the Word was made flesh": that is to say, the word, or promise, that Christ should come into the world. He "in the beginning was with God" — that is, it was in the purpose of God the Father to send God the Son into the world, to light men's way to eternal life; but that purpose was not put into execution, not actually made flesh, until then. So our Savior is there called "the Word" not because he was the promise, but because he was the thing promised.

Those who seize on this passage to call him "the Verb of God" only make the text more obscure. They might as well call him the Noun of God. By "noun," as by "verb," men understand nothing but a part of speech — a voice, a sound — that neither affirms, nor denies, nor commands, nor promises; it is no substance at all, bodily or spiritual, and therefore cannot be said to be either God or man. Our Savior is both.

This Word, which St. John in his Gospel says was with God, is in his first Epistle (verse 1) called "the Word of life," and in verse 2, "the eternal life, which was with the Father." So he can be called the Word in no other sense than that in which he is called eternal life — that is, "he that hath procured us eternal life" by his coming in the flesh. So also in Revelation 19:13, where the apostle, describing Christ clothed in a garment dipped in blood, says his name is "the Word of God." This is to be understood as if he had said his name was "He that was come according to the purpose of God from the beginning, and according to his word and promises delivered by the prophets." There is nothing here about the incarnation of a word, but about the incarnation of God the Son — called the Word because his incarnation was the performance of the promise. In just the same way the Holy Ghost is called "the Promise" (Acts 1:4; Luke 24:49).

Third, for the Words of Reason and Equity

There are also places in Scripture where "the word of God" signifies words that agree with reason and equity, even though they were spoken neither by a prophet nor by a holy man. Pharaoh Necho was an idolater; yet his words to the good King Josiah, sent by messengers to advise him not to oppose his march against Carchemish, are said to have proceeded from the mouth of God — and Josiah, not listening to them, was killed in the battle. You can read it in 2 Chronicles 35:21-23. It is true that the same story, as told in the first book of Esdras, has not Pharaoh but Jeremiah speaking these words to Josiah from the mouth of the Lord. But we are to credit the canonical Scripture, whatever may be written in the Apocrypha.

The word of God is likewise to be taken for the dictates of reason and equity whenever Scripture says that word is written in man's heart — as in Psalm 36:31, Jeremiah 31:33, Deuteronomy 30:11 and 14, and many similar places.

The Different Meanings of the Word "Prophet"

The name PROPHET signifies in Scripture sometimes a prolocutor — a spokesman, one who speaks from God to man, or from man to God. Sometimes it signifies a praedictor — a foreteller of things to come. And sometimes it signifies one who speaks incoherently, like a man out of his mind.

It is most frequently used in the sense of speaking from God to the people. In this sense Moses, Samuel, Elijah, Isaiah, Jeremiah, and others were prophets. In this sense the High Priest was a prophet, for he alone went into the Sanctum Sanctorum — the Holy of Holies — to inquire of God, and was to declare God's answer to the people. That is why, when Caiaphas said it was expedient that one man should die for the people, St. John says (John 11:51) that "he spake not this of himself, but being High Priest that year, he prophesied that one man should die for the nation." Those who taught the people in Christian congregations are likewise said to prophesy (1 Corinthians 14:3). And it is in this same sense that God says to Moses concerning Aaron (Exodus 4:16), "He shall be thy spokesman to the people; and he shall be to thee a mouth, and thou shalt be to him instead of God." What is "spokesman" here is in Exodus 7:1 rendered "prophet": "See," says God, "I have made thee a god to Pharaoh, and Aaron thy brother shall be thy prophet."

In the sense of speaking from man to God, Abraham is called a prophet in Genesis 20:7, where God speaks to Abimelech in a dream: "Now therefore restore the man his wife, for he is a prophet, and shall pray for thee." From this we may also gather that the name of prophet may be given, not improperly, to those in Christian churches whose calling is to say public prayers for the congregation.

In the same sense, the prophets who came down from the high place — the hill of God — with a psaltery, a tabret, a pipe, and a harp (1 Samuel 10:5-6), with Saul among them (verse 10), are said to prophesy: they were praising God, publicly, in that manner. In the same sense Miriam is called a prophetess (Exodus 15:20). And it is to be taken the same way in 1 Corinthians 11:4-5, where St. Paul speaks of "every man that prayeth or prophesieth with his head covered" and "every woman that prayeth or prophesieth with her head uncovered." Prophecy in that place means nothing more than praising God in psalms and holy songs — which women might do in church, though it was not lawful for them to speak to the congregation.

And it is in this signification that the poets of the pagans, who composed hymns and other poems in honor of their gods, were called vates — prophets — as anyone versed in the books of the gentiles knows well, and as is evident in Titus 1:12, where St. Paul says of the Cretans that "a prophet of their own said, they were liars." Not that St. Paul held their poets to be prophets; he merely acknowledges that the word "prophet" was commonly used for those who celebrated the honor of God in verse.

Predicting the Future Is Not Always Prophecy

When prophecy means prediction — the foretelling of future contingents — then the name covered not only those who were God's spokesmen and foretold to others what God had foretold to them, but also all those impostors who pretend, by the help of familiar spirits, or by superstitious divination from past events and false causes, to foretell similar events in time to come. Of these (as I have already shown in chapter 12 of this discourse) there are many kinds — men who gain more reputation for prophecy among the common sort by one chance event that can be twisted to their purpose than they can ever lose by any number of failures.

Prophecy is not an art; nor, when it is taken for prediction, is it a permanent vocation. It is an extraordinary and temporary employment from God — most often of good men, but sometimes also of the wicked. The woman of Endor, who is said to have had a familiar spirit and by it to have raised a phantasm of Samuel and foretold Saul his death, was not on that account a prophetess. She possessed no science by which she could raise such a phantasm; nor does it appear that God commanded the raising of it. God only guided that imposture to be a means of Saul's terror and discouragement — and, in consequence, of the defeat by which he fell.

As for incoherent speech: among the gentiles it was taken for one sort of prophecy, because the prophets of their oracles — intoxicated by a spirit, or vapor, from the cave of the Pythian oracle at Delphi — were for the time genuinely mad, and spoke like madmen. Out of their loose words a meaning could be made to fit any event whatsoever — the way all bodies are said to be made of materia prima, first matter, which fits anything because it is nothing in particular. And I find incoherent speech taken for prophecy in Scripture as well (1 Samuel 18:10): "And the evil spirit came upon Saul, and he prophesied in the midst of the house."

How God Has Spoken to the Prophets

Among so many significations of the word "prophet" in Scripture, the most frequent is this: one to whom God speaks immediately — directly — telling him what he is to say from God to some other man, or to the people. And here a question naturally arises: in what manner does God speak to such a prophet? Can it properly be said (some may ask) that God has voice and language, when it cannot properly be said that he has a tongue, or other organs, as a man does?

The prophet David argues, "Shall he that made the eye, not see? Or he that made the ear, not hear?" But this kind of speech is meant, not (as usually supposed) to describe God's nature, but to declare our intention to honor him. To see and to hear are honorable attributes, and may be given to God to declare — as far as our capacity can conceive it — his almighty power. But if the argument were taken in its strict and proper sense, then from God's making of all the parts of man's body one could conclude that he also uses them as we use them — and many of those uses are so unseemly that to attribute them to him would be the greatest insult in the world.

Therefore, when we read that God spoke to men immediately, we are to understand it of whatever way it may be by which God makes them understand his will. And the ways by which he does this are many, and to be sought only in Holy Scripture — where, although it is often said simply that God spoke to this or that person without declaring how, there are also many places that record the signs by which the prophets were to recognize his presence and his command. From these we may understand how he spoke to many of the rest.

To the Extraordinary Prophets of the Old Testament He Spoke by Dreams or Visions

In what manner God spoke to Adam and Eve, and to Cain, and to Noah, is not expressed; nor how he spoke to Abraham, until Abraham had come out of his own country to Sichem in the land of Canaan. Then (Genesis 12:7) God is said to have appeared to him. So there is one way by which God made his presence manifest: by an apparition, or vision. And again (Genesis 15:1), "The word of the Lord came to Abraham in a vision" — that is to say, something appeared as a sign of God's presence, as God's messenger, to speak to him.

Again, the Lord appeared to Abraham (Genesis 18:1) by an apparition of three angels; and to Abimelech (Genesis 20:3) in a dream; to Lot (Genesis 19:1) by an apparition of two angels; and to Hagar (Genesis 21:17) by the apparition of one angel; and to Abraham again (Genesis 22:11) by the apparition of a voice from heaven; and to Isaac in the night (Genesis 26:24) — that is, in his sleep, or by dream; and to Jacob in a dream (Genesis 28:12) — that is to say, as the text itself puts it, "Jacob dreamed that he saw a ladder"; and again (Genesis 32:1) in a vision of angels; and to Moses (Exodus 3:2) in the apparition of a flame of fire out of the midst of a bush. And after the time of Moses — wherever the Old Testament expresses the manner in which God spoke immediately to a man — he spoke always by a vision or by a dream: so to Gideon, Samuel, Elijah, Elisha, Isaiah, Ezekiel, and the rest of the prophets. And often in the New Testament too: to Joseph, to St. Peter, to St. Paul, and to St. John the Evangelist in the Apocalypse.

Only to Moses did he speak in a more extraordinary manner, on Mount Sinai and in the Tabernacle; and to the High Priest, in the Tabernacle and in the Holy of Holies of the Temple. Moses, and after him the High Priests, were prophets of a more eminent place and degree in God's favor; and God himself declares in express words that to other prophets he spoke in dreams and visions, but to his servant Moses in the way a man speaks to his friend. The words are these (Numbers 12:6-8): "If there be a prophet among you, I the Lord will make myself known to him in a vision, and will speak unto him in a dream. My servant Moses is not so, who is faithful in all my house; with him I will speak mouth to mouth, even apparently, not in dark speeches; and the similitude of the Lord shall he behold." And Exodus 33:11: "The Lord spake to Moses face to face, as a man speaketh to his friend."

And yet even this speaking of God to Moses was by the mediation of an angel, or angels — as appears expressly from Acts 7:35 and 53, and from Galatians 3:19 — and was therefore a vision, though a clearer vision than was given to other prophets. Consistent with this, when God says (Deuteronomy 13:1), "If there arise amongst you a prophet, or dreamer of dreams," the second phrase is only an interpretation of the first. And in Joel 2:28 — "Your sons and your daughters shall prophesy; your old men shall dream dreams, and your young men shall see visions" — the word "prophesy" is again expounded by dream and vision. It was in the same manner that God spoke to Solomon, promising him wisdom, riches, and honor; for the text says (1 Kings 3:15), "And Solomon awoke, and behold, it was a dream."

So, in general, the extraordinary prophets of the Old Testament took notice of the word of God in no other way than from their dreams or visions — that is to say, from the imaginations they had in their sleep, or in an ecstasy. In every true prophet these imaginations were supernatural; in false prophets they were either natural, or faked.

The same prophets were nevertheless said to speak "by the Spirit" — as in Zechariah 7:12, where the prophet, speaking of the Jews, says, "They made their hearts hard as adamant, lest they should hear the law, and the words which the Lord of Hosts hath sent in his Spirit by the former prophets." From this it is clear that speaking by the Spirit, or by inspiration, was not some particular manner of God's speaking, distinct from vision — not when those said to speak by the Spirit were extraordinary prophets, who for every new message needed a particular new commission, which is to say a new dream or vision.

To the Supreme Prophets of Perpetual Calling, God Spoke from the Mercy Seat, in a Manner Not Expressed in Scripture

Of prophets who held the office by a perpetual calling in the Old Testament, some were supreme and some subordinate. Supreme was first Moses, and after him the High Priests, each for his own time, as long as the priesthood was royal. And after the people of the Jews had rejected God, that he should no more reign over them, those kings who submitted themselves to God's government were also his chief prophets, and the High Priest's office became ministerial — an office of service. When God was to be consulted, the priests put on the holy vestments and inquired of the Lord as the king commanded them, and they were removed from office when the king thought fit.

For King Saul (1 Samuel 13:9) commanded the burnt offering to be brought; and (1 Samuel 14:18) he commands the priest to bring the Ark near him — and then (verse 19) to let it alone, because he saw an advantage over his enemies. And in the same chapter, Saul asks counsel of God. In like manner King David, after his anointing, though before he had possession of the kingdom, is said to "enquire of the Lord" (1 Samuel 23:2) whether he should fight the Philistines at Keilah; and (verse 10) David commands the priest to bring him the ephod, to inquire whether he should stay in Keilah or not. And King Solomon (1 Kings 2:27) took the priesthood from Abiathar and gave it (verse 35) to Zadok.

Therefore Moses, and the High Priests, and the pious kings — who inquired of God on all extraordinary occasions how they were to conduct themselves, or what outcome they should expect — were all sovereign prophets. But in what manner God spoke to them is not manifest. To say that when Moses went up to God on Mount Sinai it was a dream, or a vision, such as other prophets had, contradicts the distinction God himself drew between Moses and all other prophets (Numbers 12:6-8). To say God spoke or appeared as he is in his own nature is to deny his infiniteness, invisibility, and incomprehensibility. To say he spoke by inspiration, or by the infusion of the Holy Spirit — taking "Holy Spirit" to mean the Deity itself — is to make Moses equal with Christ, in whom alone (as St. Paul says, Colossians 2:9) the Godhead dwells bodily. And finally, to say he spoke by the Holy Spirit in the sense of the graces or gifts of the Holy Spirit is to attribute nothing supernatural to Moses at all; for God disposes men to piety, justice, mercy, truth, faith, and every sort of virtue, moral and intellectual, by doctrine, by example, and by many natural and ordinary occasions.

And as none of these ways can be applied to God's speaking to Moses at Mount Sinai, so none of them can be applied to his speaking to the High Priests from the Mercy Seat. Therefore, in what manner God spoke to those sovereign prophets of the Old Testament, whose office it was to inquire of him, is simply not intelligible. In the time of the New Testament there was no sovereign prophet but our Savior — who was both the God that spoke and the prophet to whom he spoke.

To Subordinate Prophets of Perpetual Calling, God Spoke by the Spirit

As for subordinate prophets of perpetual calling, I find no passage proving that God spoke to them supernaturally — only in the way he naturally inclines men to piety, to belief, to righteousness, and to the other virtues, as he does all other Christian men. That way works through constitution, instruction, education, and the occasions and invitations men have toward Christian virtue; yet it is truly attributed to the operation of the Spirit of God, or Holy Spirit (which in our language we call the Holy Ghost). For there is no good inclination that is not of the operation of God. But these operations are not always supernatural. So when a prophet is said to speak in the Spirit, or by the Spirit of God, we are to understand no more than this: that he speaks according to God's will as declared by the supreme prophet. For the most common acceptation of the word "spirit" is a man's intention, mind, or disposition.

In the time of Moses there were seventy men, besides himself, who prophesied in the camp of the Israelites. In what manner God spoke to them is declared in Numbers 11:25: "The Lord came down in a cloud, and spake unto Moses, and took of the Spirit that was upon him, and gave it to the seventy elders. And it came to pass, when the Spirit rested upon them, they prophesied, and did not cease."

From this, two things are manifest. First, that their prophesying to the people was subservient and subordinate to the prophesying of Moses; for God took of the Spirit of Moses to put upon them, so that they prophesied as Moses would have them — otherwise they would not have been allowed to prophesy at all. Indeed, a complaint was made against them to Moses (verse 27), and Joshua wanted Moses to forbid them — which he did not do, but said to Joshua, "Be not jealous on my behalf."

Second, that the Spirit of God in that place signifies nothing but the mind and disposition to obey and assist Moses in the administration of the government. For if it meant they had the substantial Spirit of God — the divine nature itself — breathed into them, then they had it in no less measure than Christ himself, in whom alone the Spirit of God dwelt bodily. It is meant, therefore, of the gift and grace of God that guided them to cooperate with Moses — from whom their spirit was derived. And it appears (verse 16) that they were men whom Moses himself was to appoint as elders and officers of the people; for the words are, "Gather unto me seventy men, whom thou knowest to be elders and officers of the people" — where "thou knowest" is the same as "thou appointest," or "hast appointed to be such." For we are told earlier (Exodus 18) that Moses, following the counsel of Jethro his father-in-law, appointed judges and officers over the people, men who feared God; and out of these came those seventy, whom God, by putting Moses' spirit upon them, inclined to aid Moses in the administration of the kingdom.

It is in this same sense that the Spirit of God is said (1 Samuel 16:13-14), immediately upon the anointing of David, to have come upon David and departed from Saul: God giving his graces to the man he chose to govern his people, and taking them away from the man he rejected. So "the Spirit" means an inclination to God's service — not any supernatural revelation.

God Sometimes Also Spoke by Lots

God also spoke many times by the outcome of lots — lots ordered by those he had put in authority over his people. So we read that God revealed, by the lots which Saul caused to be drawn (1 Samuel 14:43), the fault Jonathan had committed in eating a honeycomb, contrary to the oath the people had taken. And (Joshua 18:10) God divided the land of Canaan among the Israelites by "the lots that Joshua did cast before the Lord in Shiloh." In the same manner, it seems, God exposed the crime of Achan (Joshua 7:16 and following). These, then, are the ways by which God declared his will in the Old Testament.

And he used all the same ways in the New Testament. To the Virgin Mary, by a vision of an angel; to Joseph, in a dream; to Paul, on the road to Damascus, in a vision of our Savior; to Peter, in the vision of a sheet let down from heaven with all sorts of flesh, of clean and unclean beasts — and, in prison, by the vision of an angel; to all the apostles and writers of the New Testament, by the graces of his Spirit; and to the apostles again, at the choosing of Matthias in the place of Judas Iscariot, by lot.

Every Man Ought to Examine the Probability of a Claimed Prophet's Calling

All prophecy, then, supposes a vision or a dream (and these two, when they are natural, are the same thing), or some special gift of God so rarely observed in mankind that it is a thing to marvel at where it does appear. And such gifts, like the most extraordinary dreams and visions, may proceed from God not only by his supernatural and immediate operation, but also by his natural operation, through the mediation of second causes. So reason and judgment are needed to discern between natural and supernatural gifts, and between natural and supernatural visions or dreams.

Consequently, men need to be very circumspect and wary about obeying the voice of a man who, claiming to be a prophet, requires us to obey God in the way that he, in God's name, tells us is the way to happiness. For he who pretends to teach men the way to such great felicity pretends to govern them — that is, to rule and reign over them — which is a thing all men naturally desire, and which is therefore rightly suspected of ambition and imposture. Such a claim ought to be examined and tested by every man before he yields obedience to it — unless he has yielded that obedience already, in the institution of a commonwealth, as when the prophet is the civil sovereign, or is authorized by the civil sovereign.

If this examination of prophets and spirits were not permitted to every one of the people, it would have been pointless to set out the marks by which every man might distinguish between those he ought to follow and those he ought not. Such marks are set out — in Deuteronomy 13:1 and following, to know a prophet by, and in 1 John 4:1 and following, to know a spirit by. And seeing there is so much prophesying in the Old Testament, and so much preaching against prophets in the New, and ordinarily a far greater number of false prophets than of true ones, everyone must beware of obeying their directions — at his own peril.

First, that there were many more false prophets than true appears from this: when Ahab consulted four hundred prophets (1 Kings 22), they were all lying impostors, except the one man Micaiah. And a little before the time of the Captivity, the prophets were liars generally. "The prophets," says the Lord through Jeremiah (14:14), "prophesy lies in my name. I sent them not, neither have I commanded them, nor spake unto them; they prophesy to you a false vision, a thing of naught, and the deceit of their heart." So far did it go that God commanded the people, by the mouth of the prophet Jeremiah (23:16), not to obey them: "Thus saith the Lord of Hosts, hearken not unto the words of the prophets, that prophesy to you. They make you vain; they speak a vision of their own heart, and not out of the mouth of the Lord."

All Prophecy but the Sovereign Prophet's Is to Be Examined by Every Subject

In the time of the Old Testament, then, there were quarrels among the visionary prophets — one contesting with another, demanding, "When did the Spirit depart from me, to go to thee?" as between Micaiah and the rest of the four hundred — and prophets flatly calling one another liars (as in Jeremiah 14:14). And there are just such controversies in the New Testament's terms today, among the "spiritual" prophets of our own time. Every man, then as now, is bound to make use of his natural reason, and to apply to all prophecy the rules God has given us for discerning the true from the false.

Of these rules, in the Old Testament there were two: first, that the doctrine conform to what Moses, the sovereign prophet, had taught them; and second, the miraculous power of foretelling what God would bring to pass — both together, as I have already shown from Deuteronomy 13:1 and following. In the New Testament there was one mark only: the preaching of this doctrine, that JESUS IS THE CHRIST — that is, the King of the Jews promised in the Old Testament. Whoever denied that article was a false prophet, whatever miracles he might seem to work; and he who taught it was a true prophet.

For St. John (1 John 4:2 and following), speaking expressly of the means to examine spirits, whether they are of God or not, after telling them that false prophets would arise, says this: "Hereby know ye the Spirit of God. Every spirit that confesseth that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh, is of God" — that is, is approved and allowed as a prophet of God. Not that he is therefore a godly man, or one of the elect, merely because he confesses, professes, or preaches Jesus to be the Christ — but he is an avowed prophet. For God sometimes speaks by prophets whose persons he has not accepted: as he did by Balaam, and as he foretold Saul of his death by the witch of Endor. And again, in the next verse: "Every spirit that confesseth not that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh, is not of Christ. And this is the spirit of Antichrist." So the rule is complete on both sides: he is a true prophet who preaches the Messiah already come, in the person of Jesus; and he is a false one who denies him come, and looks for him in some future impostor who will falsely claim that honor — the impostor whom the apostle there properly calls Antichrist.

Every man therefore ought to consider who is the sovereign prophet — that is, who is God's viceregent on earth, holding, next under God, the authority to govern Christian men — and to take for his rule the doctrine which that sovereign prophet has, in the name of God, commanded to be taught; and by that rule to examine and test the truth of whatever doctrines pretended prophets, with miracles or without, shall at any time put forward. If he finds a doctrine contrary to that rule, he should do as those men did who came to Moses and complained that there were some prophesying in the camp whose authority to do so they doubted — leave it to the sovereign, as they left it to Moses, to uphold or to forbid them, as he shall see cause. If the sovereign disavows them, obey their voice no more; if he approves them, obey them as men to whom God has given a part of the spirit of their sovereign.

For when Christian men do not take their Christian sovereign for God's prophet, one of two things must follow. Either they must take their own dreams for the prophecy they mean to be governed by, and the swelling of their own hearts for the Spirit of God; or they must let themselves be led by some foreign prince, or by some of their fellow subjects — men who can bewitch them into rebellion by slandering the government, with no miracle to confirm their calling beyond the occasional extraordinary success, and impunity. And by this means, destroying all laws, both divine and human, they reduce all order, all government, and all society to the first chaos of violence and civil war.
