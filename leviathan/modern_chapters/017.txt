Part II: Of Commonwealth

Chapter 17: The Causes, Generation, and Definition of a Commonwealth

The Purpose of a Commonwealth: Personal Security

Why would men — who naturally love liberty, and naturally love power over others — ever agree to put themselves under restraint, the kind of restraint we see them living under in commonwealths? The final cause, the end, the whole design of it, is foresight: they can see that this is how they preserve their own lives and win a more contented life in the bargain. In other words, this is how they get themselves out of that miserable condition of war which, as I have shown, follows necessarily from the natural passions of men whenever there is no visible power to keep them all in awe — no power to bind them, through fear of punishment, to keep their covenants and observe those laws of nature I set down in chapters fourteen and fifteen.

Security Cannot Come from the Law of Nature Alone

For the laws of nature — justice, fairness, modesty, mercy, and, summing them all up, do to others as you would have done to you — are, by themselves, contrary to our natural passions. Without the terror of some power to make them be observed, they stand no chance against the passions that pull us toward partiality, pride, revenge, and the rest. And covenants, without the sword, are but words, with no strength to secure a man at all.

So despite the laws of nature — which everyone keeps when he is willing to keep them, which is to say, when he can do so safely — if no power has been set up, or none great enough for our security, then every man will rely, and may lawfully rely, on his own strength and cunning to guard himself against everyone else. And so it has been. In every place where men lived in small families, robbing and plundering one another was a trade. Far from being considered a violation of the law of nature, the greater the plunder a man took, the greater his honor. The only laws men observed in that business were the laws of honor: spare cruelty, leave men their lives and their farming tools. And what small families did then, cities and kingdoms — which are only larger families — do now. For their own security they enlarge their dominions on every pretext of danger, fear of invasion, or aid that might be given to invaders. They work as hard as they can to subdue or weaken their neighbors by open force and secret schemes; and because they have no other safeguard, they do it justly — and are remembered for it in later ages with honor.

Nor from Joining a Few Men or Families Together

Nor does joining together in small numbers give men this security. When numbers are small, a small addition on one side or the other makes such a large difference in strength that it is enough to decide the victory — and that very fact invites invasion. The multitude big enough to trust for our security is not fixed at any particular number. It is measured against the enemy we fear, and it is big enough when the enemy's advantage is not so visible and so obvious that it would decide the outcome of a war and so tempt him to attack.

Nor from a Great Multitude, Unless Directed by One Judgment

And let the multitude be as great as you please: if each man's actions are directed by his own private judgment and his own private appetites, they can expect no defense and no protection from it — not against a common enemy, and not against the injuries they do one another. Because they are divided in their opinions about how their strength should best be used, they do not help each other; they hinder each other, and through mutual opposition they grind their strength down to nothing. The result is that they are easily subdued by a very few who do agree together — and when there is no common enemy, they make war on each other over their private interests.

Here is the proof. If we could suppose that a great multitude of men would agree to observe justice and the other laws of nature without a common power to keep them all in awe, then we might as well suppose all mankind would do the same. And in that case there would be no civil government or commonwealth at all — and no need for one — because there would be peace without subjection.

And the Direction Must Be Continual

Nor is it enough — for the security men want to last their whole lives — that they be governed and directed by one judgment for a limited time only, as for one battle or one war. They may win a victory through their united effort against a foreign enemy. But afterward, when they have no common enemy left, or when the man one faction calls an enemy another faction calls a friend, the difference in their interests must necessarily dissolve them, and they fall back into a war among themselves.

Why Certain Creatures, Without Reason or Speech, Live in Society Without Any Coercive Power

It is true that certain living creatures, such as bees and ants, live sociably with one another — which is why Aristotle counts them among the political creatures — and yet they have no direction except their own particular judgments and appetites, and no speech by which one of them can tell another what it thinks would serve the common benefit. So someone may well want to know why mankind cannot do the same. To which I answer:

First, men are continually in competition for honor and dignity, and these creatures are not. Among men, therefore, that competition breeds envy and hatred, and finally war. Among them, it does not.

Second, among these creatures the common good does not differ from the private good. Each one, being naturally inclined to its own private benefit, serves the common benefit at the same time. But man — whose joy consists in comparing himself with other men — can relish nothing but what makes him stand out.

Third, these creatures, not having the use of reason as man does, do not see, and do not think they see, any fault in how their common business is managed. Whereas among men there are a great many who think themselves wiser and abler to govern the public than the rest. These men strive to reform and innovate, one this way, another that way — and so they drag the commonwealth into distraction and civil war.

Fourth, these creatures, though they have some use of voice to make their desires and feelings known to one another, lack that art of words by which some men can present good in the likeness of evil, and evil in the likeness of good — and inflate or shrink the apparent size of either — unsettling men and disturbing their peace whenever it pleases them.

Fifth, irrational creatures cannot tell the difference between injury and damage — between being wronged and merely being hurt. So as long as they are at ease, they take no offense at their fellows. Whereas man is most troublesome precisely when he is most at ease, for that is when he loves to show off his wisdom and second-guess the actions of those who govern the commonwealth.

Lastly, the agreement of these creatures is natural. The agreement of men is by covenant only — which is artificial. So it is no wonder that something more than covenant is required to make their agreement constant and lasting: a common power, to keep them in awe and to direct their actions toward the common benefit.

The Generation of a Commonwealth

There is only one way to erect such a common power — one able to defend them from invasion by foreigners and from the injuries they do one another, and so to secure them well enough that by their own industry, and by the fruits of the earth, they may feed themselves and live contentedly. That way is this: to confer all their power and strength upon one man, or upon one assembly of men, that can reduce all their wills, by majority voice, to one will.

This means appointing one man, or one assembly of men, to bear their person — to be their representative. And it means that every one of them owns and acknowledges himself to be the author of whatever the bearer of their person does, or causes to be done, in everything that concerns the common peace and safety. Every man submits his will to that man's will, and his judgment to that man's judgment.

This is more than consent. It is more than concord. It is a real unity of them all in one and the same person, made by covenant of every man with every man — as if every man said to every man: "I authorize and give up my right of governing myself to this man, or to this assembly of men, on this condition: that you give up your right to him too, and authorize all his actions in the same way."

When this is done, the multitude so united in one person is called a COMMONWEALTH — in Latin, CIVITAS. This is the generation of that great LEVIATHAN, or rather (to speak more reverently) of that Mortal God, to which we owe, under the Immortal God, our peace and defense. For by this authority, given to him by every individual man in the commonwealth, he has so much power and strength conferred on him that the terror of it enables him to shape the wills of them all — toward peace at home, and mutual aid against their enemies abroad.

The Definition of a Commonwealth

In him consists the essence of the commonwealth, which, to define it, is this: one person, of whose acts a great multitude, by mutual covenants with one another, have every one made themselves the author, so that he may use the strength and means of them all, as he thinks expedient, for their peace and common defense.

Sovereign and Subject Defined

The one who bears this person is called the SOVEREIGN, and is said to have sovereign power. Everyone besides him is his SUBJECT.

There are two ways of attaining this sovereign power. One is by natural force: as when a man makes his children submit themselves, and their children, to his government, because he can destroy them if they refuse; or when by war a man subdues his enemies to his will, granting them their lives on that condition. The other is when men agree among themselves to submit to some man, or assembly of men, voluntarily, trusting that he will protect them against all others.

This second kind may be called a political commonwealth, or a commonwealth by institution — by agreement. The first kind is a commonwealth by acquisition — by conquest. And I shall speak first of a commonwealth by agreement.
