Chapter 12: Of Religion

Religion Exists Only in Humans

Since there are no signs or fruits of religion anywhere but in human beings, there is no reason to doubt that the seed of religion is also found only in human beings. It must consist in some quality peculiar to us — or at least in some quality we possess to an eminent degree — that is not found in other living creatures.

First Seed: The Desire to Know Causes

First, it is peculiar to human nature to be inquisitive about the causes of the events we see. Some people ask more, some less, but everyone asks at least enough to be curious about the causes of their own good and bad fortune.

Second: Everything That Begins Must Have a Cause

Secondly, when we see anything that has a beginning, we naturally think it must have had a cause — something that determined it to begin exactly when it did, rather than sooner or later.

Third: Watching How One Thing Follows Another

Thirdly, consider the difference between us and the animals. A beast's whole happiness is its daily food, its comfort, and its appetites. It has little or no foresight about the future, because it does not observe or remember the order, sequence, and dependence of the things it sees. But man observes how one event is produced by another, and remembers what came before and what came after. And when he cannot be sure of the true causes of things — for the causes of good and bad fortune are mostly invisible — he supposes causes for them anyway: either causes his own imagination suggests, or causes he takes on the authority of other men, people he believes are his friends and wiser than himself.

The Natural Cause of Religion: Anxiety About the Future

The first two of these traits produce anxiety. Once a man is convinced that everything that has happened, or will happen, has a cause, it becomes impossible for him — constantly striving as he does to protect himself from the evils he fears and to obtain the goods he desires — not to be in perpetual worry about the future.

So every man, and especially the over-cautious man, is in a condition like that of Prometheus. The name Prometheus, translated, means "the prudent man." And Prometheus was chained to Mount Caucasus, a place with a vast view, where an eagle fed on his liver, devouring by day exactly as much as grew back at night. In the same way, the man who looks too far ahead, anxious about the future, has his heart gnawed at all day long by fear of death, poverty, or some other calamity. He gets no rest, no pause from his anxiety, except in sleep.

Which Makes Men Fear Invisible Powers

This perpetual fear, which accompanies mankind whenever we are ignorant of causes — as if we were standing in the dark — must have some object. And when there is nothing visible to blame for our good or bad fortune, we blame some invisible power or agent.

Perhaps this is what some of the old poets meant when they said that the gods were first created by human fear. Said of the gods — that is, of the many gods of the pagans — this is very true.

But the acknowledgment of one God — eternal, infinite, and omnipotent — can be derived more easily from the desire to know the causes of natural bodies and their various powers and operations than from fear of what might happen to us in the future. For take a man who, from some effect he sees, reasons back to its next and immediate cause, and from there to the cause of that cause, plunging himself deep into the pursuit of causes. He will arrive at last at this conclusion: that there must be — as even the heathen philosophers admitted — one First Mover. That is, one first and eternal cause of all things. And that is what people mean by the name God.

And he reaches it without ever thinking about his own fortune. Worry about one's fortune does the opposite: it inclines a man toward fear, and it distracts him from searching out the causes of other things — and so it gives occasion for inventing as many gods as there are men to invent them.

And Imagine Them to Be Bodiless

As for the matter, or substance, of these imagined invisible agents: natural thinking could arrive at no other idea than that they were made of the same stuff as the human soul — and that the human soul was made of the same stuff as what appears in a dream to someone sleeping, or in a mirror to someone awake. Not knowing that such apparitions are nothing but creations of the imagination, men took them to be real, external substances, and so called them ghosts — as the Romans called them imagines and umbrae, "images" and "shades" — and thought them spirits, that is, thin airy bodies. They imagined the invisible agents they feared to be like these ghosts, except able to appear and vanish at will.

But the opinion that such spirits were incorporeal — without bodies, immaterial — could never enter any man's mind by nature. Men can certainly put together words with contradictory meanings, like "spirit" and "incorporeal," but they can never form a mental image of anything answering to them. That is why men who, by their own reflection, arrive at the acknowledgment of one infinite, omnipotent, and eternal God prefer to confess that he is incomprehensible and above their understanding, rather than to define his nature as "incorporeal spirit" and then have to confess that their definition is unintelligible. Or if they do give him such a title, they do it not dogmatically — not intending to make the divine nature understood — but piously, to honor him with attributes as remote as possible from the grossness of visible bodies.

But Not Know How These Powers Cause Anything

Next, consider how men think these invisible agents work their effects — that is, what immediate causes they use to bring things about. Men who do not know what causing actually is (which is to say, almost all men) have no rule to guess by except this: observing and remembering what they have seen come before a similar effect on some earlier occasion — without seeing any dependence or connection at all between the earlier event and the later one. And so from similar things in the past, they expect similar things to come, and they hope for good or bad luck, superstitiously, from things that play no part whatsoever in causing it.

So the Athenians, facing their war at Lepanto, demanded another Phormio — a general who had won for them there before. The faction of Pompey, for their war in Africa, demanded another Scipio. And others have done the same on countless occasions since. In the same way men attribute their fortune to a bystander, to a lucky or unlucky place, to words spoken aloud — especially if the name of God is among them, as in charming and conjuring, the liturgy of witches. They go so far as to believe such words have the power to turn a stone into bread, bread into a man, or anything into anything.

Honoring Them the Way They Honor Men

Thirdly, as for the worship that men naturally offer to invisible powers: it can be nothing other than the same expressions of reverence they would use toward men. Gifts. Petitions. Thanks. Bowing the body. Respectful forms of address. Sober behavior. Carefully prepared words. Swearing oaths — that is, assuring one another of promises — by invoking them. Beyond that, reason suggests nothing. It leaves men either to stop there, or, for further ceremonies, to rely on those they believe wiser than themselves.

And Crediting Them With Every Extraordinary Event

Lastly, as for how these invisible powers reveal to men the things that are going to happen — especially their good or bad fortune in general, or their success in some particular undertaking — men are naturally at a loss. All they can do is guess at the future from the past, which makes them very apt not only to take chance occurrences, after one or two coincidences, as omens of the same outcome forever after, but also to believe similar predictions from other men, once they have formed a good opinion of them.

Four Things: The Natural Seeds of Religion

In these four things, then — opinion of ghosts, ignorance of secondary causes, devotion toward what men fear, and the taking of chance things for omens — consists the natural seed of religion. And because of the different imaginations, judgments, and passions of different people, this seed has grown up into ceremonies so varied that the ceremonies used by one man are, for the most part, ridiculous to another.

Cultivated Into Different Religions

For these seeds have been cultivated by two sorts of men. The first sort nourished and ordered them according to their own invention. The second sort did so by God's commandment and direction. But both sorts did it with the same purpose: to make the men who relied on them more apt for obedience, laws, peace, charity, and civil society.

So the religion of the first sort is part of human politics, and teaches part of the duty that earthly kings require of their subjects. The religion of the second sort is divine politics, and contains precepts for those who have yielded themselves as subjects in the kingdom of God. Of the first sort were all the founders of commonwealths and the lawgivers of the pagans. Of the second sort were Abraham, Moses, and our blessed Savior, through whom the laws of the kingdom of God have come down to us.

The Absurd Opinions of the Pagans

As for the part of religion that consists in opinions about the nature of invisible powers: there is almost nothing that has a name that the pagans did not, in one place or another, consider a god or a devil, or that their poets did not imagine to be animated, inhabited, or possessed by some spirit or other.

The unformed matter of the world was a god, under the name of Chaos.

The sky, the ocean, the planets, fire, earth, and the winds were so many gods.

Men, women, a bird, a crocodile, a calf, a dog, a snake, an onion, a leek — all deified. Beyond that, they filled almost every place with spirits called demons: the plains with Pan and his satyrs; the woods with fauns and nymphs; the sea with tritons and still more nymphs; every river and fountain with a ghost bearing its name, plus nymphs; every house with its lares, or household spirits; every man with his genius — his personal guardian spirit; hell with ghosts and spiritual officers like Charon, Cerberus, and the Furies; and the nighttime everywhere with larvae and lemures — ghosts of the dead — and a whole kingdom of fairies and bogeymen.

They also ascribed divinity, and built temples, to mere accidents and qualities: Time, Night, Day, Peace, Concord, Love, Contention, Virtue, Honor, Health, Rust, Fever, and the like. When they prayed for or against these things, they prayed to them — as if there were ghosts with those names hovering over their heads, dropping down or withholding the good or evil being prayed for or against.

They invoked their own wit under the name of the Muses; their own ignorance under the name of Fortune; their own lust under the name of Cupid; their own rage under the name of the Furies; their own private parts under the name of Priapus. And they blamed what happened in their sleep on incubi and succubi — demons who supposedly visited by night. In short, there was nothing a poet could introduce as a character in his poem that they did not make either a god or a devil.

The same authors of pagan religion noticed the second ground of religion — men's ignorance of causes, and their consequent readiness to credit their fortune to causes with no apparent connection to it at all. They seized the opportunity to foist upon that ignorance, in place of secondary causes, a kind of second-tier, ministerial gods: ascribing the cause of fertility to Venus; the cause of the arts to Apollo; of subtlety and craft to Mercury; of tempests and storms to Aeolus; and so on, other effects to other gods — until among the heathen there were almost as many gods as there were lines of business.

To the worship men naturally thought fitting for their gods — offerings, prayers, thanks, and the rest already named — these same pagan legislators added images, in both painting and sculpture, so that the more ignorant sort (that is to say, most people, the general run of them), thinking the gods these images represented were really contained in them, housed within them as it were, might stand in that much more fear of them. And they endowed these gods with lands, houses, officers, and revenues, set apart from all other human uses — that is, consecrated, made holy to those idols: caverns, groves, woods, mountains, and whole islands.

They attributed to these gods not only shapes — some human, some animal, some monstrous — but also the faculties and passions of men and beasts: sense, speech, sex, lust, reproduction. And not only reproduction by mixing with one another to propagate the race of gods, but also by mixing with men and women, begetting half-breed gods who were mere lodgers in heaven, like Bacchus, Hercules, and others. Besides these: anger, revenge, and the other passions of living creatures, and the actions that flow from them — fraud, theft, adultery, sodomy, and any vice that can be taken as an effect of power or a cause of pleasure; all those vices, in short, which among men are considered to be against the law rather than against honor.

Lastly, consider predictions of the future, which are naturally nothing but conjectures from past experience — and supernaturally, divine revelation. To these the same authors of pagan religion, partly on the strength of pretended experience, partly on pretended revelation, added innumerable other superstitious methods of divination, and made men believe they could discover their fortunes:

Sometimes in the ambiguous or senseless answers of the priests at Delphi, Delos, Ammon, and other famous oracles — answers made ambiguous by design, so that the oracle would be right either way, or made absurd by the intoxicating vapors of the place, which are very common in sulfurous caverns.

Sometimes in the leaves of the Sibyls — prophetesses whose prophecies, like perhaps those of Nostradamus (for the fragments now surviving seem to be inventions of later times), filled certain books held in high repute in the days of the Roman Republic.

Sometimes in the meaningless speeches of madmen, who were supposed to be possessed by a divine spirit — a possession they called enthusiasm, literally "a god within." These kinds of fortune-telling were accounted theomancy, or prophecy.

Sometimes in the position of the stars at a person's birth, which was called horoscopy and considered a branch of judicial astrology.

Sometimes in one's own hopes and fears, called thumomancy — divination by one's own spirit — or presage.

Sometimes in the predictions of witches who pretended to consult the dead — which is called necromancy, conjuring, and witchcraft, and is really nothing but sleight of hand and accomplices working a con.

Sometimes in the chance flight or feeding of birds, called augury.

Sometimes in the entrails of a sacrificed beast, which was aruspicina.

Sometimes in dreams.

Sometimes in the croaking of ravens or the chattering of birds.

Sometimes in the lines of the face, which was called metoposcopy; or by palmistry, in the lines of the hand; or in chance words overheard, called omina — omens.

Sometimes in monsters or unusual events — eclipses, comets, rare meteors, earthquakes, floods, deformed births, and the like — which they called portenta and ostenta, because they thought these things portended, or foreshowed, some great calamity to come.

Sometimes in pure lottery: heads or tails; counting holes in a sieve; dipping at random into the verses of Homer and Virgil; and innumerable other such empty notions.

So easily are men drawn to believe anything, by men who have gained their trust and who know how to take hold of their fear and their ignorance with gentleness and skill.

What the Founders of Pagan Religion Were After

And so the first founders and legislators of commonwealths among the pagans, whose aim was simply to keep the people in obedience and peace, took care everywhere to do three things.

First, to imprint on people's minds the belief that the precepts they gave concerning religion came not from their own devising but from the dictates of some god or other spirit — or else that the lawgivers themselves were beings of a higher nature than mere mortals — so that their laws might be the more easily accepted. So Numa Pompilius claimed to have received the ceremonies he instituted among the Romans from the nymph Egeria. The first king and founder of the kingdom of Peru claimed that he and his wife were children of the Sun. And Muhammad, to establish his new religion, claimed to have conferences with the Holy Ghost in the form of a dove.

Second, they took care to make it believed that the same things which were forbidden by the laws were also displeasing to the gods.

Third, to prescribe ceremonies, supplications, sacrifices, and festivals by which the anger of the gods could supposedly be appeased — and to teach that failure in war, great epidemics of sickness, earthquakes, and every man's private misery all came from the anger of the gods, and that the gods' anger came from neglecting their worship, or from forgetting or botching some required point of the ceremonies. And though among the ancient Romans a man was not forbidden to deny what the poets had written about the pains and pleasures after this life — indeed, several men of great authority and gravity in that state openly mocked such things in their speeches — still, the belief in them was always more encouraged than the contrary.

By these and similar institutions, they achieved their goal, which was the peace of the commonwealth. The common people, blaming their misfortunes on neglect or error in the ceremonies, or on their own disobedience to the laws, were that much less inclined to mutiny against their governors. And being entertained with the pomp and pastime of festivals and public games held in honor of the gods, they needed nothing but bread to keep them from discontent, grumbling, and uprising against the state. That is why the Romans, who had conquered the greatest part of the known world, had no scruple about tolerating any religion whatsoever in the city of Rome itself — unless it contained something incompatible with their civil government. Nor do we read of any religion being forbidden there except that of the Jews, who, being the peculiar kingdom of God, thought it unlawful to acknowledge subjection to any mortal king or state whatsoever. And so you see how the religion of the pagans was a part of their policy.

True Religion: The Laws of God's Kingdom Are the Same

But where God himself planted religion by supernatural revelation, he also made for himself a peculiar kingdom, and gave laws — not only of behavior toward himself, but of behavior toward one another. And so in the kingdom of God, policy and civil laws are a part of religion, and the distinction between temporal and spiritual dominion has no place there.

It is true that God is king of all the earth. Yet he may still be king of a peculiar, chosen nation. There is no more contradiction in that than in a general who commands the whole army also keeping a particular regiment or company of his own. God is king of all the earth by his power; but of his chosen people he is king by covenant. I will speak more fully of the kingdom of God, both by nature and by covenant, in a later part of this discourse, where I have assigned it its own place.

Why Religions Change

Given how religion is propagated, it is not hard to understand the causes of its dissolution back into its first seeds, or principles — which are nothing but an opinion of a deity, and of powers invisible and supernatural. That opinion can never be entirely rooted out of human nature; new religions can always be made to spring from it again, cultivated by men who have the reputation for such work.

For all organized religion is founded, at first, on the faith that a multitude places in some one person — someone they believe to be not only a wise man who labors for their happiness, but a holy man, to whom God himself condescends to declare his will supernaturally. It follows necessarily that when the people who govern a religion come to have their wisdom, their sincerity, or their love suspected — or when they cannot show any plausible token of divine revelation — the religion they want to uphold will be suspected too, and (without fear of the civil sword) contradicted and rejected.

Demanding Belief in Impossibilities

What destroys the reputation for wisdom, in anyone who founds a religion or adds to one already founded, is requiring belief in contradictions. Both halves of a contradiction cannot possibly be true. To demand belief in both is therefore evidence of ignorance — which exposes the author on that point, and discredits him in everything else he puts forward as supernatural revelation. For a man may indeed receive revelation of many things above natural reason, but of nothing against it.

Living Contrary to the Religion They Preach

What destroys the reputation for sincerity is doing or saying things that look like signs that the men who require others to believe do not believe it themselves. All such doings and sayings are therefore called scandalous — stumbling blocks that make men fall on the road of religion: injustice, cruelty, profanity, greed, and luxurious living. After all, who can believe that a man who routinely commits actions springing from any of these roots really believes there is any invisible power to be feared — the same power he uses to frighten other men over lesser faults?

What destroys the reputation for love is being caught pursuing private ends: when the belief they require of others leads, or seems to lead, to the acquisition of power, riches, rank, or secure pleasure for themselves alone, or for themselves especially. For whatever men reap personal benefit from, they are presumed to do for their own sakes and not for love of others.

The Lack of Miracles

Lastly, the only testimony men can give of a divine calling is the working of miracles, or true prophecy (which is also a miracle), or extraordinary success. So when points of religion received from men who did perform such miracles are supplemented with additions from men who cannot certify their calling with any miracle, those additions win no more belief than whatever the custom and laws of the place where the believers were raised have worked into them. For just as in natural matters men of judgment require natural signs and arguments, so in supernatural matters they require supernatural signs — which are miracles — before they consent inwardly, and from their hearts.

All these causes of the weakening of men's faith appear plainly in the following examples.

First, we have the example of the children of Israel. When Moses — who had certified his calling to them by miracles and by leading them successfully out of Egypt — was absent a mere forty days, they revolted from the worship of the true God he had commended to them, and setting up a golden calf as their god (Exodus 32:1-2), they relapsed into the idolatry of the very Egyptians from whom they had so recently been delivered. And again: after Moses, Aaron, Joshua, and the whole generation that had seen the great works of God in Israel were dead, another generation arose and served Baal (Judges 2:11). So when miracles failed, faith failed too.

Again: when the sons of Samuel, appointed by their father as judges in Beersheba, took bribes and judged unjustly (1 Samuel 8:3), the people of Israel refused to have God as their king any longer in any way different from how he was king of all other peoples — and so they cried out to Samuel to choose them a king after the manner of the nations. So when justice failed, faith failed too — to the point that they deposed their God from reigning over them.

And when the Christian religion was being planted, the oracles fell silent in every part of the Roman Empire, and the number of Christians grew wonderfully every day and in every place through the preaching of the apostles and evangelists. A great part of that success may reasonably be attributed to the contempt into which the pagan priests of that time had brought themselves by their uncleanness, their greed, and their scheming among princes.

The religion of the Church of Rome, likewise, was abolished in England and many other parts of Christendom partly for the same cause — for the failing of virtue in the pastors makes faith fail in the people — and partly from the importing of Aristotle's philosophy and doctrine into religion by the Scholastics. From that source arose so many contradictions and absurdities that the clergy earned a reputation both for ignorance and for fraudulent intentions, and inclined the people to revolt against them: either against the will of their own princes, as in France and Holland, or with their princes' consent, as in England.

Lastly, among the points the Church of Rome has declared necessary for salvation, there are so many that work so plainly to the advantage of the Pope and of his spiritual subjects living in the territories of other Christian princes, that if it were not for the mutual rivalry of those princes, they could exclude all foreign authority without war or trouble, as easily as it has been excluded in England.

For who fails to see whose benefit it serves to have it believed that a king does not hold his authority from Christ unless a bishop crowns him? That a king, if he is a priest, cannot marry? That whether a prince was born in lawful marriage must be judged by authority from Rome? That subjects may be freed from their allegiance if the court of Rome judges their king a heretic? That a king — like Chilperic of France — may be deposed by a pope (as he was by Pope Zachary) for no cause at all, and his kingdom given to one of his own subjects? That the clergy and the religious orders, in whatever country, shall be exempt from their king's jurisdiction in criminal cases? Or who fails to see who profits from the fees for private Masses, and the proceeds of Purgatory — along with other signs of private interest, enough to deaden the most lively faith, if (as I said) the civil magistrate and custom did not prop it up more than any opinion people hold of the sanctity, wisdom, or honesty of their teachers?

So I may attribute all the changes of religion in the world to one and the same cause: unpleasing priests. And not only among Catholics, but even in that church which has prided itself most on reformation.
