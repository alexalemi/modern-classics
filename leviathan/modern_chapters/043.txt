Chapter 42: Of Ecclesiastical Power
(Part 1 of 5)

To understand ECCLESIASTICAL POWER — what it is, and who holds it — we have to divide the time since our Savior's ascension into two periods: one before kings and men holding sovereign civil power converted to Christianity, the other after their conversion. For it was a long time after the ascension before any king or civil sovereign embraced the Christian religion and publicly allowed it to be taught.

The Holy Spirit That Fell on the Apostles

For the time in between, it is plain where ecclesiastical power resided: in the apostles; and after them, in those whom the apostles ordained to preach the gospel — to convert men to Christianity and to direct the converted in the way of salvation; and after these, the power was handed on again to others, whom they in turn ordained. This was done by the laying on of hands upon those ordained, which signified the giving of the Holy Spirit, the Spirit of God, to those they made ministers of God, to advance his kingdom. So the laying on of hands was nothing else but the seal of their commission to preach Christ and teach his doctrine; and the giving of the Holy Spirit by that ceremony of laying on hands was an imitation of what Moses did. For Moses used the same ceremony with his minister Joshua, as we read: "And Joshua the son of Nun was full of the spirit of wisdom; for Moses had laid his hands upon him" (Deuteronomy 34:9). Our Savior, then, gave his Spirit to the apostles between his resurrection and his ascension: first by breathing on them and saying, "Receive ye the Holy Spirit" (John 20:22); and after his ascension, by sending down upon them "a mighty wind, and cloven tongues of fire" (Acts 2:2-3) — not by the laying on of hands, just as God laid no hands on Moses. And the apostles afterward transmitted that same Spirit by the laying on of hands, as Moses did to Joshua. So it is clear from this in whom ecclesiastical power continually remained in those first times, when there was no Christian commonwealth: namely, in those who received it from the apostles by the successive laying on of hands.

The Trinity

Here we have the person of God borne now a third time. For as Moses and the high priests were God's representative in the Old Testament, and our Savior himself, as man, was during his stay on earth, so the Holy Spirit — that is to say, the apostles and their successors in the office of preaching and teaching, who had received the Holy Spirit — have represented him ever since. But a person (as I showed back in chapter 16) is the one who is represented, as often as he is represented. And therefore God, who has been represented — that is, personated — three times, may properly enough be said to be three persons, even though neither the word "person" nor the word "Trinity" is ever applied to him in the Bible. St. John does indeed say, "There are three that bear witness in heaven: the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit; and these three are one" (1 John 5:7). But this does not disagree with three persons in the proper meaning of the word "person" — which is, that which is represented by another — rather, it fits exactly. For God the Father, as represented by Moses, is one person; as represented by his Son, another person; and as represented by the apostles, and by the teachers who taught by authority derived from them, a third person. And yet every person here is the person of one and the same God.

But someone may ask here: what was it that these three bore witness to? St. John tells us (verse 11): they bear witness that "God has given us eternal life in his Son." And if it should be asked where that testimony shows itself, the answer is easy. He testified to it by the miracles he worked — first through Moses, secondly through his Son himself, and lastly through his apostles, who had received the Holy Spirit. All of these, each in their own time, represented the person of God, and either prophesied of Jesus Christ or preached him. As for the apostles, it was the very mark of apostleship, in the twelve first and great apostles, to bear witness to his resurrection — as appears expressly where St. Peter, when a new apostle was to be chosen in place of Judas Iscariot, uses these words: "Of these men which have companied with us all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out amongst us, beginning at the baptism of John, unto that same day that he was taken up from us, must one be ordained to be a witness with us of his resurrection" (Acts 1:21-22). Those words interpret the "bearing of witness" mentioned by St. John.

In the same place St. John mentions another trinity of witnesses — on earth. For he says, "There are three that bear witness in earth: the Spirit, and the water, and the blood; and these three agree in one" (verse 8). That is to say: the graces of God's Spirit, and the two sacraments, baptism and the Lord's Supper, which all agree in one testimony — to assure the consciences of believers of eternal life. Of this testimony he says, "He that believeth on the Son of man hath the witness in himself" (verse 10). In this trinity on earth, the unity is not a unity of the thing itself, for the Spirit, the water, and the blood are not the same substance, though they give the same testimony. But in the trinity of heaven, the persons are the persons of one and the same God, though represented on three different times and occasions.

To sum up: the doctrine of the Trinity, as far as it can be gathered directly from Scripture, is in substance this — that God, who is always one and the same, was the person represented by Moses; the person represented by his Son incarnate; and the person represented by the apostles. As represented by the apostles, the Holy Spirit by which they spoke is God. As represented by his Son, who was God and man, the Son is that God. As represented by Moses and the high priests, the Father — that is to say, the Father of our Lord Jesus Christ — is that God. And from this we can gather the reason why the names Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, as signifying the Godhead, are never used in the Old Testament. For they are persons — that is, they take their names from representing — and that could not happen until different men had represented God's person in ruling, or in directing under him.

Thus we see how ecclesiastical power was left by our Savior to the apostles, and how — so that they might exercise that power the better — they were endowed with the Holy Spirit. That is why the Spirit is sometimes called in the New Testament Paracletus — an assister, one called in for help — though it is commonly translated "Comforter." Let us now consider the power itself: what it was, and over whom.

Ecclesiastical Power Is Only the Power to Teach

Cardinal Bellarmine, in his third general Controversy, has handled a great many questions concerning the ecclesiastical power of the Pope of Rome, and he begins with this one: whether it ought to be monarchical, aristocratic, or democratic. All those kinds of power are sovereign and coercive. So if it should turn out that our Savior left them no coercive power at all, but only a power to proclaim the kingdom of Christ and to persuade men to submit to it; and, by precepts and good counsel, to teach those who have submitted what they must do to be received into the kingdom of God when it comes; and that the apostles and the other ministers of the gospel are our schoolmasters and not our commanders, their precepts not laws but wholesome counsel — then that whole dispute was in vain.

An Argument for This: The Power of Christ Himself

I have already shown (in the last chapter) that the kingdom of Christ is not of this world. Therefore neither can his ministers — unless they are kings — require obedience in his name. For if the supreme King does not exercise his royal power in this world, by what authority can obedience be required toward his officers? "As my Father sent me," says our Savior, "so send I you." But our Savior was sent to persuade the Jews to return to the kingdom of his Father, and to invite the Gentiles to receive it — not to reign in majesty, not even as his Father's lieutenant, until the day of judgment.

From the Name "Regeneration"

The time between the ascension and the general resurrection is called not a reigning but a regeneration — that is, a preparation of men for the second and glorious coming of Christ at the day of judgment. This appears in the words of our Savior — "You that have followed me in the regeneration, when the Son of man shall sit in the throne of his glory, you shall also sit upon twelve thrones" (Matthew 19:28) — and in the words of St. Paul: "Having your feet shod with the preparation of the gospel of peace" (Ephesians 6:15).

From Its Comparison to Fishing, Leaven, and Seed

Our Savior also compares this work to fishing — that is, to winning men to obedience not by coercion and punishment, but by persuasion. That is why he did not tell his apostles he would make them so many Nimrods, hunters of men, but fishers of men. It is compared also to leaven, to the sowing of seed, and to the multiplication of a grain of mustard seed. All of these images exclude compulsion; and consequently, in this time, there can be no actual reigning. The work of Christ's ministers is evangelization — that is, a proclamation of Christ and a preparation for his second coming, just as the evangelization of John the Baptist was a preparation for his first.

From the Nature of Faith

Again, the office of Christ's ministers in this world is to make men believe and have faith in Christ. But faith has no relation to compulsion or command, and no dependence on them at all. It rests only on certainty, or on the probability of arguments drawn from reason, or from things men believe already. Therefore the ministers of Christ in this world have no power, by that title, to punish any man for not believing, or for contradicting what they say. By the title of Christ's ministers, I say, they have no such power. But if they hold sovereign civil power by political institution, then they may indeed lawfully punish any contradiction of their laws whatsoever. And St. Paul, speaking of himself and of the other preachers of the gospel of that time, says in express words: "We have no dominion over your faith, but are helpers of your joy" (2 Corinthians 1:24).

From the Authority Christ Has Left to Civil Rulers

Another argument that the ministers of Christ in this present world have no right of commanding can be drawn from the lawful authority Christ has left to all princes, Christian and infidel alike. St. Paul says, "Children, obey your parents in all things, for this is well pleasing to the Lord" (Colossians 3:20). And again: "Servants, obey in all things your masters according to the flesh, not with eye-service, as men-pleasers, but in singleness of heart, as fearing the Lord" (verse 22). This is spoken to men whose masters were infidels — and yet they are told to obey them in all things. Again, concerning obedience to princes, exhorting us to "be subject to the higher powers," he says "that all power is ordained of God," and that we ought to be subject to them not only for fear of incurring their wrath, "but also for conscience' sake" (Romans 13:1-6). And St. Peter: "Submit yourselves to every ordinance of man, for the Lord's sake, whether it be to the king, as supreme, or unto governors, as to them that are sent by him for the punishment of evildoers, and for the praise of them that do well; for so is the will of God" (1 Peter 2:13-15). And St. Paul again: "Put men in mind to be subject to principalities and powers, and to obey magistrates" (Titus 3:1). These princes and powers, of whom St. Peter and St. Paul here speak, were all infidels. How much more, then, are we to obey those Christians whom God has ordained to hold sovereign power over us? How can we be obliged to do anything contrary to the command of the king, or other sovereign representative of the commonwealth of which we are members, and from whom we look for protection? It is therefore plain that Christ has not left his ministers in this world any authority to command other men — unless they are also endowed with civil authority.

What Christians May Do to Avoid Persecution

But someone may object: what if a king, or a senate, or some other sovereign person forbids us to believe in Christ? To this I answer that such forbidding has no effect, because belief and unbelief never follow men's commands. Faith is a gift of God, which man can neither give nor take away by promises of reward or threats of torture.

And if it is asked further — what if our lawful prince commands us to say with our tongue that we do not believe? Must we obey such a command? Profession with the tongue is only an external thing — no more than any other gesture by which we signify our obedience. A Christian who holds firmly in his heart the faith of Christ has, in that case, the same liberty the prophet Elisha allowed to Naaman the Syrian. Naaman was converted in his heart to the God of Israel, for he says: "Thy servant will henceforth offer neither burnt offering nor sacrifice unto other gods, but unto the Lord. In this thing the Lord pardon thy servant: that when my master goeth into the house of Rimmon to worship there, and he leaneth on my hand, and I bow myself in the house of Rimmon — when I bow myself in the house of Rimmon, the Lord pardon thy servant in this thing" (2 Kings 5:17). The prophet approved this, and told him, "Go in peace." Here Naaman believed in his heart; but by bowing before the idol Rimmon, he denied the true God in effect just as much as if he had done it with his lips.

But then what shall we answer to our Savior's saying, "Whosoever denies me before men, I will deny him before my Father which is in heaven"? We may say this: whatever a subject is compelled to do in obedience to his sovereign — as Naaman was — doing it not from his own mind but in deference to the laws of his country, that action is not his, but his sovereign's. Nor is it he who in that case denies Christ before men, but his governor, and the law of his country.

If any man accuses this doctrine of being contrary to true and sincere Christianity, I put a question to him. Suppose there were a subject in some Christian commonwealth who was inwardly, in his heart, of the Muslim religion, and his sovereign commanded him, on pain of death, to attend the divine service of the Christian church. Does he think that man is obliged in conscience to suffer death for that cause, rather than obey the command of his lawful prince? If he says the man ought rather to suffer death, then he authorizes every private man to disobey his prince in defense of his religion, true or false. If he says the man ought to obey, then he is allowing to himself what he denies to another — contrary to the words of our Savior, "Whatsoever you would that men should do unto you, that do ye unto them," and contrary to the law of nature, which is the indubitable, everlasting law of God: "Do not that to another, which thou wouldest not have done to thyself."

Martyrs

But what then shall we say of all the martyrs we read of in the history of the Church? Did they throw their lives away needlessly? To answer this, we have to distinguish among the persons who have been put to death for that cause. Some of them had received a calling to preach and to profess the kingdom of Christ openly; others had no such calling, and nothing more was required of them than their own faith.

The former sort, if they were put to death for bearing witness to this point — that Jesus Christ is risen from the dead — were true martyrs. For a MARTYR is (to give the word its true definition) a witness of the resurrection of Jesus the Messiah; and no one can be such a witness except those who lived alongside him on earth and saw him after he was risen. For a witness must have seen what he testifies to, or else his testimony is no good. That none but such can properly be called martyrs of Christ is plain from the words of St. Peter: "Wherefore of these men which have companied with us all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out amongst us, beginning from the baptism of John unto that same day he was taken up from us, must one be ordained to be a martyr (that is, a witness) with us of his resurrection" (Acts 1:21-22). Notice here: the one who is to be a witness of the truth of the resurrection of Christ — that is to say, of the truth of this fundamental article of the Christian religion, that Jesus was the Christ — must be some disciple who lived with him, and saw him before and after his resurrection; and consequently must be one of his original disciples. Those who came later can witness no more than that their predecessors said it. They are witnesses only of other men's testimony — second martyrs, so to speak: martyrs of Christ's witnesses.

A man who, in order to maintain every doctrine he himself draws out of the history of our Savior's life, or out of the Acts or the Epistles of the apostles — or which he believes on the authority of some private man — will oppose the laws and authority of the civil state, is very far from being a martyr of Christ, or even a martyr of his martyrs. There is one article only that earns a man so honorable a name when he dies for it, and that article is this: JESUS IS THE CHRIST — that is to say, the one who has redeemed us, and who shall come again to give us salvation and eternal life in his glorious kingdom. To die for every tenet that serves the ambition or the profit of the clergy is not required. Nor is it the death of the witness that makes the martyr, but the testimony itself; for the word means nothing else but a man who bears witness, whether he is put to death for his testimony or not.

Also, a man who has not been sent to preach this fundamental article, but takes it upon himself by his private authority — even though he is a witness, and consequently a martyr, whether a primary martyr of Christ or a secondary martyr of his apostles, disciples, or their successors — is not obliged to suffer death for that cause. Not being called to it, it is not required at his hands; nor should he complain if he loses the reward he expects from those who never set him to work. No one, therefore, can be a martyr of either the first or the second degree who has no warrant to preach Christ come in the flesh — that is to say, no one but those who are sent to the conversion of unbelievers. For no man is a witness to him who already believes, and therefore needs no witness — only to those who deny it, or doubt it, or have not heard it. Christ sent his apostles and his seventy disciples with authority to preach; he did not send everyone who believed. And he sent them to unbelievers: "I send you," he says, "as sheep amongst wolves" — not as sheep to other sheep.

The Argument from the Terms of Their Commission

Lastly, the points of their commission, as they are expressly set down in the gospel, contain — not one of them — any authority over the congregation.

To Preach

First, we have it (Matthew 10) that the twelve apostles were sent "to the lost sheep of the house of Israel" and commanded to preach "that the kingdom of God was at hand." Now preaching, in the original, is the act a crier, herald, or other officer performs when he publicly proclaims a king. But a crier has no right to command anyone. Likewise the seventy disciples are sent out "as laborers, not as lords of the harvest" (Luke 10:2), and are told to say, "The kingdom of God is come nigh unto you" (verse 9). And the kingdom meant here is not the kingdom of grace but the kingdom of glory; for they are commanded to pronounce it as a threat against the cities that will not receive them — that it shall be more tolerable on that day for Sodom than for such a city (verse 11). And our Savior tells his disciples, who were seeking priority of place, that their office was to minister, "even as the Son of man came not to be ministered unto, but to minister" (Matthew 20:28). Preachers, therefore, have not magisterial power but ministerial — they serve; they do not rule. "Be not called masters," says our Savior, "for one is your master, even Christ" (Matthew 23:10).

And to Teach

Another point of their commission is to teach all nations — as it stands in Matthew 28:19, or as St. Mark has it, "Go into all the world, and preach the gospel to every creature" (Mark 16:15). Teaching and preaching, then, are the same thing. For those who proclaim the coming of a king must also make known by what right he comes, if they expect men to submit themselves to him. So St. Paul did with the Jews of Thessalonica, when "three Sabbath days he reasoned with them out of the Scriptures, opening and alleging that Christ must needs have suffered, and risen again from the dead, and that this Jesus is Christ." But to teach out of the Old Testament that Jesus was the Christ — that is to say, the king — and that he rose from the dead, is not to say that men, once they believe it, are bound to obey those who tell them so against the laws and commands of their sovereigns. It is to say that they will do wisely to await the coming of Christ hereafter in patience and faith, with obedience to their present magistrates.

To Baptize

Another point of their commission is to baptize, "in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost." What is baptism? Dipping into water. But what is it to dip a man into water in the name of anything? The meaning of these words of baptism is this. He that is baptized is dipped, or washed, as a sign of becoming a new man and a loyal subject: to that God whose person was represented in old time by Moses and the high priests, when he reigned over the Jews; and to Jesus Christ his Son, God and man, who has redeemed us, and who shall, in his human nature, represent his Father's person in his eternal kingdom after the resurrection; and as a sign that he acknowledges the doctrine of the apostles — who, assisted by the Spirit of the Father and of the Son, were left as guides to bring us into that kingdom — to be the only sure way there. This is our promise in baptism. And the authority of earthly sovereigns is not to be put down until the day of judgment — for that is expressly affirmed by St. Paul, where he says: "As in Adam all die, so in Christ shall all be made alive. But every man in his own order: Christ the firstfruits, afterward they that are Christ's at his coming; then cometh the end, when he shall have delivered up the kingdom to God, even the Father, when he shall have put down all rule, and all authority and power" (1 Corinthians 15:22-24). So it is plain that in baptism we do not set up over ourselves another authority by which our external actions are to be governed in this life; we promise to take the doctrine of the apostles as our guide on the way to eternal life.

And to Forgive and Retain Sins

The power of remitting and retaining sins — called also the power of loosing and binding, and sometimes the KEYS OF THE KINGDOM OF HEAVEN — is a consequence of the authority to baptize, or to refuse baptism. For baptism is the sacrament of allegiance of those who are to be received into the kingdom of God — that is to say, into eternal life; that is to say, into the remission of sin. For as eternal life was lost by the committing of men's sins, so it is recovered by their remission. The end of baptism is the remission of sins. That is why St. Peter, when those converted by his sermon on the day of Pentecost asked him what they were to do, advised them to "repent, and be baptized in the name of Jesus, for the remission of sins." And therefore, since to baptize is to declare a man's reception into God's kingdom, and to refuse baptism is to declare his exclusion, it follows that the power to declare men cast out of it, or retained in it, was given to the same apostles, and to their substitutes and successors. And so, after our Savior had breathed upon them, saying, "Receive the Holy Ghost" (John 20:22), he adds in the next verse: "Whose soever sins ye remit, they are remitted unto them; and whose soever sins ye retain, they are retained."

These words do not grant an authority to forgive or retain sins simply and absolutely, as God forgives or retains them — God, who knows the heart of man and the truth of his penitence and conversion. They grant it conditionally: to the penitent. And this forgiveness, or absolution, in case the absolved man's repentance is feigned, is by that very fact made void — without any further act or sentence by the absolver — and has no effect at all toward salvation, but on the contrary toward the aggravation of his sin. Therefore the apostles and their successors are to follow only the outward marks of repentance. Where those marks appear, they have no authority to deny absolution; where they do not appear, they have no authority to absolve. The same is to be observed in baptism: the apostles had no power to deny baptism to a converted Jew or Gentile, nor to grant it to the unrepentant.

But since no man can discern the truth of another man's repentance further than by external marks, drawn from his words and actions — which are subject to hypocrisy — another question arises: who is constituted judge of those marks? And this question our Savior himself decides: "If thy brother shall trespass against thee," he says, "go and tell him his fault between thee and him alone; if he shall hear thee, thou hast gained thy brother. But if he will not hear thee, then take with thee one or two more. And if he shall neglect to hear them, tell it unto the church; let him be unto thee as a heathen man, and a publican" (Matthew 18:15-17). From this it is plain that the judgment concerning the truth of repentance belonged not to any one man, but to the Church — that is, to the assembly of the faithful, or to those who have authority to be their representative. But besides the judgment, the pronouncing of the sentence is also necessary; and this always belonged to the apostle, or to some pastor of the church, as its spokesman. Of this our Savior speaks in verse 18: "Whatsoever ye shall bind on earth shall be bound in heaven; and whatsoever ye shall loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven."

And the practice of St. Paul conforms to this, where he says: "For I verily, as absent in body, but present in spirit, have determined already, as though I were present, concerning him that hath so done this deed: in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, when ye are gathered together, and my spirit, with the power of our Lord Jesus Christ, to deliver such a one to Satan" (1 Corinthians 5:3-5) — that is to say, to cast him out of the Church, as a man whose sins are not forgiven. Paul here pronounces the sentence; but the assembly was first to hear the cause (for St. Paul was absent), and consequently to condemn him. But in the same chapter the judgment in such a case is attributed even more expressly to the assembly: "But now I have written unto you not to keep company, if any man that is called a brother be a fornicator, etc. — with such a one, no, not to eat. For what have I to do to judge them that are without? Do not ye judge them that are within?" (verses 11-12). The sentence by which a man was put out of the Church, then, was pronounced by the apostle or pastor; but the judgment on the merits of the case lay with the Church — that is to say (these being the times before the conversion of kings and of men holding sovereign authority in the commonwealth), with the assembly of the Christians dwelling in the same city. In Corinth, for instance, with the assembly of the Christians of Corinth.

Excommunication

This part of the power of the keys, by which men were thrust out of the kingdom of God, is what is called EXCOMMUNICATION; and to excommunicate is, in the original, aposunagogon poiein — to cast out of the synagogue, that is, out of the place of divine service. The word is drawn from the custom of the Jews of casting out of their synagogues anyone they considered contagious in conduct or in doctrine — just as lepers, by the law of Moses, were separated from the congregation of Israel until the priest should pronounce them clean.

The Use of Excommunication Without Civil Power

While excommunication was not yet reinforced with civil power, its whole use and effect was this: those who were not excommunicated were to avoid the company of those who were. It was not enough to regard them as heathens who had never been Christians, for with heathens one might eat and drink — which with excommunicated persons one might not do. This appears in the words of St. Paul (1 Corinthians 5:9-10), where he tells them he had formerly forbidden them to keep company with fornicators; but because that could not be done without going out of the world altogether, he restricts the ban to such fornicators and otherwise vicious persons as were of the brethren — "with such a one," he says, they ought not to keep company, "no, not to eat." And this is no more than what our Savior says: "Let him be to thee as a heathen, and as a publican" (Matthew 18:17). For publicans — tax farmers, the collectors of the commonwealth's revenue — were so hated and detested by the Jews who had to pay them that "publican" and "sinner" were taken among them for the same thing; so much so that when our Savior accepted the invitation of Zacchaeus, a publican, it was held against him as a crime, even though his purpose was to convert him. And therefore, when our Savior added "publican" to "heathen," he was forbidding them to eat with an excommunicated man.

As for keeping excommunicated men out of their synagogues, or places of assembly, the Church had no power to do it except the power of whoever owned the place, whether Christian or heathen. And because all places are, by right, in the dominion of the commonwealth, the man who had been excommunicated, no less than the man who had never been baptized, might enter them by commission from the civil magistrate — as Paul, before his conversion, entered the synagogues at Damascus (Acts 9:2) to arrest Christians, men and women, and carry them bound to Jerusalem, by commission from the high priest.

It Has No Effect on an Apostate

From this it appears that upon a Christian who turned apostate, in a place where the civil power persecuted the Church, or declined to assist it, excommunication carried nothing — neither damage in this world, nor terror. No terror, because of their unbelief; no damage, because casting them out simply returned them to the favor of the world, and in the world to come they would be no worse off than those who had never believed. The damage fell rather on the Church: casting them out provoked them to a freer exercise of their malice.

Only on the Faithful

Excommunication, therefore, had its effect only on those who believed that Jesus Christ was to come again in glory, to reign over and to judge both the living and the dead, and that he would refuse entrance into his kingdom to those whose sins were retained — that is, to those who had been excommunicated by the Church. And that is why St. Paul calls excommunication a delivering of the excommunicated person to Satan: for outside the kingdom of Christ, all other kingdoms after the judgment are comprehended in the kingdom of Satan. This is what the faithful stood in fear of, so long as they stood excommunicated — that is to say, in a state in which their sins were not forgiven. From this we can understand that excommunication, in the time when the Christian religion was not authorized by the civil power, was used only for the correction of conduct, not of errors of opinion. For it is a punishment that no one could feel except those who believed in, and expected, our Savior's coming again to judge the world; and those who so believed needed no other opinion — only uprightness of life — to be saved.

For What Faults Excommunication Applies

Excommunication applies to injustice — as in Matthew 18: if your brother offends you, tell him privately; then with witnesses; lastly, tell the church; and then, if he still does not obey, "let him be to thee as a heathen man, and a publican." And excommunication applies to a scandalous life, as in 1 Corinthians 5:11: "If any man that is called a brother be a fornicator, or covetous, or an idolater, or a drunkard, or an extortioner — with such a one ye are not to eat." But for excommunicating a man who held this foundation — that Jesus was the Christ — over a difference of opinion on other points, by which that foundation was not destroyed, there appears no authority in Scripture, and no example in the apostles.

There is, indeed, a text in St. Paul that seems to say the contrary: "A man that is a heretic, after the first and second admonition, reject" (Titus 3:10). For a HERETIC is one who, though a member of the Church, nevertheless teaches some private opinion the Church has forbidden; and such a man, St. Paul advises Titus, after a first and second admonition, to reject. But "to reject," in this place, is not to excommunicate the man. It is to give up admonishing him, to let him alone, to set aside disputing with him, as one who can only be convinced by himself. The same apostle says, "Foolish and unlearned questions avoid" (2 Timothy 2:23); and the word "avoid" in this place and "reject" in the other are the same word in the original, paraitou — but foolish questions may be set aside without excommunication. And again, "Avoid foolish questions" (Titus 3:9), where the original word, periistaso — set them by — is equivalent to the former word, "reject."

There is no other passage that can be drawn, with even a show of plausibility, to countenance the casting out of the Church of faithful men — men who believed the foundation, and differed only in some singular structure of their own built on top of it, proceeding perhaps from a good and pious conscience. On the contrary, all the passages that command the avoiding of such disputes are written as a lesson to pastors (such as Timothy and Titus were): not to make new articles of faith by settling every small controversy, which burdens men with a needless load on their consciences, or provokes them to break the union of the Church. And this lesson the apostles themselves observed well. St. Peter and St. Paul, though their controversy was great (as we may read in Galatians 2:11), did not cast one another out of the Church. Nevertheless, even in the apostles' time there were other pastors who did not observe it — such as Diotrephes (3 John 9), who cast out of the church men whom St. John himself thought fit to be received into it, out of the pride he took in preeminence. So early had vainglory and ambition found their way into the Church of Christ.
