Chapter 2: Of Imagination

When a thing is lying still, it will lie still forever unless something else moves it. Nobody doubts that. But here is the twin truth: when a thing is in motion, it will stay in motion forever unless something else stops it. The reasoning is exactly the same — nothing can change itself — yet people find this one much harder to accept.

Why? Because people measure everything, not just other people but all other things, by themselves. After moving around, they feel pain and fatigue, so they assume everything else gets tired of motion too and seeks rest of its own accord. It rarely occurs to them that the desire for rest they feel in themselves might just be some other motion. That is how the Scholastics ended up teaching that heavy bodies fall downward out of an "appetite for rest" — a longing to preserve their nature in the place that suits them best. Which means they were ascribing appetite, and knowledge of what is good for one's own preservation, to lifeless objects. That is more than even human beings have. Absurd.

So: once a body is in motion, it moves forever unless something hinders it. And whatever hinders it cannot kill the motion in an instant, but only over time, by degrees. Watch the water: even after the wind dies down, the waves keep rolling for a long while afterward. The same thing happens with the motion made in the internal parts of a man when he sees, dreams, and so on. After the object is taken away, or the eye is shut, we still retain an image of the thing we saw — dimmer than when we actually saw it, but there.

This is what the Romans called IMAGINATION, from the image made in seeing — and they applied the word, somewhat loosely, to all the other senses as well. The Greeks called it fancy — phantasia — which means "appearance," a word that fits any of the senses equally well. Imagination, then, is nothing but DECAYING SENSE. It is found in human beings, and in many other living creatures, both sleeping and waking.

Memory

The decay of sense in a person who is awake is not the dying-out of the motion made in sensing. It is the obscuring of that motion — the way the light of the sun obscures the light of the stars. The stars give off their light just as much in the daytime as at night; but among the many impressions striking our eyes, ears, and other organs, only the strongest one registers. Since the sun's light dominates, the action of the stars does not affect us.

In the same way, when an object is removed from our sight, the impression it made in us remains. But other, more present objects come crowding in after it and work on us, so the imagination of the past thing grows obscured and weak — like a single voice in the noise of the day. It follows that the more time passes after seeing or sensing something, the weaker the imagination of it becomes. The continual change of the human body eventually destroys the very parts that were set in motion during sensing.

So distance in time and distance in space have one and the same effect on us. At a great distance in space, whatever we look at appears dim, the small details blur, and voices grow faint and indistinct. Just so, after a great distance in time, our imagination of the past is weak: of cities we once visited, we lose many particular streets; of events, many particular details.

This decaying sense goes by two names, depending on what we want to emphasize. When we mean the thing itself — the image, the imagination itself — we call it imagination, as I said before. But when we mean the decay, signaling that the sense is fading, old, and past, we call it MEMORY. So imagination and memory are one and the same thing, given different names for different purposes.

Much memory — memory of many things — is called EXPERIENCE.

Now, imagination is only of things that were previously perceived by sense, whether all at once or in parts at different times. This gives us two kinds. The first, imagining a whole object as it was presented to the senses, is SIMPLE imagination — as when you picture a man, or a horse, that you have seen before. The second is COMPOUND imagination — as when, from seeing a man at one time and a horse at another, we put together in our mind a centaur. The same thing happens when a man combines the image of his own person with the image of another man's deeds — when he imagines himself a Hercules or an Alexander, which happens often to people who read too many romance novels. That is compound imagination, and properly speaking, it is nothing but a fiction of the mind.

There are also other imaginations that arise in people while wide awake, caused by an unusually strong impression on the senses. Stare at the sun, and the impression leaves an image of the sun before your eyes long afterward. Concentrate long and intensely on geometrical figures, and even awake in the dark you will see images of lines and angles before your eyes. This kind of imagination has no particular name, because it is not the sort of thing people usually talk about.

Dreams

The imaginations of those who sleep are what we call DREAMS. And these too, like all other imaginations, were in the senses first — either whole or in pieces. Now, in sleep, the brain and nerves — the necessary organs of sense — are so numbed that external objects cannot easily move them. So in sleep, no imagination can occur, and therefore no dream, except what comes from the agitation of the inner parts of a man's own body. Those inner parts are connected to the brain and the other organs, and when they are disturbed, they keep the brain in motion — and so the imaginations formerly made there appear as if the man were awake.

But here is the difference. Since the organs of sense are numbed, no new object can come in to overpower and obscure those images with a stronger impression. So a dream is necessarily more vivid, in this silence of the senses, than our waking thoughts are. That is why it is so hard — many people think it impossible — to tell sense and dreaming apart with certainty.

For my own part, I am satisfied on this point, and here is my reasoning. In dreams I do not often or consistently think of the same persons, places, objects, and actions that I think of while awake. Nor do I remember as long a chain of coherent thoughts while dreaming as I do at other times. And while awake I often notice how absurd my dreams were, but I never dream about the absurdities of my waking thoughts. So I am confident that when I am awake, I know I am not dreaming — even though when I dream, I think I am awake.

Since dreams are caused by disturbances in some of the body's inner parts, different disturbances must produce different dreams. Lying cold breeds dreams of fear, and raises up the thought and image of some frightening object — for the motion runs both ways, from the brain to the inner parts and from the inner parts to the brain. Likewise, just as anger heats certain parts of the body when we are awake, so when we sleep, the overheating of those same parts causes anger, and raises up in the brain the imagination of an enemy. In the same way, natural affection, when we are awake, causes desire, and desire produces heat in certain other parts of the body — so too much heat in those parts while we sleep raises up in the brain the imagination of affection shown to us. In short, our dreams are our waking imaginations run in reverse: the motion, when we are awake, begins at one end; when we dream, at the other.

Apparitions and Visions

The hardest time to tell a dream from waking thoughts is when, by some accident, we fail to notice that we have slept. This happens easily to a man full of fearful thoughts, with a troubled conscience, who falls asleep without the usual signposts — going to bed, undressing — say, a man who nods off in a chair. Someone who deliberately and carefully settles himself down to sleep will, if some bizarre, extravagant imagination comes to him, have no trouble recognizing it as a dream.

Take the famous case of Marcus Brutus — the man whose life Julius Caesar spared, who was Caesar's favorite, and who murdered him anyway. The story goes that at Philippi, on the night before he gave battle to Augustus Caesar, Brutus saw a terrifying apparition. Historians usually report it as a vision. But consider the circumstances, and you can easily judge it to have been nothing but a short dream. Sitting in his tent, brooding, haunted by the horror of his rash act, slumbering in the cold — it was not hard for him to dream of exactly what frightened him most. And as the fear gradually woke him, the apparition would just as gradually have faded. Since he had no assurance that he had been asleep, he had no reason to think it a dream rather than a vision.

This is not a rare occurrence. Even people who are fully awake — if they are timid and superstitious, their heads full of frightening tales, alone in the dark — are subject to the same imaginations, and believe they see spirits and the ghosts of dead men walking in churchyards. What they see is either their own imagination, or else the trickery of certain persons who exploit such superstitious fear in order to pass through the night in disguise, to places they would rather not be known to visit.

From this ignorance — not knowing how to distinguish dreams and other strong imaginations from vision and real sense — arose the greater part of the religion of the pagans of the past, who worshipped satyrs, fauns, nymphs, and the like. And from the same ignorance comes the opinion that uneducated people hold today about fairies, ghosts, and goblins, and about the power of witches.

As for witches: I do not think their witchcraft is any real power. Yet I think they are justly punished — for the false belief that they can do such harm, joined with the intention to do it if they could. Their trade is closer to a new religion than to any craft or science. As for fairies and walking ghosts, I think the belief in them has been deliberately taught, or at least deliberately left unrefuted, in order to keep up the market for exorcisms, crosses, holy water, and other such inventions of "ghostly men" — the clergy.

Now, there is no doubt that God can produce supernatural apparitions. But it is no article of Christian faith that he does so as often as people fear such things — any more than they fear his stopping or changing the course of nature, which he can also do. Wicked men, under the cover of "God can do anything," are bold enough to say anything when it serves their purposes, even when they know it to be untrue. The wise man's part is to believe them no further than right reason makes what they say appear credible.

If this superstitious fear of spirits were swept away — and with it, fortune-telling from dreams, false prophecies, and the many other things that depend on them, by which crafty, ambitious people prey on the simple — men would be far better fitted than they are for civil obedience.

This ought to be the work of the universities. Instead, they nourish such doctrine. Not knowing what imagination or the senses actually are, they simply teach whatever they received. Some say that imaginations arise by themselves and have no cause. Others, that they arise most commonly from the will, and that good thoughts are blown ("inspired") into a man by God, and evil thoughts by the Devil — or that good thoughts are poured ("infused") into a man by God, and evil ones by the Devil. Some say the senses receive the "species" of things and deliver them to the "common sense," and the common sense hands them over to the fancy, and the fancy to the memory, and the memory to the judgment — like passing parcels from hand to hand, with many words and nothing understood.

Understanding

The imagination that is raised in a man — or in any other creature equipped with the faculty of imagining — by words or other voluntary signs, is what we generally call UNDERSTANDING. It is common to man and beast: a dog will, by habit, understand the call or the scolding of his master, and so will many other animals. But the understanding that is unique to man is the understanding not only of another's will, but of his conceptions and thoughts — grasped through the sequence and arrangement of the names of things into affirmations, negations, and other forms of speech. Of this kind of understanding I will speak later.
