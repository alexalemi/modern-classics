Chapter 42: Of Ecclesiastical Power
(Part 4 of 5)

The Civil Sovereign, If a Christian, Is Head of the Church in His Own Dominions

From this consolidation of political right and ecclesiastical right in Christian sovereigns, one thing is evident: they have every kind of power over their subjects that can possibly be given to a man for governing people's external actions, in politics and in religion alike. They may make whatever laws they judge best for governing their own subjects — both as those subjects make up the commonwealth and as they make up the church. For state and church are the same men.

So if they please, Christian sovereigns may do what many Christian kings now do: hand the government of their subjects in matters of religion over to the Pope. But in that case the Pope is, on that point, subordinate to them. He exercises that charge inside another's dominion jure civili — by civil right, the right of the civil sovereign — not jure divino — by God's right. And he may therefore be dismissed from that office whenever the sovereign judges it necessary for the good of his subjects.

They may also, if they please, commit the care of religion to one supreme pastor, or to an assembly of pastors, and give them whatever power over the church — or over one another — they think most convenient, and whatever titles of honor they like: bishops, archbishops, priests, or presbyters. They may make whatever laws they please for the clergy's upkeep, whether by tithes or by other means — provided they do it out of a sincere conscience, of which God alone is the judge.

It is the civil sovereign who appoints the judges and interpreters of canonical Scripture, for it is he who makes them laws. It is he, too, who gives force to excommunication — which, without laws and punishments to humble obstinate freethinkers and bring them back into union with the rest of the church, would simply be shrugged off.

In sum: the sovereign holds the supreme power in all causes, ecclesiastical as well as civil, as far as actions and words are concerned. For only actions and words can be known, and only they can be accused. As for what cannot be accused, there is no judge at all but God, who alone knows the heart.

And these rights belong to all sovereigns, whether monarchs or assemblies. For those who represent a Christian people represent the church — because a church and a commonwealth of Christian people are the same thing.

Cardinal Bellarmine's Books De Summo Pontifice Considered

Now, what I have said here and elsewhere in this book seems to me clear enough to establish that supreme ecclesiastical power belongs to Christian sovereigns. But the Pope of Rome's claim to that power over all the world has been defended — chiefly, and I think as strongly as it can be defended — by Cardinal Bellarmine, in his treatise De Summo Pontifice — "On the Supreme Pontiff." So I have thought it necessary to examine, as briefly as I can, the grounds and the strength of his argument.

The First Book

He wrote five books on this subject. The first takes up three questions. First: which is simply the best government — monarchy, aristocracy, or democracy? He concludes: none of them, but rather a government mixed of all three. Second: which of these is the best government for the church? He concludes: the mixed kind again, but with the largest share of monarchy in it. Third: in this mixed monarchy, did St. Peter hold the place of the monarch?

As for his first conclusion, I have already sufficiently proved (chapter 18) that all governments which men are bound to obey are simple and absolute. In a monarchy there is only one man supreme; everyone else who holds any kind of power in the state holds it by his commission, during his pleasure, and exercises it in his name. In an aristocracy or a democracy there is only one supreme assembly, with the very same power that belongs to the monarch in a monarchy. That is not a mixed sovereignty but an absolute one.

And as for which of the three forms is best — that is not a question to be debated anywhere one of them is already established. The present government always ought to be preferred, maintained, and counted the best, because it is against both the law of nature and God's positive law to do anything tending to its overthrow.

Besides, the question of which government is best contributes nothing to the power of any pastor (unless he also holds the civil sovereignty). For a pastor's calling is not to govern men by command, but to teach them and persuade them by arguments, and then leave it to them to consider whether they will embrace or reject the doctrine taught. Monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy mark out for us three sorts of sovereigns — not three sorts of pastors. Or, as we might put it: three sorts of masters of families, not three sorts of schoolmasters for their children.

For the same reason, his second conclusion — about the best form of government for the church — has nothing to do with the question of the Pope's power outside his own dominions. For in every other commonwealth, his power (if he has any at all) is only the power of the schoolmaster, not of the master of the family.

As for the third conclusion — that St. Peter was monarch of the church — his chief argument is the passage in Matthew (Matthew 16:18-19): "Thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my Church... And I will give thee the keys of Heaven; whatsoever thou shalt bind on Earth shall be bound in Heaven, and whatsoever thou shalt loose on Earth shall be loosed in Heaven."

Considered carefully, this passage proves no more than this: that the Church of Christ has one single article for its foundation — namely the article which Peter, professing it in the name of all the Apostles, gave our Savior the occasion to speak the words quoted here. To understand this clearly, consider that our Savior — in his own person, through John the Baptist, and through his Apostles — preached nothing but this one article of faith: that Jesus is the Christ. Every other article requires faith only insofar as it rests on that one.

John began it first (Matthew 3:2), preaching only this: "The kingdom of God is at hand." Then our Savior himself preached the same (Matthew 4:17). And when he gave his twelve Apostles their commission (Matthew 10:7), there is no mention of preaching any other article but that. This was the fundamental article — the foundation of the church's faith.

Afterward, when the Apostles had returned to him, he asked them all (Matthew 16:13) — not Peter only — who men said he was. They answered that some said John the Baptist, some Elijah, others Jeremiah or one of the prophets. Then (verse 15) he asked them all again — not Peter only — "Whom say ye that I am?" And Peter answered, for them all: "Thou art Christ, the Son of the living God." That, as I said, is the foundation of the faith of the whole church. And from it our Savior takes the occasion to say, "Upon this stone I will build my Church." Which makes it plain that by the foundation-stone of the church was meant the fundamental article of the church's faith.

But then why, someone will object, does our Savior slip in the words "Thou art Peter"? If the original of this text had been strictly translated, the reason would be obvious. The Apostle Simon was surnamed Stone — that is what the Syriac word Cephas means, and the Greek word Petrus too. So after Peter confessed that fundamental article, our Savior, playing on his name, said — as if it were in English — "Thou art Stone, and upon this stone I will build my Church." Which is as much as to say: this article, that "I am the Christ," is the foundation of all the faith I require in those who are to be members of my church. There is nothing unusual about punning on a name in ordinary speech. But it would have been a strange and obscure way of talking if our Savior, intending to build his church on the person of St. Peter, had said "thou art a stone, and upon this stone I will build my Church" — when he could so easily and unambiguously have said "I will build my Church on thee," and kept the very same play on the name.

As for the words that follow — "I will give thee the keys of Heaven," and so on — this is no more than what our Savior also gave to all the rest of his disciples (Matthew 18:18): "Whatsoever ye shall bind on Earth shall be bound in Heaven, and whatsoever ye shall loose on Earth shall be loosed in Heaven." But however the passage is interpreted, there is no doubt the power granted here belongs to all supreme pastors — and that is what all Christian civil sovereigns are, in their own dominions. So much so that if St. Peter, or our Savior himself, had converted one of those sovereigns to believe in him and acknowledge his kingdom, then — because his kingdom is not of this world — he would have left the supreme care of converting that sovereign's subjects to no one but the sovereign himself. Otherwise he would have had to strip him of the sovereignty, to which the right of teaching is inseparably attached.

And that is enough in refutation of his first book, in which he tries to prove St. Peter was the universal monarch of the church — that is, of all the Christians in the world.

The Second Book

The second book has two conclusions: one, that St. Peter was Bishop of Rome and died there; the other, that the Popes of Rome are his successors. Others have disputed both points. But suppose them both true. Even so, if by "Bishop of Rome" we mean either the monarch of the church or its supreme pastor, then that bishop was not Sylvester but Constantine — the first Christian emperor. And as with Constantine, so with all the other Christian emperors: they were by right the supreme bishops of the Roman Empire. I say of the Roman Empire — not of all Christendom. For other Christian sovereigns held the same right in their own territories, as an office essentially attached to their sovereignty. And that will serve as answer to his second book.

The Third Book

In the third book he takes up the question of whether the Pope is Antichrist. For my part, I see no argument that proves he is — not in the sense in which Scripture uses the name. Nor will I draw any argument from the character of Antichrist to contradict the authority the Pope exercises, or has in the past exercised, in the dominions of any other prince or state.

It is evident that the prophets of the Old Testament foretold, and the Jews expected, a Messiah — that is, a Christ — who would re-establish among them the kingdom of God, which they had rejected in the time of Samuel, when they demanded a king after the manner of the other nations. This expectation of theirs left them exposed to the imposture of anyone who had both the ambition to attempt the kingdom and the skill to deceive the people — by counterfeit miracles, by a hypocritical life, or by plausible speeches and doctrine. So our Savior and his Apostles warned men against false prophets and false Christs.

False Christs are those who pretend to be the Christ but are not. They are properly called Antichrists, in the same sense in which, when a schism in the church produces the election of two Popes, each calls the other Antipapa — the false Pope. So Antichrist, in the proper meaning of the word, has two essential marks. One: he denies that Jesus is the Christ. The other: he professes himself to be the Christ. The first mark is set down by St. John (1 John 4:3): "Every spirit that confesseth not that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh is not of God; and this is the spirit of Antichrist." The other mark is expressed in the words of our Savior (Matthew 24:5): "Many shall come in my name, saying, I am Christ" — and again, "If any man shall say unto you, Lo, here is Christ, there is Christ, believe it not." So Antichrist must be a false Christ — one of those who pretend that they themselves are Christ. And from these two marks — denying that Jesus is the Christ, and affirming oneself to be the Christ — it follows that he must also be an adversary of the true Christ, which is another usual meaning of the word Antichrist.

Now, among these many Antichrists there is one special one: O Antichristos — THE Antichrist, definitely, one particular person; not indefinitely, an Antichrist. And seeing that the Pope of Rome neither claims to be the Christ nor denies that Jesus is the Christ, I do not see how he can be called Antichrist. That word does not mean someone who falsely pretends to be Christ's lieutenant or vicar-general — it means someone who pretends to be Christ himself.

There is also a mark of the time of this special Antichrist (Matthew 24:15): when that abominable destroyer spoken of by Daniel (Daniel 9:27) shall stand in the holy place, and there shall be such tribulation as has not been since the beginning of the world, nor ever shall be again — so severe that if it were to last long (verse 22), "no flesh could be saved; but for the elect's sake those days shall be shortened" — made fewer. But that tribulation has not yet come. For it is to be followed immediately (verse 29) by a darkening of the sun and moon, a falling of the stars, a shaking of the heavens, and the glorious coming again of our Savior in the clouds. Therefore The Antichrist has not yet come — whereas many Popes have both come and gone.

It is true that the Pope, in taking upon himself to give laws to all Christian kings and nations, usurps a kingdom in this world which Christ did not take on himself. But he does it not as Christ — he does it as standing in for Christ. And in that there is nothing of the Antichrist.

The Fourth Book

In the fourth book, to prove that the Pope is the supreme judge in all questions of faith and manners — which amounts to being the absolute monarch of all the Christians in the world — he brings three propositions: first, that his judgments are infallible; second, that he can make laws, and punish those who do not observe them; third, that our Savior conferred all ecclesiastical jurisdiction on the Pope of Rome.

Texts for the Infallibility of the Pope's Judgment in Points of Faith

For the infallibility of his judgments, he cites the Scriptures — and first, Luke 22:31: "Simon, Simon, Satan hath desired you, that he may sift you as wheat; but I have prayed for thee, that thy faith fail not; and when thou art converted, strengthen thy brethren." According to Bellarmine's exposition, Christ here gave Simon Peter two privileges: one, that his faith should never fail; the other, that neither he nor any of his successors should ever define any point of faith or manners wrongly, or contrary to a former Pope's definition. Which is a strange — a very strained — interpretation.

But whoever reads that chapter with attention will find there is no passage in the whole of Scripture that makes more against the Pope's authority than this very one. Here is the scene. The priests and scribes were seeking to kill our Savior at the Passover, and Judas was already resolved to betray him. The day for killing the Passover lamb having come, our Savior celebrated it with his Apostles — saying he would do so no more until the kingdom of God was come — and told them, besides, that one of them was to betray him. At that, they fell to questioning which of them it would be. And then — since the next Passover their Master celebrated would be when he was king — they slid into a quarrel over who would then be the greater man.

So our Savior told them: the kings of the nations have dominion over their subjects, and are called by a name that (in Hebrew) means Bountiful; but I cannot be that to you. You must strive to serve one another. I ordain you a kingdom — but it is such a kingdom as my Father has ordained me: a kingdom I am now about to purchase with my blood, and shall not possess until my second coming. Then you shall eat and drink at my table, and sit on thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel. And then, turning to St. Peter, he says: Simon, Simon, Satan seeks — by dangling present domination before you — to weaken your faith in the future; but I have prayed for thee, that thy faith shall not fail. Thou therefore (note this), once converted, and understanding my kingdom as a kingdom of another world, confirm that same faith in thy brethren. To which St. Peter answered — as a man who no longer expected any authority in this world — "Lord, I am ready to go with thee, not only to prison, but to death."

From this it is plain that St. Peter was not only given no jurisdiction in this world — he was given a charge to teach all the other Apostles that they should have none either. And as for the infallibility of St. Peter's definitive sentence in matters of faith, no more can be drawn from this text than that Peter would continue in the belief of this one point: that Christ would come again, and possess the kingdom at the day of judgment. The text did not give that to all his successors — for we see they claim the kingdom in the world that now is.

The second passage is Matthew 16: "Thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my Church, and the gates of Hell shall not prevail against it." By this — as I have already shown in this chapter — no more is proved than that the gates of hell shall not prevail against the confession of Peter, which gave occasion to that speech: namely, that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God.

The third text is John 21:16-17: "Feed my sheep." This contains no more than a commission of teaching. And if we grant that the rest of the Apostles are included in that word "sheep," then it is the supreme power of teaching — but only for the time when there were no Christian sovereigns already in possession of that supremacy. I have already proved that Christian sovereigns are the supreme pastors in their own dominions, instituted to that office by virtue of their being baptized — and without any other laying-on of hands. For such imposition of hands, being a ceremony for designating the person, is needless when the person is already designated to the power of teaching whatever doctrine he will, by his institution to absolute power over his subjects. As I have proved before, sovereigns are supreme teachers in general by their office, and therefore by their baptism they oblige themselves to teach the doctrine of Christ. And when they let others teach their people, they do it at the peril of their own souls — for it is at the hands of the heads of families that God will demand the account of how his children and servants were instructed. It is of Abraham himself, not of some hireling, that God says (Genesis 18:19), "I know him, that he will command his children and his household after him, that they keep the way of the Lord, and do justice and judgment."

The fourth passage is Exodus 28:30: "Thou shalt put in the breastplate of judgment the Urim and the Thummim" — which, he says, the Septuagint interprets as delosin kai aletheian, that is, "evidence and truth." From this he concludes that God had given evidence and truth — which is nearly infallibility — to the High Priest. Very well: whether what was given was evidence and truth itself, or merely an admonition to the priest to inform himself clearly and judge uprightly — either way, what was given to the High Priest was given to the civil sovereign. For in the commonwealth of Israel, the High Priest stood next under God. So this is actually an argument for evidence and truth — that is, for the ecclesiastical supremacy of civil sovereigns over their own subjects — and against the pretended power of the Pope.

These are all the texts he brings for the infallibility of the Pope's judgment in points of faith.

Texts for the Same in Point of Manners

For the infallibility of his judgment concerning manners, he brings one text: John 16:13, "When the Spirit of truth is come, he will lead you into all truth" — where, says Bellarmine, "all truth" means at least all truth necessary to salvation. But with that softening, he attributes no more infallibility to the Pope than to any man who professes Christianity and is not to be damned. For if any man errs on a point where not erring is necessary to salvation, it is impossible he should be saved — since "necessary to salvation" means precisely that without which being saved is impossible. What those points are, I shall set out from Scripture in the chapter that follows. Here I will say only this: even granting that the Pope could not possibly teach any error at all, that still would not entitle him to any jurisdiction in the dominions of another prince — unless we are also prepared to say that a man is bound in conscience to hire the best workman for every job, even when he has already promised the work to someone else.

Besides the text, he argues from reason, like this: if the Pope could err in things necessary, then Christ has not sufficiently provided for the church's salvation, because he has commanded the church to follow the Pope's directions. But this reasoning is worthless unless he can show when and where Christ commanded any such thing — or took any notice of a Pope at all. No: even granting that whatever was given to St. Peter was given to the Pope, still, since there is in Scripture no command to any man to obey St. Peter, no man can be acting justly who obeys him when his commands run contrary to those of his lawful sovereign.

Lastly, it has never been declared — not by the church, not by the Pope himself — that he is the civil sovereign of all the Christians in the world. Therefore all Christians are not bound to acknowledge his jurisdiction in point of manners. For civil sovereignty and supreme judicature in controversies of manners are the same thing. And the makers of civil laws are not merely the declarers but the makers of the justice and injustice of actions — for there is nothing in men's manners that makes them righteous or unrighteous except their conformity with the law of the sovereign. So when the Pope claims supremacy in controversies of manners, he is teaching men to disobey the civil sovereign — which is an erroneous doctrine, contrary to the many precepts of our Savior and his Apostles delivered to us in Scripture.

To prove the Pope has power to make laws, he cites many passages. First, Deuteronomy 17:12: "The man that will do presumptuously, and will not hearken unto the priest (that standeth to minister there before the Lord thy God, or unto the judge), even that man shall die, and thou shalt put away the evil from Israel." For the answer to this, remember that the High Priest — next and immediately under God — was the civil sovereign, and all judges were to be appointed by him. The words cited therefore amount to this: "The man who presumes to disobey the civil sovereign for the time being, or any of his officers in the execution of their offices, that man shall die," and so on. Which is clearly for civil sovereignty, and against the universal power of the Pope.

Second, he cites Matthew 16, "Whatsoever ye shall bind," and interprets that binding as the kind attributed (Matthew 23:4) to the scribes and Pharisees — "They bind heavy burdens, and grievous to be borne, and lay them on men's shoulders" — by which, he says, is meant the making of laws; and he concludes from this that the Pope can make laws. But this, too, argues only for the legislative power of civil sovereigns. For the scribes and Pharisees sat in Moses' chair — and Moses, next under God, was sovereign of the people of Israel. That is why our Savior commanded the people to do everything the scribes and Pharisees said, but not everything they did: obey their laws, but do not follow their example.

The third passage is John 21:16, "Feed my sheep" — which is not a power to make laws, but a command to teach. Making laws belongs to the lord of the family, who chooses, at his own discretion, his chaplain — and likewise a schoolmaster to teach his children.

The fourth passage, John 20:21, actually works against him. The words are: "As my Father sent me, so send I you." But our Savior was sent to redeem, by his death, such as should believe, and — by his own preaching and his Apostles' — to prepare them for their entrance into his kingdom. That kingdom, he himself says, is not of this world; he taught us to pray for its coming hereafter, though he declined (Acts 1:6-7) to tell his Apostles when it would come; and in that kingdom, when it comes, the twelve Apostles shall sit on twelve thrones — every one, perhaps, as high as St. Peter's — to judge the twelve tribes of Israel. Since, then, God the Father did not send our Savior to make laws in this present world, we may conclude from the text that neither did our Savior send St. Peter to make laws here. He sent him to persuade men to await his second coming with a steadfast faith — and in the meantime, if they were subjects, to obey their princes; and if they were princes, both to believe it themselves and to do their best to make their subjects do the same. Which is the office of a bishop. So this passage argues most strongly for joining the ecclesiastical supremacy to the civil sovereignty — the exact opposite of what Cardinal Bellarmine cites it for.

The fifth passage is Acts 15:28: "It hath seemed good to the Holy Spirit, and to us, to lay upon you no greater burden than these necessary things: that ye abstain from meats offered to idols, and from blood, and from things strangled, and from fornication." Here he seizes on the phrase "laying of burdens" as the legislative power. But who can read this text and say that the Apostles' style here could not just as properly be used in giving counsel as in making laws? The style of a law is "We command." But "We think good" is the ordinary style of those who merely give advice — and advisers do lay a burden of sorts, though a conditional one: binding only if those who receive the advice want to attain their ends. And that is exactly the kind of burden this is — abstaining from things strangled and from blood: not absolute, but binding only in case they do not wish to err. I have shown before (chapter 25) that law is distinguished from counsel in this: the reason of a law is drawn from the design and benefit of the one who prescribes it, but the reason of a counsel from the design and benefit of the one to whom the counsel is given. And here the Apostles aim only at the benefit of the converted Gentiles — namely their salvation — not at their own benefit; for having done their part, they shall have their reward whether they are obeyed or not. Therefore the acts of this council were not laws, but counsels.

The sixth passage is Romans 13: "Let every soul be subject to the higher powers, for there is no power but of God" — which, he says, is meant not only of secular princes but of ecclesiastical ones too. To which I answer, first, that there are no ecclesiastical princes except those who are also civil sovereigns — and their principalities do not extend one inch beyond the bounds of their civil sovereignty. Outside those bounds, they may be received as teachers, but they cannot be acknowledged as princes. For if the Apostle had meant that we should be subject both to our own princes and to the Pope, he would have been teaching us a doctrine Christ himself has told us is impossible — namely, "to serve two masters." And though the Apostle says in another place, "I write these things being absent, lest being present I should use sharpness, according to the power which the Lord hath given me," this does not mean he claimed a power to put any of them to death, or imprison, banish, whip, or fine them — which are punishments. He claimed only the power to excommunicate — which, without the civil power behind it, is nothing more than leaving a man's company and having no more to do with him than with a heathen or a tax collector. And on many occasions that might be a greater pain to the one doing the excommunicating than to the one excommunicated.

The seventh passage is 1 Corinthians 4:21: "Shall I come unto you with a rod, or in love, and the spirit of lenity?" But here again, the rod does not mean a magistrate's power to punish offenders. It means only the power of excommunication — which is not, by its own nature, a punishment, but only an announcement of the punishment Christ shall inflict when he is in possession of his kingdom, at the day of judgment. And even then it will not properly be a punishment — as upon a subject who has broken the law — but a revenge, as upon an enemy or a rebel who denies our Savior's right to the kingdom. So this proves no legislative power in any bishop who does not also hold the civil power.

The eighth passage is 1 Timothy 3:2: "A bishop must be the husband but of one wife, vigilant, sober," and so on — which, he says, was a law. I had thought no one could make a law in the church but the monarch of the church, St. Peter. But suppose this precept was made by St. Peter's authority. Even so, I see no reason to call it a law rather than a piece of advice — since Timothy was not St. Paul's subject but his disciple, and the flock under Timothy's charge were not his subjects in a kingdom but his students in the school of Christ. If every precept Paul gives Timothy is a law, why is this not a law too: "Drink no longer water, but use a little wine for thy health's sake"? And why aren't the prescriptions of good physicians so many laws? The answer is that it is not the imperative manner of speaking that makes a precept a law — it is absolute subjection to a person.

In the same way the ninth passage, 1 Timothy 5:19 — "Against an elder receive not an accusation, but before two or three witnesses" — is a wise precept, but not a law.

The tenth passage is Luke 10:16: "He that heareth you, heareth me; and he that despiseth you, despiseth me." No doubt about it: whoever despises the counsel of those who are sent by Christ despises the counsel of Christ himself. But who are those sent by Christ now, if not those who are ordained pastors by lawful authority? And who is lawfully ordained, who is not ordained by the sovereign pastor? And who is ordained by the sovereign pastor in a Christian commonwealth, who is not ordained by the authority of its sovereign? From this passage, then, it follows that he who hears his sovereign — his sovereign being a Christian — hears Christ; and he who despises the doctrine his Christian king authorizes despises the doctrine of Christ. Which is not what Bellarmine set out to prove here, but its exact contrary.

And in any case, none of this amounts to a law. More than that: a Christian king, as pastor and teacher of his subjects, does not thereby make his doctrines laws. He cannot oblige men to believe. As civil sovereign, he may indeed make laws to suit his doctrine, and those laws may oblige men to certain actions — sometimes to actions they would not otherwise do, and which he ought not to command. Yet when they are commanded, they are laws; and the external actions done in obedience to them, without inward approval, are the actions of the sovereign, not of the subject — who is, in that case, merely an instrument, with no motion of his own at all, because God has commanded obedience to sovereigns.

The eleventh is every passage where the Apostle uses, for counsel, some word men ordinarily use to signify command, or calls the following of his counsel by the name of obedience. So they cite 1 Corinthians 11:2: "I commend you for keeping my precepts as I delivered them to you." The Greek actually says: "I commend you for keeping those things I delivered to you, as I delivered them" — which is far from signifying that they were laws, or anything other than good counsel. And 1 Thessalonians 4:2: "You know what commandments we gave you" — where the Greek phrase, paraggelias edokamen, is equivalent to paredokamen — "what we delivered to you," just as in the passage before. This does not prove the traditions of the Apostles to be anything more than counsels — even though, as verse 8 says, "he that despiseth them, despiseth not man, but God." For our Savior himself came not to judge — that is, not to be king in this world — but to sacrifice himself for sinners, and to leave teachers in his church to lead men to Christ, not to drive them. For Christ never accepts forced actions, which are all that law produces; he accepts the inward conversion of the heart — which is not the work of laws, but of counsel and doctrine.

And then 2 Thessalonians 3:14: "If any man obey not our word by this Epistle, note that man, and have no company with him, that he may be ashamed." From the word "obey," Bellarmine would infer that this epistle was a law to the Thessalonians. Now, the epistles of the emperors were indeed laws. So if the epistle of St. Paul were also a law, the Thessalonians were to obey two masters. But the word "obey," as it stands in the Greek — upakouei — signifies hearkening to, or putting in practice: not only what is commanded by one who has the right to punish, but also what is delivered by way of counsel for our good. And notice that St. Paul does not bid them kill the man who disobeys, nor beat him, nor imprison him, nor fine him — all of which lawmakers may do. He bids them avoid his company, that he may be ashamed. From which it is evident that what the Christians stood in awe of was not the empire of an Apostle, but his reputation among the faithful.
