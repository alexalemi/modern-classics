Chapter 10: Of Power, Worth, Dignity, Honor, and Worthiness

Power

The POWER of a man, taken in the most general sense, is his present means to obtain some future apparent good. Power is either original or instrumental.

Natural power is the excellence of body or mind: exceptional strength, good looks, prudence, skill in the arts, eloquence, generosity, noble birth. Instrumental powers are the ones acquired through those natural gifts, or through luck, and they serve as means and instruments to acquire still more — riches, reputation, friends, and the secret working of God, which men call good luck. For power, in this respect, behaves like fame: it grows as it goes. Or like a falling object, which picks up speed the farther it travels.

The greatest of human powers is the kind compounded out of the powers of many men, united by consent in one person — natural or artificial — who has the use of all their powers at his command. That is the power of a commonwealth. Or it may depend on the will of each individual member, like the power of a faction, or of several factions in league. So to have servants is power. To have friends is power. They are strengths united.

Riches joined with generosity are power, because they buy friends and servants. Riches without generosity are not — because in that case wealth does not defend its owner; it just exposes him to envy, like prey.

A reputation for power is itself power, because it attracts the loyalty of people who need protection.

So is a reputation for loving one's country — what we call popularity — and for the same reason.

In fact, any quality that makes a man loved or feared by many people, or even the reputation of such a quality, is power, because it is a means of getting the help and service of many.

Success is power, because it builds a reputation for wisdom or good fortune, which makes men either fear you or rely on you.

Friendliness in those who already hold power increases their power, because it wins love.

A reputation for prudence in the conduct of peace or war is power, because we hand over the government of ourselves more willingly to prudent men than to others.

Noble birth is power — not everywhere, but only in those commonwealths where nobility carries privileges. The power consists in the privileges.

Eloquence is power, because it looks like prudence.

Good looks are power, because beauty is a promise of good things to come, and it recommends men to the favor of women and strangers.

The sciences are only a small power, because science is not eminent — it does not stand out, so nobody gives any one man credit for it. And it exists in only a few people, and even in them, only about a few subjects. For science has this peculiar nature: no one can recognize that it exists at all unless he has already mastered a good share of it himself.

Arts of public use — fortification, the making of engines and other instruments of war — are power, because they contribute to defense and victory. And though the true mother of these arts is science, namely mathematics, they get credited to the craftsman who brings them into the light. With the common crowd, the midwife passes for the mother.

Worth

The value, or WORTH, of a man is — as with everything else — his price: that is, however much would be given for the use of his power. So it is not absolute, but depends on the need and judgment of someone else. An able commander of soldiers commands a great price in wartime, or when war threatens — but not in peace. A learned and honest judge is worth a great deal in peacetime, but less in war. And with men, as with everything else, it is not the seller who sets the price, but the buyer. Let a man rate himself at the highest value he can — and most men do — his true value is still no more than what others judge it to be.

The way we show the value we place on one another is what is commonly called honoring and dishonoring. To value a man at a high rate is to honor him; at a low rate, to dishonor him. But "high" and "low" here have to be measured against the rate each man sets on himself.

Dignity

The public worth of a man — the value placed on him by the commonwealth — is what men commonly call DIGNITY. And this valuation by the commonwealth shows itself in offices of command, judgeships, public employment, or in names and titles introduced to mark such value.

To Honor and Dishonor

To ask another for help of any kind is to HONOR him, because it is a sign that we believe he has the power to help; and the harder the help asked for, the greater the honor.

To obey is to honor, because nobody obeys someone they think has no power to help or hurt them. And so, to disobey is to dishonor.

To give great gifts to a man is to honor him, because it is buying protection and acknowledging power. To give small gifts is to dishonor, because that is mere charity, and it signals an opinion that he needs only small assistance. To work hard at promoting another's good, and to flatter him, is to honor — a sign that we seek his protection or aid. To neglect him is to dishonor.

To give way, or yield place to another in any advantage, is to honor — an admission of greater power. To grab precedence for yourself is to dishonor.

To show any sign of love or fear of another is to honor, for both to love and to fear is to value. To despise someone, or to love or fear him less than he expects, is to dishonor — it is undervaluing.

To praise a man, to magnify him, to call him happy, is to honor him, because nothing is valued but goodness, power, and happiness. To mock, revile, or pity him is to dishonor.

To speak to another with consideration, to appear before him with decency and humility, is to honor him — these are signs of fear of offending. To speak to him rashly, or to do anything obscene, sloppy, or impudent in his presence, is to dishonor.

To believe, to trust, to rely on another is to honor him — a sign of confidence in his virtue and power. To distrust or disbelieve him is to dishonor.

To listen to a man's counsel, or to his talk of any kind, is to honor — a sign that we think him wise, or eloquent, or witty. To fall asleep, walk out, or chat while he speaks is to dishonor.

To do for another those things he takes for signs of honor, or that law or custom makes signs of honor, is to honor him; because in approving the honor done by others, we acknowledge the power that others acknowledge. To refuse to do them is to dishonor.

To agree with a man's opinion is to honor him — a sign of approving his judgment and wisdom. To dissent is to dishonor, and amounts to accusing him of error — and if you dissent from him on many things, of folly.

To imitate is to honor, for imitation is the most emphatic form of approval. To imitate a man's enemy is to dishonor him.

To honor those whom another honors is to honor him — a sign of approval of his judgment. To honor his enemies is to dishonor him.

To employ a man in counsel, or in difficult undertakings, is to honor him — a sign of confidence in his wisdom or other power. To deny such employment to those who seek it is to dishonor.

All these ways of honoring are natural, and they hold both inside and outside commonwealths. But inside commonwealths, where the holder of supreme authority can make anything he pleases stand for a sign of honor, there are other honors as well.

A sovereign honors a subject with any title, office, employment, or action whatsoever that he himself chooses to count as a sign of his will to honor him.

The king of Persia honored Mordecai when he ordered him led through the streets in the king's robes, on one of the king's horses, with a crown on his head and a prince walking ahead of him proclaiming, "Thus shall it be done to him whom the king will honor." And yet another king of Persia — or the same one on another occasion — granted a man who asked, as reward for some great service, permission to wear one of the king's robes. Permission granted, said the king — but he must wear it as the king's fool. And that turned it into dishonor. So much for civil honor: things like magistracies, offices, titles, and in some places painted coats of arms. Men honor those who have them, as carrying so many signs of favor in the commonwealth — and that favor is power.

Honorable is whatever possession, action, or quality serves as evidence and sign of power.

And therefore to be honored, loved, or feared by many is honorable, as evidence of power. To be honored by few or none is dishonorable.

Good fortune, if it lasts, is honorable, as a sign of the favor of God. Bad fortune and losses are dishonorable. Riches are honorable, for they are power. Poverty is dishonorable. Magnanimity, generosity, hope, courage, confidence are honorable, for they spring from an awareness of one's own power. Small-mindedness, stinginess, fear, and self-distrust are dishonorable.

Deciding promptly — settling on what to do in good time — is honorable, because it shows contempt for small difficulties and dangers. Indecision is dishonorable, as a sign of overvaluing little obstacles and little advantages. For when a man has weighed his options as long as time allows and still cannot decide, the difference between them must be slight; so if he fails to decide, he is overvaluing trifles — which is small-mindedness.

All actions and speeches that come, or seem to come, from much experience, science, discretion, or wit are honorable, for all these are powers. Actions or words that come from error, ignorance, or folly are dishonorable.

Gravity — a serious manner — is honorable insofar as it seems to come from a mind occupied with something else, because being occupied is a sign of power. But if it seems to come from a deliberate effort to look grave, it is dishonorable. The gravity of the first kind is like the steadiness of a ship loaded with merchandise; the second, like the steadiness of a ship ballasted with sand and other junk.

To be conspicuous — that is, to be widely known for wealth, office, great deeds, or any eminent good — is honorable, as a sign of the power for which one is conspicuous. Obscurity, by contrast, is dishonorable.

To be descended from famous parents is honorable, because such men more easily inherit the connections and friends of their ancestors. To be descended from obscure parentage is, by contrast, dishonorable.

Actions that spring from fairness, even at a cost to oneself, are honorable, as signs of magnanimity — and magnanimity is a sign of power. By contrast, craft, shifty dealing, and disregard of fairness are dishonorable.

And here is the uncomfortable part: it makes no difference to honor whether an action is just or unjust, so long as it is great and difficult — and therefore a sign of much power. For honor consists only in the opinion of power. That is why the ancient pagans did not think they were dishonoring the gods, but greatly honoring them, when they put them in their poems committing rapes, thefts, and other great but unjust or unclean acts. Nothing about Jupiter is celebrated so much as his adulteries; nothing about Mercury so much as his frauds and thefts. In one of Homer's hymns, the highest praise given to Mercury is this: that born in the morning, he had invented music by noon, and before nightfall had stolen the cattle of Apollo away from his herdsmen.

Among men, too, until great commonwealths were established, it was considered no dishonor to be a pirate or a highway robber — rather a lawful trade, not only among the Greeks but among all other nations, as the histories of ancient times make plain. And to this day, in this part of the world, private duels are honorable, and always will be, however unlawful — until the day honor is established for those who refuse a challenge, and disgrace for those who issue one. For duels are often genuine effects of courage, and the basis of courage is always strength or skill, which are power. Though for the most part they are the effects of rash talk, and of the fear of dishonor in one or both of the fighters — who, having committed themselves by their own rashness, are driven into the dueling ground to avoid disgrace.

Coats of arms and hereditary heraldry are honorable where they carry eminent privileges; otherwise not. For their power consists either in those privileges, or in riches, or in some other thing equally honored in other men. This kind of honor, commonly called gentry, descends from the ancient Germans. No such thing was ever known where German customs were unknown, and it is not in use anywhere today where Germans have not settled. The ancient Greek commanders, when they went to war, painted their shields with whatever designs they pleased — so much so that an unpainted shield was a sign of poverty and of a common soldier. But they did not pass these designs down as inheritance. The Romans handed down the marks of their families, but those were the portraits of their ancestors, not emblems. Among the peoples of Asia, Africa, and America there is not, and never was, any such thing. The Germans alone had that custom, and from them it spread into England, France, Spain, and Italy, when in great numbers they either fought as allies of the Romans or carved out their own conquests in these western parts of the world.

For Germany was anciently — like all other countries at their beginnings — divided among countless little lords, or masters of families, who waged continual war on one another. These masters, chiefly so that they could be recognized by their followers when they were covered in armor, and partly for ornament, painted their armor, or their shield, or their coat with the picture of some beast or other device, and also fixed some prominent, visible mark on the crest of their helmets. This ornament of arms and crest descended by inheritance to their children — to the eldest unchanged, and to the rest with some mark of difference, whatever the old master (in German, the Here-alt — the source of our word "herald") thought fit. When many such families joined together into a greater monarchy, this duty of the herald, to keep the coats of arms distinct, became a separate private office. The descendants of those lords are the great and ancient gentry, whose arms mostly display living creatures noted for courage and plunder, or castles, battlements, belts, weapons, bars, palisades, and other emblems of war — for in those days nothing was honored but military virtue. Later on, not only kings but popular commonwealths granted various coats of arms to those who marched off to war or returned from it, as encouragement or reward for their service. All of this an observant reader can find in those ancient Greek and Latin histories that mention the German nation and its customs.

Titles of Honor

Titles of honor, such as duke, count, marquis, and baron, are honorable, as signifying the value set on their holders by the sovereign power of the commonwealth. These titles were in old times titles of office and command, derived some from the Romans, some from the Germans and French. Dukes — in Latin duces — were generals in war. Counts — comites, "companions" — were those who kept the general company out of friendship, and were left behind to govern and defend conquered and pacified territory. Marquises — marchiones — were counts who governed the marches, the borderlands of the empire. These titles of duke, count, and marquis came into the empire around the time of Constantine the Great, from the customs of the German military. But baron seems to have been a title of the Gauls, meaning a great man — the kind of men the kings or princes employed in war around their persons. It seems to derive from vir — Latin for "man" — becoming ber and bar, which meant in the language of the Gauls what vir meant in Latin; thence to bero and baro, so that such men were called berones, and afterward barones, and in Spanish varones. Anyone who wants the origins of titles of honor in more detail can find them, as I found these, in Mr. Selden's most excellent treatise on the subject. In the course of time, these offices of honor — through political trouble, and for reasons of good and peaceful government — were turned into mere titles, serving mostly to mark the precedence, place, and rank of subjects in the commonwealth. Men were made dukes, counts, marquises, and barons of places where they had neither property nor command, and still other titles were invented for the same purpose.

Worthiness and Fitness

WORTHINESS is a different thing from the worth or value of a man, and different too from his merit or desert. It consists in a particular power or ability for the thing he is said to be worthy of — and that particular ability is usually called FITNESS, or aptitude.

For the worthiest man to be a commander, a judge, or to hold any other office, is the one best equipped with the qualities required to do the job well; and the worthiest of riches is the one with the qualities most needed for using riches well. Lacking any of these qualities, a man may still be a worthy man — valuable for something else. Then again, a man may be worthy of riches, office, and employment and yet have no claim to receive them ahead of anyone else, and so cannot be said to merit or deserve them. For merit presupposes a right — that the thing deserved is owed by promise. Of that I shall say more later, when I come to speak of contracts.
