Chapter 40: The Rights of the Kingdom of God — in Abraham, Moses, the High Priests, and the Kings of Judah

Abraham's Sovereign Rights

The father of the faithful, and the first member of the kingdom of God by covenant, was Abraham. The covenant was made with him first. In it, he bound himself — and his descendants after him — to acknowledge and obey the commands of God. Not just the commands he could discover for himself by the light of nature, as moral laws, but also whatever commands God should deliver to him in a special way, by dreams and visions.

Think about why that distinction matters. As far as the moral law was concerned, Abraham and his family were already obligated to obey it. There was no need to purchase their obedience with the promise of the land of Canaan. No contract could add anything to, or strengthen, the obligation by which they — and all people everywhere — were already naturally bound to obey God Almighty. So the covenant Abraham made with God came down to this: to accept as the commandment of God whatever was commanded to him in God's name, in a dream or vision, and to deliver it to his family, and make sure they observed it.

Abraham Alone Ordered the Religion of His Own People

In this contract between God and Abraham, we can observe three points of major consequence for the government of God's people.

First: when this covenant was made, God spoke only to Abraham. He made no contract with any member of Abraham's family or any of his descendants — except in the sense that their wills (which are the essence of every covenant) were already wrapped up in the will of Abraham before the contract was made. Abraham, in other words, was assumed to have lawful power to make his people perform everything he covenanted on their behalf. Scripture says exactly this (Genesis 18:18-19): "All the nations of the earth shall be blessed in him, for I know him, that he will command his children and his household after him, and they shall keep the way of the Lord."

From this we can draw our first conclusion: those to whom God has not spoken directly are to receive the positive commandments of God from their sovereign — just as Abraham's family and descendants received them from Abraham, their father, their lord, and their civil sovereign. And consequently, in every commonwealth, those who have no supernatural revelation to the contrary ought to obey the laws of their own sovereign in the external acts and profession of religion. As for the inward thoughts and beliefs of men — which human governors cannot take notice of, for God alone knows the heart — these are not voluntary. They are not the effect of laws, but of the unrevealed will and power of God, and so they fall under no obligation at all.

No Claim of "Private Spirit" Against Abraham's Religion

From the same contract follows a second point. Suppose one of Abraham's subjects claimed a private vision, or spirit, or some other revelation from God, to support a doctrine Abraham had forbidden. It was not unlawful for Abraham to punish that person — and to punish anyone who followed or sided with such a pretender. And consequently, it is lawful now for the sovereign to punish any man who sets up his "private spirit" against the laws. For the sovereign holds the same place in the commonwealth that Abraham held in his own family.

Abraham the Sole Judge and Interpreter of What God Said

And from the same contract arises a third point: just as no one but Abraham in his family could take notice of what was, or was not, the word of God, so no one but the sovereign in a Christian commonwealth can do so. God spoke only to Abraham, and Abraham alone was in a position to know what God said and to interpret it to his family. Therefore those who hold the place of Abraham in a commonwealth are the only interpreters of what God has spoken.

What Moses' Authority Rested On

The same covenant was renewed with Isaac, and afterwards with Jacob — but then no more, until the Israelites were freed from the Egyptians and arrived at the foot of Mount Sinai. There it was renewed by Moses (as I said before, in chapter 35), in such a way that from that time forward the Israelites became the peculiar kingdom of God, whose lieutenant was Moses for his own lifetime, with the succession to that office settled on Aaron and his heirs after him — to be to God a priestly kingdom forever.

By this constitution, a kingdom is acquired to God. But notice a difficulty. Moses had no authority to govern the Israelites as a successor to Abraham's right, because he could not claim it by inheritance. So it does not yet appear that the people were obligated to take him for God's lieutenant any longer than they believed that God actually spoke to him. His authority — despite the covenant they had made with God — still depended entirely on the opinion they held of his holiness, of the reality of his conversations with God, and of the truth of his miracles. And if that opinion changed, they were no longer obligated to accept anything he proposed to them in God's name as the law of God.

We have to ask, then: what other ground was there for their obligation to obey him? It could not be the commandment of God itself, because God did not speak to them directly — only through the mediation of Moses himself. Our Savior says of himself (John 5:31), "If I bear witness of myself, my witness is not true." If that holds for Christ, then much less should Moses' testimony about himself be accepted — especially when he was claiming kingly power over God's people. His authority, therefore, like the authority of all other princes, must be grounded on the consent of the people and their promise to obey him.

And so it was. For the people (Exodus 20:18), "when they saw the thunderings, and the lightnings, and the noise of the trumpet, and the mountain smoking, moved back and stood far off. And they said to Moses: speak thou with us, and we will hear; but let not God speak with us, lest we die." Here was their promise of obedience. By this promise they obligated themselves to obey whatever he delivered to them as the commandment of God.

Moses Was Sovereign of the Jews (Under God) All His Lifetime, Though Aaron Held the Priesthood

Now, the covenant did establish a priestly kingdom — that is, a kingdom hereditary to Aaron. But that must be understood of the succession after Moses was dead. Whoever orders and establishes the political system, as first founder of a commonwealth — whether monarchy, aristocracy, or democracy — must necessarily have sovereign power over the people the whole time he is doing it. And that Moses held that power his whole lifetime is plainly affirmed in Scripture.

First, in the text just cited: the people promised obedience to him, not to Aaron.

Second (Exodus 24:1-2): "And God said unto Moses, Come up unto the Lord, thou, and Aaron, Nadab and Abihu, and seventy of the elders of Israel. And Moses alone shall come near the Lord, but they shall not come nigh, neither shall the people go up with him." This makes it plain that Moses — who alone was called up to God, not Aaron, not the other priests, not the seventy elders, not the people, who were forbidden to come up — was alone the one who represented the person of God to the Israelites. That is to say, he was their sole sovereign under God. It is true that afterwards (verse 9) we read: "Then went up Moses, and Aaron, Nadab, and Abihu, and seventy of the elders of Israel, and they saw the God of Israel, and there was under his feet, as it were, a paved work of a sapphire stone," and so on. But this did not happen until after Moses had been with God before, and had already brought down to the people the words God had spoken to him. He alone went up on the people's business; the others, like the nobles of his retinue, were admitted purely as an honor, to a special grace not allowed to the people — which was (as the following verse shows) to see God and live: "God laid not his hand upon them; they saw God, and did eat and drink" — that is, they lived — but they carried no commandment from him to the people.

Third: everywhere the text says "The Lord spoke unto Moses" — in all matters of government, and also in the ordering of the ceremonies of religion contained in chapters 25 through 31 of Exodus and throughout Leviticus. To Aaron, rarely. Indeed, the calf that Aaron made, Moses threw into the fire.

Fourth: the question of Aaron's authority — raised when he and Miriam mutinied against Moses — was judged by God himself in Moses' favor (Numbers 12).

And likewise in the dispute between Moses and the people, when Korah, Dathan, and Abiram, and two hundred and fifty princes of the assembly "gathered themselves together against Moses, and against Aaron" (Numbers 16:3), "and said unto them, Ye take too much upon you, seeing all the congregation are holy, every one of them, and the Lord is amongst them; why lift you up yourselves above the congregation of the Lord?" God caused the earth to swallow Korah, Dathan, and Abiram alive, with their wives and children, and consumed those two hundred and fifty princes with fire.

So: not Aaron, not the people, not any aristocracy of the chief princes of the people — Moses alone had, next under God, the sovereignty over the Israelites. And not only in matters of civil policy, but in religion too. For only Moses spoke with God, and therefore only Moses could tell the people what it was that God required of them. No man, on pain of death, could be so presumptuous as to approach the mountain where God talked with Moses. "Thou shalt set bounds to the people round about," says the Lord (Exodus 19:12), "and say, Take heed to yourselves that you go not up into the mount, or touch the border of it; whosoever toucheth the mount shall surely be put to death." And again (verse 21): "Get down, charge the people, lest they break through unto the Lord to gaze."

From all this we may conclude: whoever in a Christian commonwealth holds the place of Moses is the sole messenger of God and interpreter of his commandments. Accordingly, no man ought, in interpreting Scripture, to go beyond the bounds set by his own sovereign. For the Scriptures — since God now speaks in them — are Mount Sinai, and its boundaries are the laws of those who represent God's person on earth. To look upon the Scriptures, and behold in them the wondrous works of God, and learn to fear him — that is allowed. But to interpret them — that is, to pry into what God says to the man he has appointed to govern under him, and to make ourselves judges of whether he governs as God commands him or not — is to transgress the bounds God has set us, and to gaze upon God irreverently.

All Spirits Were Subordinate to the Spirit of Moses

In Moses' time there was no prophet, and no claimant to the spirit of God, except those Moses had approved and authorized. There were in his time only seventy men who are said to prophesy by the spirit of God, and all seventy were of Moses' own choosing. Concerning them, God says to Moses (Numbers 11:16): "Gather to me seventy of the elders of Israel, whom thou knowest to be the elders of the people." To these God imparted his spirit — but it was not a different spirit from the spirit of Moses. For the text says (verse 25): "God came down in a cloud, and took of the spirit that was upon Moses, and gave it to the seventy elders."

Now, as I showed before (chapter 36), by "spirit" is understood the mind. So the meaning of the passage is simply this: God endowed them with a mind conformable and subordinate to the mind of Moses, so that they might prophesy — that is, speak to the people in God's name — in such a way as to promote, as ministers of Moses and by his authority, doctrine that agreed with Moses' doctrine. They were only ministers. And when two of them prophesied in the camp, it was considered a new and unlawful thing; as verses 27 and 28 of the same chapter show, they were accused of it, and Joshua advised Moses to forbid them — not realizing that it was by Moses' own spirit that they prophesied. From which it is plain that no subject ought to claim prophecy, or the spirit, in opposition to the doctrine established by the man whom God has set in the place of Moses.

After Moses, Sovereignty Belonged to the High Priest

When Aaron was dead, and after him Moses, the kingdom — being a priestly kingdom — descended, by virtue of the covenant, to Aaron's son Eleazar, the high priest. And God declared him sovereign, next under himself, at the very moment he appointed Joshua general of the army. For God says expressly concerning Joshua (Numbers 27:21): "He shall stand before Eleazar the priest, who shall ask counsel for him before the Lord; at his word shall they go out, and at his word they shall come in, both he, and all the children of Israel with him." The supreme power of making war and peace, then, was in the priest. The supreme power of judicature belonged to the high priest as well: the Book of the Law was in the priests' keeping, and the priests and Levites alone were the subordinate judges in civil cases, as appears in Deuteronomy 17:8-10. And as for the manner of God's worship, no one ever doubted that the high priest, until the time of Saul, had the supreme authority over it.

Therefore the civil and the ecclesiastical power were both joined together in one and the same person — the high priest. And they ought to be so joined in whoever governs by divine right; that is, by authority that comes immediately from God.

Sovereign Power Between Joshua and Saul

After the death of Joshua, until the time of Saul, the Book of Judges notes repeatedly "that there was in those days no king in Israel" — sometimes adding that "every man did that which was right in his own eyes." By "there was no king" we should understand "there was no sovereign power" in Israel. And so it was — if we consider the actual exercise of such power. For after the deaths of Joshua and Eleazar, "there arose another generation" (Judges 2:10) "that knew not the Lord, nor the works which he had done for Israel, but did evil in the sight of the Lord, and served Baalim." The Jews had that quality St. Paul notes — they "look for a sign" — not only before they would submit to the government of Moses, but even after they had bound themselves by their submission. Yet signs and miracles had as their purpose to produce faith, not to keep men from violating faith once given; for to keep faith, men are already obligated by the law of nature.

But if we consider not the exercise of governing but the right of it, the sovereign power was still in the high priest. So whatever obedience was given to any of the Judges — men chosen by God in an extraordinary way, to rescue his rebellious subjects from the hands of their enemies — cannot be used as an argument against the right of the high priest to sovereign power in all matters, both of policy and of religion. Neither the Judges nor Samuel himself had an ordinary calling to government, but an extraordinary one; and the Israelites obeyed them not out of duty, but out of reverence for their evident favor with God, displayed in their wisdom, their courage, or their success. Up to this point, then, the right of regulating the state and the right of regulating religion were inseparable.

The Rights of the Kings of Israel

After the Judges came kings. And whereas before, all authority — in religion and in policy alike — had been in the high priest, now it was all in the king. For the sovereignty over the people, which had rested not only on God's divine power but also on the particular pact the Israelites made with God, belonged next under him to the high priest, as his viceregent on earth. And that sovereignty was cast off by the people, with the consent of God himself. When they said to Samuel (1 Samuel 8:5), "make us a king to judge us, like all the nations," they were declaring that they would no longer be governed by the commands laid on them by the priest in the name of God, but by a man who would command them the same way all other nations were commanded. So in stripping the high priest of royal authority, they deposed that peculiar government of God. And yet God consented to it, saying to Samuel (verse 7): "Hearken unto the voice of the people, in all that they shall say unto thee; for they have not rejected thee, but they have rejected me, that I should not reign over them."

Having rejected God, in whose right the priests had governed, the people left the priests no authority at all except whatever the king was pleased to allow them — and that was more or less, depending on whether the kings were good or evil. As for the government of civil affairs, it was obviously all in the king's hands. In the same chapter (verse 20) the people say they want to be like all the nations: their king shall be their judge, and go before them, and fight their battles. In other words, he shall have the whole authority, in peace and in war alike. And that authority contained the ordering of religion within it — because in those days there was no other word of God by which to regulate religion than the Law of Moses, which was their civil law.

The kings' actual conduct confirms this. We read (1 Kings 2:27) that Solomon "thrust out Abiathar from being priest before the Lord." So he had authority over the high priest, exactly as over any other subject — which is a great mark of supremacy in religion. We read (1 Kings 8) that Solomon dedicated the Temple, that he blessed the people, and that he himself, in person, made that excellent prayer now used in the consecration of all churches and houses of prayer — another great mark of supremacy in religion. Again, we read (2 Kings 22) that when a question arose about the Book of the Law found in the Temple, it was not the high priest who decided it; Josiah sent both him and others to inquire about it from Huldah the prophetess — another mark of supremacy in religion. Lastly, we read (1 Chronicles 26:30) that David made Hashabiah and his brothers, Hebronites, officers of Israel on the west side of Jordan, "in all business of the Lord, and in the service of the king"; and likewise (verse 32) that he made other Hebronites "rulers over the Reubenites, the Gadites, and the half tribe of Manasseh" — the rest of Israel, who lived beyond Jordan — "for every matter pertaining to God, and affairs of the king." Is that not full power, both "temporal" and "spiritual" — as those who would like to divide it call the two halves?

To conclude: from the first institution of God's kingdom down to the Captivity, the supremacy in religion was in the same hand as the civil sovereignty. And after the election of Saul, the office of the priests was not magisterial but ministerial — they served; they did not rule.

In Practice, the Kings' Supremacy in Religion Did Not Match Their Right

Now, although the government of policy and religion was joined — first in the high priests, and afterwards in the kings — as far as the right was concerned, the same holy history shows that the people never understood it that way. A great part of them — probably the greatest part — gave sufficient credit to the fame of Moses, or to the conversations between God and the priests, only so long as they saw great miracles, or (what is equivalent to a miracle) great abilities or great success in the undertakings of their governors. So whenever their governors displeased them, they seized the occasion — blaming sometimes the policy, sometimes the religion — to change the government or revolt from their obedience whenever they pleased. And from this came, time after time, the civil troubles, divisions, and calamities of the nation.

For example: after the deaths of Eleazar and Joshua, the next generation — which had not seen the wonders of God, and was left to its own weak reason — did not consider itself bound by the covenant of a priestly kingdom. They paid no further attention to the commandment of the priest, nor to any law of Moses, but every man did what was right in his own eyes. In civil affairs they obeyed whatever men they thought, from time to time, were able to deliver them from the neighboring nations that oppressed them. And they consulted not God (as they ought to have done), but whatever men or women they guessed to be prophets by their predictions of things to come. Even with an idol in their chapel, if they had a Levite for their chaplain, they reckoned they were worshipping the God of Israel.

And later, when they demanded a king after the manner of the nations, it was not with any design of abandoning the worship of God their King. Despairing of justice from the sons of Samuel, they wanted a king to judge them in civil matters — but not a king they would allow to change the religion they believed had been handed down to them by Moses. So they always kept a pretext in reserve — either justice or religion — to release themselves from their obedience whenever they had hope of prevailing. Samuel was displeased with the people for desiring a king (for God was their king already, and Samuel had only an authority under him); yet Samuel himself, when Saul failed to follow his counsel by destroying Agag as God had commanded, anointed another king — namely David — to take the succession away from Saul's heirs. Rehoboam was no idolater; but when the people thought him an oppressor, that civil pretext carried ten tribes away from him to Jeroboam, who was an idolater. And throughout the whole history of the kings, of Judah and of Israel alike, there were always prophets rebuking the kings for transgressing the religion — and sometimes for errors of state as well. Jehoshaphat was reproved by the prophet Jehu for aiding the king of Israel against the Syrians (2 Chronicles 19:2); Hezekiah was reproved by Isaiah for showing his treasures to the ambassadors of Babylon.

All of this shows that although the power of both state and religion was in the kings, none of them exercised it uncontested — except those who were popular for their own natural abilities or their good fortune. So from the practice of those times, no argument can be drawn that the right of supremacy in religion was not in the kings. Unless, that is, we place it in the prophets — and conclude that because Hezekiah, praying to the Lord before the cherubim, was not answered from there and then, but afterwards by the prophet Isaiah, Isaiah was therefore the supreme head of the Church. Or that because Josiah consulted Huldah the prophetess about the Book of the Law, neither he nor the high priest but Huldah the prophetess held the supreme authority in matters of religion. Which, I think, is not the opinion of any theologian.

After the Captivity, the Jews Had No Settled Commonwealth

During the Captivity, the Jews had no commonwealth at all. After their return, though they renewed their covenant with God, no promise of obedience was made — not to Esdras, not to anyone else. And shortly afterwards they became subjects of the Greeks, from whose customs and demonology, and from the doctrine of the Cabalists, their religion became badly corrupted. The result is such confusion, in state and religion alike, that nothing can be gathered from that period concerning the supremacy in either.

And therefore, as far as the Old Testament is concerned, we may conclude this: whoever held the sovereignty of the commonwealth among the Jews also held the supreme authority in the matter of God's external worship, and represented God's person — that is, the person of God the Father. Though he was not called by the name of Father until the time when God sent into the world his Son, Jesus Christ, to redeem mankind from their sins and bring them into his everlasting kingdom, to be saved forevermore. Of which we are to speak in the chapter that follows.
