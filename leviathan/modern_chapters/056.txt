A Review and Conclusion

Some people have argued, from the way certain natural faculties of the mind work against each other — and certain passions against other passions — that no one man could ever be fit for every kind of civil duty. Severity of judgment, they say, makes men harsh critics, slow to pardon the errors and weaknesses of others. Quickness of imagination, on the other side, makes the thoughts less steady than they need to be to tell right from wrong exactly. Again, in every deliberation and every courtroom argument, solid reasoning is necessary — without it, men's decisions are rash and their verdicts unjust. And yet without powerful eloquence to win attention and consent, reason accomplishes almost nothing. But these two are contrary faculties: reasoning is grounded on principles of truth, while eloquence is grounded on opinions already received, true or false, and on the passions and interests of men, which are different and always shifting.

And among the passions, courage — by which I mean contempt for wounds and violent death — inclines men toward private revenge, and sometimes toward unsettling the public peace; while timidity often disposes them to desert the public defense. Both of these, they say, cannot live in the same person.

And when you consider how much men's opinions and manners differ in general, it is impossible, they say, to keep up a steady civil friendship with everyone the business of the world forces us to deal with — a business that consists in almost nothing else but a perpetual contest for honor, riches, and authority.

To this I answer: these are indeed great difficulties, but not impossibilities. By education and discipline they can be reconciled, and sometimes are. Judgment and imagination can both have a place in the same man — but by turns, as the goal he is aiming at requires. Just as the Israelites in Egypt were sometimes tied to their labor of making bricks, and at other times ranged abroad to gather straw, so the judgment can sometimes be fixed on one particular question, and the imagination at another time go wandering about the world. So too reason and eloquence can stand very well together — perhaps not in the natural sciences, but certainly in the moral ones. For wherever there is room to dress up and promote error, there is far more room to dress up and promote truth, if you have truth to dress. Nor is there any contradiction between fearing the laws and not fearing a public enemy; nor between refraining from injury yourself and pardoning it in others. So there is no such incompatibility between human nature and civil duties as some people think. I have known clearness of judgment and largeness of imagination, strength of reason and graceful speech, courage for war and fear of the laws, all eminently combined in one man — and that was my most noble and honored friend, Mr. Sidney Godolphin: who, hating no man and hated by none, was unfortunately killed at the start of the late civil war, in the public quarrel, by an undiscerned and undiscerning hand.

To the laws of nature set out in Chapter 15, I would have this one added: that every man is bound by nature, as far as in him lies, to protect in war the authority by which he is himself protected in time of peace. For a man who claims a right of nature to preserve his own body cannot claim a right of nature to destroy the very power by whose strength he is preserved. That is a plain contradiction of himself. He is obliged to protect his protector. And though this law can be deduced from some of those already listed there, the times require that it be drummed in and remembered.

And because I find from various English books lately printed that the civil wars have not yet sufficiently taught men at what point in time a subject becomes obliged to a conqueror — nor what conquest is, nor how it comes about that conquest obliges men to obey the conqueror's laws — I will say it plainly, for their further satisfaction. The point in time at which a man becomes the subject of a conqueror is the point at which, having the liberty to submit to him, he consents — either by express words or by some other sufficient sign — to be his subject. As for when a man has the liberty to submit, I showed that already at the end of Chapter 21: for a man who has no obligation to his former sovereign beyond that of an ordinary subject, it is when the means of his life lie within the guards and garrisons of the enemy. For at that point he no longer has protection from his old sovereign, but is protected by the opposing side in exchange for his contribution. The end of obedience is protection. And since such contribution is everywhere treated as lawful, as an unavoidable thing — even though it is assistance to the enemy — total submission, which is nothing more than assistance to the enemy, cannot be treated as unlawful. Besides, consider this: those who submit assist the enemy with only part of their estates, while those who refuse assist him with the whole of theirs. So there is no reason to call their submission, or their settlement with the conqueror, assistance to the enemy. It is rather a loss to him.

But if a man, on top of his obligation as a subject, has taken on the new obligation of a soldier, then he does not have the liberty to submit to a new power as long as the old one keeps the field and provides him the means of subsistence, either in its armies or in its garrisons. In that case he cannot complain of lacking protection and the means to live as a soldier. But when that fails too, a soldier also may seek his protection wherever he has the most hope of finding it, and may lawfully submit himself to his new master. So much for the time when he may do it lawfully, if he chooses. And if he does do it, he is without question bound to be a true subject: for a contract lawfully made cannot lawfully be broken.

From this a man can also understand when people may be said to be conquered, and what the nature of conquest and the right of a conqueror consist in — for this act of submission contains them all. Conquest is not the victory itself, but the acquisition, by victory, of a right over the persons of men. The man who is killed is overcome, but not conquered. The man who is captured and put in prison or in chains is not conquered, though he is overcome — for he is still an enemy, and may save himself if he can. But the man who, on a promise of obedience, is allowed his life and liberty — he is then conquered, and a subject, and not before. The Romans used to say that their general had "pacified" such-and-such a province — that is to say, in plain English, conquered it — and that the country was pacified by victory when its people had promised imperata facere — to do what the Roman people commanded them. That was what it meant to be conquered. But this promise may be either express or tacit: express, by an actual promise; tacit, by other signs. For example, a man who has never been called on to make an express promise (perhaps because his power is not considerable enough to matter) is nevertheless understood to submit himself to the government if he lives openly under its protection. But if he lives there secretly, he is liable to anything that may be done to a spy and enemy of the state. I do not say he commits any injustice — acts of open hostility do not bear that name — but I do say he may justly be put to death. Likewise, if a man is out of his country when it is conquered, he is not conquered, and not a subject; but if on his return he submits to the government, he is bound to obey it. So conquest, to define it, is the acquiring of the right of sovereignty by victory. And that right is acquired in the people's submission, by which they contract with the victor, promising obedience in exchange for life and liberty.

In Chapter 29 I set down, as one of the causes of the dissolution of commonwealths, their imperfect generation — their lacking an absolute and unconditional legislative power, for want of which the civil sovereign is forced to handle the sword of justice waveringly, as if it were too hot to hold. One reason for this, which I did not mention there, is that sovereigns will all insist on justifying the war by which their power was first gotten, as if their right depended on the goodness of that old cause and not on their present possession. As if, for example, the right of the kings of England depended on the goodness of William the Conqueror's cause, and on their lineal and most direct descent from him — by which standard there would perhaps be no tie of obedience binding any subject to any sovereign anywhere in the world today. In needlessly trying to justify themselves this way, they justify every successful rebellion that ambition shall ever raise against them and their successors. Therefore I set this down as one of the most effective seeds of the death of any state: that conquerors demand not only a submission of men's actions for the future, but also an approval of all their own actions past — when there is scarcely a commonwealth in the world whose beginnings can in good conscience be justified.

And because the name of tyranny signifies nothing more and nothing less than the name of sovereignty — whether in one man or many — except that those who use the word "tyranny" are understood to be angry at the men they call tyrants, I think that tolerating a professed hatred of tyranny is tolerating a hatred of commonwealth in general, and is another evil seed, not much different from the first. For to justify the cause of a conqueror, men generally find it necessary to vilify the cause of the conquered — but neither the one nor the other is necessary for the obligation of the conquered. And this much I have thought fit to say on review of the first and second parts of this discourse.

In Chapter 35 I showed sufficiently from Scripture that in the commonwealth of the Jews, God himself was made the sovereign by pact with the people — who were therefore called his peculiar people, to distinguish them from the rest of the world, over whom God reigned not by their consent but by his own power — and that in this kingdom Moses was God's lieutenant on earth, and it was he who told them what laws God had appointed. But I did not then deal with who was to carry out those laws, especially in capital punishments — not thinking it then a matter requiring as much consideration as I have since found it does. We know that in commonwealths generally, the execution of corporal punishments was either assigned to the guards or other soldiers of the sovereign power, or given to men in whom poverty, contempt for honor, and hardness of heart combined to make them apply for such an office. But among the Israelites it was a positive law of God their sovereign that whoever was convicted of a capital crime should be stoned to death by the people — and that the witnesses should cast the first stone, and after the witnesses, the rest of the people. This was a law that designated who the executioners were to be. It did not allow anyone to throw a stone before conviction and sentence, where the congregation was the judge. The witnesses still had to be heard before the execution proceeded, unless the deed had been committed in the presence of the congregation itself, or in the sight of the lawful judges — for then no other witnesses were needed but the judges themselves. Nevertheless, because this procedure has not been thoroughly understood, it has given occasion to a dangerous opinion: that any man may kill another, in some cases, by a "right of zeal" — as if the executions done upon offenders in the kingdom of God in ancient times proceeded not from the sovereign's command, but from the authority of private zeal. If we look at the very texts that seem to favor this idea, they say quite the contrary.

First, when the Levites fell upon the people who had made and worshipped the golden calf, and killed three thousand of them, it was by the commandment of Moses, from the mouth of God — as is plain in (Exodus 32:27). And when the son of an Israelite woman blasphemed God, those who heard it did not kill him, but brought him before Moses, who put him in custody until God should give sentence against him — as appears in (Leviticus 24:11-12). Again, in (Numbers 25:6-7), when Phinehas killed Zimri and Cozbi, it was not by right of private zeal. Their crime was committed in the sight of the whole assembly, so no witness was needed; the law was known; Phinehas was heir apparent to the sovereignty; and — the principal point — the lawfulness of his act depended entirely on a subsequent ratification by Moses, of which he had no cause to doubt. And this presumption of a future ratification is sometimes necessary to the safety of a commonwealth: in a sudden rebellion, any man who can suppress it by his own power, in the place where it begins, may lawfully do so, and arrange to have his act ratified or pardoned while it is being done, or after it is done. Also, (Numbers 35:30) says expressly, "Whosoever shall kill the murderer shall kill him upon the word of witnesses" — but witnesses presuppose a formal court, and so this text condemns the whole pretense of jus zelotarum — the supposed right of zealots. The law of Moses concerning a man who entices others to idolatry — that is to say, in the kingdom of God, to a renouncing of their allegiance — forbids concealing him (Deuteronomy 13:8) and commands the accuser to have him put to death and to cast the first stone at him: but not to kill him before he is condemned. And in (Deuteronomy 17:4-6) the legal process against idolatry is set down exactly. God there speaks to the people as judge, and commands them, when a man is accused of idolatry, to inquire diligently into the fact; and if they find it true, then to stone him — but still the hand of the witness throws the first stone. This is not private zeal; it is public condemnation. In the same way, when a father has a rebellious son, the law (Deuteronomy 21:18) is that he shall bring him before the judges of the town, and all the people of the town shall stone him. Lastly, it was under pretense of these laws that St. Stephen was stoned — not under any pretense of private zeal: for before he was carried away to execution, he had pleaded his cause before the High Priest. There is nothing in all this, nor in any other part of the Bible, to support executions by private zeal — which, being most of the time nothing but a combination of ignorance and passion, is against both the justice and the peace of a commonwealth.

In Chapter 36 I said that it is not declared in what manner God spoke supernaturally to Moses. I did not mean that he never spoke to Moses by dreams and visions and by a supernatural voice, as he did to other prophets. The manner in which God spoke to him from the mercy-seat is set down expressly in (Numbers 7:89), in these words: "From that time forward, when Moses entered into the Tabernacle of the Congregation to speak with God, he heard a Voice which spake unto him from over the Mercy-Seat, which is over the Ark of the Testimony; from between the Cherubims he spake unto him." But what is not declared is what made the manner of God's speaking to Moses superior to his speaking to other prophets — to Samuel, say, or to Abraham, to whom he also spoke by a voice, that is, by vision — unless the difference lay in the clearness of the vision. For "face to face" and "mouth to mouth" cannot be understood literally of the infiniteness and incomprehensibility of the divine nature.

As for the doctrine of this book as a whole, I still cannot see anything wrong with it: the principles of it are true and proper, and the reasoning solid. For I ground the civil right of sovereigns, and both the duty and the liberty of subjects, on the known natural inclinations of mankind and on the articles of the law of nature — things no man ought to be ignorant of who claims even reason enough to govern his own household. And as for the ecclesiastical power of those same sovereigns, I ground it on texts that are both evident in themselves and in harmony with the aim of the whole of Scripture. And therefore I am persuaded that whoever reads this book with no other purpose than to be informed, will be informed by it. But those who by their writing, or public speaking, or prominent actions have already committed themselves to maintaining the contrary opinions will not be so easily satisfied. In such cases it is natural for men to do two things at once: to keep reading, and to lose their attention in hunting for objections to what they read before. And in a time when men's interests have changed — since much of the doctrine that serves to establish a new government must necessarily run contrary to the doctrine that helped dissolve the old — there cannot help but be very many such objections.

In the part that deals with a Christian commonwealth there are some new doctrines which, in a state where the contrary had already been fully settled, it would perhaps be a fault for a subject to publish without permission — that would be usurping the place of a teacher. But in this time, when men are calling not only for peace but also for truth, to offer such doctrines as I think true — doctrines that plainly tend toward peace and loyalty — for the consideration of those who are still deliberating, is nothing more than offering new wine to be put into new casks, so that both may be preserved together. And I suppose that when novelty can breed no trouble or disorder in a state, men are not generally so devoted to the reverence of antiquity as to prefer ancient errors over new and well-proved truth.

There is nothing I distrust more than my own style — which nevertheless I am confident is not obscure, apart from the accidents of the printing press. As for my having neglected the fashionable ornament of quoting ancient poets, orators, and philosophers, contrary to the custom of recent times — whether I have done well or badly in it — that came from my own judgment, grounded on many reasons. First, all truth of doctrine depends either on reason or on Scripture, both of which lend credibility to many writers but never receive credibility from any. Second, the matters in question here are not matters of fact but matters of right, in which there is no place for witnesses. Third, there is scarcely one of those old writers who does not sometimes contradict both himself and the others, which makes their testimony insufficient. Fourth, opinions taken purely on the credit of antiquity are not really the judgment of those who cite them, but words that pass — like yawning — from mouth to mouth. Fifth, men often have a fraudulent design when they stud their corrupt doctrine with the cloves of other men's wit. Sixth, I do not find that the ancients they cite considered it an ornament to do the same with those who wrote before them. Seventh, it is a sign of indigestion when Greek and Latin sentences come back up unchewed, as they usually do — unchanged. Lastly, though I revere those men of ancient times who either wrote the truth clearly or set us on a better path to find it out for ourselves, I think nothing is owed to antiquity itself. For if we are going to revere age, the present is the oldest age there is. And if it is the age of the writer we revere — I am not sure that the men generally given such honor were any more ancient when they wrote than I am, who am writing now. But consider it carefully, and the praise of ancient authors proceeds not from reverence for the dead, but from the competition and mutual envy of the living.

To conclude: there is nothing in this whole discourse, nor in what I wrote before on the same subject in Latin, that I can perceive to be contrary either to the Word of God or to good morals, or that tends to disturb the public tranquility. Therefore I think it may profitably be printed, and even more profitably taught in the universities — if those to whom the judgment of such things belongs agree. For since the universities are the fountains of civil and moral doctrine, from which the preachers and the gentry draw such water as they find there and sprinkle it on the people (both from the pulpit and in their conversation), there certainly ought to be great care taken to keep that water pure — pure both from the venom of heathen politicians and from the incantation of deceiving spirits. By that means most men, knowing their duties, will be less liable to serve the ambition of a few discontented persons in their schemes against the state; they will be less aggrieved by the taxes necessary for their peace and defense; and the governors themselves will have less cause to maintain, at the common expense, any larger army than is necessary to secure the public liberty against the invasions and encroachments of foreign enemies.

And so I have brought to an end my discourse of civil and ecclesiastical government, occasioned by the disorders of the present time — without partiality, without aiming at any person, and with no other design than to set before men's eyes the mutual relation between protection and obedience, of which the condition of human nature and the laws of God, both natural and positive, require an inviolable observance. And though in the upheaval of states there can be no very favorable constellation for truths of this kind to be born under — facing the angry glare of those who are dissolving an old government, and seeing only the backs of those who are erecting a new one — still I cannot think this book will be condemned at this time, either by the public judge of doctrine or by anyone who desires the continuance of the public peace. And in that hope I return to my interrupted speculation on natural bodies, in which — if God gives me the health to finish it — I hope the novelty will please as much as the novelty in this doctrine of the artificial body has tended to offend. For such truth as opposes no man's profit, nor his pleasure, is to all men welcome.
