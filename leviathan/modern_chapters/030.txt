Chapter 29: The Things That Weaken a Commonwealth, or Tend to Dissolve It

The Dissolution of Commonwealths Comes from Defective Founding

Nothing that mortals make can be immortal. Still, if men actually had the use of the reason they claim to have, their commonwealths could at least be secured against dying of internal diseases — those seeds of natural mortality I spoke of before. By the nature of their founding, they are designed to live as long as mankind itself, or as long as the laws of nature, or as long as justice itself — the thing that gives them life. So when a commonwealth comes apart, not through outside violence but through internal disorder, the fault does not lie in men as the material; it lies in men as the makers and arrangers of it.

Here is what happens. Men eventually grow weary of the irregular jostling and hacking at one another, and they desire with all their hearts to fit themselves together into one firm and lasting building. But they lack two things: the art of making good laws to square their actions by, and the humility and patience to let the rough, awkward edges of their present greatness be chiseled off. So without the help of a very able architect, they cannot be assembled into anything but a rickety structure — one that barely lasts out their own lifetime, and is certain to fall on the heads of their children.

Among the infirmities of a commonwealth, then, I will count first those that arise from a defective founding. These resemble the diseases of a natural body that come from defective procreation.

Lack of Absolute Power

Here is one such defect: a man, in order to obtain a kingdom, sometimes settles for less power than the peace and defense of the commonwealth actually require. The result is this. When the power he set aside has to be picked up again for the public safety, taking it back looks like an unjust act — and that appearance disposes great numbers of men, whenever the occasion presents itself, to rebel. It is exactly like the bodies of children begotten by diseased parents: they are subject either to early death, or to purging the bad quality inherited from their flawed conception by breaking out in boils and scabs.

And when kings deny themselves some necessary power like this, it is not always out of ignorance of what their office requires — though sometimes it is. Often it is out of a hope of taking the power back whenever they please. In this they reason badly. Because the people who will hold them to their promises will be propped up against them by foreign commonwealths, which, for the good of their own subjects, let slip few chances to weaken their neighbors. That is how Thomas Becket, Archbishop of Canterbury, was supported against Henry the Second by the Pope — the subjection of churchmen to the commonwealth having been waived by William the Conqueror at his coronation, when he swore an oath not to infringe the liberty of the Church. And that is how the barons — whose power William Rufus had inflated, to win their help in shifting the succession from his elder brother to himself, to a degree incompatible with sovereign power — were backed by the French in their rebellion against King John.

Nor does this happen only in monarchy. The official style of the ancient Roman commonwealth was "The Senate and People of Rome" — and neither senate nor people claimed the whole power. This first produced the seditions of Tiberius Gracchus, Caius Gracchus, Lucius Saturninus, and others; and afterward the wars between the senate and the people under Marius and Sulla, and again under Pompey and Caesar — ending in the extinction of their democracy and the setting up of monarchy.

The people of Athens bound themselves on only one single point: that no man, on pain of death, should propose renewing the war for the island of Salamis. And yet because of that one restriction, they would have had an enemy permanently stationed at the very gates of their city — if Solon had not spread the word that he was mad, and then, in the gestures and costume of a madman, proposed the war in verse to the people who crowded around him. Such is the damage, and such are the tricks, that every commonwealth is forced into when its power is limited even a little.

Private Judgment of Good and Evil

In the second place, I observe the diseases of a commonwealth that come from the poison of seditious doctrines. One of these is: "Every private man is the judge of good and evil actions."

This is true in the state of nature, where there are no civil laws. It is also true under civil government, in cases the law has not settled. But otherwise it is plain that the measure of good and evil actions is the civil law, and the judge is the legislator — who is always the representative of the commonwealth. From this false doctrine, men are inclined to debate with themselves and dispute the commands of the commonwealth, and then to obey or disobey them as their private judgments see fit. And by that, the commonwealth is distracted and weakened.

The Erroneous Conscience

Another doctrine repugnant to civil society is: "Whatever a man does against his conscience is sin." It depends on the same presumption — making oneself the judge of good and evil. For a man's conscience and his judgment are the same thing, and just as the judgment can be wrong, so can the conscience.

So yes: a man subject to no civil law sins in everything he does against his conscience, because he has no other rule to follow but his own reason. But it is not so for the man who lives in a commonwealth. There, the law is the public conscience — the one he has already agreed to be guided by. Otherwise, in such diversity as there is of private consciences — which are nothing but private opinions; the most their owners actually know is that they think so — the commonwealth would inevitably be torn apart, and no man would dare obey the sovereign power any further than it seemed good in his own eyes.

The Pretense of Inspiration

It has also commonly been taught that "faith and holiness are not attained by study and reason, but by supernatural inspiration, or infusion." Grant that, and I do not see why any man should give a reason for his faith; or why every Christian should not also be a prophet; or why any man should take the law of his country, rather than his own inspiration, as the rule of his conduct. And so we fall back into the same fault: taking it on ourselves to judge of good and evil — or making judges of it out of those private men who claim to be supernaturally inspired — to the dissolution of all civil government.

Faith comes by hearing, and hearing comes by those circumstances that bring us into the presence of the people who speak to us. Those circumstances are all arranged by God Almighty — and yet they are not supernatural; it is only that the number of them working together in every effect is too great to observe. Faith and holiness are, indeed, not very common; but they are not miracles. They are brought about by education, discipline, correction, and other natural means, by which God works them in his chosen, at whatever time he thinks fit.

And these three opinions, so destructive to peace and government, have in this part of the world come chiefly from the tongues and pens of unlearned theologians, who, stringing together the words of Holy Scripture in ways that do not agree with reason, do all they can to make men think that holiness and natural reason cannot stand together.

Making the Sovereign Subject to the Civil Laws

A fourth opinion repugnant to the nature of a commonwealth is this: "The holder of the sovereign power is subject to the civil laws."

It is true that sovereigns are all subject to the laws of nature, because those laws are divine and cannot be repealed by any man or any commonwealth. But to the laws that the sovereign himself makes — that is, that the commonwealth makes — he is not subject. To be subject to the laws is to be subject to the commonwealth, that is, to the sovereign representative, that is, to himself; and that is not subjection, but freedom from the laws. And here is why the error matters: because it sets the laws above the sovereign, it also sets a judge above him, and a power to punish him — which is to make a new sovereign. And then, by the same reasoning, a third sovereign to punish the second; and so on forever, to the confusion and dissolution of the commonwealth.

Giving Subjects Absolute Property

A fifth doctrine that tends to dissolve a commonwealth is: "Every private man has an absolute property in his goods — a property that excludes even the right of the sovereign."

Every man does indeed have a property that excludes the right of every other subject. But he has it only from the sovereign power; without that power's protection, every other man would have an equal right to the same things. If the right of the sovereign is also shut out, then he cannot perform the office they put him into — which is to defend them both from foreign enemies and from each other's injuries — and consequently there is no longer a commonwealth.

And if the property of subjects does not exclude the sovereign representative's right to their goods, much less does it exclude his right to their offices of judging and enforcement, in which they represent the sovereign himself.

Dividing the Sovereign Power

There is a sixth doctrine, plainly and directly against the very essence of a commonwealth, and it is this: "The sovereign power may be divided." For what is dividing the power of a commonwealth, other than dissolving it? Powers divided destroy each other. As I showed before, these rights are incommunicable and inseparable. And for these doctrines men are chiefly indebted to certain professors of the law, who labor to make the laws depend on their own learning rather than on the legislative power.

Imitating Neighboring Nations

Along with false doctrine, the example of a different form of government in a neighboring nation often disposes men to alter the form already established. That is how the people of the Jews were stirred up to reject God and demand that the prophet Samuel give them a king "after the manner of the nations." That is how the lesser cities of Greece were continually convulsed by the seditions of aristocratic and democratic factions — one party in almost every commonwealth wanting to imitate the Spartans, the other the Athenians. And I have no doubt that many men were content to watch the recent troubles in England out of imitation of the Low Countries — supposing that all it took to grow rich was to change, as the Dutch had done, the form of their government.

For man's nature is, of itself, prone to crave novelty. So when men are provoked toward it by the nearness of neighbors who have grown rich by it, it is almost impossible for them not to embrace the agitators who urge them to change. They love the first beginnings of the disorder even while they are tormented by its continuance — like hot-blooded men who, having caught the itch, tear at themselves with their own nails until they can no longer stand the pain.

Imitating the Greeks and Romans

As for rebellion against monarchy in particular, one of its most frequent causes is the reading of the political books and histories of the ancient Greeks and Romans. From these books, young men — and everyone else who lacks the antidote of solid reason — receive a strong and delightful impression of the great exploits of war achieved by the commanders of those armies, and along with it a pleasing notion of everything else those nations did. They imagine that all that prosperity came not from the rivalry of particular men, but from the virtue of their popular form of government — never considering the constant seditions and civil wars produced by the defects of that very system.

From the reading of such books, I say, men have taken it upon themselves to kill their kings — because the Greek and Latin writers, in their books and discourses on politics, make it lawful and even praiseworthy for any man to do so, provided that before he does it, he first calls his king a tyrant. For they do not say that regicide — the killing of a king — is lawful; they say that tyrannicide — the killing of a tyrant — is lawful. (And a tyrant, remember, is nothing but a monarch disliked.) From the same books, those who live under a monarch form the opinion that subjects in a popular commonwealth enjoy liberty, while in a monarchy they are all slaves. Notice: I say those who live under a monarchy form that opinion — not those who actually live under popular government, for they find no such thing.

In sum, I cannot imagine anything more damaging to a monarchy than allowing such books to be publicly read without immediately applying the correctives of careful teachers, fit to draw out their venom. And that venom I will not hesitate to compare to the bite of a mad dog — a disease the physicians call hydrophobia, or fear of water. The man so bitten suffers a continual torment of thirst, and yet he recoils from water, as if the poison were laboring to turn him into a dog. In just the same way, when a monarchy has once been bitten to the quick by those democratic writers who never stop snarling at that form of government, what it needs most is a strong monarch — and yet, out of a kind of tyrannophobia, a fear of being strongly governed, the people abhor him when they have him.

Just as there have been doctors who hold that there are three souls in a man, so there are also those who think there can be more souls — that is, more sovereigns — than one in a commonwealth. They set up a "supremacy" against the sovereignty; canons against laws; a "ghostly" authority against the civil. They work on men's minds with words and distinctions that in themselves signify nothing, but betray by their very obscurity that there walks abroad — invisibly, some think — another kingdom, a kind of kingdom of fairies, in the dark.

Now it is plain that the civil power and the power of the commonwealth are the same thing; and that supremacy, and the power of making canons and granting church offices, implies a commonwealth. It follows that where one man is sovereign and another supreme — where one can make laws and another can make canons — there must be two commonwealths over the very same subjects. That is a kingdom divided against itself, and it cannot stand. For all the empty distinction between "temporal" and "ghostly," they are still two kingdoms, and every subject is subject to two masters. The ghostly power claims the right to declare what is sin, and so by consequence claims to declare what is law — sin being nothing but the transgression of the law. The civil power, meanwhile, claims to declare what is law. So every subject must obey two masters, each insisting that his commands be treated as law — which is impossible. Or, if there is really only one kingdom, then either the civil power, which is the power of the commonwealth, must be subordinate to the ghostly, or the ghostly must be subordinate to the temporal — in which case there is no supremacy but the temporal.

When these two powers oppose each other, then, the commonwealth cannot help but stand in great danger of civil war and dissolution. The civil authority, being more visible, and standing in the clearer light of natural reason, cannot fail to draw to itself, in every age, a very considerable part of the people. And the spiritual authority, though it stands in the darkness of Scholastic distinctions and hard words, can never lack a party big enough to trouble a commonwealth, and sometimes to destroy it — because the fear of darkness and ghosts is greater than other fears.

And this is a disease that may quite fitly be compared to epilepsy, or the falling sickness — which the Jews took to be a kind of possession by spirits — in the natural body. In that disease, an unnatural spirit, or wind, in the head obstructs the roots of the nerves. Shaking them violently, it takes away the motion they should naturally receive from the power of the soul in the brain, and so causes violent and irregular motions in the limbs — what men call convulsions — so that the person seized by it falls down, sometimes into the water and sometimes into the fire, like a man deprived of his senses. So too in the body politic. When the spiritual power moves the members of a commonwealth by the terror of punishments and the hope of rewards — which are its nerves — in ways other than the civil power, which is the soul of the commonwealth, would move them, and suffocates the people with strange and hard words, it either overwhelms the commonwealth with oppression, or throws it into the fire of a civil war.

Mixed Government

Sometimes, even in purely civil government, there is more than one soul. This happens when the power of raising money (which is the nutritive faculty) depends on a general assembly; the power of military conduct and command (which is the motive faculty) depends on one man; and the power of making laws (which is the rational faculty) depends on the chance agreement not only of those two but of a third party as well. This endangers the commonwealth — sometimes for lack of consent to good laws, but most often for lack of the nourishment necessary to its life and motion.

Few people see it, but such a government is not government at all; it is the division of the commonwealth into three factions. Men call it "mixed monarchy," but the truth is that it is not one independent commonwealth but three independent factions — not one representative person, but three. In the kingdom of God there may be three persons who are independent without any breach of unity in the God who reigns; but where men reign — men subject to a diversity of opinions — it cannot be so. So if the king bears the person of the people, and the general assembly also bears the person of the people, and another assembly bears the person of a part of the people, they are not one person and one sovereign, but three persons and three sovereigns.

To what disease in the natural body of man I can exactly compare this deformity of a commonwealth, I do not know. But I once saw a man who had another man growing out of his side, with a head, arms, chest, and stomach of his own. If he had had yet another man growing out of his other side, the comparison would have been exact.

Lack of Money

So far I have named the diseases of a commonwealth that pose the greatest and most immediate danger. There are others, not so great, that are nevertheless worth observing.

First: the difficulty of raising money for the necessary uses of the commonwealth, especially as war approaches. This difficulty arises from the opinion every subject holds that he has a property in his lands and goods that excludes the sovereign's right to the use of them. The result is this. The sovereign power, which foresees the necessities and dangers of the commonwealth — finding the flow of money to the public treasury blocked by the tight grip of the people — ought to extend itself to meet and prevent those dangers at their beginnings. Instead it contracts itself as long as it can; and when it can contract no longer, it wrestles with the people through legal stratagems to obtain little sums, which do not suffice; until at last it is forced to break open the way violently for an immediate supply, or perish. Driven often to these extremities, it finally either brings the people back to their proper temper, or the commonwealth must die.

We may compare this distemper very aptly to an ague — a malarial fever. The fleshy parts grow congealed, or are blocked by venomous matter, so that the veins, which by their natural course empty into the heart, are not supplied from the arteries as they should be. First comes a cold contraction and trembling of the limbs; then a hot, violent effort of the heart to force a passage for the blood. Until it can do that, it makes do with the small refreshment of cooling things, until — if nature is strong enough — it finally breaks the resistance of the obstructed parts and sweats out the venom. If nature is too weak, the patient dies.

Monopolies, and the Abuses of Tax-Farmers

Again, there is sometimes in a commonwealth a disease resembling pleurisy. That is when the treasure of the commonwealth, flowing out of its proper channels, pools in excessive abundance in one or a few private men, through monopolies or through the farming of the public revenues — just as the blood in a pleurisy, getting into the membrane of the chest, breeds an inflammation there, attended by fever and painful stabbing.

Popular Men

The popularity of a powerful subject is also a dangerous disease, unless the commonwealth has very good security for his loyalty. The people, who ought to receive their motion from the authority of the sovereign, are drawn away from their obedience to the laws by the flattery and reputation of an ambitious man, to follow someone of whose virtues and designs they know nothing. This is generally more dangerous in a popular government than in a monarchy, because an army can easily be made to believe that it is the people. That is how Julius Caesar — set up by the people against the senate — having won the affections of his army, made himself master of senate and people alike. And this maneuver of popular and ambitious men is plain rebellion, and may be compared to the effects of witchcraft.

The Excessive Size of a Town; the Multitude of Corporations

Another infirmity of a commonwealth is the immoderate size of a single town, when it can supply, out of its own limits, the manpower and the funding of a great army. So too the great number of corporations, which are like so many lesser commonwealths in the bowels of a greater one — like worms in the entrails of a natural man.

The Liberty of Disputing Against the Sovereign Power

To these we may add the liberty of disputing against absolute power, taken by self-styled experts in political prudence. Bred mostly in the dregs of the people, yet animated by false doctrines, they are perpetually meddling with the fundamental laws, to the harassment of the commonwealth — like the little worms physicians call ascarides.

We may further add the insatiable appetite — the bulimia — of enlarging dominion, with the often incurable wounds taken from the enemy in the process; and the wens — those useless lumps of growth — of conquests never united with the body, which are many times a burden, and lost with less danger than they are kept; and likewise the lethargy of ease, and the consumption brought on by riot and extravagant spending.

The Dissolution of the Commonwealth

Lastly: when, in a war foreign or internal, the enemies win a final victory — so that the forces of the commonwealth no longer keep the field, and there is no further protection for subjects in their loyalty — then the commonwealth is DISSOLVED, and every man is at liberty to protect himself by whatever means his own discretion suggests.

For the sovereign is the public soul, giving life and motion to the commonwealth; and when that soul expires, the members are no more governed by it than the carcass of a man is governed by his departed — though immortal — soul. The right of a sovereign monarch cannot be extinguished by anyone else's act; but the obligation of the members can be. A man who lacks protection may seek it anywhere; and when he has found it, he is obliged — without the fraudulent pretense of having submitted merely out of fear — to protect his protector for as long as he is able. But when the power of a sovereign assembly is once suppressed, its right perishes utterly, because the assembly itself is extinct — and so there is no possibility for that sovereignty to ever re-enter.
