Chapter 27: Of Crimes, Excuses, and Extenuations

What Sin Is

A SIN is not only a violation of a law, but also any contempt for the lawgiver. For such contempt is a breach of all his laws at once. So sin can consist not only in committing an act, or speaking words the laws forbid, or failing to do what the law commands, but also in the intention or purpose to break the law. The mere purpose to break the law is already a degree of contempt toward the one whose job it is to see the law enforced.

But notice the line here. To take pleasure in the mere imagination of possessing another man's goods, servants, or wife — with no intention of taking them from him by force or fraud — is no breach of the law that says "Thou shalt not covet." Nor is it a sin to take pleasure in imagining or dreaming of the death of a man from whose life you expect nothing but damage and grief. The sin begins when you resolve to put some act in execution that tends toward it. For to be pleased by the fiction of something that would please you if it were real is a passion so built into the nature of man — and of every other living creature — that to make it a sin would be to make a sin of being human. Thinking this through has made me consider those people too severe, both on themselves and on others, who maintain that the first stirrings of the mind are sins, even when checked by the fear of God. Though I confess it is safer to err on that side than on the other.

What a Crime Is

A CRIME is a sin that consists in committing — by deed or word — what the law forbids, or omitting what it has commanded. So every crime is a sin; but not every sin is a crime. To intend to steal or kill is a sin, even if it never shows itself in word or deed: God, who sees the thoughts of man, can lay it to his charge. But until it appears in something done or said, from which a human judge can argue the intention, it does not have the name of crime.

The Greeks observed this distinction in their words: hamartema on one side, and egklema or aitia on the other. The first (translated "sin") means any swerving from the law whatsoever; the latter two (translated "crime") mean only that kind of sin for which one man may accuse another. For intentions that never appear in any outward act, there is no place for human accusation. The Latins did the same: peccatum, which is sin, covers every kind of deviation from the law; but crimen — a word they derive from cerno, "to perceive" — means only such sins as can be made to appear before a judge, and therefore are not mere intentions.

Where There Is No Civil Law, There Is No Crime

From this relation of sin to law, and of crime to civil law, several things follow.

First: where law ceases, sin ceases. But because the law of nature is eternal, violation of covenants, ingratitude, arrogance, and all acts contrary to any moral virtue can never cease to be sins.

Second: where the civil law ceases, crimes cease. For when no law remains but the law of nature, there is no place for accusation — every man is then his own judge, accused only by his own conscience and cleared by the uprightness of his own intention. So when his intention is right, his act is no sin; when it is not, his act is a sin — but not a crime.

Third: when the sovereign power ceases, crime ceases too. For where there is no such power, there is no protection to be had from the law, and so everyone may protect himself by his own strength. No man, in setting up a sovereign power, can be supposed to have given away the right of preserving his own body — the very thing all sovereignty was established to secure. But this holds only for those who did not themselves help to destroy the power that protected them. For that was a crime from the beginning.

Ignorance of the Law of Nature Excuses No One

The source of every crime is some defect of the understanding, or some error in reasoning, or some sudden force of the passions. The defect in understanding is ignorance; in reasoning, erroneous opinion.

Ignorance, in turn, comes in three sorts: ignorance of the law, of the sovereign, and of the penalty.

Ignorance of the law of nature excuses no one, because every man who has reached the use of reason is supposed to know that he ought not do to another what he would not have done to himself. So wherever a man goes, if he does anything contrary to that law, it is a crime. If a man comes here from the Indies and persuades people to receive a new religion, or teaches them anything that tends toward disobedience of this country's laws — however sincerely convinced he is of the truth of what he teaches — he commits a crime, and may justly be punished for it. Not only because his doctrine is false, but because he is doing what he would never approve in another: namely, someone traveling from here to there and trying to change the religion in his country. But ignorance of the civil law does excuse a man in a foreign country, until that law is declared to him — because until then, no civil law binds him.

Ignorance of the Civil Law Sometimes Excuses

Likewise, if the civil law of a man's own country has not been declared clearly enough for him to know it if he wishes, and the action is not against the law of nature, his ignorance is a good excuse. In all other cases, ignorance of the civil law excuses nothing.

Ignorance of the Sovereign Does Not Excuse

Ignorance of the sovereign power in the place where a man ordinarily lives does not excuse him; he ought to take notice of the power by which he has been protected there.

Ignorance of the Penalty Does Not Excuse

Ignorance of the penalty, where the law has been declared, excuses no one. For in breaking the law — which without fear of penalty would not be a law at all, just empty words — he takes on the penalty even if he does not know what it is. Whoever voluntarily does any action accepts all the known consequences of it; and punishment is a known consequence of breaking the laws in every commonwealth. If the punishment is already fixed by law, he is subject to that; if not, he is subject to arbitrary punishment. And this is only reasonable: he who does injury with no limit but his own will should suffer punishment with no limit but the will of him whose law he violated.

Punishments Declared Before the Fact Excuse from Greater Punishments After It

But when a penalty is attached to the crime in the law itself, or has usually been inflicted in similar cases, the offender is excused from any greater penalty. For a punishment known in advance, if it is not great enough to deter men from the action, is an invitation to it. When men compare the benefit of their injustice with the harm of their punishment, by necessity of nature they choose what looks best for themselves. So when they are punished more than the law had previously fixed, or more than others were punished for the same crime, it is the law that tempted and deceived them.

Nothing Can Be Made a Crime by a Law Made After the Fact

No law made after an act is done can make that act a crime. If the act is against the law of nature, then the law existed before the act; and a positive law cannot be taken notice of before it is made, and therefore cannot bind. But when the law forbidding an act was made before the act was done, the man who does it is liable to a penalty ordained afterward — provided no lesser penalty had been made known before, either in writing or by example — for the reason just given.

False Principles of Right and Wrong as Causes of Crime

From defect in reasoning — that is, from error — men are prone to violate the laws in three ways.

First, by presuming false principles. Men observe how, in all places and all ages, unjust actions have been authorized by the force and victories of those who committed them; how powerful men break through the cobweb laws of their country while the weaker sort, and those who failed in their schemes, are counted the only criminals. From this they adopt principles and grounds of reasoning such as: "Justice is just an empty word. Whatever a man can get by his own effort and risk is his own. The practice of all nations cannot be unjust. The examples of former times are good arguments for doing the same again" — and many more of that kind. Grant these, and no act can be a crime in itself; it must be made one not by the law, but by the success of those who commit it. The same act becomes virtuous or vicious as Fortune pleases — so that what Marius makes a crime, Sulla makes meritorious, and Caesar (the same laws still standing) turns back into a crime again, to the perpetual disturbance of the peace of the commonwealth.

False Teachers Misinterpreting the Law of Nature

Second, by false teachers, who either misinterpret the law of nature so that it contradicts the civil law, or teach as laws their own doctrines, or traditions of former times, that are inconsistent with the duty of a subject.

False Inferences from True Principles, by Teachers

Third, by erroneous inferences from true principles. This commonly happens to men who are hasty and rash in concluding and deciding what to do — the kind who have a great opinion of their own understanding and believe that matters of this sort require no time or study, only common experience and a good natural wit, which no man thinks himself short of. Yet the knowledge of right and wrong, which is no less difficult, is something no one would claim without great and long study.

Of these defects in reasoning, none can excuse a crime (though some may extenuate one) in any man who claims to manage his own private business — much less in those who take on a public office. For they claim to possess the very reason whose absence they would plead as their excuse.

The Passions as Causes of Crime

Of the passions that most often cause crime, one is vainglory — a foolish overrating of one's own worth, as if differences in worth were the effect of one's wit, or riches, or blood, or some other natural quality, rather than depending on the will of those who hold the sovereign authority. From this comes a presumption that the punishments ordained by the laws, and extended generally to all subjects, ought not to be inflicted on them with the same rigor with which they are inflicted on poor, obscure, simple men — the people lumped together under the name of "the vulgar."

Presumption of Riches

So it commonly happens that those who measure their value by the greatness of their wealth venture on crimes, hoping to escape punishment by corrupting public justice, or by buying a pardon with money or other rewards.

And of Friends

And those who have a multitude of powerful relatives, and popular men who have gained a reputation among the multitude, take courage to violate the laws from the hope of overpowering the very authority whose job it is to enforce them.

And of Wisdom

And those who have a great and false opinion of their own wisdom take it upon themselves to criticize the actions and question the authority of those who govern, and to unsettle the laws with their public talk, so that nothing shall count as a crime except what their own designs require to be one. These same men are also prone to all the crimes that consist in craft and in deceiving their neighbors, because they think their schemes are too subtle to be detected.

These, I say, are the effects of a false presumption of their own wisdom. For of those who are the first movers in the disturbance of a commonwealth — which can never happen without a civil war — very few live long enough to see their new designs established. The benefit of their crimes goes to posterity, and to the very people who would least have wished it: which proves they were not as wise as they thought. And those who deceive in the hope of not being noticed usually deceive themselves — the darkness they believe they hide in being nothing but their own blindness. They are no wiser than children who think everything is hidden when they cover their own eyes.

And in general, all vainglorious men (unless they are also timid) are subject to anger, being more prone than others to read contempt into the ordinary liberties of conversation. And there are few crimes that anger cannot produce.

Hatred, Lust, Ambition, and Covetousness as Causes of Crime

As for the passions of hate, lust, ambition, and covetousness — what crimes they tend to produce is so obvious to every man's experience and understanding that nothing needs to be said of them, except this: they are infirmities so fastened to the nature of man, and of all other living creatures, that their effects cannot be prevented except by an extraordinary use of reason, or a constant severity in punishing them.

In the things men hate, they find a continual and unavoidable irritation; so either a man's patience must be endless, or he must relieve himself by removing the power of whatever torments him. The first is difficult; the second is often impossible without some violation of the law. Ambition and covetousness are passions that press on a man perpetually, while reason is not perpetually present to resist them — so whenever the hope of impunity appears, their effects follow. And as for lust, what it lacks in duration it makes up in vehemence, which is enough to outweigh the apprehension of any mild or uncertain punishment.

Fear Is Sometimes a Cause of Crime, When the Danger Is Neither Present Nor Bodily

Of all the passions, the one that least inclines men to break the laws is fear. Indeed — setting aside some generous natures — fear is the only thing that makes men keep the laws when breaking them promises profit or pleasure. And yet in many cases a crime may be committed through fear.

For not every fear justifies the action it produces. Only the fear of bodily hurt — what we call bodily fear — and only when a man can see no way to be delivered from it except by the action. A man is attacked, fears immediate death, and sees no escape except by wounding his attacker: if he wounds him to death, this is no crime. For no man, at the making of a commonwealth, is supposed to have abandoned the defense of his life or limbs where the law cannot arrive in time to help him.

But to kill a man because I conclude from his actions or his threats that he will kill me when he can — when I have the time and means to demand protection from the sovereign power — is a crime.

Again: a man receives insulting words, or some petty injuries (the kind for which the lawmakers assigned no punishment, and which they thought no man with the use of reason should even take notice of), and he is afraid that unless he avenges them he will fall into contempt, and so be exposed to the same injuries from others. To avoid this he breaks the law, protecting himself for the future by the terror of his private revenge. This is a crime. For the hurt is not bodily but imaginary — and (though in this corner of the world a custom begun not many years ago, among young and vain men, has made it feel real) it is so slight that a gallant man, sure of his own courage, cannot be bothered to notice it.

A man may also stand in fear of spirits — through his own superstition, or through giving too much credit to other men who tell him of strange dreams and visions — and so be made to believe these spirits will hurt him for doing or not doing various things, when doing or not doing them is contrary to the laws. What is done or omitted on these grounds is not excused by this fear; it is a crime. For as I showed back in the second chapter, dreams are naturally nothing but the imaginings that remain in sleep after the impressions our senses received while awake; and when men, by some accident, are not sure they have been asleep, dreams seem to be real visions. So the man who presumes to break the law on the strength of his own dream, or another man's, or a pretended vision, or any other fancy about the power of invisible spirits beyond what the commonwealth permits, is abandoning the law of nature — which is a certain offense — to follow the imagery of his own or some other private man's brain. He can never know whether it means anything or nothing, nor whether the man who reports his dream is telling the truth or lying. And if every private man were given leave to do this (as they all must be, by the law of nature, if any one of them is), no law could be made to hold, and every commonwealth would dissolve.

Crimes Are Not Equal

From these different sources of crime, it is already clear that not all crimes are of the same weight, as the Stoics of old maintained. There is room not only for EXCUSE, by which what seemed a crime is proved to be none at all, but also for EXTENUATION, by which a crime that seemed great is made less. For though all crimes equally deserve the name of injustice — just as every deviation from a straight line is equally crookedness, which the Stoics rightly observed — it does not follow that all crimes are equally unjust, any more than that all crooked lines are equally crooked. Failing to see this, the Stoics held it as great a crime to kill a hen against the law as to kill one's father.

Total Excuses

The only thing that totally excuses an act, and strips it of the nature of a crime, is whatever at the same time takes away the obligation of the law. For once an act is committed against the law, if the one who committed it is bound by that law, it can be nothing other than a crime.

The lack of means to know the law totally excuses: a law a man has no means to inform himself of is not binding on him. But lack of diligence in inquiring shall not count as lack of means. Nor shall any man who claims reason enough to manage his own affairs be supposed to lack the means to know the laws of nature, because they are known by the very reason he claims to have. Only children and madmen are excused from offenses against the natural law.

Where a man is a captive, or in the power of the enemy — and he is in the power of the enemy when his person or his means of living is — and this is through no fault of his own, the obligation of the law ceases. He must obey the enemy or die, and so such obedience is no crime. For no man is obliged, when the protection of the law fails, not to protect himself by the best means he can.

If a man is compelled by the terror of immediate death to do an act against the law, he is totally excused, because no law can oblige a man to abandon his own preservation. And even supposing such a law were binding, a man would reason like this: "If I do not do it, I die now; if I do it, I die later; so by doing it, I gain time of life." Nature itself compels him to the act.

When a man lacks food or some other thing necessary for his life, and cannot preserve himself in any way except by some act against the law — as when, in a great famine, he takes by force or stealth the food he cannot obtain for money or by charity, or when, in defense of his life, he snatches away another man's sword — he is totally excused, for the reason just given.

Excuses Against the Author

Again, acts done against the law by the authority of another are excused, by that authority, as against the author — for no man ought to accuse, in another who is merely his instrument, an act that is his own. But the act is not thereby excused against any third person injured by it; for in the violation of the law, both the author and the actor are criminals.

From this it follows that when the man or assembly that holds the sovereign power commands a man to do something contrary to a former law, the doing of it is totally excused. The sovereign ought not condemn it, since he is its author; and what cannot justly be condemned by the sovereign cannot justly be punished by anyone else. Besides, when the sovereign commands something to be done against his own former law, the command, as to that particular act, is an abrogation of the law.

If the man or assembly holding the sovereign power disclaims some right essential to sovereignty, and a subject thereby acquires some liberty inconsistent with the sovereign power — that is, with the very existence of a commonwealth — then if the subject refuses to obey a command contrary to that granted liberty, it is nevertheless a sin, and contrary to the duty of a subject. He ought to take notice of what is inconsistent with sovereignty, because sovereignty was erected by his own consent and for his own defense; and he ought to recognize that such a liberty was granted only through ignorance of its evil consequences. But if he not only disobeys, but also resists a public official in the execution of the command, then it is a crime — because he could have had his wrong righted, without any breach of the peace, by complaint.

The degrees of crime are measured on several scales: first, by the malignity of the source or cause; second, by the contagion of the example; third, by the mischief of the effect; and fourth, by the circumstances of times, places, and persons.

Presumption of Power Aggravates

The same act against the law is a greater crime if it proceeds from a presumption of strength, riches, or friends to resist those who must enforce the law, than if it proceeds from a hope of not being discovered, or of escaping by flight. For presuming on impunity by force is a root from which springs, at all times and under every temptation, a contempt of all laws; whereas in the latter case, the apprehension of danger that makes a man flee makes him more obedient for the future. A crime we know to be a crime is greater than the same crime committed under a false belief that it is lawful. For he who commits it against his own conscience is presuming on his force or other power, which encourages him to do it again; but he who does it in error, once the error is shown to him, conforms to the law.

Evil Teachers Extenuate

He whose error proceeds from the authority of a teacher, or of a publicly authorized interpreter of the law, is not as much at fault as he whose error proceeds from stubbornly following his own principles and reasoning. For what is taught by one who teaches by public authority, the commonwealth teaches; and it carries a resemblance of law until the same authority overrules it. In all crimes that do not contain a denial of the sovereign power, and are not against an evident law, this excuses totally. But he who grounds his actions on his own private judgment ought to stand or fall according to whether that judgment was right or wrong.

Examples of Impunity Extenuate

The same act is a greater crime if it has been consistently punished in other men than if there have been many prior examples of impunity. For those examples are so many hopes of impunity given by the sovereign himself; and since he who supplies a man with such a hope and presumption of mercy — encouraging him to offend — has his own part in the offense, he cannot reasonably charge the offender with the whole of it.

Premeditation Aggravates

A crime arising from a sudden passion is not as great as the same crime arising from long deliberation. In the first case there is room for extenuation in the common infirmity of human nature. But he who acts with premeditation has looked around carefully; he has cast his eye on the law, on the punishment, and on the consequences for human society — and in committing the crime, he has held all of these in contempt and ranked them below his own appetite. Yet no suddenness of passion is enough for a total excuse. For all the time between first knowing the law and committing the act shall be counted as time for deliberation, because a man ought, by meditating on the law, to correct the irregularity of his passions.

Where the law is publicly and diligently read and explained before all the people, an act against it is a greater crime than where men are left without such instruction — left to inquire after the law with difficulty, uncertainty, and interruption of their work, and to be informed by private men. In the latter case, part of the fault falls on common infirmity; but in the former there is plain negligence, which carries some contempt of the sovereign power.

Tacit Approval by the Sovereign Extenuates

Acts that the law expressly condemns, but that the lawmaker by other clear signs of his will tacitly approves, are lesser crimes than the same acts condemned both by the law and by the lawmaker. For since the will of the lawmaker is a law, in this case two contradictory laws appear — which would excuse totally, if men were bound to take notice of the sovereign's approval by any signs other than what his commands express. But because there are punishments attached not only to breaking his law, but also to keeping it, he is in part a cause of the transgression, and so cannot reasonably charge the whole crime to the offender.

Take an example. The law condemns duels, and makes the punishment capital. Yet on the other side, the man who refuses a duel is exposed to contempt and scorn, with no remedy — and is sometimes judged by the sovereign himself unworthy of any command or promotion in war. If, on that account, he accepts the duel — considering that all men lawfully try to win the good opinion of those who hold the sovereign power — he ought not in reason to be punished rigorously, since part of the fault may be laid on the punisher. I say this not because I wish for liberty of private revenge, or any other kind of disobedience, but to urge a care in governors not to wink at anything sideways which they directly forbid. The examples princes set, for those who watch them, are and always have been more powerful in governing men's actions than the laws themselves. And though it is our duty to do not what they do but what they say, that duty will never be performed until it pleases God to give men an extraordinary and supernatural grace to follow that precept.

Comparing Crimes by Their Effects

Again, if we compare crimes by the mischief of their effects: first, the same act is greater when its damage reaches many than when it hurts only a few. And so, when an act hurts not only in the present but also — by example — in the future, it is a greater crime than one that hurts only in the present. The former is a fertile crime, multiplying to the hurt of many; the latter is barren.

To maintain doctrines contrary to the religion established in the commonwealth is a greater fault in an authorized preacher than in a private person. So is living profanely or unchastely, or doing any irreligious act whatsoever. Likewise, for a professor of the law to maintain any point, or do any act, that tends to weaken the sovereign power is a greater crime than in another man. So too with a man whose reputation for wisdom is such that his counsels are followed and his actions imitated by many: his act against the law is a greater crime than the same act in another. For such men do not merely commit a crime — they teach it as law to everyone else. And in general, all crimes are made greater by the scandal they give: by becoming stumbling blocks to the weak, who look not so much at the road they walk as at the light that other men carry before them.

Laesae Majestas

Also, acts of hostility against the present state of the commonwealth are greater crimes than the same acts done to private men, for the damage extends to everyone. Such are betraying the strongholds of the commonwealth, or revealing its secrets to an enemy; all attempts on the representative of the commonwealth, whether a monarch or an assembly; and all endeavors, by word or deed, to diminish its authority, either now or in the succession. The Latins call these crimes crimina laesae majestatis — crimes of injured majesty — and they consist in a design or act contrary to a fundamental law.

Bribery and False Testimony

Likewise, crimes that make judgments worthless are greater crimes than injuries done to one or a few persons. To take money to give false judgment, or false testimony, is a greater crime than to cheat a man out of the same sum, or even a greater one, by other means. For not only is the man wronged who falls by such judgments, but all judgments are made useless, and the door is opened to force and private revenge.

Embezzlement

Also, robbery and embezzlement of the public treasury or revenues is a greater crime than robbing or defrauding a private man, because to rob the public is to rob many at once.

Counterfeiting Authority

So too the counterfeit usurpation of public office, and the counterfeiting of public seals or public coin, are greater crimes than counterfeiting a private man's person or his seal, because the fraud spreads its damage to many.

Crimes Against Private Men, Compared

Of acts against the law done to private men, the greater crime is the one whose damage, in the common opinion of mankind, is felt most keenly. And therefore:

To kill against the law is a greater crime than any other injury that leaves life preserved.

To kill with torment is greater than simply to kill.

Mutilation of a limb is greater than robbing a man of his goods.

Robbing a man of his goods by terror of death or wounds is greater than by secret theft.

Secret theft is greater than taking by consent fraudulently obtained.

Violation of chastity by force is greater than by seduction.

And of a married woman, greater than of an unmarried one.

For all these things are commonly valued this way — though some men feel a given offense more, and some less. But the law looks not to the particular person, but to the general inclination of mankind.

That is why the offense men take at insults — in words or gestures — when they produce no harm beyond the momentary grief of the man insulted, has been ignored in the laws of the Greeks, the Romans, and other commonwealths both ancient and modern. They supposed the true cause of such grief to lie not in the insult (which gets no grip on men conscious of their own virtue) but in the small-mindedness of the man who is offended by it.

A crime against a private man is also much aggravated by the person, the time, and the place. To kill one's parent is a greater crime than to kill anyone else, for the parent ought to have the honor of a sovereign — though he has surrendered his power to the civil law — because he held it originally by nature. And to rob a poor man is a greater crime than to rob a rich man, because to the poor man it is a damage felt far more keenly.

And a crime committed at a time or place appointed for worship is greater than the same crime committed at another time or place, for it proceeds from a greater contempt of the law.

Many other cases of aggravation and extenuation could be added; but from those I have set down, it is easy for anyone to take the measure of any other crime proposed.

What Public Crimes Are

Lastly: because in almost all crimes an injury is done not only to some private man but also to the commonwealth, the same crime is called a PUBLIC CRIME when the accusation is brought in the name of the commonwealth, and a private crime when brought in the name of a private man. The pleas are named accordingly: public pleas — judicia publica, or pleas of the Crown — and private pleas. So in an accusation of murder, if the accuser is a private man, the plea is a private plea; if the accuser is the sovereign, the plea is a public plea.
