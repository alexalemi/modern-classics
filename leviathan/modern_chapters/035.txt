Chapter 34: What "Spirit," "Angel," and "Inspiration" Mean in the Books of Holy Scripture

How "Body" and "Spirit" Are Used in Scripture

The foundation of all true reasoning is the constant meaning of words. And in the doctrine that follows, that meaning does not depend (as in natural science) on the will of the writer, nor (as in everyday conversation) on common usage, but on the sense the words carry in Scripture. So before I go any further, I need to determine, out of the Bible itself, the meaning of certain words whose ambiguity could make whatever I build on them obscure or open to dispute. I will begin with the words BODY and SPIRIT — which in the language of the Scholastics are called "corporeal substance" and "incorporeal substance."

The word "body," in its most general sense, means that which fills or occupies some definite space, or imagined place; something that does not depend on our imagination, but is a real part of what we call the universe. For since the universe is the sum of all bodies, there is no real part of it that is not also a body, and nothing is properly a body that is not also part of that sum of all bodies, the universe. And because bodies are subject to change — that is, to appearing differently to the senses of living creatures — a body is also called a substance, meaning a subject of various properties: sometimes moving, sometimes standing still; seeming to our senses sometimes hot, sometimes cold; now one color, smell, taste, or sound, now another. This diversity of seeming, produced by the different ways bodies act on the organs of our senses, we attribute to alterations in the bodies that act on us, and we call these their "accidents" — their changing properties. Taken this way, "substance" and "body" mean the same thing. And therefore "incorporeal substance" is a pair of words that, when joined together, destroy each other — as if a man were to say "an incorporeal body."

But ordinary people do not call the whole universe "body" — only those parts of it they can detect by touch, resisting their force, or by sight, blocking their view of what lies beyond. So in common speech, air and airy substances are usually not counted as bodies. Instead, whenever people notice their effects, they call them wind, or breath, or (because the Latin word for these is spiritus) spirits — as when they call the airy substance that gives life and motion to the body of a living creature its "vital and animal spirits." As for those idols of the brain that show us bodies where no bodies are — in a mirror, in a dream, or to a fevered brain awake — they are (as the Apostle says of all idols generally) nothing. Nothing at all, I say, in the place where they seem to be; and in the brain itself, nothing but commotion, arising either from the action of external objects or from the disordered agitation of our organs of sense. People too busy to search into the causes of these things do not know, on their own, what to call them. So they are easily persuaded by those whose learning they revere: some to call them bodies, and to think them made of air compacted by supernatural power, because sight judges them to be solid; others to call them spirits, because touch finds nothing in the place where they appear to resist the fingers. So the proper meaning of "spirit" in common speech is either a subtle, fluid, invisible body, or else a ghost — some other idol or phantasm of the imagination. Beyond that, the word has many metaphorical meanings. Sometimes it stands for a disposition or inclination of the mind: the disposition to contradict what other men say is "a spirit of contradiction"; a disposition to uncleanness, "an unclean spirit"; perverseness, "a froward spirit"; sullenness, "a dumb spirit"; and an inclination to godliness and God's service, "the Spirit of God." Sometimes it stands for some exceptional ability, or extraordinary passion, or disease of the mind — as when great wisdom is called "the spirit of wisdom," and madmen are said to be "possessed with a spirit."

I find no other meaning of "spirit" anywhere. And where none of these meanings can make sense of the word in Scripture, the passage simply lies beyond human understanding, and our faith in it consists not in our opinion but in our submission — as in all the places where God is said to be a spirit, or where "the Spirit of God" means God himself. For the nature of God is incomprehensible: we understand nothing of what he is, only that he is. And therefore the attributes we give him are not meant to tell one another what he is, nor to express our opinion of his nature, but to express our desire to honor him with the names we count most honorable among ourselves.

First: "Spirit of God" Sometimes Means a Wind, or Breath

Genesis 1:2 — "The Spirit of God moved upon the face of the waters." Here, if "the Spirit of God" meant God himself, then motion would be attributed to God, and consequently place — and motion and place are intelligible only of bodies, not of "incorporeal substances." In that case the passage is beyond our understanding; for we can conceive of nothing that moves without changing place, or that has no dimension, and whatever has dimension is body. But the meaning of these words is best understood from a similar passage, Genesis 8:1. When the earth was covered with water, as in the beginning, and God intended to make the waters recede and uncover the dry land again, he uses similar words: "I will bring my Spirit upon the earth, and the waters shall be diminished." There, "Spirit" means a wind — that is, air, or spirit, in motion — which might well be called (as in the earlier passage) the Spirit of God, because it was God's work.

Second: For Extraordinary Gifts of Understanding

Genesis 41:38 — Pharaoh calls the wisdom of Joseph "the Spirit of God." Joseph having advised him to find a wise and discreet man and set him over the land of Egypt, Pharaoh says: "Can we find such a man as this, in whom is the Spirit of God?" And in Exodus 28:3, God says, "You shall speak to all who are wise-hearted, whom I have filled with the spirit of wisdom, to make Aaron garments, to consecrate him." Here extraordinary skill — even though only in making garments — is called the Spirit of God, because it is the gift of God. The same usage appears again in Exodus 31:3-6 and 35:31. And in Isaiah 11:2-3, the prophet, speaking of the Messiah, says: "The Spirit of the Lord shall abide upon him, the spirit of wisdom and understanding, the spirit of counsel and fortitude, and the spirit of the fear of the Lord." Plainly this does not mean so many ghosts, but so many eminent graces that God would give him.

Third: For Extraordinary Passions

In the Book of Judges, an extraordinary zeal and courage in defense of God's people is called the Spirit of God — as when it stirred Othniel, Gideon, Jephthah, and Samson to deliver them from servitude (Judges 3:10, 6:34, 11:29, 13:25, 14:6, 19). And of Saul, on the news of the Ammonites' outrage against the men of Jabesh-Gilead, it is said (1 Samuel 11:6) that "the Spirit of God came upon Saul, and his anger" (or, as the Latin has it, his fury) "was kindled greatly." It is not likely that a ghost is meant here, but an extraordinary zeal to punish the cruelty of the Ammonites. In the same way, the Spirit of God that came upon Saul when he was among the prophets praising God with songs and music (1 Samuel 19:20) should be understood not as a ghost, but as an unexpected and sudden zeal to join them in their devotions.

Fourth: For the Gift of Prediction by Dreams and Visions

The false prophet Zedekiah says to Micaiah (1 Kings 22:24), "Which way did the Spirit of the Lord go from me to speak to you?" This cannot be understood of a ghost; for Micaiah had declared the outcome of the battle to the kings of Israel and Judah as something seen in a vision, not as something a spirit spoke inside him.

In the same way it appears throughout the books of the prophets that although they spoke "by the Spirit of God" — that is, by a special grace of prediction — their knowledge of the future came not from a ghost within them, but from some supernatural dream or vision.

Fifth: For Life

Genesis 2:7 says, "God made man of the dust of the earth, and breathed into his nostrils (spiraculum vitae — the breath of life) the breath of life, and man became a living soul." There, the breath of life breathed in by God means nothing more than that God gave him life. And Job 27:3, "as long as the Spirit of God is in my nostrils," means nothing more than "as long as I live." So in Ezekiel 1:20, "the spirit of life was in the wheels" is equivalent to "the wheels were alive." And in Ezekiel 2:30, "the spirit entered into me, and set me on my feet" means "I recovered my vital strength" — not that any ghost or incorporeal substance entered into his body and possessed it.

Sixth: For Subordination to Authority

In Numbers 11:17, God says, "I will take of the spirit which is upon you, and will put it upon them, and they shall bear the burden of the people with you" — meaning the seventy elders. Then two of the seventy are said to have prophesied in the camp; some complained about it, and Joshua asked Moses to forbid them, which Moses would not do. From this it appears that Joshua did not know they had received authority to prophesy — and that they prophesied according to the mind of Moses; that is, by a spirit, or authority, subordinate to his own.

In the same sense we read (Deuteronomy 34:9) that "Joshua was full of the spirit of wisdom, because Moses had laid his hands upon him" — that is, because Moses had ordained him to carry forward the work he himself had begun (bringing God's people into the promised land) but, cut short by death, could not finish.

In the same sense it is said (Romans 8:9), "If any man does not have the Spirit of Christ, he is none of his" — meaning not the ghost of Christ, but submission to his doctrine. So also 1 John 4:2: "By this you shall know the Spirit of God: every spirit that confesses that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh is of God." What is meant is the spirit of unfeigned Christianity — submission to the central article of Christian faith, that Jesus is the Christ — which cannot be interpreted as a ghost.

Likewise the words of Luke 4:1, "And Jesus, full of the Holy Ghost" (that is, as Matthew 4:1 and Mark 1:12 express it, "of the Holy Spirit"), may be understood as zeal to do the work for which he was sent by God the Father. But to interpret it as a ghost is to say that God himself (for so our Savior was) was filled with God — which is improper and meaningless. How we ever came to translate "spirits" by the word "ghosts" — a word that signifies nothing, in heaven or on earth, except the imaginary inhabitants of man's brain — I will not examine. But this I do say: the word "spirit" in the text means no such thing. It means either, properly, a real substance, or, metaphorically, some extraordinary ability or passion of the mind or of the body.

Seventh: For Airy Bodies

The disciples of Christ, seeing him walk on the sea (Matthew 14:26 and Mark 6:49), supposed him to be a spirit — meaning by that an airy body, not a phantasm. For it is said that they all saw him, which cannot be true of delusions of the brain (those are not common to many people at once, the way visible bodies are, but private to each, because everyone's imagination differs); it can be true only of bodies. The same applies where the same apostles took him for a spirit (Luke 24:3, 7). So also in Acts 12:15: when St. Peter was delivered out of prison and no one would believe it, and the servant girl said he was at the door, they said, "It is his angel." By that they must have meant a corporeal substance — or else we must say the disciples themselves followed the common opinion of both Jews and Gentiles that some such apparitions were not imaginary but real, things that did not need a man's imagination in order to exist. The Jews called these "spirits" and "angels," good or bad — just as the Greeks called the same things "demons." And some such apparitions may indeed be real and substantial: that is, subtle bodies, which God can form by the same power by which he formed all things, and can use as ministers and messengers — that is to say, angels — to declare his will and carry it out when he pleases, in an extraordinary and supernatural way. But once he has formed them, they are substances endowed with dimension; they take up room and can be moved from place to place, which is the distinctive mark of bodies. Therefore they are not incorporeal ghosts — that is, ghosts that are in no place; that is, that are nowhere; that is, that seeming to be something, are nothing. But if "corporeal" is taken in the most common sense, for substances perceptible by our external senses, then an "incorporeal substance" is something not imaginary but real: namely, a thin, invisible substance, but one that has the same dimensions found in grosser bodies.

What an Angel Is

The name ANGEL signifies, in general, a messenger — and most often, a messenger of God. And "a messenger of God" means anything that makes known his extraordinary presence: that is, an extraordinary manifestation of his power, especially by a dream or vision.

Concerning the creation of angels, Scripture delivers nothing at all. That they are spirits is often repeated; but by the name of "spirit" is meant — in Scripture and in common speech alike, among Jews and Gentiles alike — sometimes thin bodies, like the air, the wind, or the vital and animal spirits of living creatures; and sometimes the images that rise in the imagination in dreams and visions, which are not real substances but accidents of the brain. Yet when God raises such images supernaturally, to signify his will, it is not improper to call them God's messengers — that is to say, his angels.

The Gentiles commonly mistook the imagery of the brain for things really existing outside them, independent of imagination, and out of those images they built their opinions of demons, good and evil — which, because they seemed to exist on their own, they called substances, and because they could not feel them with their hands, incorporeal. In just the same way the Jews, on the same grounds — with nothing in the Old Testament compelling them to it — held the general opinion (the sect of the Sadducees excepted) that those apparitions, which it pleased God sometimes to produce in men's imaginations for his own service (and which he therefore called his angels), were substances independent of the imagination: permanent creatures of God. Those they thought favorable to them they esteemed the angels of God; those they thought would hurt them they called evil angels, or evil spirits — such as the spirit of Python, and the spirits of madmen, of lunatics, and of epileptics. For they considered anyone troubled with such diseases to be possessed by demons.

But if we examine the places in the Old Testament where angels are mentioned, we will find that in most of them nothing can be understood by the word "angel" except some image raised, supernaturally, in the imagination, to signify the presence of God in the performance of some supernatural work. And therefore in the remaining places, where the angels' nature is not spelled out, it may be understood the same way.

For we read in Genesis 16 that the same apparition is called not only an angel but God: what verse 7 calls "the angel of the Lord" says to Hagar in verse 10, "I will multiply your descendants exceedingly" — that is, it speaks in the person of God. And this apparition was not even a visible figure, but a voice. From this it is plain that "angel" there signifies nothing but God himself, who caused Hagar, supernaturally, to apprehend a supernatural voice testifying to God's special presence there. Why, then, should the angels that appeared to Lot — who are called "men" in Genesis 19:13, and whom Lot, though they were two, addresses (verse 18) as one, and that one as God (for the words are, "Lot said to them, Oh, not so, my Lord") — not be understood as images of men supernaturally formed in the imagination, just as before, by "angel," a fancied voice was understood? When the angel called to Abraham from heaven to stay his hand from slaying Isaac (Genesis 22:11), there was no apparition at all, only a voice — which was nevertheless properly enough called a messenger, or angel, of God, because it declared God's will supernaturally; and this spares us the trouble of supposing any permanent ghosts. The angels Jacob saw on the ladder of heaven (Genesis 28:12) were a vision of his sleep — therefore only imagination, and a dream; yet because they were supernatural, and signs of God's special presence, those apparitions are not improperly called angels. The same is to be understood in Genesis 31:11, where Jacob says, "The angel of the Lord appeared to me in my sleep." For an apparition made to a man in his sleep is what all men call a dream, whether the dream is natural or supernatural. And what Jacob there calls an angel was God himself; for the same angel says (verse 13), "I am the God of Bethel."

So too in Exodus 14:9: the angel that went before the army of Israel to the Red Sea, and then moved behind it, is (verse 19) the Lord himself. And he appeared not in the form of a beautiful man, but by day in the form of a pillar of cloud and by night in the form of a pillar of fire; and yet this pillar was the entire apparition — the whole angel promised to Moses (Exodus 14:9) to guide the army. For this cloudy pillar is said to have descended, and stood at the door of the Tabernacle, and talked with Moses.

There you see motion and speech — the things commonly attributed to angels — attributed to a cloud, because the cloud served as a sign of God's presence; and it was no less an angel than if it had had the form of a man, or of a child of the greatest imaginable beauty, or wings, as angels are usually painted, for the false instruction of common people. For it is not their shape but their use that makes them angels: and their use is to be signs of God's presence in supernatural operations. So when Moses (Exodus 33:14) asked God to go along with the camp, as he had always done before the making of the golden calf, God did not answer "I will go," nor "I will send an angel in my place," but this: "My presence shall go with you."

To list every place in the Old Testament where the name "angel" occurs would take too long. So, to cover them all at once, I say this: there is no text in that part of the Old Testament which the Church of England holds canonical from which we can conclude that there is, or ever has been created, any permanent thing — understood by the name of spirit or angel — that does not have quantity; that cannot be divided by the understanding, that is, considered in parts, so that one part is in one place and the next part in the next place; and, in sum, that is not (taking "body" for whatever is something, or somewhere) corporeal. In every such place, the sense will bear the interpretation of "angel" as "messenger" — just as John the Baptist is called an angel, and Christ the Angel of the Covenant; and just as (by the same analogy) the dove and the fiery tongues, being signs of God's special presence, might also be called angels. We do find in Daniel two names of angels, Gabriel and Michael. But it is clear from the text itself (Daniel 12:1) that by Michael is meant Christ — not as an angel, but as a prince; and that Gabriel (like the similar apparitions made to other holy men in their sleep) was nothing but a supernatural phantasm, by which it seemed to Daniel, in his dream, that two saints were talking, and one of them said to the other, "Gabriel, let us make this man understand his vision." For God has no need to distinguish his celestial servants by names, which are useful only to the short memories of mortals. Nor is there any place in the New Testament from which it can be proved that angels — except where the word stands for those men whom God has made the messengers and ministers of his word or works — are things both permanent and incorporeal. That they are permanent may be gathered from the words of our Savior himself (Matthew 25:41), where he says it shall be said to the wicked on the last day, "Go, you cursed, into everlasting fire, prepared for the Devil and his angels." This passage is clear evidence for the permanence of evil angels (unless we suppose that "the Devil and his angels" may be understood as the Church's adversaries and their ministers) — but then it is incompatible with their immateriality, because everlasting fire is no punishment to substances incapable of suffering, as all incorporeal things would be. Angels, therefore, are not proved incorporeal from that text. Likewise where St. Paul says (1 Corinthians 6:3), "Do you not know that we shall judge the angels?" and 2 Peter 2:4, "For if God did not spare the angels that sinned, but cast them down into hell," and Jude 1:6, "And the angels that did not keep their first estate, but left their own habitation, he has reserved in everlasting chains under darkness until the judgment of the last day" — though these prove the permanence of angelic nature, they also confirm its materiality. And Matthew 22:30: "In the resurrection men neither marry nor are given in marriage, but are as the angels of God in heaven." But in the resurrection men will be permanent and not incorporeal; so, therefore, are the angels.

There are various other passages from which the same conclusion can be drawn. To anyone who understands what the words "substance" and "incorporeal" signify — taking "incorporeal" to mean not a subtle body, but no body at all — they imply a contradiction: so that to say an angel or spirit is, in that sense, an incorporeal substance is in effect to say there is no angel or spirit at all. Considering, therefore, what the word "angel" signifies in the Old Testament, and the nature of dreams and visions that come to men in the ordinary course of nature, I was inclined to this opinion: that angels were nothing but supernatural apparitions of the imagination, raised by the special and extraordinary operation of God, to make his presence and his commandments known to mankind, and chiefly to his own people. But the many passages of the New Testament, and our Savior's own words — in texts where there is no suspicion that the Scripture has been corrupted — have extorted from my feeble reason an acknowledgment and belief that there are also angels that are substantial and permanent. But to believe they are in no place — that is to say, nowhere; that is to say, nothing — as those who insist they are incorporeal (though indirectly) do say, cannot be proved from Scripture.

What Inspiration Is

On the meaning of the word "spirit" depends the meaning of the word INSPIRATION. Taken properly, inspiration is nothing but the blowing into a man of some thin and subtle air or wind, the way a man fills a bladder with his breath. Or, if spirits are not corporeal but exist only in the imagination, then it is the blowing in of a phantasm — which is improper to say, and impossible, for phantasms do not exist; they only seem to be something. The word, therefore, is used in Scripture only metaphorically. When it is said (Genesis 2:7) that God inspired into man the breath of life, nothing more is meant than that God gave him vital motion. We are not to think that God first made a living breath and then blew it into Adam after he was made — whether that breath was real or only apparent — but only, as Acts 17:25 puts it, "that he gave him life and breath": that is, made him a living creature. And when it is said (2 Timothy 3:16) that "all Scripture is given by inspiration from God" — speaking there of the Scripture of the Old Testament — it is an easy metaphor, signifying that God inclined the spirit, or mind, of those writers to write what would be useful for teaching, reproving, correcting, and instructing men in the way of righteous living. But where St. Peter (2 Peter 1:21) says that "prophecy came not in old time by the will of man, but holy men of God spoke as they were moved by the Holy Spirit," by "the Holy Spirit" is meant the voice of God in a dream or supernatural vision — which is not inspiration. Nor, when our Savior breathed on his disciples and said, "Receive the Holy Spirit," was that breath the Spirit; it was a sign of the spiritual graces he gave to them. And though it is said of many, and of our Savior himself, that he was "full of the Holy Spirit," that fullness is not to be understood as an infusion of the substance of God, but as an accumulation of his gifts — such as the gift of holiness of life, of tongues, and the like — whether attained supernaturally or by study and hard work; for in every case they are the gifts of God. So likewise where God says (Joel 2:28), "I will pour out my Spirit upon all flesh, and your sons and your daughters shall prophesy, your old men shall dream dreams, and your young men shall see visions," we are not to take this in the literal sense, as if his Spirit were like water, subject to pouring out or pouring in; rather, it is as if God had promised to give them prophetic dreams and visions. For the strict use of the word "infused," in speaking of the graces of God, is an abuse of the word; those graces are virtues, not bodies to be carried here and there and poured into men as into barrels.

In the same way, to take "inspiration" in its literal sense — to say that good spirits entered into men to make them prophesy, or evil spirits into those who became frenzied, lunatic, or epileptic — is not to take the word as Scripture uses it. For "the Spirit" there stands for the power of God, working by causes unknown to us. So too the wind (Acts 2:2) that is said to fill the house where the apostles were assembled on the day of Pentecost is not to be understood as the Holy Spirit, which is the Deity itself, but as an external sign of God's special working on their hearts — working to produce in them the internal graces and holy virtues he thought necessary for the performance of their apostleship.
