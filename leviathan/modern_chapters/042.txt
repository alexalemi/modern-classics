Chapter 41: The Office of Our Blessed Savior

The Three Parts of the Office of Christ

We find in Holy Scripture three parts of the office of the Messiah. The first is that of a REDEEMER, or savior. The second is that of a pastor, counselor, or teacher — that is, of a prophet sent from God to convert those whom God has elected to salvation. The third is that of a king — an eternal king, but under his Father, as Moses and the high priests were in their several times.

And to these three parts correspond three different times. Our redemption he accomplished at his first coming, by the sacrifice in which he offered himself up for our sins on the cross. Our conversion he worked partly then, in his own person, and partly works now, through his ministers — and he will continue working it until his coming again. And after his coming again shall begin his glorious reign over his elect, which is to last eternally.

His Office as Redeemer

To the office of a redeemer — that is, of one who pays the ransom of sin, and that ransom is death — it belongs that he was sacrificed, and thereby bore upon his own head, and carried away from us, our iniquities, in the manner God had required. Not that the death of one man, even a man without sin, can satisfy for the offenses of all men by the strict rigor of justice. It satisfies through the mercy of God, who ordained such sacrifices for sin as he was pleased, in his mercy, to accept.

Under the old law (as we may read in Leviticus 16), the Lord required that once every year an atonement be made for the sins of all Israel, priests and people alike. To perform it, Aaron alone was to sacrifice a young bull for himself and the priests; and for the rest of the people, he was to receive from them two young goats. One of these he was to sacrifice. But the other was the SCAPEGOAT: Aaron was to lay his hands on its head, and by confessing the iniquities of the people, lay them all on that head; and then, by some suitable man, he was to have the goat led into the wilderness, there to escape — and carry away with it the iniquities of the people.

As the sacrifice of the one goat was a sufficient price for the ransom of all Israel — sufficient because acceptable to God — so the death of the Messiah is a sufficient price for the sins of all mankind, because no more was required. Our Savior Christ's sufferings seem to be prefigured here as clearly as in the offering of Isaac, or in any other type — prophetic foreshadowing — of him in the Old Testament. He was both the sacrificed goat and the scapegoat. "He was oppressed, and he was afflicted; he opened not his mouth; he was brought as a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep is dumb before the shearer, so he opened not his mouth" (Isaiah 53:7) — there he is the sacrificed goat. "He has borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows" (verse 4); and again, "the Lord has laid on him the iniquities of us all" (verse 6) — and so he is the scapegoat. "He was cut off from the land of the living for the transgression of my people" (verse 8) — there again he is the sacrificed goat. And once more, "he shall bear their sins" (verse 11) — there he is the scapegoat. Thus the Lamb of God is equivalent to both of those goats: sacrificed, in that he died; and escaping, in his resurrection — raised at the fitting moment by his Father, and removed from the dwelling place of men in his ascension.

Christ's Kingdom Is Not of This World

Now, a redeemer has no title to the thing he redeems before the redemption is complete and the ransom paid; and this ransom was the death of the redeemer himself. So it is plain that our Savior, as man, was not king of those he redeemed before he suffered death — that is, during the time he lived bodily on the earth. I say he was not then king in the present, by virtue of the pact which the faithful make with him in baptism. Nevertheless, by renewing their pact with God in baptism, they were obliged to obey him as king — under his Father — whenever he should be pleased to take the kingdom upon him.

And this is exactly what our Savior himself says expressly: "My kingdom is not of this world" (John 18:36). Now Scripture speaks of only two worlds: this one, which stands until the day of judgment — which is therefore also called the last day — and the one that shall be a new heaven and a new earth. So the kingdom of Christ is not to begin until the general resurrection. That is what our Savior means when he says, "The Son of man shall come in the glory of his Father, with his angels; and then he shall reward every man according to his works" (Matthew 16:27). To reward every man according to his works is to exercise the office of a king; and this is not to happen until he comes in the glory of his Father, with his angels.

When our Savior says, "The scribes and Pharisees sit in Moses' seat; all therefore whatsoever they bid you do, that observe and do" (Matthew 23:2), he declares plainly that he assigns kingly power, for that time, not to himself but to them. He does the same where he says, "Who made me a judge or a divider over you?" (Luke 12:14), and "I came not to judge the world, but to save the world" (John 12:47).

And yet our Savior did come into this world so that he might be a king, and a judge, in the world to come. For he was the Messiah — that is, the Christ; that is, the anointed priest and the sovereign prophet of God. He was to have, in other words, all the power that was in Moses the prophet, in the high priests who succeeded Moses, and in the kings who succeeded the priests. And St. John says expressly, "The Father judges no man, but has committed all judgment to the Son" (John 5:22). This does not contradict the other passage, "I came not to judge the world" — for that one is spoken of the present world, the other of the world to come. So also with what is said of the second coming of Christ: "You that have followed me in the regeneration, when the Son of man shall sit on the throne of his glory, you shall also sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel" (Matthew 19:28).

The Purpose of Christ's Coming Was to Renew the Covenant of the Kingdom of God, and to Persuade the Elect to Embrace It — the Second Part of His Office

If, then, Christ had no kingdom in this world while he was on earth, what was the purpose of his first coming? It was to restore to God, by a new covenant, the kingdom which was his by the old covenant, but had been cut off by the rebellion of the Israelites in the election of Saul. To do this, he was to preach to them that he was the Messiah — that is, the king promised to them by the prophets — and to offer himself as a sacrifice for the sins of those who would, by faith, submit themselves to him; and, if the nation as a whole should refuse him, to call to his obedience such of the Gentiles as would believe in him.

So there are two parts of our Savior's office during his stay on earth: one, to proclaim himself the Christ; the other, by teaching and by working miracles, to persuade men — and prepare them — to live in such a way as to be worthy of the immortality that believers were to enjoy when he comes in majesty to take possession of his Father's kingdom. That is why he himself often calls the time of his preaching the REGENERATION. The regeneration is not properly a kingdom, and so it gave no one a warrant to deny obedience to the magistrates who then ruled — for he commanded his followers to obey those who sat in Moses' chair, and to pay tribute to Caesar. It was only an earnest — a down payment — of the kingdom of God that was to come, given to those to whom God had given the grace to be his disciples and to believe in him. For this reason the godly are said to be already in the kingdom of grace, as people already naturalized in that heavenly kingdom.

The Preaching of Christ Was Not Contrary to the Law of the Jews, Nor of Caesar

So far, then, there is nothing done or taught by Christ that tends to diminish the civil rights of the Jews or of Caesar. As for the commonwealth that then existed among the Jews: both those who ruled and those who were governed were all expecting the Messiah and the kingdom of God — which they could not have done if their laws had forbidden him, when he came, to manifest and declare himself. Since he did nothing but go about proving himself, by preaching and miracles, to be that very Messiah, he did nothing in this against their laws. The kingdom he claimed was to be in another world. He taught all men to obey, in the meantime, those who sat in Moses' seat. He allowed them to give Caesar his tribute, and he refused to take it upon himself to be a judge. How then could his words or actions be seditious, or tend to the overthrow of their civil government at the time?

But God had determined Christ's sacrifice as the means of bringing his elect back to their formerly covenanted obedience; and as the instrument for bringing this about, he made use of their malice and ingratitude. Nor was any of it contrary to the laws of Caesar. For though Pilate himself, to gratify the Jews, delivered him up to be crucified, yet before he did so, he pronounced openly that he found no fault in him. And as the title of his condemnation, Pilate put not what the Jews demanded — "that he pretended to be king" — but simply, "that he was King of the Jews"; and despite their clamor, he refused to alter it, saying, "What I have written, I have written."

The Third Part of His Office: To Be King — Under His Father — of the Elect

As for the third part of his office, which was to be king, I have already shown that his kingdom is not to begin until the resurrection. But then he shall be king — not only as God, in which sense he is king already, and ever shall be, of all the earth, by virtue of his omnipotence; but also, in a special way, king of his own elect, by virtue of the pact they make with him in their baptism. That is why our Savior says (Matthew 19:28) that his apostles shall sit upon twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel, "when the Son of man shall sit on the throne of his glory" — signifying that he shall reign then in his human nature. And again: "The Son of man shall come in the glory of his Father, with his angels, and then he shall reward every man according to his works" (Matthew 16:27). We may read the same in Mark 13:26 and 14:26, and — more expressly as to the time — in Luke 22:29-30: "I appoint unto you a kingdom, as my Father has appointed to me, that you may eat and drink at my table in my kingdom, and sit on thrones judging the twelve tribes of Israel." From all this it is plain that the kingdom of Christ, appointed to him by his Father, is not to be until the Son of man comes in glory and makes his apostles judges of the twelve tribes of Israel.

But here a man may ask: since there is no marriage in the kingdom of heaven, shall men really eat and drink there? What kind of eating is meant in this place? Our Savior expounds it himself: "Labor not for the meat which perishes, but for that meat which endures unto everlasting life, which the Son of man shall give you" (John 6:27). So eating at Christ's table means eating of the tree of life — that is to say, enjoying immortality in the kingdom of the Son of man. From these passages, and many more, it is evident that our Savior's kingdom is to be exercised by him in his human nature.

Christ's Authority in the Kingdom of God Is Subordinate to His Father

Again, he is to be king then in no other way than as subordinate — a viceregent of God the Father, as Moses was in the wilderness, as the high priests were before the reign of Saul, and as the kings were after it. For it is one of the prophecies concerning Christ that he should be like Moses in his office: "I will raise them up a Prophet from among their brethren, like unto thee, and will put my words into his mouth," says the Lord (Deuteronomy 18:18).

And this likeness to Moses shows plainly in the actions of our Savior himself while he lived on earth. As Moses chose twelve princes of the tribes to govern under him, so our Savior chose twelve apostles, who shall sit on twelve thrones and judge the twelve tribes of Israel. As Moses authorized seventy elders to receive the Spirit of God and to prophesy to the people — that is, as I have said before, to speak to them in the name of God — so our Savior also ordained seventy disciples to preach his kingdom and salvation to all nations. And as Moses, when a complaint was brought to him against certain of the seventy who prophesied in the camp of Israel, justified them in it, as serving under his government, so our Savior, when St. John complained to him of a certain man who cast out devils in his name, justified the man, saying, "Forbid him not, for he that is not against us is on our side" (Luke 9:50).

Again, our Savior resembled Moses in instituting sacraments — both the sacrament of admission into the kingdom of God, and the sacrament of commemoration of his deliverance of his elect from their miserable condition.

As the children of Israel had, for the sacrament of their reception into the kingdom of God before the time of Moses, the rite of circumcision — a rite that was omitted in the wilderness, and restored as soon as they came into the promised land — so also the Jews, before the coming of our Savior, had a rite of baptizing: washing with water all those Gentiles who embraced the God of Israel. St. John the Baptist used this rite in receiving all those who gave their names to the Christ, whom he preached as already come into the world; and our Savior instituted the same rite as a sacrament to be taken by all who believed in him.

Where the rite of baptism first came from is not formally stated in Scripture. But it may plausibly be thought an imitation of the law of Moses concerning leprosy, by which a leprous man was commanded to be kept out of the camp of Israel for a certain time; after which, being judged clean by the priest, he was admitted into the camp again after a solemn washing. This may well be a type of the washing in baptism, in which men cleansed of the leprosy of sin by faith are received into the Church with the solemnity of baptism. There is another conjecture, drawn from the ceremonies of the pagans of the past, in a case that rarely happens: when a man who was thought dead chanced to recover, other men were reluctant to keep company with him — as they would be reluctant to keep company with a ghost — unless he were received back into the number of men by washing, just as newborn children were washed from the uncleanness of their birth, which was a kind of new birth. This Greek ceremony, in the days when Judaea was under the dominion of Alexander and the Greeks who succeeded him, may plausibly enough have crept into the religion of the Jews. But since it is not likely our Savior would countenance a heathen rite, it is most likely that baptism proceeded from the legal ceremony of washing after leprosy.

As for the other sacrament — the eating of the paschal lamb — it is manifestly imitated in the sacrament of the Lord's Supper, in which the breaking of the bread and the pouring out of the wine keep in memory our deliverance from the misery of sin by Christ's passion, just as the eating of the paschal lamb kept in memory the deliverance of the Jews out of the bondage of Egypt.

Since the authority of Moses, then, was only subordinate — he was but a lieutenant to God — it follows that Christ, whose authority as man was to be like that of Moses, was no more than subordinate to the authority of his Father. The same is signified even more expressly by his teaching us to pray, "Our Father, thy kingdom come," and "For thine is the kingdom, the power, and the glory"; by the saying that he "shall come in the glory of his Father"; by what St. Paul says — "then comes the end, when he shall have delivered up the kingdom to God, even the Father" (1 Corinthians 15:24) — and by many other most express passages.

One and the Same God Is the Person Represented by Moses and by Christ

Our Savior, therefore, both in teaching and in reigning, represents — as Moses did — the person of God; the God who from that time forward, but not before, is called the Father. And being still one and the same substance, God is one person as represented by Moses, and another person as represented by his Son, the Christ. For since "person" is a term relative to a representer, a plurality of representers necessarily brings with it a plurality of persons — even though the substance represented is one and the same.
