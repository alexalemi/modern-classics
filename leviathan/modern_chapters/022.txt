Chapter 22: Of Subordinate Systems — Political and Private


The Different Kinds of Systems of People

Having spoken of the generation, form, and power of a commonwealth, the order of my subject brings me next to its parts. And first to systems, which resemble the similar parts — the muscles — of a natural body. By SYSTEMS I mean any number of men joined together in one interest or one business. Some of these are regular, and some irregular. Regular systems are those in which one man, or one assembly of men, is constituted representative of the whole number. All the rest are irregular.

Of regular systems, some are absolute and independent, subject to none but their own representative. Only commonwealths are of this kind, and of those I have spoken already in the five preceding chapters. The others are dependent — that is, subordinate to some sovereign power, to which every member, and the representative too, is subject.

Of subordinate systems, some are political and some private. Political systems — otherwise called bodies politic, or persons in law — are those made by authority from the sovereign power of the commonwealth. Private systems are those that subjects set up among themselves, or that are constituted by authority from a stranger. For no authority derived from a foreign power is public within the dominion of another; there, it is private.

And of private systems, some are lawful, some unlawful. Lawful are those the commonwealth allows; all others are unlawful. Irregular systems — those with no representative at all — consist only in a gathering of people. If such a gathering is not forbidden by the commonwealth and not made with evil design — like the flow of people to markets, or to public shows, or for any other harmless purpose — it is lawful. But when the intention is evil, or (if the number is considerable) unknown, it is unlawful.


In All Political Bodies, the Power of the Representative Is Limited

In bodies politic, the power of the representative is always limited; and what prescribes the limits is the sovereign power. For unlimited power is absolute sovereignty, and the sovereign in every commonwealth is the absolute representative of all the subjects. No one else, therefore, can be representative of any part of them except so far as the sovereign gives leave. And to give a body politic of subjects leave to have an absolute representative, for all intents and purposes, would be to abandon the government of that much of the commonwealth, and to divide the dominion — contrary to the people's peace and defense. The sovereign cannot be understood to do that by any grant which does not plainly and directly discharge them of their subjection. For when one reading of his words contradicts what he clearly intends, those words are not signs of his will; they are signs of error and miscalculation — to which all mankind is far too prone.

The bounds of the power given to the representative of a body politic are to be learned from two things: one, their writ, or letters from the sovereign; the other, the law of the commonwealth.


By Letters Patent

In the founding or acquisition of a commonwealth, which is independent, no writing is needed, because the power of its representative has no bounds except those set out by the unwritten law of nature. But in subordinate bodies, the necessary limitations — concerning their business, their times, and their places — are so various that they can neither be remembered without letters nor taken notice of unless those letters are patent: open letters, which can be read out to everyone, and sealed or certified with the seals or other permanent signs of the sovereign authority.


And by the Laws

And because such limitation is not always easy, or even possible, to set down in writing, the ordinary laws, common to all subjects, must determine what the representative may lawfully do in every case where the letters themselves are silent. And therefore:


When the Representative Is One Man, His Unauthorized Acts Are His Alone

In a body politic, if the representative is one man, then whatever he does in the person of the body that is not warranted by his letters or by the laws is his own act — not the act of the body, nor of any member of it besides himself. For beyond what his letters or the laws allow, he represents no one's person but his own. But what he does within those bounds is the act of every member. For everyone is author of the sovereign's acts, since the sovereign is their unlimited representative; and the act of a man who does not stray from the sovereign's letters is the act of the sovereign — so every member of the body is author of it.


When It Is an Assembly, the Act Belongs Only to Those Who Voted for It

But if the representative is an assembly, then whatever the assembly decrees without warrant from their letters or the laws is the act of the assembly — the body politic — and the act of every man by whose vote the decree was made. It is not the act of any man who, being present, voted against it, nor of any man who was absent, unless he voted by proxy. It is the act of the assembly because it was voted by the majority; and if it is a crime, the assembly may be punished as far as it is capable of punishment — by dissolution, or by forfeiture of its letters (which to such artificial and fictitious bodies is capital punishment), or, if the assembly has a common stock in which none of the innocent members has property, by a fine. For nature has exempted all bodies politic from bodily penalties. But those who did not give their vote are innocent, because the assembly cannot represent any man in things unwarranted by its letters, and so they are not implicated by its votes.


When the Representative Is One Man, He Alone Is Liable for Money Borrowed or Owed — Not the Members

If the person of the body politic rests in one man, and he borrows money from a stranger — that is, from someone not of the same body (for no letters need limit borrowing, since limiting the lending is left to men's own inclination) — the debt is the representative's. For if his letters gave him authority to make the members pay what he borrows, he would by consequence have sovereignty over them. In that case the grant would either be void, as proceeding from error — a failing common to human nature, and an insufficient sign of the granter's will — or, if the sovereign stands by it, then the representative is sovereign, and falls outside the present question, which concerns subordinate bodies only. No member, therefore, is obliged to pay the debt so borrowed; only the representative himself. For the lender, being a stranger to the letters and to the constitution of the body, counts as his debtors only those who are actually engaged; and since the representative can engage himself and no one else, the lender has him alone for debtor. He must pay it out of the common stock, if there is one, or, if there is none, out of his own estate.

If he comes into debt by contract, or by a fine, the case is the same.


When It Is an Assembly, Only Those Who Assented Are Liable

But when the representative is an assembly and the debt is owed to a stranger, then all those, and only those, are responsible for the debt who gave their votes to the borrowing, or to the contract that made it due, or to the act for which the fine was imposed. For every one of them, in voting, engaged himself for the payment. He who is author of the borrowing is obliged to pay even the whole debt — though once any one of them pays it, the rest are discharged.


If the Debt Is to a Member of the Assembly, Only the Body Is Obliged

But if the debt is owed to one of the assembly itself, then only the assembly is obliged to pay it, out of their common stock, if they have one. For having liberty of vote, if he voted that the money should be borrowed, he voted that it should be paid back. If he voted against the borrowing, or was absent, then by lending the money he votes for the borrowing after all, contradicting his earlier vote; he is bound by the later one, and becomes both borrower and lender. Consequently he cannot demand payment from any particular man, but only from the common treasury. And if that fails, he has no remedy and no complaint except against himself — who, knowing the acts of the assembly and its means to pay, and being under no compulsion, nevertheless lent his money through his own folly.


Protesting the Decrees of Political Bodies Is Sometimes Lawful — But Against the Sovereign Power, Never

From all this it is clear that in political bodies which are subordinate and subject to a sovereign power, it is sometimes not only lawful but prudent for a particular man to protest openly against the decrees of the representative assembly, and to have his dissent registered, or witnessed — because otherwise he may be obliged to pay debts contracted, and answer for crimes committed, by other men. But in a sovereign assembly that liberty is taken away: first, because he who protests there denies the assembly's sovereignty; and second, because whatever the sovereign power commands is, as far as the subject is concerned (though not always in the sight of God), justified by the command itself — for of every such command, every subject is the author.


Political Bodies for the Government of a Province, Colony, or Town

The variety of bodies politic is almost infinite. They are distinguished not only by the different kinds of business for which they are constituted — and the diversity there is beyond description — but also by their times, places, and numbers, which are subject to many limitations. As to their business, some are ordained for government. First, the government of a province may be committed to an assembly of men, in which all resolutions depend on the votes of the majority. That assembly is then a body politic, and its power is limited by its commission. The word "province" signifies a charge, or care of business, which the man whose business it is commits to another to administer for him and under him. So when one commonwealth contains several countries with laws distinct from one another, or far distant in place, and the administration of government is committed to different persons, those countries where the sovereign does not reside, but governs by commission, are called provinces.

But of a province governed by an assembly residing in the province itself, there are few examples. The Romans, who held sovereignty over many provinces, always governed them by presidents and praetors — not by assemblies, the way they governed the city of Rome and the adjacent territories. Likewise, when colonies went out from England to plant Virginia and the Summer Islands — Bermuda — though their government here at home was committed to assemblies in London, those assemblies never committed the government under them to any assembly there. They sent each plantation one governor. For though every man, where he can be present, naturally desires to participate in government, yet where men cannot be present, they are by nature also inclined to commit the government of their common interest to a monarchical rather than a popular form. You can see the same thing in men with great private estates: when they are unwilling to take the pains of managing their own business, they would rather trust one servant than an assembly of their friends or servants.

But however it stands in fact, we may suppose the government of a province or colony committed to an assembly. And when it is, what I have to say here is this: whatever debt that assembly contracts, and whatever unlawful act it decrees, is the act only of those who assented, not of any who dissented or were absent — for the reasons already given. Also, an assembly residing outside the bounds of the colony it governs cannot exercise any power over the persons or goods of any member of the colony, to seize them for debt or any other duty, in any place outside the colony itself; it has no jurisdiction or authority elsewhere, and is left to whatever remedy the law of that place allows it. And though the assembly has the right to impose a fine on any of its members who break the laws it makes, it has no right to enforce that fine outside the colony itself. What is said here of the rights of an assembly governing a province or colony applies equally to an assembly governing a town, a university, a college, a church, or any other government over the persons of men.

And generally, in all bodies politic, if any particular member thinks himself injured by the body itself, the hearing of his case belongs to the sovereign, and to those whom the sovereign has appointed — or shall appoint — as judges of such cases. It does not belong to the body itself. For in this case the whole body is merely his fellow subject. In a sovereign assembly it is otherwise: for there, if the sovereign is not the judge, even in his own cause, there can be no judge at all.


Political Bodies for the Ordering of Trade

In a body politic formed for the ordering of foreign trade, the most suitable representative is an assembly of all the members — that is, an assembly arranged so that every man who ventures his money may be present, if he chooses, at all the deliberations and resolutions of the body. To see why, consider the purpose for which merchants — who are free to buy and sell, export and import, each at his own discretion — nevertheless bind themselves together into one corporation.

It is true that few merchants can fill a whole ship with the goods they buy at home for export, or with what they buy abroad to bring home; so they need to join together in one society, where every man may either share in the gains in proportion to his stake, or take his own goods and sell what he transports or imports at whatever prices he sees fit. But that alone is no body politic, for there is no common representative to bind them to any law beyond what binds every other subject. No — the purpose of their incorporating is to make their gains greater, and that is done two ways: by being the only buyer and the only seller, both at home and abroad. So to grant a company of merchants the status of a corporation, a body politic, is to grant them a double monopoly: one as sole buyers, the other as sole sellers. For when a company is incorporated for trade with a particular foreign country, they alone export the goods marketable in that country — which makes them the sole buyer at home and the sole seller abroad. At home there is only one buyer, and abroad only one seller: both profitable to the merchant, because he thereby buys at home at lower rates and sells abroad at higher. And abroad there is only one buyer of foreign goods, and only one who sells them at home: both, again, profitable to the investors.

Of this double monopoly, one half is disadvantageous to the people at home, the other to foreigners. For at home, by their sole right of exportation, they set whatever price they please on the farming and handiwork of the people; and by their sole right of importation, whatever price they please on all the foreign goods the people need — both bad for the people. Conversely, by being sole seller of native goods abroad and sole buyer of foreign goods on the spot, they raise the price of the one and beat down the price of the other, to the foreigner's disadvantage. For where only one sells, the goods are dearer; and where only one buys, cheaper. Such corporations, therefore, are nothing but monopolies. Yet they would be very profitable for a commonwealth if they were bound into one body in foreign markets only, and left at liberty at home — every man free to buy and sell at whatever price he could.

The purpose of these bodies of merchants, then, is not any common benefit to the whole body (which in this case has no common stock except what is deducted from the members' individual stakes for building, buying, supplying, and manning ships), but the particular gain of each individual investor. It is reasonable, therefore, that every one of them be acquainted with the management of his own money — that is, that every one be a member of the assembly that has the power to order it, and be acquainted with its accounts. And so the representative of such a body must be an assembly at which every member may be present at the consultations, if he wishes.

If a body politic of merchants contracts a debt to a stranger by the act of their representative assembly, every member is liable by himself for the whole. For a stranger can take no notice of their private rules; he regards them simply as so many particular men, each obliged for the whole payment, until payment by any one of them discharges all the rest. But if the debt is owed to one of the company, the creditor is debtor for the whole to himself, and so cannot demand his debt from any particular man — only from the common stock, if there is one.

If the commonwealth imposes a tax on the body, it is understood to be laid on every member in proportion to his particular stake in the company. For in this case there is no other common stock than what is made up of their individual investments.

If a fine is laid on the body for some unlawful act, only those are liable by whose votes the act was decreed, or by whose assistance it was carried out; for in none of the rest is there any crime except belonging to the body — and if that were a crime, it is not theirs, because the body was ordained by the authority of the commonwealth.

If one of the members is in debt to the body, the body may sue him; but his goods cannot be seized, nor his person imprisoned, by the body's own authority — only by the authority of the commonwealth. For if they could do it by their own authority, they could by their own authority give judgment that the debt is due — which would be to make them judges in their own cause.


A Political Body for Giving Counsel to the Sovereign

These bodies made for the government of men, or of trade, are either perpetual, or limited to a time prescribed in writing. But there are also bodies whose duration is limited only by the nature of their business. For example, suppose a sovereign monarch, or a sovereign assembly, thinks fit to command the towns and other parts of the territory to send him their deputies, to inform him of the condition and needs of the subjects, or to advise with him on the making of good laws, or for any other purpose — dealing with them as with one person representing the whole country. Such deputies, having a place and time of meeting assigned to them, are, in that place and at that time, a body politic representing every subject of that dominion — but only for such matters as are put before them by the man or assembly that summoned them by the sovereign authority. And when it is declared that nothing more shall be proposed or debated by them, the body is dissolved. For if they were the absolute representative of the people, they would be the sovereign assembly — and then there would be two sovereign assemblies, two sovereigns, over the same people, which cannot stand with their peace. Therefore, once there is a sovereignty, there can be no absolute representation of the people except by it. As for the limits of how far such a body shall represent the whole people, those are set out in the writing by which they were summoned. For the people cannot choose their deputies for any other purpose than the one expressed in the writing directed to them by their sovereign.


A Regular and Lawful Private Body: the Family

Private bodies that are regular and lawful are those constituted without letters or any other written authority beyond the laws common to all other subjects. Because they are united under one representative person, they count as regular. Of this kind are all families, in which the father, or master, orders the whole household. He obliges his children and servants as far as the law permits, but no further; for none of them is bound to obey in actions the law has forbidden. In all other actions, for as long as they are under domestic government, they are subject to their fathers and masters as to their immediate sovereigns. For the father and the master, having been absolute sovereigns in their own families before commonwealths were instituted, lose afterward no more of their authority than the law of the commonwealth takes from them.


Regular but Unlawful Private Bodies

Private bodies that are regular but unlawful are those that unite themselves under one representative person without any public authority at all. Such are the corporations of beggars, thieves, and gypsies, formed the better to order their trade of begging and stealing; and the corporations of men who, by authority from some foreign person, unite themselves within another's dominion, for the easier spreading of doctrines and for building a party against the power of the commonwealth.


Irregular Systems, Such as Private Leagues

Irregular systems are, in their nature, nothing but leagues — or sometimes the mere gathering of people without union for any particular design, not bound by any obligation of one to another, but arising only from a similarity of wills and inclinations. They become lawful or unlawful according to the lawfulness or unlawfulness of each particular man's design in them; and his design is to be judged by the occasion.

Leagues of subjects — leagues being commonly made for mutual defense — are, within a commonwealth (which is itself no more than a league of all the subjects together), for the most part unnecessary; they smell of unlawful design, and are for that cause unlawful, and commonly go by the name of factions, or conspiracies. For a league is a connection of men by covenants; and if no power is given to any one man or assembly to compel performance (as in the condition of mere nature), it remains valid only so long as no just cause of distrust arises. Leagues between commonwealths, over whom there is no human power established to keep them all in awe, are therefore not only lawful but profitable for as long as they last. But leagues of the subjects of one and the same commonwealth — where every man can obtain his right by means of the sovereign power — are unnecessary for maintaining peace and justice, and, if their design is evil or unknown to the commonwealth, unlawful. For all banding together of strength by private men is, if for evil intent, unjust; if for an intent unknown, dangerous to the public, and unjustly concealed.


Secret Cabals

If the sovereign power rests in a great assembly, and a number of men who are part of that assembly consult apart, without authority, to contrive the guidance of the rest — that is a faction, an unlawful conspiracy, a fraudulent seduction of the assembly for their own particular interest. But if a man whose private interest is to be debated and judged in the assembly makes as many friends there as he can, there is no injustice in him; for in this case he is no part of the assembly. And even if he hires such friends with money (unless there is an express law against it), it is still not injustice. For sometimes — such are men's manners — justice cannot be had without money; and every man may think his own cause just until it has been heard and judged.


Feuds of Private Families

In every commonwealth, if a private man keeps more servants than the management of his estate and his lawful employment for them require, it is faction, and unlawful. For having the protection of the commonwealth, he has no need of private force to defend him. And whereas in nations not thoroughly civilized, numerous families have lived in continual hostility and invaded one another with private force, it is plain enough that either they acted unjustly, or else they had no commonwealth at all.


Factions for Government

And as with factions for kindred, so with factions for the government of religion — Papists, Protestants, and so on — or of the state, like the patricians and plebeians of old Rome, or the aristocratic and democratic factions of old Greece: all are unjust, being contrary to the peace and safety of the people, and a taking of the sword out of the hand of the sovereign.

A gathering of people is an irregular system, whose lawfulness or unlawfulness depends on the occasion and on the number of those assembled. If the occasion is lawful and evident, the gathering is lawful — like the usual meeting of men at church, or at a public show, in the usual numbers. But if the numbers are extraordinarily great, the occasion is not evident; and consequently anyone who cannot give a particular and good account of his being among them is to be judged conscious of an unlawful and tumultuous design. It may be lawful for a thousand men to join in a petition to be delivered to a judge or magistrate; yet if a thousand men come to present it, that is a tumultuous assembly — because one or two are all that purpose requires. But in such cases it is not some set number that makes the assembly unlawful; it is a number too great for the officers then present to suppress and bring to justice.

When an unusual number of men assemble against a man whom they accuse, the assembly is an unlawful tumult, since they could deliver their accusation to the magistrate by a few, or by one man. Such was the case of St. Paul at Ephesus, where Demetrius, with a great number of other men, brought two of Paul's companions before the magistrate, crying out with one voice, "Great is Diana of the Ephesians!" — which was their way of demanding justice against them, for teaching the people a doctrine that was against both their religion and their trade. The occasion, considering the laws of that people, was a just one; yet their assembly was judged unlawful, and the magistrate rebuked them for it in these words (Acts 19:40): "If Demetrius and the other workmen can accuse any man of anything, there are pleas, and deputies; let them accuse one another. And if you have any other thing to demand, your case may be judged in an assembly lawfully called. For we are in danger of being accused for this day's sedition, because there is no cause by which any man can give a reason for this gathering of people." Here he calls an assembly of which men can give no just account a sedition — one they could not answer for.

And this is all I shall say concerning systems and assemblies of people, which may be compared, as I said, to the similar parts of a man's body: those that are lawful, to the muscles; those that are unlawful, to wens, boils, and abscesses, bred by the unnatural gathering of evil humors.
