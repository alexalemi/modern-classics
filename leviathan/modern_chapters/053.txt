Chapter 46: Darkness from Vain Philosophy and Fabulous Traditions
(Part 1 of 2)

What Philosophy Is

By PHILOSOPHY is understood the knowledge acquired by reasoning — reasoning from the way a thing is generated to its properties, or from its properties to some possible way of generating it — with the aim of being able to produce, as far as matter and human power permit, the effects that human life requires. So the geometrician, by reasoning from the construction of figures, discovers many of their properties; and from those properties, new ways of constructing them — all so that he can measure land and water, and for countless other uses. So the astronomer, from the rising, setting, and movement of the sun and stars in different parts of the heavens, discovers the causes of day and night and of the different seasons of the year, and by this keeps an account of time. And the same goes for the other sciences.

Prudence Is Not Philosophy

From this definition it is evident that we should not count as any part of philosophy that original knowledge called experience, which is what prudence consists of. Prudence is not attained by reasoning. It is found in brute beasts as well as in man, and it is nothing but a memory of how events have followed one another in the past — where leaving out any little circumstance that changes the outcome defeats the expectation of even the most prudent. Whereas right reasoning produces nothing but general, eternal, and unchanging truth.

False Doctrine Is Not Philosophy

Nor should we give the name philosophy to any false conclusions. For whoever reasons rightly, in words he understands, can never conclude an error.

Neither Is Supernatural Revelation

Nor to what any man knows by supernatural revelation — because that is not acquired by reasoning.

Nor Learning Taken on the Authority of Books

Nor to what is gotten by reasoning from the authority of books — because that is not reasoning from cause to effect, nor from effect to cause; and it is not knowledge, but faith.

The Beginnings and Progress of Philosophy

Since the faculty of reasoning follows from the use of speech, it could not be otherwise: some general truths must have been found out by reasoning almost as long ago as language itself. The indigenous peoples of the Americas, as Hobbes' century knew of them, are not without some good moral sayings; they also have a little arithmetic, enough to add and divide numbers that are not too large. But that does not make them philosophers. Think of it this way. There were scattered plants of grain and grapevine growing in small quantities in the fields and woods before men knew their value, or used them for food, or planted them apart in fields and vineyards — and in that time men fed on acorns and drank water. In the same way, there have been various true, general, and profitable speculations from the beginning, since they are the natural plants of human reason. But at first they were few. Men lived on crude experience. There was no method — that is, no sowing or planting of knowledge by itself, set apart from the weeds and common plants of error and guesswork. And the cause of this was the lack of leisure: men were too busy getting the necessities of life and defending themselves against their neighbors. Until great commonwealths were erected, it could not be otherwise.

Leisure is the mother of philosophy, and commonwealth is the mother of peace and leisure. Where there were first great and flourishing cities, there was first the study of philosophy. The Gymnosophists of India, the Magi of Persia, and the priests of Chaldea and Egypt are counted the most ancient philosophers — and those countries were the most ancient kingdoms. Philosophy had not yet risen among the Greeks and the other peoples of the West, whose commonwealths — no bigger, perhaps, than Lucca or Geneva — never had peace except when their fears of one another were evenly matched, and never had the leisure to observe anything but each other. At length, when war had united many of these smaller Greek cities into fewer and greater ones, then seven men from various parts of Greece began to get the reputation of being wise — the Seven Sages of Greece — some of them for moral and political sayings, others for the learning of the Chaldeans and Egyptians, which was astronomy and geometry. But we still hear nothing of any schools of philosophy.

The Schools of Philosophy in Athens

After the Athenians, by defeating the Persian armies, had won dominion of the sea — and with it all the islands and coastal cities of the Aegean, in Asia as well as Europe — and had grown wealthy, the men who had no employment, at home or abroad, had little else to do with themselves but (as St. Luke says, Acts 17:21) "telling and hearing news," or holding forth on philosophy in public to the young men of the city. Every master took some spot for the purpose. Plato taught in certain public walks called the Academia, after one Academus. Aristotle taught in the walk of the temple of Pan, called the Lyceum. Others taught in the Stoa — a covered walkway where the merchants' goods were brought ashore. Others taught in other places, where they spent their leisure time teaching or disputing their opinions — and some in any place at all where they could gather the youth of the city to hear them talk. This is exactly what Carneades did at Rome, when he was there as an ambassador — which led Cato to advise the Senate to send him home quickly, for fear of corrupting the morals of the young men who delighted in hearing him say what they took to be fine things.

This is how the place where any of them taught and disputed came to be called schola, which in their language means leisure, and their disputations diatribae — that is to say, passing the time. The philosophers themselves got the names of their sects, in some cases, from their schools. The followers of Plato's doctrine were called Academics; the followers of Aristotle, Peripatetics, from the walk where he taught; and those Zeno taught, Stoics, from the Stoa. It is as if we were to name men after Moorfields, after St. Paul's church, and after the Exchange, because they often meet there to chatter and loiter.

Nevertheless, men were so taken with this custom that in time it spread over all of Europe and the best part of Africa, so that schools were publicly erected and maintained for lectures and disputations in almost every commonwealth.

The Schools of the Jews

There were also schools, in ancient times, both before and after the time of our Savior, among the Jews — but they were schools of their Law. For though they were called synagogues, that is to say, congregations of the people, yet since the Law was read, expounded, and disputed in them every Sabbath, they differed from public schools in name only, not in nature. And they existed not only in Jerusalem but in every Gentile city where the Jews lived. There was such a school at Damascus, which Paul entered to persecute. There were others at Antioch, Iconium, and Thessalonica, which he entered to dispute in. And such was the synagogue of the Libertines, Cyrenians, Alexandrians, Cilicians, and those of Asia — that is to say, the school of the Libertines and of Jews who were foreigners in Jerusalem. It was members of this school who disputed with St. Stephen.

The Greek Schools Were Useless

But what good have those schools ever done? What science do we possess today that was acquired by their readings and disputings? The geometry we have — which is the mother of all natural science — we do not owe to the schools. Plato, the best philosopher of the Greeks, forbade entrance into his school to anyone who was not already something of a geometrician. Many men studied that science to the great advantage of mankind; but there is no mention of their schools, there was never any sect of geometricians, and they did not pass under the name of philosophers.

The natural philosophy of those schools was rather a dream than science, and it was set forth in senseless and insignificant language — which cannot be avoided by anyone who tries to teach philosophy without first attaining real knowledge of geometry. For nature works by motion, and the ways and degrees of motion cannot be known without knowing the proportions and properties of lines and figures.

Their moral philosophy is nothing but a description of their own passions. The rule of conduct, where there is no civil government, is the law of nature; and where there is civil government, it is the civil law — that is what determines what is honest and dishonest, just and unjust, and in general what is good and evil. But these philosophers made the rules of good and bad according to their own likes and dislikes. And with so great a diversity of taste, the result is that nothing is generally agreed on; everyone does (as far as he dares) whatever seems good in his own eyes — to the subversion of the commonwealth.

Their logic, which should be the method of reasoning, is nothing but word-traps: tricks invented to stump anyone who tries to pin them down.

To sum up: there is nothing so absurd that some of the old philosophers have not maintained it — as Cicero says, and he was one of them. And I believe that scarcely anything can be more absurdly said in natural philosophy than what is now called Aristotle's Metaphysics; nor anything more repugnant to government than much of what he said in his Politics; nor anything more ignorant than a great part of his Ethics.

The Jewish Schools Were Useless Too

The school of the Jews was originally a school of the Law of Moses, who commanded (Deuteronomy 31:10) that at the end of every seventh year, at the Feast of Tabernacles, the Law should be read to all the people, so that they might hear it and learn it. So the reading of the Law every Sabbath (a practice in use after the Captivity) ought to have had no purpose but acquainting the people with the commandments they were to obey, and expounding to them the writings of the prophets. But it is plain, from our Savior's many rebukes of them, that they corrupted the text of the Law with their false commentaries and vain traditions, and understood the prophets so little that they recognized neither Christ nor the works he did — the very works the prophets had prophesied. So by their lectures and disputations in their synagogues, they turned the doctrine of their Law into a fantastical kind of philosophy about the incomprehensible nature of God and of spirits. They cooked this up out of the vain philosophy and theology of the Greeks, mixed with their own fancies drawn from the obscurer passages of Scripture — the ones most easily twisted to their purpose — and from the fabulous traditions of their ancestors.

What a University Is

What is now called a university is a joining together — an incorporation under one government — of many public schools in one and the same town or city. In a university, the principal schools were established for the three professions: Roman religion, Roman law, and the art of medicine. As for the study of philosophy, it has no place there except as a handmaid to the Roman religion. And since the authority of Aristotle is the only currency accepted there, that study is not properly philosophy (whose nature does not depend on authors) but Aristotelity. As for geometry, until very recently it had no place at all — since it serves nothing but rigid truth. And if any man, by the ingenuity of his own nature, attained any degree of perfection in it, he was commonly thought a magician, and his art diabolical.

Errors Brought into Religion from Aristotle's Metaphysics

Now let us descend to the particular tenets of vain philosophy that were handed down to the universities, and from there into the Church — derived partly from Aristotle, partly from sheer blindness of understanding. I shall first consider their principles.

There is a certain philosophia prima — first philosophy — on which all other philosophy ought to depend. It consists principally in correctly limiting the meanings of the most universal names of all. These limitations serve to avoid ambiguity and equivocation in reasoning, and are commonly called definitions: definitions of body, time, place, matter, form, essence, subject, substance, accident, power, act, finite, infinite, quantity, quality, motion, action, passion, and various others necessary for explaining a man's conceptions about the nature and generation of bodies.

The explication — that is, the settling of the meaning — of these and similar terms is commonly called, in the universities, metaphysics, this being part of the philosophy of Aristotle that bears that title. But the title means something different there. In Aristotle it signifies only "the books written, or placed, after his natural philosophy." The Scholastics, however, take them for books of supernatural philosophy — for the word "metaphysics" will bear both senses. And in truth, what is written there is for the most part so far beyond any possibility of being understood, and so repugnant to natural reason, that whoever thinks there is anything in it to be understood must necessarily conclude that it is supernatural.

Errors About Abstract Essences

From this metaphysics, mixed with Scripture to make what they call School divinity, we are told that there exist in the world certain essences separated from bodies, which they call "abstract essences" and "substantial forms." To interpret this jargon, more than ordinary attention is needed here. I also beg the pardon of those who are not used to this kind of discussion, because here I am addressing those who are.

The world — and I mean not just the earth, which gives the lovers of it the name "worldly men," but the universe, the whole mass of all things that exist — is corporeal. That is to say: body. It has the dimensions of magnitude, namely length, breadth, and depth. Every part of body is likewise body, and has the same dimensions. Consequently, every part of the universe is body, and whatever is not body is no part of the universe. And because the universe is everything, whatever is no part of it is nothing — and consequently, nowhere.

It does not follow from this that spirits are nothing. Spirits have dimensions, and are therefore really bodies — even though, in common speech, the name "body" is given only to bodies that are visible or touchable, that is, bodies with some degree of opacity. As for spirits, men call them incorporeal — which is a name of greater honor, and may therefore with more piety be attributed to God himself. For when we speak of God, we are not considering which attribute best expresses his nature, which is incomprehensible, but which best expresses our desire to honor him.

Now, to know on what grounds they say there are "abstract essences" or "substantial forms," we must consider what those words properly signify.

The use of words is to register our thoughts and conceptions to ourselves, and to make them known to others. Of these words, some are names of the things conceived — like the names of all sorts of bodies that work on the senses and leave an impression in the imagination. Others are names of the imaginations themselves — that is, of the ideas or mental images we have of all the things we see or remember. Others again are names of names, or of different kinds of speech: "universal," "plural," "singular," "negation," "true," "false," "syllogism," "interrogation," "promise," "covenant" — these are names of certain forms of speech. And still others serve to show the connection, or the contradiction, between one name and another. When someone says "a man is a body," he means that the name "body" necessarily follows from the name "man," since they are simply different names of the same thing — man. And that connection is signified by coupling the two names together with the word "is."

As we use the verb "is," so the Latins used their verb est, and the Greeks their esti through all its forms. Whether all the other nations of the world have a word answering to it in their various languages, I cannot say — but I am sure they have no need of one. Simply placing two names side by side could serve to signify their connection, if that were the custom (for custom is what gives words their force), just as well as the words "is," or "be," or "are," and the like.

And suppose there were a language with no verb answering to est, or "is," or "be." The men who used it would be not one bit less capable of inferring, concluding, and every kind of reasoning than the Greeks and Latins were. But then what would become of these terms — "entity," "essence," "essential," "essentially" — which are derived from that verb, along with the many more that depend on them, applied the way they most commonly are? They are not names of things at all. They are signs by which we make known that we conceive one name or attribute as following from another. When we say "a man is a living body," we do not mean that the man is one thing, the living body another, and the "is" or "being" a third. We mean that the man and the living body are the same thing — because the inference "if he is a man, he is a living body" is a true inference, and that is what the word "is" signifies. Therefore "to be a body," "to walk," "to be speaking," "to live," "to see," and similar infinitives — and likewise "corporeity," "walking," "speaking," "life," "sight," and the like, which signify just the same — are the names of nothing. I have explained this more fully elsewhere.

But someone may ask: what is the point of such fine-grained analysis in a work like this, where I claim to deal with nothing but what is necessary to the doctrine of government and obedience? The point is this: that men may no longer let themselves be abused by those who, using this doctrine of "separated essences" built on the vain philosophy of Aristotle, would frighten them away from obeying the laws of their country with empty names — the way men frighten birds from the corn with an empty coat, a hat, and a crooked stick.

For it is on this ground that they say, when a man is dead and buried, that his soul (that is, his life) can walk around separated from his body, and is seen by night among the graves. On the same ground they say that the shape, color, and taste of a piece of bread have a being in a place where, they tell us, there is no bread. And on the same ground they say that faith, and wisdom, and other virtues are sometimes "poured" into a man, sometimes "blown" into him from heaven — as if the virtuous and their virtues could exist apart — along with a great many other things that serve to weaken the dependence of subjects on the sovereign power of their country. For who will make any effort to obey the laws, if he expects obedience to be poured or blown into him? Who will not obey a priest who can make God, rather than obey his sovereign — rather, indeed, than obey God himself? And who that is afraid of ghosts will not show great respect to those who can make the holy water that drives the ghosts away?

Let this serve as one example of the errors brought into the Church from the "entities" and "essences" of Aristotle. It may be that Aristotle himself knew it was false philosophy, but wrote it as something agreeable to, and supportive of, the religion of his time — fearing the fate of Socrates.

Once fallen into this error of "separated essences," they are necessarily entangled in many other absurdities that follow from it. Since they insist these forms are real, they are obliged to assign them some place. But because they hold them to be incorporeal, without any dimension of quantity — and everyone knows that place is dimension, and can be filled only by what is corporeal — they are driven to prop up their credibility with a distinction: the forms are not anywhere "circumscriptively," but "definitively." These terms are mere words, and in this case insignificant; they pass current only in Latin, so that their emptiness can be concealed. For the circumscription of a thing is nothing other than the determination — the defining — of its place; both terms of the distinction mean exactly the same thing. And regarding the essence of a man in particular, which they say is his soul, they affirm that all of it is in his little finger, and all of it in every other part of his body, however small — and yet there is no more soul in the whole body than in any one of those parts. Can any man think God is served by such absurdities? And yet all of this must be believed by anyone who will believe in the existence of an incorporeal soul separated from the body.

And when they are asked to explain how an incorporeal substance can be capable of pain, and be tormented in the fire of hell or purgatory, they have no answer at all — except that it cannot be known how fire can burn souls.
