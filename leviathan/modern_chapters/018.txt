Chapter 18: The Rights of Sovereigns by Institution


What It Means to Institute a Commonwealth

A commonwealth is said to be INSTITUTED — established by the agreement of the people themselves, rather than by conquest — when a multitude of men agree and covenant, every one with every one, as follows: that whatever man or assembly of men the major part chooses to bear the person of them all — that is, to be their representative — every one of them, the man who voted for it and the man who voted against it alike, shall authorize all the actions and judgments of that man or assembly, exactly as if they were his own. And the purpose of all this is to live peaceably among themselves, and to be protected against other men.


What Follows from This Institution

From this institution of a commonwealth are derived all the rights and powers of the man, or assembly of men, on whom the sovereign power is conferred by the consent of the assembled people.


1. The Subjects Cannot Change the Form of Government

First, because they covenant, it is to be understood that they are not bound by any former covenant to anything that contradicts this one. Consequently, those who have already instituted a commonwealth — being bound by covenant to own the actions and judgments of one sovereign — cannot lawfully make a new covenant among themselves to obey anyone else, in anything whatsoever, without the sovereign's permission.

So the subjects of a monarch cannot, without his leave, cast off monarchy and return to the confusion of a disunited multitude; nor can they transfer their person from the man who bears it to another man, or to another assembly. For they are bound, every man to every man, to own — and to be counted author of — everything that the man who is already their sovereign shall do, and judge fit to be done. If even one man dissented, all the rest would be breaking the covenant they made with that man, which is injustice. Besides, each of them has given the sovereignty to the one who bears their person; so if they depose him, they take from him what is his own — and that, again, is injustice.

What's more, if a man who attempts to depose his sovereign is killed or punished by him for the attempt, he is the author of his own punishment, since by the institution he is the author of everything his sovereign does. And because it is injustice for a man to do anything for which he may be punished by his own authority, he is unjust on that ground as well.

Some men have excused their disobedience to their sovereign by claiming a new covenant — made not with men, but with God. This too is unjust. For there is no covenant with God except through the mediation of somebody who represents God's person; and nobody does that but God's lieutenant, who holds the sovereignty under God. But this pretense of a covenant with God is so obvious a lie, even in the pretenders' own consciences, that it is the act not merely of an unjust disposition, but of a vile and cowardly one.


2. Sovereign Power Cannot Be Forfeited

Secondly, because the right of bearing the person of them all is given to the man they make sovereign by a covenant made only between subject and subject — not by any covenant between him and any of them — there can be no breach of covenant on the sovereign's part. And consequently none of his subjects can be freed from subjection by any pretense that he has forfeited his power.

That the man who is made sovereign makes no covenant with his subjects beforehand is plain to see. Either he would have to make it with the whole multitude as one party to the covenant, or he would have to make a separate covenant with every individual man. With the whole multitude as one party, it is impossible — because before the institution they are not yet one person. And if he makes as many separate covenants as there are men, those covenants become void the moment he has the sovereignty; for whatever act any one of them might point to as a breach is the act both of the sovereign and of all the rest, since it was done in the person, and by the right, of every one of them in particular.

Besides, if any one or more of them claim the sovereign broke the covenant made at his institution, and others — or even one other subject, or the sovereign alone — claim there was no such breach, there is in this case no judge to decide the controversy. So the question returns to the sword; and every man recovers the right of protecting himself by his own strength — contrary to the very design they had in the institution. It is therefore pointless to grant sovereignty by way of a prior covenant.

The opinion that any monarch receives his power by covenant — that is, on condition — comes from failing to understand this easy truth: covenants, being nothing but words and breath, have no force to oblige, contain, constrain, or protect any man except what they get from the public sword — that is, from the untied hands of the man or assembly that holds the sovereignty, whose actions are vouched for by them all, and performed by the strength of them all, united in him.

But when an assembly of men is made sovereign, nobody imagines any such covenant to have passed at the institution. No man is so dull as to say, for example, that the people of Rome made a covenant with the Romans to hold the sovereignty on such-and-such conditions — and that if the conditions were not met, the Romans might lawfully depose the Roman people. That men fail to see that the reasoning is the same in a monarchy as in a popular government comes from the ambition of some, who are kinder to government by an assembly — in which they may hope to take part — than to monarchy, which they have no hope of ever enjoying.


3. No One Can Justly Protest Against the Institution of the Sovereign Declared by the Major Part

Thirdly, because the major part has, by consenting voices, declared a sovereign, the man who dissented must now consent with the rest. That is, he must be content to own all the actions the sovereign shall do — or else justly be destroyed by the rest. For if he voluntarily entered the congregation of those who were assembled, he sufficiently declared by that act his will to stand by whatever the major part should ordain — and therefore tacitly covenanted to do so. So if he refuses to stand by it, or protests against any of their decrees, he acts contrary to his covenant, and therefore unjustly. And whether he was part of the congregation or not, and whether his consent was asked or not, he must either submit to their decrees or be left in the condition of war he was in before — in which anyone at all might destroy him without injustice.


4. The Sovereign's Actions Cannot Be Justly Accused by the Subject

Fourthly, because by this institution every subject is author of all the actions and judgments of the instituted sovereign, it follows that whatever the sovereign does can be no injury to any of his subjects, nor ought he to be accused of injustice by any of them. For a man who does something by authority from another does no injury, in doing it, to the one by whose authority he acts. But by this institution of a commonwealth, every particular man is author of everything the sovereign does. So a man who complains of injury from his sovereign is complaining of something he himself is the author of; and therefore he ought to accuse no one but himself — no, and not even himself, because to do injury to one's self is impossible.

It is true that those who have sovereign power may commit iniquity — moral wrong. But they cannot commit injustice, or injury in the proper meaning of the word.


5. Whatever the Sovereign Does Is Unpunishable by the Subject

Fifthly, and as a consequence of the last point, no man who has sovereign power can justly be put to death, or punished in any other manner, by his subjects. For seeing every subject is author of the actions of his sovereign, he would be punishing another for actions committed by himself.


6. The Sovereign Is Judge of What Is Necessary for the Peace and Defense of His Subjects

And because the end of this institution is the peace and defense of them all, and whoever has a right to the end has a right to the means, it belongs by right to whatever man or assembly holds the sovereignty to be judge both of the means of peace and defense, and of the hindrances and disturbances to them; and to do whatever he shall think necessary to be done — both in advance, to preserve peace and security by preventing discord at home and hostility from abroad, and, when peace and security are lost, to recover them. And therefore:


And Judge of What Doctrines Are Fit to Be Taught

Sixthly, it is annexed to the sovereignty to be judge of which opinions and doctrines work against peace, and which promote it; and consequently, on what occasions, how far, and in what matters men are to be trusted when speaking to multitudes of people; and who shall examine the doctrines of all books before they are published. For the actions of men proceed from their opinions; and in the good governing of opinions consists the good governing of men's actions, with a view to their peace and concord.

And though in matters of doctrine nothing ought to be regarded but the truth, this is not in conflict with regulating doctrine for the sake of peace. For a doctrine in conflict with peace can no more be true than peace and concord can be against the law of nature.

It is true that in a commonwealth where false doctrines have, through the negligence or clumsiness of governors and teachers, become generally accepted over time, the contrary truths may be generally offensive. Yet even the most sudden and rough bursting-in of a new truth never breaks the peace — it only sometimes wakes the war. For men governed so loosely that they dare take up arms to defend or introduce an opinion are still at war; their condition is not peace, but only a cease-fire out of fear of one another; they live, as it were, perpetually on the edge of the battlefield. It belongs, therefore, to the holder of the sovereign power to be judge — or to appoint all the judges — of opinions and doctrines, as a thing necessary to peace, in order to prevent discord and civil war.


7. The Right of Making Rules, So That Every Subject Knows What Is His Own

Seventhly, annexed to the sovereignty is the whole power of prescribing the rules by which every man may know what goods he may enjoy, and what actions he may do, without being harassed by any of his fellow subjects. And this is what men call PROPERTY. For before the constitution of sovereign power, as I have already shown, all men had a right to all things — which necessarily causes war. So this property, being necessary to peace and depending on sovereign power, is the act of that power, made for the sake of public peace. These rules of property (or meum and tuum — mine and thine), and of good, evil, lawful, and unlawful in the actions of subjects, are the CIVIL LAWS — that is, the laws of each commonwealth in particular; though the name "civil law" is now restricted to the ancient civil laws of the city of Rome — for Rome being the head of a great part of the world, her laws in those days were, in these parts, the civil law.


8. To Him Also Belongs the Right of All Judicature and the Deciding of Controversies

Eighthly, annexed to the sovereignty is the right of judicature — that is, of hearing and deciding all controversies that may arise concerning law, whether civil or natural, or concerning fact. For without the deciding of controversies there is no protection of one subject against the injuries of another; the laws concerning mine and thine are in vain; and there remains to every man, from his natural and necessary appetite for his own preservation, the right of protecting himself by his private strength — which is the condition of war, and contrary to the end for which every commonwealth is instituted.


9. And of Making War and Peace as He Shall Think Best

Ninthly, annexed to the sovereignty is the right of making war and peace with other nations and commonwealths — that is, of judging when it serves the public good, and how great the forces to be assembled, armed, and paid for that end should be, and of levying money on the subjects to cover the expense. For the power by which the people are to be defended consists in their armies; and the strength of an army consists in the union of their strength under one command — which command the instituted sovereign therefore has, because the command of the militia, even without any other institution, makes the man who holds it sovereign. And therefore, whoever may be made general of an army, the holder of the sovereign power is always generalissimo — the supreme commander.


10. And of Choosing All Counselors and Ministers, Both of Peace and War

Tenthly, annexed to the sovereignty is the choosing of all counselors, ministers, magistrates, and officers, both in peace and in war. For since the sovereign is charged with the end — the common peace and defense — he is understood to have the power to use whatever means he shall think most fit to discharge that duty.


11. And of Rewarding and Punishing — at His Discretion, Where No Former Law Has Set the Measure

Eleventhly, to the sovereign is committed the power of rewarding with riches or with honor, and of punishing with bodily or monetary punishment, or with public disgrace, every subject — according to the law he has previously made; or, if no law has been made, according to what he shall judge will most encourage men to serve the commonwealth, or deter them from doing it disservice.


12. And of Honor and Rank

Lastly, consider what value men are naturally apt to set on themselves, what respect they expect from others, and how little they value other men. From all this there continually arise among them rivalry, quarrels, factions, and at last war — to the destroying of one another, and the weakening of their strength against a common enemy. So it is necessary that there be laws of honor, and a public scale of the worth of men who have deserved well of the commonwealth, or are able to deserve well of it; and that there be force in the hands of someone or other to put those laws into execution. But it has already been shown that not only the whole militia — the armed forces of the commonwealth — but also the judging of all controversies, is annexed to the sovereignty. To the sovereign, therefore, it also belongs to give titles of honor, and to appoint what rank of place and dignity each man shall hold, and what signs of respect, in public or private meetings, they shall give to one another.


These Rights Are Indivisible

These are the rights that make up the essence of sovereignty; and they are the marks by which a man may discern in what man, or assembly of men, the sovereign power is placed and resides. For these rights are incommunicable and inseparable. The power to coin money, to dispose of the estates and persons of underage heirs, to have pre-emption — the right of first purchase — in markets, and all other prerogatives granted by statute, may be transferred away by the sovereign, and the power to protect his subjects still retained. But if he transfers the militia, he retains the judicature in vain, for want of the power to execute the laws. If he grants away the power of raising money, the militia is in vain. If he gives away the government of doctrines, men will be frightened into rebellion by the fear of spirits. So if we consider any one of these rights, we shall quickly see that holding on to all the rest will produce no effect in preserving peace and justice — the end for which all commonwealths are instituted.

It is of this division that it is said, "A kingdom divided in itself cannot stand." For unless this division comes first, division into opposing armies can never happen. If the opinion had not first taken hold of the greatest part of England that these powers were divided between the King, the Lords, and the House of Commons, the people would never have been divided, and fallen into this civil war — first between those who disagreed about politics, and afterward between the dissenters about the liberty of religion. And the war has so instructed men on this point of sovereign right that there are few now in England who do not see that these rights are inseparable. So it will be generally acknowledged at the next return of peace — and so it will continue, until their miseries are forgotten, and no longer, unless the common people are taught better than they have been so far.


And No Grant Can Pass Them Away Without a Direct Renouncing of the Sovereign Power

And because these are essential and inseparable rights, it follows necessarily that whatever the words by which any of them may seem to be granted away, the grant is void — unless the sovereign power itself is renounced in direct terms, and the name of sovereign is no longer given by the grantees to the man who granted them. For when he has granted away all he can, if we grant back the sovereignty, everything is restored, as inseparably annexed to it.


The Power and Honor of Subjects Vanish in the Presence of the Sovereign Power

Since this great authority is indivisible, and inseparably annexed to the sovereignty, there is little ground for the opinion of those who say of sovereign kings that though they are singulis majores — of greater power than each of their subjects taken one by one — they are nonetheless universis minores — of less power than all of them together. For if by "all together" they do not mean the collective body as one person, then "all together" and "every one" signify the same thing, and the statement is absurd. But if by "all together" they understand them as one person — which person the sovereign bears — then the power of all together is the same as the sovereign's power, and again the statement is absurd. This absurdity they see well enough when the sovereignty rests in an assembly of the people; in a monarch they fail to see it — and yet the power of sovereignty is the same wherever it is placed.

And as with the power, so also the honor of the sovereign ought to be greater than that of any one of his subjects, or of all of them together. For the sovereignty is the fountain of honor. The dignities of lord, earl, duke, and prince are its creatures. As servants are equal in the presence of their master, and without any honor at all, so are subjects in the presence of the sovereign. And though they shine, some more, some less, when they are out of his sight, in his presence they shine no more than the stars in the presence of the sun.


Sovereign Power Is Not as Harmful as the Lack of It — and the Harm Comes Mostly from Not Readily Submitting to a Lesser Evil

But here a man may object that the condition of subjects is very miserable, exposed as they are to the lusts and other irregular passions of the man, or men, who hold such unlimited power in their hands. Commonly, those who live under a monarch think it is the fault of monarchy; and those who live under the government of democracy, or some other sovereign assembly, attribute all the inconvenience to that form of commonwealth — when in fact the power is the same in all forms, provided the forms are complete enough to protect them.

Such objectors fail to consider that the estate of man can never be without some inconvenience or other; and that the greatest inconvenience that can possibly happen to the people in general, under any form of government, is scarcely noticeable next to the miseries and horrible calamities that accompany a civil war — or that dissolute condition of masterless men, without subjection to laws, and without a coercive power to tie their hands from plunder and revenge.

Nor do they consider that the heaviest pressure from sovereign governors comes not from any delight or profit the governors can expect from damaging or weakening their subjects — for in their subjects' vigor consists their own strength — but from the subjects themselves: who, contributing unwillingly to their own defense, make it necessary for their governors to draw from them whatever they can in time of peace, so that they may have the means, in any emergency or sudden need, to resist their enemies, or to gain advantage over them.

For all men are by nature equipped with remarkable magnifying glasses — their passions and their self-love — through which every little payment looks like a great grievance. But they lack the telescopes — namely, moral and civil science — to see, far off, the miseries that hang over them, and that cannot be avoided without such payments.
