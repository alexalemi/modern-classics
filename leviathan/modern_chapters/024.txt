Chapter 24: The Nutrition and Procreation of a Commonwealth

A Commonwealth Is Nourished by the Commodities of Sea and Land

The NUTRITION of a commonwealth consists in these things: plenty, meaning an abundance of the materials that sustain life; the distribution of those materials; their CONCOCTION — that is, their preparation, their digestion into a usable form; and, once prepared, their conveyance through convenient channels to public use.

As for plenty, nature itself sets the limit. The materials of life are those commodities which, from the two breasts of our common mother — the land and the sea — God either gives freely to mankind, or sells to us for the price of our labor.

The matter of this nourishment consists of animals, plants, and minerals, and God has laid them freely before us, on or near the face of the earth. Nothing more is required than the labor and industry of gathering them in. So plenty depends — next to God's favor — purely on the labor and industry of men.

This matter, commonly called commodities, is partly native and partly foreign. Native commodities are those available within the territory of the commonwealth; foreign ones are imported from outside. And because no territory under the dominion of a single commonwealth — unless it is enormous — produces everything needed for the maintenance and motion of the whole body, and because few territories fail to produce something beyond their own needs, the surplus commodities at home stop being surplus at all. They become the means of supplying what is lacking at home, by importing what can be had abroad — whether by exchange, by just war, or by labor. For a man's labor, too, is a commodity that can be exchanged for benefit, as much as any other thing. Indeed, there have been commonwealths with no more territory than they needed to live on that nevertheless not only maintained but increased their power — partly by the labor of carrying trade from one place to another, and partly by selling manufactured goods made from materials brought in from elsewhere.

And by the Right of Distributing Them

The distribution of the materials of this nourishment is the establishment of mine, and thine, and his — in one word, PROPERTY — and in every kind of commonwealth it belongs to the sovereign power. For where there is no commonwealth, there is (as I have already shown) a perpetual war of every man against his neighbor, in which everything belongs to whoever can grab it and keep it by force. That is neither property nor common ownership; it is uncertainty. This is so obvious that even Cicero — a passionate defender of liberty — attributed all property to the civil law, in a public court. "Let the civil law be once abandoned," he said, "or even carelessly guarded — not to say oppressed — and there is nothing any man can be sure of receiving from his ancestors, or of leaving to his children." And again: "Take away the civil law, and no man knows what is his own and what belongs to another."

Property, then, is an effect of the commonwealth — property begins with the commonwealth. And since the commonwealth can do nothing except through the person who represents it, distribution is the act of the sovereign alone, and it consists in laws, which no one can make who does not hold the sovereign power. The ancients understood this well. They called law Nomos — which means "distribution" — and they defined justice as distributing to every man his own.

All Private Land Comes Originally from the Sovereign's Distribution

In this distribution, the first law concerns the division of the land itself. The sovereign assigns every man a portion according to what he — and not any subject, or any number of subjects — judges consistent with equity and the common good. The children of Israel were a commonwealth in the wilderness, but they lacked the commodities of the earth until they became masters of the Promised Land. That land was afterward divided among them, not at their own discretion, but at the discretion of Eleazar the priest and Joshua their general. There were twelve tribes — thirteen, once the tribe of Joseph was subdivided — yet they made only twelve portions of land, ordained no land at all for the tribe of Levi, and assigned the Levites a tenth of the whole harvest instead. The division, in other words, was arbitrary: it followed the judgment of those in authority, not some pre-existing right.

And though a people who take possession of a land by war do not always exterminate the former inhabitants, as the Jews did, but leave many or most or all of them their estates, it is still clear that they hold those estates afterward as grants from the victor's distribution — just as the people of England held everything they had from William the Conqueror.

A Subject's Property Shuts Out Other Subjects, Not the Sovereign

From all this we can conclude what a subject's property in his land actually amounts to: a right to exclude every other subject from using it — not a right to exclude his sovereign, whether that sovereign is an assembly or a monarch. For the sovereign — that is, the commonwealth, whose person he bears — is understood to do nothing except for the sake of the common peace and security, and this distribution of lands is to be understood as done for the same end. It follows that any distribution the sovereign makes that damages the common peace and security is contrary to the will of every subject who entrusted his peace and safety to the sovereign's discretion and conscience, and so by the will of every one of them it is to be counted void.

It is true that a sovereign monarch, or the greater part of a sovereign assembly, may order many things done in pursuit of their passions, against their own consciences — and that is a breach of trust and of the law of nature. But this is not enough to authorize any subject to make war on his sovereign, or even to accuse him of injustice, or to speak evil of him in any way. The subjects authorized all his actions, and in granting him the sovereign power they made those actions their own. In what cases the commands of sovereigns are contrary to equity and the law of nature is a question I will take up later, in another place.

The Public Cannot Be Put on a Diet

In the distribution of land, one might imagine the commonwealth itself taking a portion — possessing it and improving it through its representative — and that this portion could be made large enough to cover the whole cost of the common peace and defense. And that would be perfectly true, if you could imagine a representative free of human passions and weaknesses. But human nature being what it is, setting aside public land, or any fixed revenue for the commonwealth, is pointless. It tends toward the dissolution of government, back into the condition of mere nature and war, the moment the sovereign power falls into the hands of a monarch or an assembly that is either too careless with money or too reckless in committing the public stock to a long or costly war.

Commonwealths can endure no diet. Their expenses are not limited by their own appetite but by external accidents — and by the appetites of their neighbors. So the public riches cannot be capped at any limit other than what emergencies happen to require. In England, the Conqueror reserved various lands for his own use (besides forests and hunting chases, kept for his recreation or for preserving the woods), and he reserved various services on the land he gave his subjects. Yet it seems these were reserved not for his maintenance in his public capacity but in his natural one — for he and his successors, despite all those reserves, laid arbitrary taxes on all their subjects' land whenever they judged it necessary. And if those public lands and services were intended as a sufficient maintenance for the commonwealth, the plan ran contrary to its own purpose: the taxes that followed prove the revenue was insufficient, and the recent state of the Crown's revenue proves it was subject to being sold off and whittled away. It is therefore pointless to assign a portion to the commonwealth — a commonwealth which may sell it or give it away, and which does sell it and give it away whenever its representative does so.

Where and in What Goods to Trade Abroad Depends on the Sovereign Too

Just as the distribution of lands at home belongs to the sovereign, so does the assignment of where, and in what commodities, the subject may trade abroad. For if that were left to the discretion of private persons, some of them would be drawn by profit both to supply the enemy with the means to hurt the commonwealth, and to hurt it themselves by importing things that please men's appetites but are nonetheless harmful — or at least useless — to them. And therefore it belongs to the commonwealth (that is, to the sovereign only) to approve or disapprove both the places and the goods of foreign trade.

The Laws of Transferring Property Also Belong to the Sovereign

Further: it is not enough for the sustenance of a commonwealth that every man have property in a portion of land, or in a few commodities, or a natural property in some useful skill — for there is hardly a skill in the world that is not necessary to the existence, or at least the well-being, of almost every individual man. So it is necessary that men distribute what they can spare, and transfer their property in it to one another, by exchange and mutual contract. And therefore it belongs to the commonwealth — that is to say, to the sovereign — to appoint the manner in which all kinds of contract between subjects (buying, selling, exchanging, borrowing, lending, leasing, and hiring) are to be made, and by what words and signs they shall be understood as valid. As for the matter and distribution of the nourishment to the several members of the commonwealth, this much — considering the scale of the whole work — is sufficient.

Money: The Blood of the Commonwealth

By concoction I mean this: reducing all the commodities that are not consumed right away, but stored up as nourishment for the future, into something of equal value — and something so portable that it does not hinder a man's movement from place to place, so that wherever he goes, he can buy whatever nourishment that place affords. And this something is nothing other than gold, and silver, and money. Gold and silver, as it happens, are highly valued in almost every country of the world, which makes them a convenient measure of the value of all other things between nations. And money — of whatever material, so long as it is coined by the sovereign of a commonwealth — is a sufficient measure of the value of all things between the subjects of that commonwealth.

By means of these measures, all commodities, movable and immovable, are made to accompany a man wherever he goes, inside or outside his ordinary place of residence. Money passes from man to man within the commonwealth, and goes round and round, nourishing every part of it as it circulates. In this way concoction is, so to speak, the sanguification of the commonwealth — its blood-making. For natural blood is made the same way, from the fruits of the earth, and as it circulates it nourishes every member of the body of man along its route.

And because silver and gold get their value from the material itself, they enjoy two privileges. First, their value cannot be altered by the power of one commonwealth, or even a few, since they are a common measure of the commodities of all places — whereas base money can easily be raised or lowered in value. Secondly, they give commonwealths the power to move, and to stretch out their arms, when need requires, into foreign countries — supplying not only private subjects who travel, but whole armies with provisions. But coin that is valued not for its material but only for the stamp of the place that minted it cannot endure a change of air. It has its effect at home only — and even there it is subject to changes in the law, which can shrink its value, often to the loss of the very people who hold it.

The Conduits That Carry Money to Public Use

The conduits and channels by which money is conveyed to public use are of two sorts: one that carries it into the public coffers, and another that issues it out again for public payments. Of the first sort are collectors, receivers, and treasurers. Of the second are the treasurers again, together with the officers appointed to pay the various public and private ministers. And in this, too, the artificial man keeps up his resemblance to the natural one. The veins of the natural body receive blood from all the parts of the body and carry it to the heart; there the blood is made vital, and the heart, through the arteries, sends it out again to enliven all the members and enable them to move.

Colonies: The Children of a Commonwealth

The PROCREATION, or children, of a commonwealth are what we call plantations, or colonies: groups of men sent out from the commonwealth under a conductor or governor, to inhabit a foreign country — either one previously empty of inhabitants, or one emptied at the time, by war.

When a colony is settled, one of two things happens. Either the colonists become a commonwealth of their own, discharged from their subjection to the sovereign who sent them — as many commonwealths of ancient times did. In that case the commonwealth they came from was called their metropolis, or mother city, and it requires no more of them than fathers require of the children they emancipate and set free from the family's government: namely, honor and friendship. Or else they remain united to their metropolis, as the colonies of the Roman people did — and then they are not commonwealths themselves, but provinces, parts of the commonwealth that sent them out. So the rights of colonies (apart from honor, and alliance with their metropolis) depend entirely on their license, or charter — the letters by which their sovereign authorized them to plant.
