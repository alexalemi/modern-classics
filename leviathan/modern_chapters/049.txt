Part IV: Of the Kingdom of Darkness

Chapter 44: Spiritual Darkness from the Misinterpretation of Scripture
(Part 1 of 2)

What the Kingdom of Darkness Is

Besides the sovereign powers I have discussed so far — divine and human — Scripture mentions another power: that of "the rulers of the darkness of this world" (Ephesians 6:12), "the kingdom of Satan" (Matthew 12:26), and "the principality of Beelzebub over demons" (Matthew 9:34) — that is, over phantasms that appear in the air. For this reason Satan is also called "the prince of the power of the air" (Ephesians 2:2); and, because he rules in the darkness of this world, "the prince of this world" (John 16:11). And accordingly, those under his dominion — in opposition to the faithful, who are the children of the light — are called the children of darkness. Since Beelzebub is prince of phantasms, the inhabitants of his dominion of air and darkness, the children of darkness and these demons, phantasms, or spirits of illusion all signify, allegorically, the same thing.

With this in mind, the KINGDOM OF DARKNESS, as it is set out in these and other places of Scripture, is nothing else but a confederacy of deceivers who, in order to obtain dominion over men in this present world, work by dark and erroneous doctrines to extinguish in them the light both of nature and of the gospel — and so to leave them unprepared for the kingdom of God to come.

The Church Is Not Yet Fully Free of Darkness

Men who have been utterly deprived of bodily sight from birth have no idea of light at all; and no man can imagine any greater light than the greatest he has at some time actually perceived with his outward senses. The same holds for the light of the gospel and the light of the understanding: no one can conceive of a greater degree of it than the degree he has already attained. And so the only means men have of recognizing their own darkness is to reason backward from the unforeseen disasters that befall them along their way.

The darkest part of Satan's kingdom is the part outside the Church of God — among those who do not believe in Jesus Christ. But we cannot say that the Church therefore enjoys, like the land of Goshen, all the light necessary for the work God has assigned us. If it is not night among us, or at least a mist, then how has it come about that in Christendom, almost from the time of the apostles, there has been such jostling of one another out of their places, by foreign war and civil war alike? Such stumbling at every little roughness in their own fortune, and every little eminence in another man's? Such a diversity of roads taken in running toward the same goal — happiness? We are, therefore, still in the dark.

The Four Causes of Spiritual Darkness

The Enemy has been here in the night of our natural ignorance and sown the tares — the weeds — of spiritual error. He did it, first, by abusing and putting out the light of the Scriptures; for we err by not knowing the Scriptures. Second, by introducing the demonology of the heathen poets — their fabulous doctrine concerning demons, which are nothing but idols, phantasms of the brain, with no real nature of their own distinct from human imagination: dead men's ghosts, and fairies, and the other stuff of old wives' tales. Third, by mixing into Scripture various relics of the religion of the Greeks, and much of their vain and erroneous philosophy — especially Aristotle's. Fourth, by mingling with both of these false or uncertain traditions, and feigned or uncertain history.

And so we come to err by "giving heed to seducing spirits," and to the demonology of those "that speak lies in hypocrisy" — or, as it reads in the original (1 Timothy 4:1-2), "of those that play the part of liars" — "with a seared conscience"; that is, against their own knowledge. Concerning the first of these — the seducing of men by the abuse of Scripture — I intend to speak briefly in this chapter.

Errors About the Kingdom of God, from Misinterpreting the Scriptures

The greatest and main abuse of Scripture — the one to which nearly all the rest are either consequences or servants — is the wresting of it to prove that the kingdom of God, mentioned so often in Scripture, is the present Church: the multitude of Christians now living, or now living together with those who, being dead, are to rise again at the last day. Whereas in fact the kingdom of God was first instituted by the ministry of Moses, over the Jews only — who were for that reason called his peculiar people — and it ceased afterward in the election of Saul, when they refused to be governed by God any longer and demanded a king after the manner of the nations; and God himself consented to it, as I proved at length in Chapter 35. After that time there was no other kingdom of God in the world by any covenant. God, of course, always was, is, and shall be king of all men and all creatures in another sense — governing according to his will, by his infinite power.

Nevertheless, he promised through his prophets to restore his government to them again, when the time he has appointed in his secret counsel has fully come, and when they turn back to him in repentance and amendment of life. And not only that: he also invited the Gentiles to come in and enjoy the happiness of his reign, on the same conditions of conversion and repentance. And he promised to send his Son into the world, to expiate the sins of them all by his death, and to prepare them by his teaching to receive him at his second coming. Since that second coming has not yet happened, the kingdom of God is not yet come; and we are now under no kings by covenant except our civil sovereigns — with this one qualification, that Christians are already in the kingdom of grace, inasmuch as they already have the promise of being received at his coming again.

The Error That the Kingdom of God Is the Present Church

Watch what follows from this error — that the present Church is Christ's kingdom. If it were true, there would have to be some one man or assembly by whose mouth our Savior, now in heaven, speaks and gives law, and who represents his person to all Christians; or else several men or assemblies who do the same for the several parts of Christendom. This regal power under Christ is claimed universally by the Pope, and within particular commonwealths by assemblies of the local pastors — when Scripture gives it to none but civil sovereigns. And the claim comes to be disputed so passionately that it puts out the light of nature, and causes so great a darkness in men's understanding that they cannot see who it is they have pledged their obedience to.

And That the Pope Is His Vicar General

From the Pope's claim to be Vicar General of Christ in the present Church — supposed to be that very kingdom of Christ to which the gospel directs us — follows the doctrine that a Christian king must receive his crown from a bishop; as if it were from that ceremony that he derives the phrase Dei gratia — "by the grace of God" — in his title; as if he were made king by God's favor only when he is crowned by the authority of God's universal viceregent on earth. And it follows likewise that every bishop, whoever his sovereign may be, takes at his consecration an oath of absolute obedience to the Pope.

From the same claim follows the doctrine of the Fourth Lateran Council, held under Pope Innocent III (Chapter 3, De Haereticis — "On Heretics"): that if a king, at the Pope's admonition, does not purge his kingdom of heresies, and, being excommunicated for it, does not give satisfaction within a year, his subjects are absolved of the bond of their obedience. Where by "heresies" are understood all opinions the Church of Rome has forbidden to be maintained. And by this means, whenever the political designs of the Pope clash with those of other Christian princes — as they very often do — there arises such a mist among their subjects that they cannot tell a stranger who has thrust himself into the throne of their lawful prince from the man they themselves placed there. And in this darkness of mind they are made to fight one against another, unable to tell their enemies from their friends, under the conduct of another man's ambition.

And That the Pastors Are the Clergy

From the same opinion — that the present Church is the kingdom of God — it comes about that pastors, deacons, and all other ministers of the Church take to themselves the name of "the clergy," and give all other Christians the name of "laity," that is, simply "the people." For "clergy" signifies those whose maintenance is that revenue which God, having reserved it to himself during his reign over the Israelites, assigned to the tribe of Levi to be their inheritance — they were to be his public ministers, and had no portion of land set out for them to live on, as their brethren had. The Pope, therefore, pretending the present Church to be, like the realm of Israel, the kingdom of God, claimed for himself and his subordinate ministers the same kind of revenue, as the inheritance of God; and the name "clergy" suited that claim. And that is why tithes and other tributes, paid to the Levites as God's right among the Israelites, have for a long time been demanded and taken from Christians by ecclesiastics, jure divino — that is, "in God's right."

By this means the people everywhere were obliged to pay a double tribute: one to the State, another to the clergy. And the clergy's share — a tenth of their revenue — is double what a certain king of Athens, a man counted a tyrant, exacted from his subjects to cover all public expenses. He demanded no more than a twentieth part, and maintained the commonwealth abundantly with it. And in the kingdom of the Jews, during the priestly reign of God, the tithes and offerings were the entire public revenue.

From the same mistaking of the present Church for the kingdom of God came the distinction between the civil and the canon laws: the civil law being the acts of sovereigns in their own dominions, and the canon law being the acts of the Pope in those same dominions. These canons, though they were only canons — that is, rules proposed, and received voluntarily by Christian princes — became rules commanded once the Empire passed to Charlemagne and the Pope's power increased; and the emperors themselves, to avoid the greater mischiefs a blinded people might be led into, were forced to let them pass for laws.

Hence it is that in all dominions where the Pope's ecclesiastical power is fully received, Jews, Turks, and pagans are tolerated by the Roman Church in their religion, so far as they do not offend against the civil power in exercising and professing it; whereas for a Christian, even a foreigner, not to be of the Roman religion is a capital offense — because the Pope claims that all Christians are his subjects. Otherwise it would be just as much against the law of nations to persecute a Christian stranger for professing the religion of his own country as to persecute an infidel — or rather more so, since those who are not against Christ are with him.

From the same source it comes that in every Christian state there are certain men exempt, by "ecclesiastical liberty," from the taxes and the tribunals of the civil state. So are the secular clergy, besides the monks and friars — who in many places make up so great a proportion of the common people that, if the need arose, an army could be raised out of them alone, sufficient for any war the Church militant might choose to employ them in, against their own princes or anyone else's.

The Error of Turning Consecration into Conjuration

A second general abuse of Scripture is the turning of CONSECRATION into CONJURATION, or enchantment. To consecrate, in Scripture, is to offer, give, or dedicate a man or any other thing to God, in pious and decent language and gesture, by separating it from common use — that is, to sanctify it, to make it God's, to be used only by those whom God has appointed to be his public ministers (as I have already proved at length in Chapter 35). Consecration changes not the thing consecrated, but only the use of it: from profane and common, to holy and set apart for God's service. But when such words are claimed to change the very nature or quality of the thing itself, that is not consecration. It is either an extraordinary work of God, or a vain and impious conjuration. And since they claim a change of nature in their consecrations constantly — far too frequently for it to be counted an extraordinary work — it is nothing other than a conjuration or incantation, by which they would have men believe in an alteration of nature that is not there, against the testimony of a man's own sight and of all the rest of his senses.

Take the example. In the sacrament of the Lord's Supper, the priest's task is to consecrate bread and wine to God's particular service — which is nothing but a separating of them from common use, to signify, that is, to put men in mind of, their redemption by the passion of Christ, whose body was broken and blood shed upon the cross for our transgressions. Instead, the priest pretends that by speaking the words of our Savior — "Take, eat: this is my body," and "This is my blood" — the nature of bread is no longer there at all, but Christ's very body; even though nothing appears to the sight, or to any other sense of the receiver, that did not appear before the consecration.

The Egyptian conjurers who are said to have turned their rods into serpents and the water into blood are thought only to have deluded the spectators' senses with a false show of things — and yet they are counted enchanters. But what should we have thought of them if there had appeared in their rods nothing like a serpent, and in the enchanted water nothing like blood, nor like anything else but water — and they had simply faced down the king, insisting that these were serpents that looked like rods, and that it was blood that seemed to be water? That would have been both enchantment and lying. And yet in this daily act of the priest, they do the very same thing. They turn the holy words into the manner of a charm — a charm which now produces nothing at all to the senses — and then they face us down that it has turned the bread into a man; no, more than that: into a god; and they require men to worship it as if it were our Savior himself, present as God and man, and thereby to commit the grossest idolatry. For if it is enough to clear the practice of idolatry to say, "it is no longer bread, but God" — why should not the same excuse have served the Egyptians, supposing they had had the face to say that the leeks and onions they worshipped were not really leeks and onions, but a divinity hidden under their "species," or likeness?

The words "This is my body" are equivalent to "This signifies, or represents, my body." That is an ordinary figure of speech. To take it literally is an abuse; and even taken literally, it can extend no further than to the bread which Christ himself, with his own hands, consecrated. For he never said that whatever bread any priest whatsoever pronounces the words "This is my body" or "This is Christ's body" over should instantly be transubstantiated. Nor did the Church of Rome ever establish this transubstantiation until the time of Innocent III — not much more than five hundred years ago, when the power of the Popes was at its highest, and the darkness of the times had grown so thick that men could no longer make out the bread that was given them to eat; especially when it was stamped with the figure of Christ upon the cross — as if they would have men believe it was transubstantiated not only into the body of Christ, but into the wood of his cross as well, and that they ate both together in the sacrament.

Incantation in the Ceremonies of Baptism

The same incantation, in place of consecration, is used also in the sacrament of baptism. There the abuse of God's name in each separate person, and in the whole Trinity, with the sign of the cross at each name, makes up the charm. First, in making the holy water, the priest says: "I conjure thee, thou creature of water, in the name of God the Father Almighty, and in the name of Jesus Christ his only Son our Lord, and in virtue of the Holy Ghost, that thou become conjured water, to drive away all the powers of the Enemy, and to eradicate and supplant the Enemy," and so on. The same in the blessing of the salt to be mingled with it: "That thou become conjured salt, that all phantasms and knavery of the Devil's fraud may fly and depart from the place wherein thou art sprinkled, and every unclean spirit be conjured by Him that shall come to judge the quick and the dead." The same in the blessing of the oil: "That all the power of the Enemy, all the host of the Devil, all assaults and phantasms of Satan, may be driven away by this creature of oil."

And the infant who is to be baptized is subject to many charms of his own. First, at the church door, the priest blows three times in the child's face and says, "Go out of him, unclean spirit, and give place to the Holy Ghost the Comforter" — as if all children, until blown on by the priest, were demoniacs. Again, before the child enters the church, the priest says as before, "I conjure thee, etc., to go out and depart from this servant of God." And the same exorcism is repeated once more before the baptism itself. These and certain other incantations and consecrations fill the administration of the sacraments of baptism and the Lord's Supper — in which everything that serves those holy men, except the unhallowed spittle of the priest, has its own set form of exorcism.

In Marriage, in Visitation of the Sick, and in Consecration of Places

Nor are the other rites — marriage, extreme unction, visitation of the sick, the consecrating of churches and churchyards, and the like — exempt from charms; for they too involve the use of enchanted oil and water, together with the abuse of the cross and of David's holy words, Asperges me Domine hyssopo — "Purge me with hyssop, O Lord" — used as things with power in themselves to drive away phantasms and imaginary spirits.

Errors About Eternal Life and Everlasting Death

Another general error comes from misinterpreting the words "eternal life," "everlasting death," and "the second death." Read Holy Scripture plainly and you find this. God created Adam in a condition of living forever — but conditionally; that is, on condition that he did not disobey the commandment. That immortality was not essential to human nature; it was a consequence of the virtue of the tree of life, which Adam was free to eat from as long as he had not sinned. And after he sinned, he was thrust out of paradise precisely so that he would not eat of that tree and live forever. And Christ's passion is a discharge of sin to all who believe in him — and therefore a restoration of eternal life to all the faithful, and to them only.

Yet the doctrine now — and for a long time past — has been far otherwise: namely, that every man has eternal life by nature, because his soul is immortal. On that view, the flaming sword at the entrance of paradise may keep a man from reaching the tree of life, but it does not keep him from the immortality God took from him for his sin; nor does it leave him needing the sacrifice of Christ to recover it. And the consequence follows: not only the faithful and righteous, but the wicked too, and the heathen, shall enjoy eternal life, without any death at all — much less a second and everlasting death. To patch this up, they say that by "second and everlasting death" is meant a second and everlasting life — but in torments: a figure of speech never used anywhere but in this very case.

All of this doctrine is founded only on some of the obscurer places of the New Testament — places which, when the whole scope of Scripture is considered, are clear enough in a different sense, and unnecessary to the Christian faith. For suppose that when a man dies, nothing remains of him but his carcass. Cannot God, who raised inanimate dust and clay into a living creature by his word, just as easily raise a dead carcass to life again by another word, and continue him alive forever — or make him die again? The "soul," in Scripture, always signifies either the life, or the living creature; and "body and soul" together signify the body alive. On the fifth day of creation, God said, let the water produce reptile animae viventis — "the creeping thing that hath in it a living soul"; the English translates it, "that hath life." Again, God created whales et omnem animam viventem, which in the English is "every living creature." And likewise of man: God made him of the dust of the earth, and breathed in his face the breath of life, et factus est homo in animam viventem — that is, "and man was made a living creature." And after Noah came out of the ark, God says he will no more smite omnem animam viventem — that is, "every living creature." And Deuteronomy 12:23: "Eat not the blood, for the blood is the soul" — that is, "the life."

If "soul" in these places meant an incorporeal substance, with an existence separate from the body, then the same could be inferred just as well of any other living creature as of man. No: the souls of the faithful remain in their bodies, from the resurrection to all eternity, not by their own nature, but by God's special grace — which I think I have already sufficiently proved out of the Scriptures in Chapter 38. And as for the places of the New Testament where it is said that a man shall be cast body and soul into hell fire, that means no more than body and life; that is to say, they shall be cast alive into the perpetual fire of Gehenna.

Hence the Doctrines of Purgatory, Exorcisms, and the Invocation of Saints

This is the window that lets in the dark doctrine, first, of eternal torments; and after that, of purgatory; and consequently of the walking abroad — especially in consecrated, solitary, or dark places — of the ghosts of the dead; and from there, the pretenses of exorcism and the conjuring of phantasms; and likewise the invocation of dead men; and the doctrine of indulgences — that is, of exemption, for a time or forever, from the fire of purgatory, in which these incorporeal substances are supposedly cleansed by burning and made fit for heaven. Here is how it happened. Before the time of our Savior, men were generally possessed — by contagion from the demonology of the Greeks — of the opinion that the souls of men were substances distinct from their bodies; and therefore that when the body died, the soul of every man, godly or wicked, had to subsist somewhere by virtue of its own nature, without acknowledging any supernatural gift of God in the matter. The doctors of the Church doubted for a long time what place these souls were to wait in, until they should be reunited with their bodies at the resurrection — supposing, for a while, that they lay under the altars. But afterward the Church of Rome found it more profitable to build for them this place of purgatory; which some other churches, in this later age, have demolished.

The Texts Cited for These Doctrines Have Been Answered Already

Let us now consider which texts of Scripture seem most to confirm these three general errors I have touched on here. As for the texts Cardinal Bellarmine alleges in favor of the present kingdom of God administered by the Pope — and none make a better show of proof — I have answered them already, and made it evident that the kingdom of God instituted by Moses ended in the election of Saul. After that time, the priest never, by his own authority, deposed any king. What the high priest did to Athaliah was done not in his own right, but in the right of the young king Joash, her son. Solomon, on the other hand, in his own right deposed the high priest Abiathar, and set up another in his place.

The most difficult passage of all that can be brought to prove that the kingdom of God through Christ is already in this world is alleged not by Bellarmine, nor by anyone else of the Church of Rome, but by Beza — who would have the kingdom begin at the resurrection of Christ. Whether he intends by this to entitle the presbytery to the supreme ecclesiastical power in the commonwealth of Geneva (and consequently every presbytery in every other commonwealth), or to entitle princes and other civil sovereigns to it, I do not know. For the presbytery has claimed the power to excommunicate its own kings, and to be the supreme moderator in religion wherever that form of church government exists, no less than the Pope claims it universally.

Answer to the Text from Which Beza Infers That the Kingdom of Christ Began at the Resurrection

The words are these (Mark 9:1): "Verily I say unto you, that there be some of them that stand here, which shall not taste of death, till they have seen the kingdom of God come with power." Taken grammatically, these words make one of two things certain: either some of the men who stood by Christ at that time are still alive, or else the kingdom of God must already be in this present world.

But then there is another, harder place. When the apostles, after our Savior's resurrection and immediately before his ascension, asked him (Acts 1:6), "Wilt thou at this time restore again the kingdom to Israel?" — he answered them: "It is not for you to know the times and the seasons, which the Father hath put in his own power; but ye shall receive power by the coming of the Holy Ghost upon you, and ye shall be my (martyrs) witnesses both in Jerusalem, and in all Judaea, and in Samaria, and unto the uttermost part of the earth." Which is as much as to say: my kingdom is not yet come, and you shall not know in advance when it will come, for it will come like a thief in the night; but I will send you the Holy Ghost, and by him you shall have power to bear witness to all the world, by your preaching, of my resurrection, of the works I have done, and of the doctrine I have taught, so that men may believe in me and expect eternal life at my coming again. How does that square with Christ's kingdom coming at the resurrection?

And there is what St. Paul says (1 Thessalonians 1:9-10): "that they turned from idols, to serve the living and true God, and to wait for his Son from heaven." To wait for his Son from heaven is to wait for his coming to be king in power — which would not be necessary if his kingdom were present already. Again: if the kingdom of God began at the resurrection, as Beza on that place (Mark 9:1) would have it, what reason have Christians had, ever since the resurrection, to say in their prayers, "Thy kingdom come"? It is therefore plain that the words of St. Mark are not to be interpreted that way. "There be some of them that stand here," says our Savior, "that shall not taste of death till they have seen the kingdom of God come in power." If this kingdom were to come at the resurrection of Christ, why does he say "some of them," rather than all? For they all lived until after Christ had risen.

Explaining the Passage in Mark 9:1

But those who demand an exact interpretation of this text should first interpret the similar words of our Savior to St. Peter concerning St. John (John 21:22): "If I will that he tarry till I come, what is that to thee?" On those words was grounded a report that John would never die; yet the truth of that report was neither confirmed as well grounded, nor refuted as ill grounded, by those words. It was simply left as a saying not understood. The same difficulty stands in the place in St. Mark. And if it is lawful to make a guess at the meaning from what immediately follows — both here and in St. Luke, where the same words are repeated — it is not improbable that they refer to the Transfiguration, which is described in the very next verses. There it is said that "after six days Jesus taketh with him Peter, and James, and John" — not all, but some of his disciples — "and leadeth them up into an high mountain apart by themselves: and he was transfigured before them. And his raiment became shining, exceeding white as snow; so as no fuller on earth can white them. And there appeared unto them Elias with Moses: and they were talking with Jesus," and so forth. So they saw Christ in glory and majesty, as he is to come — so much so that "they were sore afraid." And thus the promise of our Savior was fulfilled by way of vision. For it was a vision, as may probably be inferred from St. Luke, who recites the same story (Luke 9:28) and says that Peter and those with him were heavy with sleep; and most certainly from Matthew 17:9, where the same is related again, for our Savior charged them, saying, "Tell no man the vision, until the Son of man be risen from the dead." However it may be, no argument can be drawn from this passage to prove that the kingdom of God begins before the day of judgment.
