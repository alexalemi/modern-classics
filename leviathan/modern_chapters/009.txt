Chapter 9: The Different Branches of Knowledge

There are two kinds of KNOWLEDGE. One is knowledge of fact. The other is knowledge of the consequence of one statement to another.

The first kind is nothing more than sense and memory, and it is absolute knowledge — as when we see something happening, or remember seeing it done. This is the knowledge required of a witness.

The second kind is called science, and it is conditional — as when we know that if the figure shown is a circle, then any straight line through the center will divide it into two equal parts. This is the knowledge required of a philosopher — that is, of anyone who claims to reason.

The record of knowledge of fact is called HISTORY, and it comes in two sorts. One is natural history: the history of facts, or effects of nature, that do not depend on human will — histories of metals, plants, animals, regions, and the like. The other is civil history: the history of the voluntary actions of people living in commonwealths.

The records of science are the books that contain demonstrations of how one statement follows from another. These are commonly called books of philosophy, and they come in many sorts, depending on the variety of their subject matter. They may be divided as I have divided them in the following table.

SCIENCE — that is, knowledge of consequences, which is also called PHILOSOPHY:

A. Consequences from the properties of natural bodies — which is called NATURAL PHILOSOPHY.

   1. Consequences from the properties common to all natural bodies, which are quantity and motion.

      a. Consequences from quantity and motion in the abstract — indeterminate, not yet tied to any particular thing. Because these are the principles, the first foundation of philosophy, this is called philosophia prima:
         FIRST PHILOSOPHY

      b. Consequences from motion and quantity determined — pinned down to particular cases.

         1) Consequences from quantity and motion determined by figure and by number — the mathematical sciences:
            GEOMETRY
            ARITHMETIC

         2) Consequences from the motion and quantity of particular kinds of bodies.

            a) Consequences from the motion and quantity of the great parts of the world, such as the earth and the stars — cosmography:
               ASTRONOMY
               GEOGRAPHY

            b) Consequences from the motion of particular kinds and shapes of bodies — mechanics, the doctrine of weight — the science of:
               ENGINEERS
               ARCHITECTURE
               NAVIGATION

   2. PHYSICS — consequences from qualities.

      a. Consequences from the qualities of transient bodies, the kind that sometimes appear and sometimes vanish:
         METEOROLOGY

      b. Consequences from the qualities of permanent bodies.

         1) Consequences from the qualities of the stars.

            a) Consequences from the light of the stars. From this, together with the motion of the sun, is made the science of:
               SCIOGRAPHY — the science of shadows, used for telling time

            b) Consequences from the influence of the stars:
               ASTROLOGY

         2) Consequences from the qualities of the liquid bodies that fill the space between the stars — such as the air, or the ethereal substance.

         3) Consequences from the qualities of earthly bodies.

            a) Consequences from the parts of the earth that have no sense.

               1] Consequences from the qualities of minerals, such as stones, metals, and so on.

               2] Consequences from the qualities of plants.

            b) Consequences from the qualities of animals.

               1] Consequences from the qualities of animals in general.

                  a] Consequences from vision:
                     OPTICS

                  b] Consequences from sounds:
                     MUSIC

                  c] Consequences from the rest of the senses.

               2] Consequences from the qualities of human beings in particular.

                  a] Consequences from the passions of men:
                     ETHICS

                  b] Consequences from speech.

                     i) In magnifying, vilifying, and the like:
                        POETRY

                     ii) In persuading:
                        RHETORIC

                     iii) In reasoning:
                        LOGIC

                     iv) In contracting — in making agreements — the science of:
                        JUST and UNJUST

B. Consequences from the properties of political bodies — which is called POLITICS, or CIVIL PHILOSOPHY.

   1. Consequences from the institution of commonwealths, concerning the rights and duties of the body politic, or sovereign.

   2. Consequences from the same, concerning the duties and rights of the subjects.
