Chapter 6: The Inner Beginnings of Voluntary Motion — Commonly Called the Passions — and the Speech That Expresses Them


Vital Motion and Voluntary Motion

Animals have two kinds of motion that belong to them alone. One is called vital motion: it begins at conception and continues without interruption through the whole of life. The circulation of the blood, the pulse, breathing, digestion, nutrition, excretion — these are vital motions, and they need no help from the imagination. The other kind is animal motion, otherwise called voluntary motion: walking, speaking, moving any of our limbs in whatever way we first picture in our minds.

I have already shown, in the first and second chapters, that sense is motion in the organs and inner parts of the body, caused by the action of the things we see and hear, and that imagination is nothing but the leftovers of that same motion, remaining after sense. And because walking, speaking, and other voluntary motions always depend on a prior thought of where, which way, and what, it is clear that the imagination is the first internal beginning of all voluntary motion.

Now, people who have never studied the matter cannot conceive of any motion at all when the thing moved is invisible, or when the space it moves through is too small to notice. But that does not mean such motions do not exist. However small a space may be, whatever moves across a larger space — of which that small one is a part — must move across the small one first.

These small beginnings of motion inside the human body, before they show themselves in walking, speaking, striking, and other visible actions, are commonly called ENDEAVOR — the tiny internal beginnings of motion.


Endeavor: Appetite, Desire, Hunger, Thirst, Aversion

When this endeavor is directed toward the thing that causes it, it is called APPETITE, or DESIRE. Desire is the general name; appetite is often reserved for the desire of food — namely hunger and thirst. And when the endeavor moves away from something, it is generally called AVERSION.

The words "appetite" and "aversion" come to us from the Latin, and both signify motions: one of approaching, the other of retreating. So do the Greek words for the same things, horme and aphorme. Nature herself often presses these truths on men — truths they later stumble over when they go looking for something beyond nature. The Scholastics, for instance, can find no actual motion at all in the mere appetite to walk or move. But since they must admit some motion is there, they call it "metaphorical motion" — which is simply absurd speech. Words may be metaphorical. Bodies and motions cannot.

What men desire, they are also said to LOVE; and they are said to HATE the things they have aversion for. So desire and love are the same thing, except that by desire we always mean the object is absent, and by love we usually mean it is present. Likewise, by aversion we signify the absence of the object, and by hate, its presence.

Of appetites and aversions, some are born with us: the appetite for food, the appetite for excretion and relief (which might more properly be called aversions, since they come from something men feel in their own bodies), and a few others — not many. The rest, the appetites for particular things, come from experience — from testing their effects on ourselves or watching their effects on other men. For things we do not know at all, or do not believe exist, we can have no further desire than the desire to taste and try them. But we feel aversion not only toward things we know have hurt us, but also toward things when we do not know whether they will hurt us or not.


Contempt

The things we neither desire nor hate, we are said to despise. CONTEMPT is nothing but an immobility, a stubbornness of the heart in resisting the action of certain things — and it comes either from the heart being already moved in another direction by more powerful objects, or from lack of experience of the things despised.

And because the makeup of a man's body is in constant change, it is impossible for all the same things to always cause in him the same appetites and aversions. Much less can all men agree in desiring almost any single object.


Good and Evil

But whatever a man desires, that is what he, for his part, calls good. Whatever he hates, he calls evil. And whatever he despises, he calls worthless and beneath notice. For these words — good, evil, contemptible — are always used in relation to the person using them. Nothing is simply and absolutely good or evil. There is no common rule of good and evil to be found in the nature of the objects themselves. The rule comes from the person of the man, where there is no commonwealth; or, in a commonwealth, from the person who represents it; or from an arbitrator or judge whom disagreeing men set up by consent, making his decision the rule.


Pulchrum and Turpe; Delightful and Profitable; Unpleasant and Unprofitable

Latin has two words whose meanings come close to good and evil but are not quite the same: pulchrum and turpe. The first signifies that which, by some visible sign, promises good; the second, that which promises evil. Our language has no names that general. For pulchrum we say, depending on the thing, fair, beautiful, handsome, gallant, honorable, comely, or lovely; for turpe we say foul, deformed, ugly, base, nauseating, and the like, as the subject requires. All these words, used properly, signify nothing but the look or appearance that promises good or evil.

So there are three kinds of good: good in the promise — that is pulchrum; good in effect, as the end desired — which is called jucundum, delightful; and good as the means — which is called utile, profitable. And there are just as many kinds of evil: evil in the promise is what they call turpe; evil in effect and end is molestum — unpleasant, troublesome; and evil in the means is inutile — unprofitable, hurtful.


Delight and Displeasure

In sense, as I have said before, what is really inside us is only motion, caused by the action of external objects — though it appears to the sight as light and color, to the ear as sound, to the nostril as odor, and so on. In the same way, when the action of that same object continues from the eyes, ears, and other organs to the heart, the real effect there is nothing but motion, or endeavor, which consists in appetite or aversion toward or away from the object. But the appearance, the felt sense of that motion, is what we call either DELIGHT or TROUBLE OF MIND.


Pleasure and Offense

This motion called appetite — and, in its appearance, delight and pleasure — seems to be a strengthening of the vital motion, a help to it. That is why the things that caused delight were quite properly called jucunda, from juvare, to help or fortify; and their contraries molesta, offensive, from hindering and troubling the vital motion.

PLEASURE, therefore (or delight), is the appearance or sense of good; and MOLESTATION or DISPLEASURE is the appearance or sense of evil. And so all appetite, desire, and love comes with some delight, more or less; and all hatred and aversion with more or less displeasure and offense.


Pleasures of Sense; Pleasures of the Mind; Joy, Pain, Grief

Of pleasures, or delights, some arise from the sense of an object that is present. These may be called pleasures of sense. (The word "sensual," as used by those who condemn such pleasures, has no place until there are laws.) Of this kind are all the fillings and emptyings of the body, and everything pleasant to sight, hearing, smell, taste, or touch.

Other pleasures arise from expectation — from foreseeing the end or consequence of things, whether those things please or displease in the sense itself. These are pleasures of the mind in the person who draws those consequences, and they are generally called JOY. In the same way, some displeasures are in the sense, and are called PAIN; others are in the expectation of consequences, and are called GRIEF.

These simple passions — appetite, desire, love, aversion, hate, joy, and grief — take different names for different reasons. First, when they follow one after another, they are named differently according to the opinion men have of their chances of getting what they desire. Secondly, from the object loved or hated. Thirdly, from considering many of them together. Fourthly, from the alternation or sequence itself.

Hope — Appetite combined with an opinion of attaining the thing is called HOPE.

Despair — The same, without that opinion, is DESPAIR.

Fear — Aversion, with the opinion that the object will hurt us, is FEAR.

Courage — The same, with hope of avoiding that hurt by resisting, is COURAGE.

Anger — Sudden courage is ANGER.

Confidence — Constant hope is CONFIDENCE in ourselves.

Diffidence — Constant despair is DIFFIDENCE in ourselves — a settled distrust of our own powers.

Indignation — Anger at great hurt done to another, when we believe it was done by injury, is INDIGNATION.

Benevolence — Desire of good for another is BENEVOLENCE, GOOD WILL, CHARITY. Directed at mankind in general, it is GOOD NATURE.

Covetousness — Desire of riches is COVETOUSNESS — a name always used as blame, because men competing for riches are displeased when others get them. The desire itself, though, deserves blame or approval according to the means by which the riches are sought.

Ambition — Desire of office or precedence is AMBITION — a name also used in the worse sense, for the reason just mentioned.

Pusillanimity — Desire of things that help our purposes only a little, and fear of things that hinder them only a little, is PUSILLANIMITY — small-mindedness.

Magnanimity — Contempt for little helps and little hindrances is MAGNANIMITY — greatness of mind.

Valor — Magnanimity in danger of death or wounds is VALOR, or FORTITUDE.

Liberality — Magnanimity in the use of riches is LIBERALITY.

Miserableness — Pusillanimity in the same is WRETCHEDNESS, MISERABLENESS, or PARSIMONY — depending on whether the speaker approves or disapproves.

Kindness — Love of persons for company and society is KINDNESS.

Natural Lust — Love of persons only for pleasing the senses is NATURAL LUST.

Luxury — Love of the same, acquired by rumination — that is, by imagining past pleasure — is LUXURY.

The Passion of Love; Jealousy — Love of one person singularly, with desire to be singularly loved in return, is THE PASSION OF LOVE. The same, with fear that the love is not mutual, is JEALOUSY.

Revengefulness — Desire to hurt another in order to make him condemn some act of his own is REVENGEFULNESS.

Curiosity — Desire to know why, and how, is CURIOSITY. No living creature has it but man — so man is distinguished from the other animals not only by his reason but by this singular passion as well. In the other animals, the appetite for food and the other pleasures of sense dominate, and crowd out any care for knowing causes. Curiosity is a lust of the mind that, by a persistence of delight in the continual and tireless generation of knowledge, outlasts the short intensity of any bodily pleasure.

Religion; Superstition; True Religion — Fear of invisible power, whether invented by the mind or imagined from stories that are publicly allowed, is RELIGION; from stories not allowed, SUPERSTITION. And when the imagined power really is such as we imagine it, TRUE RELIGION.

Panic Terror — Fear without any grasp of why or what is PANIC TERROR — named after the fables that make the god Pan its author. In truth, the person who first feels such fear always has some idea of the cause; the rest simply run because everyone else is running, each one assuming his neighbor knows why. That is why this passion strikes no one except in a crowd or multitude of people.

Admiration — Joy from the sense of novelty is ADMIRATION — proper to man, because it stirs up the appetite for knowing the cause.

Glory; Vainglory — Joy arising from imagining one's own power and ability is that exultation of the mind called GLORYING. If it is grounded on the experience of one's own past actions, it is the same as confidence. But if it is grounded on the flattery of others, or merely supposed by the man himself for the delight he takes in its consequences, it is called VAINGLORY — vanity. The name fits, because a well-grounded confidence leads a man to attempt things, whereas merely supposed power does not, and is therefore rightly called vain.

Dejection — Grief from the opinion that one lacks power is called DEJECTION of mind.

The vainglory that consists in pretending or supposing abilities in ourselves that we know we do not have is most common in young men. It is fed by the histories and fictions of gallant heroes, and is often corrected by age and employment.

Sudden Glory; Laughter — SUDDEN GLORY is the passion that produces those grimaces we call LAUGHTER. It is caused either by some sudden act of our own that pleases us, or by spotting some defect in another person, by comparison with which we suddenly applaud ourselves. It strikes most often those who are conscious of having the fewest abilities themselves — people forced to stay in their own good graces by noticing the imperfections of other men. That is why a great deal of laughter at the defects of others is a sign of pusillanimity. One of the proper works of a great mind is to help others and free them from scorn, and to compare itself only with the most able.

Sudden Dejection; Weeping — On the contrary, SUDDEN DEJECTION is the passion that causes WEEPING. It is brought on by accidents that suddenly snatch away some intense hope, or some prop of a person's power. Those most subject to it are those who rely chiefly on external supports — such as women and children. So some weep for the loss of friends, others for friends' unkindness, others when reconciliation puts a sudden stop to their thoughts of revenge. But in all cases, both laughter and weeping are sudden motions, and habit wears them both away. No one laughs at an old joke, or weeps over an old calamity.

Shame; Blushing — Grief at the discovery of some defect in our ability is SHAME — the passion that reveals itself in BLUSHING. It consists in the awareness of something dishonorable. In young men it is a sign of the love of a good reputation, and commendable. In old men it is a sign of the same, but because it comes too late, not commendable.

Impudence — Contempt for good reputation is called IMPUDENCE.

Pity — Grief at the calamity of another is PITY, and it arises from imagining that the same calamity might befall ourselves. That is why it is also called COMPASSION, and, in the phrase of the present time, FELLOW-FEELING. And that is also why the best men have the least pity for calamity that comes from great wickedness — and why those who think themselves least exposed to a given calamity feel the least pity for it.

Cruelty — Contempt for, or insensitivity to, the calamity of others is what men call CRUELTY, and it comes from a sense of security about one's own fortune. For I do not believe it possible that any man takes pleasure in the great harms of other men with no end of his own in view.

Emulation; Envy — Grief at a competitor's success in wealth, honor, or some other good, joined with the endeavor to strain our own abilities to equal or surpass him, is called EMULATION. Joined instead with the endeavor to supplant or hinder the competitor, it is ENVY.


Deliberation

When appetites and aversions, hopes and fears about one and the same thing arise in a man's mind by turns — when the various good and evil consequences of doing or not doing the thing pass through his thoughts in succession, so that sometimes he has an appetite for it, sometimes an aversion from it, sometimes hope of managing it, sometimes despair or fear of attempting it — the whole sum of those desires, aversions, hopes, and fears, continued until the thing is either done or judged impossible, is what we call DELIBERATION.

It follows that there is no deliberation about things past, because they obviously cannot be changed; nor about things known or believed to be impossible, because men know, or think, that deliberating about them is pointless. But about impossible things we believe to be possible, we may deliberate — not knowing that it is in vain. And it is called deliberation because it is a putting an end to the liberty we had of doing the thing, or not doing it, according to our own appetite or aversion.

This alternating succession of appetites, aversions, hopes, and fears occurs no less in other living creatures than in man. Therefore beasts also deliberate.

Every deliberation is said to end when the thing deliberated about is either done or judged impossible — because until then we keep the liberty of doing it or not, according to our appetite or aversion.


The Will

In deliberation, the last appetite or aversion — the one immediately attached to the action or to the omission of it — is what we call the WILL: the act (not the faculty) of willing. And beasts that have deliberation must necessarily also have will.

The definition of the will commonly given by the Scholastics — that it is a "rational appetite" — is not a good one. If it were, there could be no voluntary act against reason, since a voluntary act is precisely one that proceeds from the will and from nothing else. But if instead of "rational appetite" we say "an appetite resulting from prior deliberation," then their definition becomes the same as mine. The will, therefore, is the last appetite in deliberating.

It is true that in everyday talk we say a man once had a will to do a thing that he nevertheless refrained from doing. But that, properly speaking, was only an inclination — and an inclination makes no action voluntary, because the action depends not on it but on the last inclination or appetite. If the intervening appetites made an action voluntary, then by the same logic all the intervening aversions would make the same action involuntary — and one and the same action would be both voluntary and involuntary.

From this it is clear that voluntary actions include not only those that begin in covetousness, ambition, lust, or other appetites for the thing proposed, but also those that begin in aversion, or in fear of the consequences of leaving it undone.


The Forms of Speech for the Passions

The forms of speech that express the passions are partly the same as those that express our thoughts, and partly different. First, and generally, all passions can be expressed in the indicative: I love, I fear, I rejoice, I deliberate, I will, I command. But some passions have particular expressions of their own — expressions which, nonetheless, are not affirmations, unless they happen to serve some further inference beyond the passion they spring from.

Deliberation is expressed in the subjunctive — the form of speech proper for stating suppositions and their consequences, as in: if this is done, then this will follow. It differs from the language of reasoning only in this: reasoning works in general terms, while deliberation is mostly about particulars.

The language of desire and aversion is the imperative: do this, do not do that. When the person addressed is obliged to do or not do the thing, this is command; otherwise it is prayer, or else counsel.

The language of vainglory, of indignation, of pity, and of revengefulness is the optative — the language of wishing. But for the desire to know there is a special expression called the interrogative: What is it? When will it happen? How is it done? Why so?

Other language of the passions I find none. Cursing, swearing, reviling, and the like do not signify as speech at all — they are just the actions of a tongue that has gotten used to them.

These forms of speech, I say, are expressions, or voluntary indications, of our passions. But they are not certain signs of them, because men may use them at will, whether they feel the passions or not. The best signs of a passion actually present are found in the face, the motions of the body, the actions, and the ends or aims we otherwise know the man to have.


Apparent Good and Evil

In deliberation, the appetites and aversions are raised by foreseeing the good and evil consequences and sequels of the action we are deliberating about. So the good or evil effect of the action depends on foreseeing a long chain of consequences — and very seldom is any man able to see to the end of it. But as far as a man does see: if the good in those consequences outweighs the evil, the whole chain is what writers call apparent or seeming good; and when the evil outweighs the good, the whole is apparent or seeming evil. So the man who has, by experience or reason, the longest and surest view of the consequences deliberates best himself, and is able, when he chooses, to give the best counsel to others.


Happiness

Continual success in obtaining the things a man desires from time to time — continual prospering, in other words — is what men call FELICITY: happiness. I mean the happiness of this life. For there is no such thing as perpetual tranquility of mind while we live here, because life itself is nothing but motion, and can no more be without desire and without fear than it can be without sense. What kind of happiness God has prepared for those who devoutly honor him, a man will know no sooner than he enjoys it — those are joys as incomprehensible now as the Scholastics' phrase "beatifical vision" is unintelligible.


Praise and Magnification

The form of speech by which men signify their opinion of the goodness of anything is PRAISE. The form by which they signify its power and greatness is MAGNIFYING. And the form by which they signify their opinion of a man's happiness the Greeks called makarismos — calling someone blessed — for which we have no word in our language.

And this much is sufficient, for the present purpose, to have said about the passions.
