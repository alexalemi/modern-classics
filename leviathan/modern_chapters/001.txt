Chapter 1: Of Sense

I want to examine the thoughts of man — first one at a time, and afterward in trains, where each thought depends on the one before it.

Taken singly, every thought is a representation or appearance of some quality, or some other feature, of a body outside us — what we commonly call an OBJECT. The object works on the eyes, the ears, and the other parts of the body, and by working in different ways, it produces different appearances.

The origin of all of them is what we call SENSE. For there is no conception in a person's mind that was not first, in whole or in part, born in the organs of sense. Everything else derives from that original.

Knowing the natural cause of sense is not strictly necessary for the business now at hand, and I have written about it at length elsewhere. Still, to fill in each part of my present method, I will briefly lay it out here.

The cause of sense is the external body, or object, which presses on the organ proper to each sense — directly, as in taste and touch, or indirectly, as in seeing, hearing, and smelling. That pressure is carried inward, by way of the nerves and the other strings and membranes of the body, until it reaches the brain and the heart. There it causes a resistance, a counter-pressure — an endeavor of the heart to push back, to deliver itself. And because that effort presses outward, it seems to be some matter outside us.

This seeming, this appearance — call it the fancy — is exactly what people mean by sense. To the eye, it consists in a light, or a color with a shape. To the ear, in a sound. To the nostril, in an odor. To the tongue and palate, in a flavor. And to the rest of the body, in heat, cold, hardness, softness, and the other qualities we pick out by feeling.

Now, all these qualities we call "sensible" are, in the object that causes them, nothing but so many different motions of matter — motions by which the object presses on our organs in different ways. And in us, the ones being pressed, they are nothing but different motions either. For motion produces nothing but motion.

But how they appear to us is fancy — and the appearance is the same whether we are awake or dreaming. Press, rub, or strike your eye, and you will fancy you see a light. Press your ear, and it produces a ringing din. The bodies we actually see and hear do the very same thing — they produce these appearances by their own strong, though unobserved, action.

For if those colors and sounds really lived in the bodies, or objects, that cause them, they could never be separated from them. But they can be — we see it done. Mirrors and lenses sever the image from the thing; echoes, by reflection, sever the sound from its source. In those cases we know the thing we see is in one place, and the appearance is in another. And though at a certain distance the real, actual object seems to be clothed in the very fancy it begets in us, still the object is one thing, and the image or fancy is another.

So sense, in every case, is nothing else but original fancy — caused, as I have said, by the pressure, that is, the motion, of external things upon our eyes, ears, and the other organs appointed for it.

But the philosophy schools, throughout all the universities of Christendom, teach a different doctrine, grounded on certain texts of Aristotle. To explain vision, they say that the thing seen sends out, in every direction, a visible species — in plain English, a visible show, apparition, or aspect, a "being seen" — and that the receiving of this into the eye is seeing. To explain hearing, they say the thing heard sends out an audible species — that is, an audible aspect, or an audible "being seen" — which enters at the ear and makes hearing. And they go further: to explain understanding itself, they say the thing understood sends out an intelligible species — an intelligible "being seen" — which comes into the understanding and makes us understand.

I do not say this to disapprove of universities. But because I will have to speak later about their proper role in a commonwealth, I must show you along the way, at every opportunity, what things in them need to be amended. And among those things, the sheer frequency of insignificant speech — words that signify nothing — is one.
