The Epistle Dedicatory

To my most honored friend, Mr. Francis Godolphin of Godolphin.

Honored Sir,

Your most worthy brother, Mr. Sidney Godolphin, while he lived, was kind enough to think my studies worth something — and, as you know, to back that good opinion with real acts of generosity, great in themselves and greater still for the worth of the man who gave them. There is no virtue that fits a man for the service of God or the service of his country, for civil society or for private friendship, that did not show plainly in everything he did — not as something acquired out of necessity, or put on for the occasion, but inborn, shining out of a generous nature. So in honor and gratitude to him, and with devotion to you, I humbly dedicate to you this discourse of mine on the commonwealth.

I don't know how the world will receive it, or how it may reflect on those who seem to favor it. The road I am walking is lined on one side by people fighting for too much liberty, and on the other by people fighting for too much authority, and it is hard to pass between those two points unwounded. Still, it seems to me that an effort to strengthen the civil power should not be condemned by the civil power itself; nor should private men, by attacking that effort, announce that they think such power is already too great.

Besides, I am not speaking of men, but in the abstract, of the seat of power — like those simple and impartial creatures in the Roman Capitol that defended the people inside with their noise, not because of who those people were, but because that is where they were. So I offend no one, I think, except those outside the walls, or anyone inside (if there is any such person) who favors them.

What may give the most offense are certain texts of Holy Scripture that I cite for purposes other than the ones they are ordinarily put to by others. But I have done this with due submission, and also — given my subject — out of necessity. For those texts are the outworks of the enemy: the forward fortifications from which they attack the civil power.

If, despite all this, you find my work generally condemned, you may excuse yourself by saying that I am a man who loves his own opinions and thinks everything he says is true; that I honored your brother, and honor you; and that on the strength of that, I have presumed — without your knowledge — to take the title of being, as I truly am,

Sir,

Your most humble and most obedient servant,
Thomas Hobbes.

Paris, April 15/25, 1651.


Introduction

Nature — the art by which God has made and governs the world — can be imitated by the art of man, here as in so many other things, even to the point that man can make an artificial animal. After all, life is just a motion of limbs, set going from some central part inside. So why shouldn't we say that all automata — machines that move themselves by springs and wheels, the way a watch does — have an artificial life? For what is the heart, but a spring? What are the nerves, but so many strings? What are the joints, but so many wheels, giving motion to the whole body, just as the maker intended?

But art goes further still, imitating that rational and most excellent work of nature: man himself. For by art is created that great LEVIATHAN called a COMMONWEALTH, or STATE (in Latin, civitas), which is nothing other than an artificial man — though one of greater stature and strength than the natural man, for whose protection and defense it was built.

In this artificial man, sovereignty — the supreme power, whether held by a king or an assembly — is an artificial soul, giving life and motion to the whole body. The magistrates and other officers of justice and law enforcement are artificial joints. Reward and punishment, by which every joint and member is fastened to the seat of sovereignty and moved to do its duty, are the nerves, doing the same job they do in a natural body. The wealth and riches of all the individual members are its strength. Salus populi — the people's safety — is its business. Counselors, who supply it with everything it needs to know, are its memory. Equity and laws are an artificial reason and will. Concord is its health; sedition is its sickness; and civil war is its death. And lastly, the pacts and covenants — the binding agreements — by which the parts of this body politic were first made, fitted together, and united, resemble that Fiat, that "Let us make man," which God pronounced at the Creation.

To describe the nature of this artificial man, I will consider:

First, the matter it is made of, and its maker — both of which are man.

Secondly, how it is made, and by what covenants; what the rights and just power or authority of a sovereign are; and what preserves a commonwealth and what dissolves it.

Thirdly, what a Christian commonwealth is.

Lastly, what the Kingdom of Darkness is.

Concerning the first point, there is a saying that has been thrown around a lot lately: that wisdom is acquired not by reading books, but by reading men. Following that idea, certain people — who mostly can give no other proof of being wise — take great delight in showing off what they think they have read in other men, by passing unkind judgments on one another behind each other's backs.

But there is another saying, not well understood these days, by which they might learn to truly read one another, if they were willing to take the trouble. It is Nosce teipsum: Read yourself. This was never meant, as it is used now, to excuse the barbarous behavior of men in power toward their inferiors, or to encourage men of low rank to act insolently toward their betters. It was meant to teach us this: because one man's thoughts and passions are so similar to another's, whoever looks into himself — and considers what he is doing when he thinks, judges, reasons, hopes, fears, and so on, and on what grounds — will thereby read and know what the thoughts and passions of all other men are on similar occasions.

I mean the similarity of the passions themselves, which are the same in all men: desire, fear, hope, and the rest. I do not mean the similarity of the objects of the passions — the particular things desired, feared, hoped for, and so on. Those vary with each person's individual makeup and particular upbringing, and they are so easily hidden from us that the characters written on a man's heart — blotted and scrambled as they are by deceit, lying, faking, and false doctrines — are legible only to the One who searches hearts. It is true that men's actions sometimes reveal their designs. But to read those actions without comparing them to our own, and without weighing all the circumstances that might change the case, is to try to decipher a code without the key — and to be deceived most of the time, either by trusting too much or by trusting too little, depending on whether the reader is himself a good man or a bad one.

But even if a man could read another man perfectly by his actions, that would serve him only with his acquaintances, and they are few. Whoever is to govern a whole nation must read in himself not this or that particular man, but mankind. That is hard to do — harder than learning any language or science. Yet once I have set down my own reading in an orderly and clear way, the only work left to anyone else will be to consider whether he does not find the same things in himself. For this kind of doctrine admits no other demonstration.
