Chapter 8: The Virtues Commonly Called Intellectual, and Their Opposite Defects

Intellectual Virtue Defined

Virtue, in every kind of subject, is something valued for standing out. It exists by comparison. If everyone had every quality in equal measure, nothing would be prized in anyone.

By INTELLECTUAL VIRTUES, we always mean those abilities of the mind that people praise, value, and wish they had themselves. They usually go under the name of a good wit — though the word "wit" is also used to single out one particular ability from the rest.

Wit: Natural or Acquired

These virtues come in two sorts: natural and acquired.

By "natural," I do not mean what a man has from birth. What we are born with is nothing but sense, and in sense men differ so little from one another — and from brute beasts — that it cannot be counted among the virtues. I mean instead the wit that is gained by use and experience alone, without method, training, or instruction.

This NATURAL WIT consists mainly of two things: quickness of imagining — that is, a swift movement from one thought to the next — and steady direction toward some chosen goal.

The opposite, a slow imagination, makes the defect of mind commonly called DULLNESS, stupidity, and sometimes other names that signify slowness of motion, or difficulty in being moved at all.

Good Wit, or Fancy; Good Judgment; Discretion

This difference in quickness comes from the difference in men's passions. People love and dislike different things, so their thoughts run in different directions, and they hold onto and notice different things as they pass through the imagination.

Now, when thoughts follow one another, there are really only a few things to notice about them: how they are alike, how they are unlike, what they are good for, or how they serve a particular purpose.

Those who notice the likenesses between things — especially likenesses that others rarely spot — are said to have a good wit, by which, in this case, we mean a good FANCY: a creative quickness of imagination.

Those who notice the differences and dissimilarities — which is called distinguishing, discerning, and judging between thing and thing — are said, when such discerning is not easy, to have a good JUDGMENT. And in matters of conversation and business in particular, where you must read the times, the places, and the people, this virtue is called DISCRETION.

The first of these, fancy without the help of judgment, is not praised as a virtue. But the second — judgment, and discretion — is praised for its own sake, with or without any fancy to assist it.

Besides the discretion about times, places, and persons that a good fancy requires, something else is needed too: a frequent steering of one's thoughts toward their end — that is, toward some use to be made of them. A man who does this will find himself easily supplied with comparisons that please, not only because they illustrate his speech and decorate it with new and fitting metaphors, but because of the sheer rarity of their invention.

But without steadiness and direction toward some end, a great fancy is one kind of madness. You see it in people who, entering into any conversation, get snatched away from their point by every thought that crosses their mind, into so many digressions, and such long ones, and so many parentheses, that they completely lose themselves. I know of no particular name for this kind of folly. Its cause is sometimes lack of experience, which makes a thing seem new and remarkable to one man that seems ordinary to everyone else; sometimes it is small-mindedness, which makes something seem important to him that other men consider a trifle. And whatever seems new or important — and therefore worth telling — pulls a man, step by step, away from the road his talk was supposed to travel.

In a good poem, whether epic or dramatic, and likewise in sonnets, epigrams, and other pieces, both judgment and fancy are required — but the fancy must be the more prominent, because such works please by their extravagance. They simply must not offend through indiscretion.

In a good history, judgment must be the more prominent, because the goodness of a history lies in its method, its truth, and its choice of the actions most worth knowing. Fancy has no place there except in dressing up the style.

In speeches of praise, and in attacks and invectives, fancy dominates, because the goal is not truth but honoring or dishonoring someone — which is done through noble or vile comparisons. Judgment only supplies the circumstances that make an action praiseworthy or blameworthy.

In exhortations and in courtroom pleadings, judgment or fancy is most needed depending on whether truth or disguise serves the purpose at hand.

In demonstration, in counsel, and in every rigorous search for truth, judgment does everything — except that sometimes the understanding needs to be opened up by an apt comparison, and then there is that much use for fancy. But metaphors, in this case, are utterly excluded. Since metaphors openly profess deceit, to admit them into counsel or reasoning would be obvious folly.

And in any discourse whatsoever, if a lack of discretion shows, the whole performance will be taken as a sign of poor wit, no matter how extravagant the fancy. And the reverse holds too: where the discretion is obvious, the discourse will never be taken for witless, however ordinary the fancy.

The secret thoughts of a man range over everything — holy, profane, clean, obscene, grave, and frivolous — without shame or blame. Spoken discourse cannot do that; it can go only as far as judgment approves of the time, the place, and the company. An anatomist or a physician may speak or write his professional opinion about unclean things, because his purpose is not to please but to be useful. But for another man to publish his extravagant and entertaining fancies about the same subjects is like a man who has been tumbled in the mud coming to present himself in good company. The difference is nothing but a lack of discretion.

Likewise, in openly relaxed moods and among familiar company, a man may play with the sounds and double meanings of words, and often with flashes of extraordinary fancy. But in a sermon, or in public, or before strangers, or before people we ought to respect, there is no jingling wordplay that will not be counted as folly. And again, the only difference is the lack of discretion.

So where wit is lacking, it is not fancy that is missing, but discretion. Judgment without fancy is wit; fancy without judgment is not.

Prudence

When a man with some project in hand runs his thoughts over a multitude of things and observes how they contribute to that project — or what project they might contribute to — and when his observations are neither easy nor common, this wit of his is called prudence. It depends on a great deal of experience, and on memory of similar things and their consequences in the past.

In prudence there is not as much difference between men as there is in their fancies and judgments. The experience of men of equal age is not very unequal in quantity; it simply lies in different fields, since everyone has his own private business. Governing a household well and governing a kingdom well are not different degrees of prudence — they are different sorts of business, just as painting a miniature and painting a portrait at life size or larger are not different degrees of art. A plain farmer is more prudent in the affairs of his own house than a privy counselor is in the affairs of another man.

Craft

Add to prudence the use of unjust or dishonest means — the kind that fear or poverty usually suggests to men — and you get that crooked wisdom called CRAFT, which is a sign of small-mindedness. For magnanimity, greatness of mind, is precisely the contempt for unjust or dishonest shortcuts.

And what the Romans called versutia — in English, "shifting" — is the trick of escaping a present danger or difficulty by getting into a bigger one, as when a man robs one person to pay another. It is just a shorter-sighted craft. The name comes from versura, which means borrowing money at interest to pay the interest you already owe.

Acquired Wit

As for acquired wit — I mean wit acquired by method and instruction — there is only one kind: reason. Reason is grounded in the right use of speech, and it produces the sciences. But I have already spoken of reason and science in the fifth and sixth chapters.

The causes of these differences in wit lie in the passions, and the differences in the passions come partly from men's different bodily constitutions and partly from their different upbringings. If the difference came from the makeup of the brain and the organs of sense, exterior or interior, men would differ as much in their sight and hearing and other senses as they do in their fancies and discretions. So the difference comes from the passions — which vary not only with men's physical temperaments but also with their differences of custom and education.

The passions that most of all cause differences of wit are, principally, a greater or lesser desire for power, for riches, for knowledge, and for honor. And all of these can be reduced to the first — the desire for power. For riches, knowledge, and honor are just different kinds of power.

Giddiness; Madness

So a man who has no great passion for any of these things — who is, as people say, indifferent — may well be a good man in the sense that he gives no offense, but he cannot possibly have either a great fancy or much judgment. For the thoughts are to the desires what scouts and spies are to an army: they range abroad and find the way to the things desired. All steadiness of the mind's motion, and all its quickness, comes from that.

To have no desires is to be dead. To have weak passions is dullness. To have passions spread indifferently across everything is GIDDINESS and distraction. And to have stronger, more vehement passions for something than is ordinarily seen in other men — that is what men call MADNESS.

Of madness there are almost as many kinds as there are passions. Sometimes the extraordinary and extravagant passion comes from a bad constitution of the body's organs, or from damage done to them; and sometimes the damage and disorder of the organs is caused by the vehemence or long persistence of the passion itself. But in both cases the madness is of one and the same nature.

The passion whose violence or persistence produces madness is either great vainglory — commonly called pride and self-conceit — or great dejection of mind.

Rage

Pride makes a man prone to anger, and the excess of anger is the madness called RAGE and FURY. And so it happens that an excessive desire for revenge, once it becomes habitual, injures the organs and turns into rage. Excessive love, joined with jealousy, also becomes rage. An excessive opinion of oneself — as divinely inspired, or as wise, learned, handsome, and so on — becomes distraction and giddiness; the same opinion joined with envy becomes rage. And a vehement conviction of the truth of anything, when contradicted by others — rage.

Melancholy

Dejection makes a man prone to groundless fears, which is the madness commonly called MELANCHOLY. It too shows itself in various ways: in haunting lonely places and graves, in superstitious behavior, in fearing some particular thing — one man one thing, another man another.

In sum, all passions that produce strange and unusual behavior go by the general name of madness. As for the particular kinds of madness, anyone who cared to take the trouble could enroll a legion of them. And if the excesses are madness, there is no doubt that the passions themselves, when they tend toward evil, are degrees of the same thing.

Take an example. The effect of folly in people possessed by an opinion of being divinely inspired may not always show itself in any one man through some wildly extravagant action. But when many of them conspire together, the rage of the whole multitude is visible enough. What greater proof of madness could there be than to clamor against, strike, and throw stones at our best friends? Yet a multitude like that will do something even worse: they will clamor against, fight, and destroy the very people who, all their lives before, protected them and kept them safe from injury. And if this is madness in the multitude, it is the same madness in every individual man.

Think of it this way. In the middle of the sea, a man hears no sound from the patch of water right beside him — yet he can be quite sure that patch contributes as much to the roaring of the sea as any other patch of the same size. In the same way, though we may notice no great disturbance in one or two individual men, we can be quite sure their individual passions are parts of the seditious roaring of a troubled nation.

And even if nothing else betrayed their madness, the very act of claiming such inspiration for themselves is proof enough. If a man in Bedlam — the madhouse — entertained you with perfectly sober conversation, and as you took your leave you asked who he was, so you could repay his courtesy another time, and he told you he was God the Father, I think you would need to wait for no extravagant action to be sure of his madness.

This opinion of inspiration, commonly called the "private spirit," very often begins with a man's lucky discovery of an error that everyone else holds. Not knowing — or not remembering — by what chain of reasoning he arrived at this singular truth (as he thinks it is, though it is often an untruth he has stumbled onto), he immediately admires himself as someone in the special grace of God Almighty, who has revealed this to him supernaturally by his Spirit.

Again, that madness is nothing other than passion showing itself too plainly can be gathered from the effects of wine, which are the same as the effects of disordered organs. The variety of behavior in men who have drunk too much is exactly the variety found in madmen: some raging, some loving, some laughing — all extravagantly, each according to whichever passion dominates in him. For all the wine does is strip away dissimulation, and take from them the sight of how deformed their passions look.

For I believe the most sober of men, when they walk alone with nothing to occupy or discipline the mind, would be unwilling for the vanity and extravagance of their thoughts in those moments to be publicly seen. Which is a confession that passions, left unguided, are for the most part sheer madness.

The world's opinions about the cause of madness, in ancient times and in later ones, have been two. Some derived madness from the passions. Others derived it from demons or spirits, good or bad, which they believed could enter a man, possess him, and move his organs in the strange and uncouth way that madmen move. The first sort, accordingly, called such men madmen. The second sort called them sometimes demoniacs (that is, possessed by spirits), sometimes energumeni (that is, agitated or moved by spirits). And in Italy today they are called not only pazzi, madmen, but also spiritati — men possessed.

There was once a great crowd gathered at Abdera, a Greek city, for a performance of the tragedy of Andromeda, on an extremely hot day. A great many of the spectators fell into fevers, and the heat and the tragedy together produced this odd result: they did nothing but recite iambic verses full of the names of Perseus and Andromeda. The madness, along with the fever, was cured by the coming of winter — and this madness was thought to come from the passion imprinted on them by the tragedy.

Likewise, a fit of madness once swept through another Greek city, seizing only the young women, and caused many of them to hang themselves. Most people at the time thought it the work of the Devil. But one man suspected that this contempt for life might come from some passion of the mind, and reckoned that the young women did not hold their honor in the same contempt. So he advised the magistrates to strip the bodies of those who hanged themselves and let them hang out naked. This, the story says, cured the madness.

But on the other side, those same Greeks often attributed madness to the operation of the Eumenides, the Furies — and sometimes of Ceres, Phoebus, and other gods. So much did men credit phantasms that they took them for living aerial bodies, and gave them the general name of spirits. And as the Romans held the same opinion as the Greeks, so did the Jews. They called madmen prophets, or, depending on whether they thought the spirits good or bad, demoniacs. Some of them called both prophets and demoniacs madmen, and some called the same man both demoniac and madman.

For the pagans of the past, this is no surprise, since diseases and health, vices and virtues, and many natural accidents were named and worshipped by them as demons — so that by "demon" a man might understand a fever as easily as a devil. But for the Jews to hold such an opinion is somewhat strange. For neither Moses nor Abraham claimed to prophesy by possession of a spirit; they prophesied from the voice of God, or by a vision or dream. Nor is there anything in their law, moral or ceremonial, by which they were taught that there was any such thing as inspiration-by-possession, or possession at all.

When God is said (Numbers 11:25) to take from the spirit that was in Moses and give it to the seventy elders, the Spirit of God — if we take it for the substance of God — is not something that can be divided up. What the Scriptures mean by the Spirit of God in a man is the man's own spirit, inclined to godliness. And where it is said (Exodus 28:3), "whom I have filled with the spirit of wisdom to make garments for Aaron," it does not mean a spirit inserted into them that can sew garments; it means the wisdom of their own spirits in that kind of work.

In the same sense, the spirit of a man, when it produces unclean actions, is ordinarily called an unclean spirit; and so with other "spirits" — not always, but whenever the virtue or vice in question is extraordinary and conspicuous. Nor did the other prophets of the Old Testament claim possession-style inspiration, or claim that God spoke in them. God spoke to them — by voice, vision, or dream. And the "burden of the Lord" was not possession; it was command.

How then could the Jews fall into this opinion of possession? I can imagine no reason except the one common to all mankind: the lack of curiosity to search out natural causes, and the habit of placing happiness in acquiring the coarse pleasures of the senses and whatever leads most directly to them. For when people see some strange and unusual ability or defect in a man's mind, and cannot also see what cause it might naturally come from, they can hardly believe it natural. And if not natural, it must, they think, be supernatural. And then what can it be, but that either God or the Devil is in him?

That is how it came about that when our Savior (Mark 3:21) was pressed by the multitude, those of his own household suspected he was mad, and went out to restrain him — while the scribes said he had Beelzebub, and that this was how he cast out devils: as if the greater madman overawed the lesser. And it is how (John 10:20) some said, "He has a devil, and is mad," while others, holding him for a prophet, said, "These are not the words of one who has a devil." So too in the Old Testament: the man who came to anoint Jehu (2 Kings 9:11) was a prophet, but some of the company asked Jehu, "What did that madman come for?"

In sum, it is clear that whoever behaved in an extraordinary way was thought by the Jews to be possessed by either a good or an evil spirit — except by the Sadducees, who erred so far in the other direction as not to believe there were any spirits at all (which comes very near to outright atheism), and who thereby, perhaps, only provoked others all the more to call such men demoniacs rather than madmen.

But why, then, does our Savior go about curing them as if they were possessed, and not as if they were mad? I can give no other kind of answer than the one given to those who press Scripture the same way against the motion of the Earth. The Scripture was written to show men the kingdom of God, and to prepare their minds to become his obedient subjects — leaving the world, and the philosophy of the world, to the disputation of men, for the exercise of their natural reason. Whether it is the Earth's motion or the Sun's that makes day and night, and whether the wild actions of men come from passion or from the Devil (so long as we do not worship him), makes no difference at all to our obedience and subjection to God Almighty — which is the thing the Scripture was written for.

As for our Savior speaking to the disease as if to a person: that is the usual manner of all who cure by words alone, as Christ did (and as enchanters pretend to do, whether they speak to a devil or not). Is not Christ also said (Matthew 8:26) to have rebuked the winds? Is he not said (Luke 4:39) to rebuke a fever? Yet that does not prove a fever is a devil.

And where many of these devils are said to confess Christ, there is no need to interpret those passages as anything more than that those madmen confessed him. And where our Savior (Matthew 12:43) speaks of an unclean spirit that goes out of a man, wanders through dry places seeking rest and finding none, and returns into the same man with seven other spirits worse than himself — that is plainly a parable, alluding to a man who makes a small effort to quit his lusts, is defeated by their strength, and ends up seven times worse than he was.

So I see nothing at all in Scripture that requires us to believe demoniacs were anything other than madmen.

Insignificant Speech

There is yet another fault in the discourses of some men which may also be numbered among the kinds of madness: the abuse of words I spoke of earlier, in the fifth chapter, under the name of absurdity. This is when men speak words which, put together, have no signification at all. Some fall into it by misunderstanding words they have received and now repeat by rote; others do it deliberately, intending to deceive through obscurity.

This affliction strikes no one but those who traffic in questions about incomprehensible matters, like the Scholastics, or in questions of abstruse philosophy. The common run of men seldom speak insignificantly — and are therefore counted idiots by those distinguished persons. But to be sure their words have nothing in the mind corresponding to them, we need some examples. If any man requires one, let him take a Scholastic into his hands and see whether he can translate any single chapter on any difficult point — the Trinity, the Deity, the nature of Christ, transubstantiation, free will, and so on — into any modern language so as to make it intelligible, or into any tolerable Latin of the kind familiar to those who lived when Latin was a living, common tongue.

What is the meaning of these words: "The first cause does not necessarily inflow anything into the second, by force of the essential subordination of the second causes, by which it may help it to work"? That is the translation of the title of the sixth chapter of Suárez's first book, Of the Concourse, Motion, and Help of God. When men write whole volumes of such stuff, are they not mad — or trying to make others so?

And take in particular the question of transubstantiation. After certain words have been spoken, there are men who say that the white-ness, the round-ness, the magni-tude, the quali-ty, the corruptibili-ty — all of them incorporeal, and so on — go out of the wafer and into the body of our blessed Savior. Do they not make those nesses, tudes, and ties into so many spirits possessing his body? For by "spirits" they always mean things that, while incorporeal, can nevertheless be moved from one place to another.

So this kind of absurdity may rightly be counted among the many sorts of madness. And all the time these men spend guided by the clear thoughts of their worldly ambition — when they refrain from disputing and writing this way — is nothing but their lucid intervals.

And so much for the intellectual virtues and defects.
