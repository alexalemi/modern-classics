Chapter 39: What the Word "Church" Means in Scripture

Church: The Lord's House

The word "church" — in Greek, ecclesia — means several different things in the books of Holy Scripture. Sometimes, though not often, it means God's house: a temple where Christians gather to perform their holy duties in public. That is the sense in "Let your women keep silence in the churches" (1 Corinthians 14:34). But even there the word is used metaphorically, standing for the congregation gathered inside the building. Since then it has come to be used for the building itself, to distinguish the temples of Christians from the temples of idolaters.

The Temple of Jerusalem was God's house, the house of prayer; and in the same way, any building that Christians dedicate to the worship of Christ is Christ's house. That is why the Greek Fathers called such a building Kuriake — "the Lord's house" — and from that word, in our own language, came "kirk," and then "church."

What Ecclesia Properly Means

When "church" does not mean a building, it means what ecclesia meant in the Greek commonwealths: a congregation, an assembly of citizens called together to hear the magistrate speak to them. In the Roman commonwealth the same thing was called a concio, and the man who spoke was called ecclesiastes or concionator — the speaker, the addresser of assemblies. When the citizens were called together by lawful authority, as in (Acts 19:39), the gathering was ecclesia legitima — a lawful church, in Greek ennomos ecclesia. But when they were stirred up by riotous and seditious shouting, it was a confused church — ecclesia sugkechumene.

Sometimes the word is used for all the people who have the right to belong to the congregation, even when they are not actually assembled — that is, for the whole multitude of Christians, however widely scattered they may be. That is the sense in which "Saul made havoc of the church" (Acts 8:3), and the sense in which Christ is called the head of the church. Sometimes it means a particular group of Christians, as in "Salute the church that is in his house" (Colossians 4:15). Sometimes it means the elect only, as in "a glorious church, without spot or wrinkle, holy and without blemish" (Ephesians 5:27) — which refers to the church triumphant, the church that is to come. And sometimes it means a congregation actually assembled, made up of people who profess Christianity, whether their profession is sincere or counterfeit. That is how it is understood in (Matthew 18:17): "Tell it to the church, and if he neglect to hear the church, let him be to thee as a Gentile or publican."

In What Sense the Church Is One Person; the Church Defined

Only in this last sense can the church be considered one person — one body that can be said to have the power to will, to pronounce, to command, to be obeyed, to make laws, or to perform any other action at all. Here is why. Without authority from a lawful congregation, whatever act is done by a crowd of people is the particular act of each individual who was present and lent a hand to it. It is not the act of them all together, as one body. Still less is it the act of those who were absent, or of those who were present but did not want it done.

According to this sense, I define a CHURCH to be: a company of men professing Christian religion, united in the person of one sovereign, at whose command they ought to assemble, and without whose authority they ought not to assemble.

And because in every commonwealth an assembly that gathers without warrant from the civil sovereign is unlawful, it follows that a church assembled in any commonwealth that has forbidden it to assemble is an unlawful assembly.

A Christian Commonwealth and a Church Are One and the Same

It also follows that there is no universal church on earth that all Christians are bound to obey — because there is no power on earth to which all the commonwealths of the world are subject. There are Christians living under many different princes and states, but each of those Christians is a subject of the commonwealth he himself belongs to, and so he cannot be subject to the commands of any other person.

Therefore a church capable of acting — capable of commanding, judging, absolving, condemning, or doing anything else — is the same thing as a civil commonwealth made up of Christians. It is called a civil state because its subjects are men, and a church because its subjects are Christians. Temporal and spiritual government are but two words brought into the world to make men see double and mistake their lawful sovereign.

It is true that after the resurrection the bodies of the faithful will be not only spiritual but eternal. In this life, however, they are coarse and corruptible. So in this life there is no government of either state or religion except temporal government; and no doctrine may lawfully be taught to any subject if the governor of both state and religion forbids it to be taught. And that governor must be one. Otherwise faction and civil war must follow in the commonwealth — between church and state; between the party of the spiritual and the party of the temporal; between the sword of justice and the shield of faith; and, worse than all of these, a war inside every Christian's own breast, between the Christian and the man.

The doctors of the church are called pastors. So are civil sovereigns. But if pastors are not subordinated one to another, so that there is one chief pastor, men will be taught contrary doctrines — both of which may be false, and one of which must be. Who that one chief pastor is, according to the law of nature, has already been shown: it is the civil sovereign. And to whom Scripture has assigned that office, we shall see in the chapters that follow.
