Chapter 20: Parental Dominion and Despotic Dominion

A commonwealth by acquisition — by conquest — is one where the sovereign power is acquired by force. And it is acquired by force when men, singly or many together by majority vote, authorize all the actions of the man or assembly that holds their lives and liberty in his power, and do so out of fear of death or chains.


How It Differs from a Commonwealth by Agreement

This kind of dominion, or sovereignty, differs from sovereignty by agreement in only one respect. Men who choose their sovereign by agreement do it out of fear of one another, not fear of the man they are installing. In conquest, they subject themselves to the very man they are afraid of.

In both cases, notice, they do it out of fear. That point deserves the attention of those who hold that all covenants made out of fear of death or violence are void. If that were true, no one in any kind of commonwealth could be obligated to obey at all.

It is true that in a commonwealth, once it is set up — whether by agreement or by conquest — promises made out of fear of death or violence are not covenants, and do not bind, when the thing promised is against the laws. But the reason is not that the promise was made out of fear. The reason is that the person promising had no right to the thing he promised. And when he could lawfully perform his promise and chooses not to, what releases him is not any invalidity in the covenant — it is the sentence of the sovereign. Otherwise we would have a contradiction: a man would promise lawfully and break his promise unlawfully. But when the sovereign — who is the actor here — acquits him, then he is acquitted by the very person who extorted the promise, as the author of his release.


The Rights of Sovereignty Are the Same in Both

But the rights and consequences of sovereignty are the same in both. The conquering sovereign's power cannot be transferred to another without his consent. He cannot forfeit it. He cannot be accused of injury by any of his subjects. He cannot be punished by them. He is the judge of what is necessary for peace, and the judge of doctrines. He is the sole lawmaker, and the supreme judge of disputes, and of the times and occasions of war and peace. It belongs to him to choose magistrates, counselors, commanders, and all other officers and ministers, and to decide rewards and punishments, honor and rank. The reasons for all of this are the same ones I gave in the previous chapter for the identical rights and consequences of sovereignty by agreement.


Parental Dominion: Acquired Not by Begetting, but by Consent

Dominion is acquired in two ways: by generation and by conquest.

The right of dominion by generation is the right a parent has over his children, and it is called PARENTAL dominion (Hobbes' term: paternal). But it does not actually come from the begetting — as if the parent had dominion over the child simply because he produced him. It comes from the child's consent, either expressed outright or declared by other sufficient signs.

Here is why begetting cannot be the source. As to generation, God ordained a helper for man, and there are always two people who are equally parents. So dominion over the child should belong equally to both, and the child should be equally subject to both — which is impossible, for no one can obey two masters.

Some have assigned the dominion to the man alone, on the grounds that he is the more excellent sex. They miscalculate. There is not always such a difference of strength or judgment between the man and the woman that the right could be settled without a fight.

In commonwealths, this dispute is decided by the civil law, and for the most part — though not always — the ruling favors the father. But that is only because commonwealths have mostly been founded by fathers of families, not by mothers. The question before us, though, concerns the pure state of nature, where there are presumed to be no laws of marriage and no laws for the raising of children — nothing but the law of nature and the natural inclination of the sexes toward each other and toward their children.

In this state of pure nature, either the parents settle the dominion over the child between themselves by contract, or they do not settle it at all. If they settle it, the right passes according to the contract. We find in history that the Amazons made exactly such a contract with the men of neighboring countries, to whom they went to have children: any male child would be sent back to the father, but the females would stay with them. So the dominion over the females rested with the mother.


Or by Raising the Child

If there is no contract, the dominion belongs to the mother. For in the state of pure nature, where there are no marriage laws, no one can know who the father is unless the mother declares it. The right of dominion over the child therefore depends on her will, and is consequently hers.

Consider it another way. The infant is first of all in the mother's power, so that she may either nourish it or abandon it. If she nourishes it, the child owes its life to her, and is therefore bound to obey her rather than anyone else — and so the dominion over it is hers. But if she abandons it and someone else finds it and nourishes it, the dominion belongs to the one who nourishes it. For the child ought to obey the person who preserves it. Preservation of life is the whole purpose for which one man becomes subject to another, so every man is presumed to promise obedience to the one who has the power to save or destroy him.


Or by One Parent's Prior Subjection to the Other

If the mother is the father's subject, the child is in the father's power. And if the father is the mother's subject — as when a sovereign queen marries one of her own subjects — the child is subject to the mother, because the father is her subject too.

If a man and a woman who are monarchs of two separate kingdoms have a child, and they make a contract about who shall have dominion over him, the right passes by the contract. If they make no contract, the dominion follows the dominion of the place where the child lives — for the sovereign of each country has dominion over everyone who resides in it.

Whoever has dominion over the child has dominion also over the child's children, and over their children's children. For he that has dominion over a man's person has dominion over everything that is his. Without that, dominion would be a mere title, with no effect.


Succession Follows the Rules of Possession

The right of succession to parental dominion proceeds in the same way as the right of succession to a monarchy — which I have already covered sufficiently in the previous chapter.


Despotic Dominion: How It Is Acquired

Dominion acquired by conquest, or victory in war, is what some writers call DESPOTIC, from the Greek despotes — a lord or master. It is the dominion of a master over his servant.

This dominion is acquired by the victor when the vanquished, to avoid the death-blow hanging over him, covenants — either in express words or by other sufficient signs of his will — that so long as his life and the liberty of his body are allowed him, the victor shall have the use of them at his pleasure.

Only after such a covenant is made is the vanquished a SERVANT — not before. By the word servant (whether it comes from servire, to serve, or from servare, to save — a question I leave to the grammarians) I do not mean a captive kept in prison or in chains until his captor, or whoever bought him from his captor, decides what to do with him. Such men — commonly called slaves — have no obligation at all. They may break their chains or their prison, and may kill their master or carry him off captive, with perfect justice. A servant is something different: a man who, having been captured, is allowed his bodily freedom, and who, upon his promise not to run away and not to do violence to his master, is trusted by him.


Not the Victory, but the Consent of the Vanquished

So it is not the victory that gives the right of dominion over the vanquished — it is his own covenant. He is not obligated because he was conquered, that is, beaten and captured or put to flight. He is obligated because he comes in and submits to the victor.

Nor is the victor obligated to spare an enemy who surrenders without any promise of his life. Yielding at discretion obligates the victor no longer than his own discretion shall see fit.

What men do when they ask for what we now call quarter (the Greeks called it zogria — "taking alive") is to dodge the immediate fury of the victor by submitting, and to bargain for their lives with ransom or service. So a man who has been given quarter has not had his life given to him — only deferred until further deliberation. He has not yielded on condition of keeping his life; he has yielded at discretion. Only then is his life secure, and his service owed, when the victor has trusted him with his bodily freedom. For slaves who work in prisons or in shackles do not work out of duty, but to avoid the cruelty of their taskmasters.

The master of a servant is master also of all he has, and may demand the use of it — his goods, his labor, his servants, and his children — as often as he sees fit. For the servant holds his life from his master by the covenant of obedience: that is, by owning and authorizing whatever the master shall do. And if the master kills him, or throws him into chains, or otherwise punishes him for refusing to obey, the servant is himself the author of that punishment, and cannot accuse the master of injury.

In sum: the rights and consequences of both parental and despotic dominion are exactly the same as those of a sovereign by agreement, and for the same reasons — the reasons laid out in the previous chapter. So consider a man who is monarch of several nations, holding sovereignty in one by the agreement of the assembled people, and in another by conquest — that is, by the submission of each individual to avoid death or chains. If he demands more from the conquered nation than from the other, on the grounds of his title by conquest, that is an act of ignorance of the rights of sovereignty. For the sovereign is equally absolute over both — or else there is no sovereignty at all, and then every man may lawfully protect himself, if he can, with his own sword. Which is the condition of war.


The Difference Between a Family and a Kingdom

From all this it appears that a great family, if it is not part of some commonwealth, is in itself — as far as the rights of sovereignty go — a little monarchy. This holds whether the family consists of a man and his children, or a man and his servants, or a man and his children and servants together; the father or master is the sovereign.

And yet a family is not properly a commonwealth, unless it has such power — by its own numbers or by other advantages — that it cannot be subdued without the hazard of war. For where a group of men is plainly too weak to defend itself even united, each member may use his own reason in time of danger to save his own life, by flight or by submission to the enemy, as he thinks best — just as a very small company of soldiers, surprised by an army, may throw down their weapons and ask for quarter, or run, rather than be put to the sword.

And that shall suffice for what I find, by speculation and deduction, concerning sovereign rights — derived from the nature, the needs, and the purposes of men in setting up commonwealths and placing themselves under monarchs or assemblies entrusted with power enough to protect them.


The Right of Monarchy from Scripture

Now let us consider what Scripture teaches on the same point.

To Moses, the children of Israel say this: "Speak thou to us, and we will hear thee; but let not God speak to us, lest we die" (Exodus 20:19). That is absolute obedience to Moses.

Concerning the right of kings, God himself, by the mouth of Samuel, says: "This shall be the right of the king you will have to reign over you. He shall take your sons, and set them to drive his chariots, and to be his horsemen, and to run before his chariots; and to gather in his harvest; and to make his engines of war, and instruments of his chariots. And he shall take your daughters to make perfumes, to be his cooks and bakers. He shall take your fields, your vineyards, and your olive groves, and give them to his servants. He shall take the tithe of your grain and wine, and give it to the men of his chamber, and to his other servants. He shall take your menservants and your maidservants, and the choice of your youth, and employ them in his business. He shall take the tithe of your flocks; and you shall be his servants" (1 Samuel 8:11-17). That is absolute power, and it is summed up in the last words: you shall be his servants.

Again, when the people heard what power their king was to have, they consented to it anyway, saying: "We will be as all other nations, and our king shall judge our causes, and go before us, to conduct our wars" (1 Samuel 8:19-20). Here is confirmed the right that sovereigns have both to the armed forces and to all judicature — which together contain as absolute a power as one man can possibly transfer to another.

Again, the prayer of King Solomon to God was this: "Give to thy servant understanding, to judge thy people, and to discern between good and evil" (1 Kings 3:9). It belongs to the sovereign, then, to be judge, and to prescribe the rules for telling good from evil. Those rules are laws — and therefore the legislative power is in him.

Saul sought the life of David. Yet when it was in David's power to kill Saul, and his servants would gladly have done it, David forbade them, saying: "God forbid I should do such an act against my Lord, the anointed of God" (1 Samuel 24:9).

For the obedience of servants, St. Paul says: "Servants, obey your masters in all things" (Colossians 3:20), and "Children, obey your parents in all things" (verse 22). That is simple obedience in those who are subject to parental or despotic dominion.

Again: "The Scribes and Pharisees sit in Moses' chair, and therefore all that they shall bid you observe, that observe and do" (Matthew 23:2-3). There again is simple obedience. And St. Paul: "Warn them that they subject themselves to princes, and to those that are in authority, and obey them" (Titus 3:2). This obedience is also simple.

Lastly, our Savior himself acknowledges that men ought to pay whatever taxes kings impose — "Give to Caesar that which is Caesar's" — and he paid such taxes himself. He acknowledged too that the king's word is sufficient to take anything from any subject when there is need, and that the king is the judge of that need. For he himself, as King of the Jews, commanded his disciples to take the donkey and its colt to carry him into Jerusalem: "Go into the village over against you, and you shall find a she-ass tied, and her colt with her; untie them, and bring them to me. And if any man ask you what you mean by it, say, the Lord hath need of them; and they will let them go" (Matthew 21:2-3). The disciples will not ask whether his need is a sufficient title, nor whether he is the judge of that need. They simply acquiesce in the will of the Lord.

To these passages we may add the one from Genesis: "You shall be as gods, knowing good and evil" (Genesis 3:5), and verse 11: "Who told thee that thou wast naked? Hast thou eaten of the tree, of which I commanded thee thou shouldest not eat?"

The judging of good and evil was forbidden under the name of the fruit of the tree of knowledge, as a trial of Adam's obedience. The Devil, to inflame the ambition of the woman — to whom that fruit already looked beautiful — told her that by tasting it, they would be as gods, knowing good and evil. And so, having both eaten, they did indeed take upon themselves God's office, which is the judging of good and evil. But they acquired no new ability to tell the two apart correctly.

As for the line that having eaten, they saw they were naked — no one has ever read that passage to mean they had been blind before and could not see their own skin. The meaning is plain: that was the moment they first judged their nakedness (in which it was God's will to create them) to be indecent. And by being ashamed of it, they silently passed judgment on God himself. Whereupon God says, "Hast thou eaten...?" — as if to say: do you, who owe me obedience, take it upon yourself to judge my commandments? By this it is clearly signified — though allegorically — that the commands of those who have the right to command are not to be censured or disputed by their subjects.


Sovereign Power Ought to Be Absolute in Every Commonwealth

So it appears plainly, to my understanding, from both reason and Scripture, that the sovereign power — whether placed in one man, as in monarchy, or in one assembly of men, as in popular and aristocratic commonwealths — is as great as men can possibly be imagined to make it.

People may imagine many evil consequences flowing from so unlimited a power. But the consequences of the lack of it — perpetual war of every man against his neighbor — are far worse. The condition of man in this life will never be free of inconveniences. But no commonwealth suffers any great inconvenience except what proceeds from the subjects' disobedience, and their breach of the very covenants from which the commonwealth had its being. And whoever thinks sovereign power too great, and seeks to make it less, must subject himself to a power that can limit it — which is to say, to a greater one.

The strongest objection is the objection from practice: men ask where and when such power has ever been acknowledged by subjects. But one may ask them in return: when, or where, has there ever been a kingdom long free from sedition and civil war? In those nations whose commonwealths have lived long, and have been destroyed only by foreign war, the subjects never disputed the sovereign power at all. But in any case, an argument from the practice of men — men who have never sifted the question to the bottom, who have never weighed with exact reason the causes and nature of commonwealths, and who suffer daily the miseries that flow from their ignorance of them — is no argument at all. Even if everyone in the world built their houses on sand, it would not follow that houses ought to be built that way.

The skill of making and maintaining commonwealths consists in certain rules, as arithmetic and geometry do — not, like tennis, in practice alone. And these rules are ones that poor men have not had the leisure to find out, and that men who have had the leisure have so far lacked the curiosity, or the method, to discover.
