Chapter 26: Of Civil Laws
(Part 2 of 2)

A Judge's Ruling Does Not Bind Him, or Any Other Judge, to Rule the Same Way in Similar Cases Forever After

But no judge, subordinate or sovereign, is immune to error in a judgment of equity. So if, in a later case of the same kind, he finds it more consistent with equity to give the contrary ruling, he is obliged to do so. No man's error becomes his own law, or obliges him to persist in it. And for the same reason, it does not become law for other judges either, even if they have sworn to follow it.

Granted: when a wrong ruling is given by the sovereign's authority, and the sovereign knows of it and allows it, then in laws that are changeable it amounts to the making of a new law — for cases in which every little circumstance is the same. But in laws that cannot change, such as the laws of nature, wrong rulings are no laws at all: not to the same judge, not to other judges, not in similar cases, ever after. Princes succeed one another; one judge passes and another comes; indeed, heaven and earth shall pass away, but not one tittle of the law of nature shall pass — for it is the eternal law of God. Therefore all the rulings of all the judges who have ever lived cannot, even taken together, make a law contrary to natural equity. Nor can the examples of former judges justify an unreasonable ruling, or excuse the present judge from the work of studying what equity requires in the case before him, from the principles of his own natural reason.

Take an example. It is against the law of nature to punish the innocent — and an innocent man is one who clears himself in court and is acknowledged innocent by the judge. Now suppose a man is accused of a capital crime. Seeing the power and malice of some enemy, and how often judges are corrupt and partial, he runs away for fear of how it might turn out. Later he is captured and brought to a lawful trial, where he shows convincingly that he was not guilty of the crime, and is acquitted of it — and is nevertheless condemned to forfeit his goods. This is a plain condemnation of an innocent man. So I say there is no place in the world where this can count as an interpretation of a law of nature, or be made law by the rulings of earlier judges who did the same thing. The judge who first ruled that way ruled unjustly, and no injustice can be a pattern of judgment for the judges who come after.

A written law may forbid innocent men to flee, and they may be punished for fleeing. But to treat flight from fear of injury as a presumption of guilt, after a man has already been judicially absolved of the crime, is contrary to the very nature of a presumption — which has no place once judgment has been given. Yet a great lawyer sets exactly this down as the common law of England. "If a man," he says, "that is innocent, be accused of felony, and for fear flees for the same; although he judicially acquits himself of the felony, yet if it be found that he fled for the felony, he shall, notwithstanding his innocence, forfeit all his goods, chattels, debts, and duties. For as to the forfeiture of them, the law will admit no proof against the presumption in law, grounded upon his flight."

Look at what is being said here. An innocent man, judicially acquitted, despite his innocence — and when no written law forbade him to flee — is condemned, after his acquittal, on a mere presumption in law, to lose everything he owns. If the law grounds on his flight a presumption of the deed itself — and the deed was capital — then the sentence should have been capital. If the presumption was not of the deed, then why should he lose his goods? This, therefore, is no law of England; and the condemnation rests not on a presumption of law, but on the presumption of the judges.

It is also against law to say that no proof shall be admitted against a presumption of law. All judges, sovereign and subordinate, who refuse to hear proof, refuse to do justice. Even if the sentence happens to be just, judges who condemn without hearing the proofs offered are unjust judges, and their presumption is nothing but prejudice — which no man ought to carry with him to the seat of justice, whatever precedent judgments or examples he claims to be following. There are other matters of this kind in which men's judgments have been warped by trusting to precedents; but this much is enough to show that although a judge's sentence is law to the party pleading before him, it is no law to any judge who succeeds him in that office.

Likewise, when the question concerns the meaning of written laws, the interpreter is not whoever writes a commentary on them. Commentaries are usually more open to quibbling than the text itself, and so they need further commentaries — and there is no end to that kind of interpretation. So unless there is an interpreter authorized by the sovereign, from whom the subordinate judges may not depart, the interpreter can be none other than the ordinary judges, exactly as in cases of unwritten law; and their sentences are to be taken as law by the parties pleading — in that particular case — but they do not bind other judges to rule the same way in similar cases. A judge can err even in interpreting written laws; but no error of a subordinate judge can change the law, which is the general sentence of the sovereign.

The Difference Between the Letter and the Meaning of the Law

With written laws, men commonly distinguish between the letter of the law and its sentence — its meaning. If by the letter is meant whatever can be squeezed out of the bare words, the distinction is a good one. For the meanings of almost all words, either in themselves or in their metaphorical uses, are ambiguous, and a clever argument can stretch them into many senses — but there is only one sense of the law. If, however, by the letter is meant the literal sense, then the letter and the sentence — the intention — of the law are one and the same. For the literal sense is the sense the legislator intended the letter of the law to convey; and the intention of the legislator is always presumed to be equity. For a judge to think otherwise of the sovereign would be a serious insult.

So if the words of the law do not fully authorize a reasonable sentence, the judge ought to fill the gap with the law of nature; or, if the case is difficult, postpone judgment until he has received fuller authority. For example: a written law ordains that whoever is thrust out of his house by force shall be restored to it by force. Now it happens that a man carelessly leaves his house empty, and on returning is kept out of it by force — a case for which no specific law was ordained. It is evident that this case is contained in the same law; otherwise the man has no remedy at all, and that, we must suppose, is against the intention of the legislator.

Again, the words of the law command the judge to rule according to the evidence. A man is falsely accused of a deed that the judge himself saw done — by someone else, not by the accused. In this case the judge should neither follow the letter of the law into condemning the innocent, nor give sentence against the evidence of the witnesses, since the letter of the law runs the other way. Instead he should arrange with the sovereign for another judge to be appointed, and serve as a witness himself. So the awkward results that follow from the bare words of a written law may lead a judge to the intention of the law, and so to a better interpretation of it — but no awkward result can justify a sentence against the law. For a judge of right and wrong is not a judge of what is convenient or inconvenient to the commonwealth.

The Abilities Required in a Judge

The abilities required in a good interpreter of the law — that is, in a good judge — are not the same as those of an advocate, namely the study of the laws. A judge ought to take notice of the facts from nothing but the witnesses; and in the same way he ought to take notice of the law from nothing but the statutes and constitutions of the sovereign, cited in the pleadings, or declared to him by those who have authority from the sovereign power to declare them. He has no need to work out in advance what his judgment will be: what he shall say about the facts will be given to him by witnesses, and what he shall say on points of law will come from those who present it in their pleadings and interpret it, with authority, on the spot.

The Lords of Parliament in England were judges, and the most difficult cases have been heard and decided by them; yet few of them were deeply versed in the study of the laws, and fewer still had practiced law as a profession. And although they consulted with lawyers appointed to attend for that very purpose, the Lords alone had the authority to give sentence. In the same way, in ordinary trials of right, twelve men of the common people are the judges, and they give sentence not only on the facts but on the right itself, pronouncing simply for the plaintiff or for the defendant. That is, they are judges not only of fact but also of right; and in a criminal case they determine not only whether the deed was done or not done, but whether it was murder, homicide, felony, assault, and the like — which are determinations of law. But because they are not expected to know the law on their own, someone with authority is there to inform them of it in the particular case they are to judge. And yet if they do not rule according to what he tells them, they face no penalty for it — unless it can be shown that they ruled against their consciences, or had been corrupted by a bribe.

The things that make a good judge, or a good interpreter of the laws, are these. First, a right understanding of that principal law of nature called equity — which depends not on reading other men's writings, but on the goodness of a man's own natural reason and his own reflection, and is therefore presumed to be strongest in those who have had the most leisure, and the most inclination, to reflect on it. Second, contempt for unnecessary riches and promotions. Third, the ability, in judging, to strip himself of all fear, anger, hatred, love, and compassion. Fourth and last, patience to hear, diligent attention in hearing, and the memory to retain, digest, and apply what he has heard.

Divisions of Law

The classification and division of the laws has been done in different ways, according to the different methods of the men who have written about them. It is a matter that depends not on nature but on the writer's purpose, and it serves each man's own method. In the Institutes of Justinian, we find seven sorts of civil laws:

1. The edicts, constitutions, and epistles of the prince — that is, of the emperor — because the whole power of the people resided in him. The proclamations of the kings of England are like these.

2. The decrees of the whole people of Rome (including the senate), when the question was put to them by the senate. These were laws, at first, by virtue of the sovereign power residing in the people; and those of them that the emperors did not repeal remained laws by the imperial authority. For all binding laws are understood to be laws by the authority of whoever has the power to repeal them. Somewhat like these laws are the Acts of Parliament in England.

3. The decrees of the common people (excluding the senate), when the question was put to them by the tribune of the people. Those of them not repealed by the emperors remained laws by the imperial authority. Like these were the orders of the House of Commons in England.

4. Senatus consulta — the orders of the senate. When the people of Rome grew so numerous that it became inconvenient to assemble them, the emperor thought it fitting that men should consult the senate instead of the people. These bear some resemblance to the acts of council.

5. The edicts of praetors and, in some cases, of the aediles — like the chief justices in the courts of England.

6. Responsa prudentum — the answers of the learned: the rulings and opinions of those lawyers whom the emperor gave authority to interpret the law, and to give answers to those who came to them for legal advice. Judges were obliged by the emperor's constitutions to observe those answers in giving judgment; and they would be like the reports of adjudged cases in England, if other judges were bound by the law of England to observe them. For the judges of the common law of England are not, properly speaking, judges, but juris consulti — legal advisers — of whom the real judges, who are either the Lords or twelve men of the county, are to ask advice on points of law.

7. Also unwritten customs, which by their own nature are an imitation of law. By the tacit consent of the emperor, provided they are not contrary to the law of nature, these are genuinely laws.

Another division of laws is into natural and positive. Natural laws are those that have been laws from all eternity. They are called not only natural but also moral laws, consisting in the moral virtues — justice, equity, and all the habits of mind that lead to peace and charity — of which I have already spoken in the fourteenth and fifteenth chapters.

Positive laws are those that have not existed from eternity, but were made laws by the will of those who held sovereign power over others; and they are either written, or made known to men by some other sign of the will of their legislator.

Another Division of Law

Again, of positive laws, some are human and some divine. And of human positive laws, some are distributive and some penal. Distributive laws are those that determine the rights of subjects, declaring to every man the terms on which he acquires and holds property in lands or goods, and a right or liberty of action; and these speak to all the subjects. Penal laws are those that declare what penalty shall be inflicted on those who violate the law; and these speak to the ministers and officers appointed to carry out the punishment. For although everyone ought to be informed in advance of the punishments ordained for transgression, the command is not addressed to the offender — who cannot be expected to punish himself faithfully — but to the public ministers appointed to see the penalty executed. These penal laws are for the most part written together with the distributive laws, and are sometimes called judgments. For all laws are general judgments, or sentences, of the legislator; and likewise every particular judgment is a law to the man whose case is judged.

How Divine Positive Law Is Made Known to Be Law

Divine positive laws (for natural laws, being eternal and universal, are all divine) are those commandments of God which are not from all eternity, nor addressed universally to all men, but only to a certain people or to certain persons — and which are declared to be God's laws by those whom God has authorized to declare them. But how can this human authority to declare God's positive laws be known? God may command a man, in a supernatural way, to deliver laws to other men. But it is of the essence of law that the person to be bound by it be assured of the authority of the one who declares it — and we cannot naturally perceive that authority to come from God. So two questions arise. How can a man, without a supernatural revelation of his own, be assured of the revelation received by the declarer? And how can he be bound to obey those laws?

To the first question — how a man can be assured of another's revelation without a revelation made particularly to himself — the answer is that it is evidently impossible. A man may be moved to believe in such a revelation by the miracles he sees the declarer perform, or by the extraordinary holiness of his life, or his extraordinary wisdom, or the extraordinary success of his actions — all of which are marks of God's extraordinary favor. But they are not assured evidence of special revelation. Miracles are marvelous works; but what is marvelous to one man may not be marvelous to another. Holiness can be faked; and the visible successes of this world are most often the work of God through natural and ordinary causes. So no man can infallibly know, by natural reason, that another man has had a supernatural revelation of God's will. He can have only a belief — and each man's belief will be firmer or weaker as the signs of it appear greater or smaller.

The second question — how he can be bound to obey such laws — is not so hard. If the law declared is not against the law of nature (which is undoubtedly God's law), and he undertakes to obey it, then he is bound by his own act. Bound, I say, to obey it — but not bound to believe it. For men's beliefs and inward thoughts are not subject to commands; they answer only to the working of God, ordinary or extraordinary. Faith in a supernatural law is not a fulfilling of it but only an assenting to it — not a duty we offer to God, but a gift God freely gives to whom he pleases. Just so, unbelief is not a breach of any of God's laws, but a rejection of them all — except the laws of nature.

What I am saying here will be made still clearer by the examples and testimonies on this point in holy Scripture. The covenant God made with Abraham — in a supernatural manner — was this: "This is the covenant which thou shalt observe between me and thee and thy seed after thee" (Genesis 17:10). Abraham's descendants did not receive this revelation; they did not yet exist. Yet they are a party to the covenant, and bound to obey whatever Abraham declared to them as God's law. And they could not be bound except through the obedience they owed to their parents — who, when they are subject to no other earthly power, as in Abraham's case, hold sovereign power over their children and servants. Again, where God says to Abraham, "In thee shall all nations of the earth be blessed: for I know thou wilt command thy children, and thy house after thee, to keep the way of the Lord, and to observe righteousness and judgment," it is plain that the obedience of his family, who had no revelation of their own, depended on their prior obligation to obey their sovereign.

At Mount Sinai, only Moses went up to God; the people were forbidden to approach, on pain of death — and yet they were bound to obey everything Moses declared to them as God's law. On what ground, except this submission of their own: "Speak thou to us, and we will hear thee; but let not God speak to us, lest we die"? These two passages make it sufficiently clear that in a commonwealth, a subject who has no certain and assured revelation made particularly to himself concerning the will of God is to obey, as God's will, the command of the commonwealth. For if men were free to take their own dreams and fancies — or the dreams and fancies of private men — for God's commandments, scarcely two men would agree on what God's commandments are; and yet, out of regard for those fancies, every man would despise the commandments of the commonwealth.

I conclude, therefore, that in all things not contrary to the moral law — that is to say, to the law of nature — all subjects are bound to obey as divine law whatever is declared to be so by the laws of the commonwealth. This is evident to anyone's reason. Whatever is not against the law of nature may be made law in the name of those who hold the sovereign power; and there is no reason men should be any less obliged by it when it is propounded in the name of God. Besides, there is no place in the world where men are permitted to claim commandments of God other than those the commonwealth declares to be such. Christian states punish those who revolt from the Christian religion; and all other states punish those who set up any religion their laws forbid. And in whatever is not regulated by the commonwealth, it is equity — which is the law of nature, and therefore an eternal law of God — that every man equally enjoy his liberty.

Another Division of Laws

There is also another distinction of laws: into fundamental and not fundamental. I confess I could never discover, in any author, what a fundamental law is supposed to mean. Nevertheless, one may very reasonably distinguish laws in that manner.

What a Fundamental Law Is

A FUNDAMENTAL LAW, in every commonwealth, is one which, if it is taken away, the commonwealth fails and is utterly dissolved — like a building whose foundation is destroyed. A fundamental law, therefore, is the law by which subjects are bound to uphold whatever power is given to the sovereign, whether a monarch or a sovereign assembly, without which the commonwealth cannot stand: such as the power of war and peace, of judicature, of choosing officers, and of doing whatever the sovereign judges necessary for the public good. A law is not fundamental when repealing it does not drag the dissolution of the commonwealth along with it — such as the laws concerning disputes between one subject and another. So much for the division of laws.

The Difference Between Law and Right

I find the terms lex civilis and jus civile — civil law and civil right — used interchangeably for the same thing, even in the most learned authors; and they ought not to be. For right is liberty — namely, the liberty the civil law leaves us. But civil law is an obligation, and takes from us the liberty the law of nature gave us. Nature gave every man a right to secure himself by his own strength, and to strike a suspected neighbor first, as a precaution; the civil law takes away that liberty in every case where the law's protection can safely be waited for. So lex and jus — law and right — differ as much as obligation and liberty.

And Between a Law and a Charter

Likewise, laws and charters are taken indiscriminately for the same thing. Yet charters are donations of the sovereign — not laws, but exemptions from law. The language of a law is jubeo, injungo — "I command and enjoin." The language of a charter is dedi, concessi — "I have given, I have granted." And what is given or granted to a man is not forced upon him by a law. A law may be made to bind all the subjects of a commonwealth; a liberty, or charter, applies only to one man, or to some one part of the people. For to say that all the people of a commonwealth have liberty in some case whatsoever is only to say that, in such a case, no law has been made — or that, having been made, it has now been repealed.
