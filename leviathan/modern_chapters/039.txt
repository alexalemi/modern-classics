Chapter 38: What Scripture Means by Eternal Life, Hell, Salvation, the World to Come, and Redemption

Civil society depends on justice, and justice depends on the power of life and death — along with the lesser rewards and punishments — which resides in those who hold the sovereignty of the commonwealth. It is therefore impossible for a commonwealth to stand where anyone other than the sovereign has the power to give rewards greater than life, or to inflict punishments greater than death. Now, eternal life is a greater reward than this present life, and eternal torment is a greater punishment than natural death. So for everyone who wants to avoid the calamities of confusion and civil war by obeying authority, it is well worth considering carefully what Holy Scripture means by eternal life and eternal torment; for what offenses, committed against whom, men are to be eternally tormented; and for what actions they are to obtain eternal life.

Where Adam Would Have Lived Forever, Had He Not Sinned: The Earthly Paradise

First, we find that Adam was created in such a condition of life that, had he not broken God's commandment, he would have enjoyed it in the paradise of Eden everlastingly. For the tree of life was there, and he was allowed to eat from it for as long as he refrained from eating from the tree of knowledge of good and evil, which was forbidden to him. And so, as soon as he had eaten from it, God thrust him out of paradise, "lest he put forth his hand, and take also of the tree of life, and live forever" (Genesis 3:22).

From this it seems to me — with submission, nevertheless, in this and in all questions whose resolution depends on Scripture, to the interpretation of the Bible authorized by the commonwealth whose subject I am — that if Adam had not sinned, he would have had an eternal life on earth, and that mortality entered into him and his descendants through his first sin. Not that actual death entered then; for in that case Adam could never have had children, whereas in fact he lived long afterward and saw numerous descendants before he died. But where it is said, "In the day that you eat of it, you shall surely die," the meaning must be his mortality — the certainty of death.

Since eternal life was lost by Adam's forfeiture in committing sin, whoever was to cancel that forfeiture would thereby recover that life again. Now, Jesus Christ has satisfied for the sins of all who believe in him, and has therefore recovered for all believers that ETERNAL LIFE which was lost by the sin of Adam. And it is in this sense that St. Paul's comparison holds (Romans 5:18-19): "As by the offense of one, judgment came upon all men to condemnation, even so by the righteousness of one, the free gift came upon all men to justification of life." The same point is delivered even more clearly in these words (1 Corinthians 15:21-22): "For since by man came death, by man came also the resurrection of the dead. For as in Adam all die, even so in Christ shall all be made alive."

Texts on Where Believers Will Enjoy Eternal Life

Concerning the place where men shall enjoy that eternal life which Christ has obtained for them, the texts just cited seem to put it on earth. For if, as in Adam, all die — that is, all have forfeited paradise and eternal life on earth — even so, in Christ, all shall be made alive; then all men shall be made to live on earth, for otherwise the comparison would not hold.

The Psalmist seems to agree (Psalm 133:3): "Upon Zion God commanded the blessing, even life for evermore" — and Zion is in Jerusalem, on earth. So does St. John (Revelation 2:7): "To him that overcomes I will give to eat of the tree of life, which is in the midst of the paradise of God." That was the tree of Adam's eternal life — and his life was to have been on earth. The same seems confirmed again by St. John (Revelation 21:2), where he says, "I John saw the Holy City, New Jerusalem, coming down from God out of heaven, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband" — and again at verse 10 to the same effect. As if he were saying: the new Jerusalem, the paradise of God, at the second coming of Christ, will come down to God's people from heaven; they will not go up to it from earth.

This matches exactly what the two men in white clothing — that is, the two angels — said to the apostles as they watched Christ ascend (Acts 1:11): "This same Jesus, who is taken up from you into heaven, shall so come, as you have seen him go up into heaven." Which sounds as if they had said: he will come down to govern them, under his Father, eternally here — not take them up to govern them in heaven. And this agrees with the restoration of the kingdom of God instituted under Moses, which was a political government of the Jews on earth.

Again, our Savior's saying (Matthew 22:30) "that in the resurrection they neither marry, nor are given in marriage, but are as the angels of God in heaven" is a description of an eternal life resembling the one we lost in Adam, on the point of marriage. For since Adam and Eve, had they not sinned, would have lived on earth eternally in their individual persons, it is obvious they would not have gone on procreating forever. If immortals reproduced the way mankind does now, the earth in a short time could not have offered them so much as a place to stand. The Jews who asked our Savior whose wife the woman who had married many brothers would be in the resurrection did not understand the consequences of immortality: that there will be no generation, and consequently no marriage — any more than there is marriage or generation among the angels.

The comparison between the eternal life Adam lost and the eternal life our Savior recovered by his victory over death holds in this point too: just as Adam lost eternal life by his sin and yet lived on for a time afterward, so the faithful Christian has recovered eternal life through Christ's passion, even though he dies a natural death and remains dead for a time — namely, until the resurrection. For just as death is reckoned from Adam's condemnation, not from its execution, so life is reckoned from the absolution, not from the resurrection, of those who are elected in Christ.

Ascension into Heaven

That the place where men are to live eternally after the resurrection is the heavens — meaning by heaven those parts of the world most remote from earth, where the stars are, or above the stars in another, higher heaven called caelum empyreum, the "empyrean heaven" (of which there is no mention in Scripture, nor any ground in reason) — is not easily drawn from any text I can find.

By the kingdom of heaven is meant the kingdom of the King who dwells in heaven; and his kingdom was the people of Israel, whom he ruled through the prophets, his lieutenants — first Moses, and after him Eleazar and the sovereign priests — until in the days of Samuel they rebelled and demanded a mortal man for their king, after the manner of other nations. And when our Savior Christ, through the preaching of his ministers, shall have persuaded the Jews to return and called the Gentiles to his obedience, then there shall be a new kingdom of heaven — because our King will then be God, whose throne is heaven — without any necessity, evident in Scripture, that man shall ascend to his happiness any higher than God's footstool, the earth.

On the contrary, we find it written (John 3:13) that "no man has ascended into heaven, but he that came down from heaven, even the Son of man, that is in heaven." And I observe in passing that these words are not, like the ones immediately before them, the words of our Savior, but of St. John himself — for Christ was at that time not in heaven, but on earth. The same is said of David (Acts 2:34), where St. Peter, in order to prove the ascension of Christ, takes the words of the Psalmist (Psalm 16:10), "You will not leave my soul in hell, nor suffer your Holy One to see corruption," and says they were spoken not of David but of Christ — and to prove it, adds this reason: "For David is not ascended into heaven."

But to this a man may easily answer: though their bodies were not to ascend until the general day of judgment, their souls were in heaven as soon as they departed from their bodies. And this seems to be confirmed by the words of our Savior (Luke 20:37-38), who, proving the resurrection out of the words of Moses, says this: "That the dead are raised, even Moses showed, at the bush, when he calls the Lord the God of Abraham, and the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob. For he is not a God of the dead, but of the living; for they all live to him." But if these words are to be understood only of the immortality of the soul, they do not prove at all what our Savior intended to prove — which was the resurrection of the body, that is to say, the immortality of the man. Therefore our Savior means that those patriarchs were immortal not by some property that follows from the essence and nature of mankind, but by the will of God, who was pleased, of his mere grace, to bestow eternal life on the faithful. And though at that time the patriarchs and many other faithful men were dead, yet — as the text says — they lived to God; that is, they were written in the Book of Life with those who were absolved of their sins and ordained to eternal life at the resurrection.

That the soul of man is by its own nature eternal, a living creature independent of the body — or that any mere man is immortal otherwise than by the resurrection on the last day (Enoch and Elijah excepted) — is a doctrine not apparent in Scripture. The whole of chapter 14 of Job, which is the speech not of his friends but of Job himself, is a complaint about this mortality of our nature — and yet no contradiction of immortality at the resurrection. "There is hope of a tree," he says at verse 7, "if it be cast down. Though the root of it grows old, and the stock of it dies in the ground, yet when it scents the water it will bud, and bring forth boughs like a plant. But man dies, and wastes away; yes, man gives up the ghost, and where is he?" And at verse 12: "man lies down, and rises not, till the heavens be no more."

But when is it that the heavens shall be no more? St. Peter tells us: at the general resurrection. For in his second epistle, chapter 3, verse 7, he says that "the heavens and the earth that are now, are reserved unto fire against the day of judgment, and perdition of ungodly men"; and at verse 12, "looking for, and hastening to the coming of God, wherein the heavens shall be on fire, and shall be dissolved, and the elements shall melt with fervent heat. Nevertheless, we according to the promise look for new heavens, and a new earth, wherein dwells righteousness." So when Job says man does not rise until the heavens are no more, it is the same as if he had said: the immortal life (and soul and life in Scripture usually signify the same thing) does not begin in man until the resurrection and day of judgment, and its cause is not his specific nature and generation, but the promise. For St. Peter does not say, "We look for new heavens and a new earth from nature," but "from promise."

Lastly: it has already been proved, from many clear places of Scripture, in chapter 35 of this book, that the kingdom of God is a civil commonwealth, in which God himself is sovereign — by virtue first of the Old Covenant and then of the New — and in which he reigns by his vicar, or lieutenant. Those same places therefore also prove that after the second coming of our Savior in his majesty and glory, to reign actually and eternally, the kingdom of God is to be on earth. But because this doctrine, though proved out of places of Scripture neither few nor obscure, will strike most men as a novelty, I do no more than propose it. I maintain nothing in this, or in any other paradox of religion. I am waiting for the end of that dispute of the sword — not yet decided among my countrymen — concerning the authority by which all sorts of doctrine are to be approved or rejected, and whose commands, in speech and in writing, whatever the opinions of private men, must be obeyed by everyone who means to be protected by their laws. For the points of doctrine concerning the kingdom of God have so great an influence on the kingdom of man that they are not to be determined by anyone except those who, under God, hold the sovereign power.

The Place, After Judgment, of Those Who Were Never in the Kingdom of God, or Who Were Cast Out of It

Just as the kingdom of God and eternal life have their place on earth, so also, by the Scripture, do God's enemies and their torments after judgment. The name of the place where all men remain until the resurrection — those who were buried, or swallowed up by the earth — is usually given in Scripture by words that signify under ground; which the Latins generally read as infernus and inferni, and the Greeks as Hades, that is to say, a place where men cannot see. It includes the grave as well as any other, deeper place.

But as for the place of the damned after the resurrection, neither the Old Testament nor the New fixes it by any marker of location — only by the company. It shall be where those wicked men were whom God, in former times, destroyed from the face of the earth in extraordinary and miraculous ways. For example, the damned are said to be in inferno, in Tartarus, or in the bottomless pit, because Korah, Dathan, and Abiram were swallowed up alive into the earth. Not that the writers of Scripture would have us believe there could be, in the globe of the earth — which is not only finite but, compared to the height of the stars, of no considerable size at all — a pit without a bottom, a hole of infinite depth, such as the Greeks in their demonology (that is, their doctrine concerning demons), and the Romans after them, called Tartarus. Of it Virgil says:

    Bis patet in praeceps, tantum tenditque sub umbras,
    Quantus ad aethereum coeli suspectus Olympum

— "it gapes sheer downward into the shadows twice as far as the upward gaze from earth to heavenly Olympus." The proportion of earth to heaven simply cannot bear such a thing. Rather, we should believe the damned are there, indefinitely, where those men are on whom God inflicted that exemplary punishment.

The Congregation of Giants

Again: those mighty men of the earth who lived in the time of Noah, before the flood — whom the Greeks called heroes and the Scripture giants, and who, both agree, were begotten by copulation of the children of God with the children of men — were destroyed for their wicked lives by the general deluge. The place of the damned is therefore also sometimes marked out by the company of those dead giants. So Proverbs 21:16: "The man that wanders out of the way of understanding shall remain in the congregation of the giants." And Job 26:5: "Behold the giants groan under water, and they that dwell with them." Here the place of the damned is under the water. And Isaiah 14:9: "Hell is troubled how to meet thee" — that is, the king of Babylon — "and will displace the giants for thee." Here again the place of the damned, if the sense is literal, is under water.

The Lake of Fire

Thirdly: because the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah were consumed for their wickedness, by the extraordinary wrath of God, with fire and brimstone, and the country around them turned with them into a stinking, bituminous lake, the place of the damned is sometimes expressed by fire, and by a fiery lake. So in the Apocalypse (Revelation 21:8): "But the fearful, unbelieving, and abominable, and murderers, and whoremongers, and sorcerers, and idolaters, and all liars, shall have their part in the lake that burns with fire and brimstone; which is the second death."

So it is plain that hell fire, which is expressed here by metaphor from the real fire of Sodom, does not signify any particular kind or place of torment, but is to be taken indefinitely, for destruction — as in chapter 20, verse 14 (Revelation 20:14), where it is said that "Death and Hell were cast into the lake of fire"; that is to say, they were abolished and destroyed. As if to say: after the day of judgment there shall be no more dying, and no more going into hell — that is, no more going to Hades (from which word, perhaps, our word hell is derived) — which is the same as saying: no more dying.

Utter Darkness

Fourthly: from the plague of darkness inflicted on the Egyptians — of which it is written (Exodus 10:23), "They saw not one another, neither rose any man from his place for three days; but all the children of Israel had light in their dwellings" — the place of the wicked after judgment is called utter darkness, or, as it stands in the original, darkness without. So it is expressed in Matthew 22:13, where the king commands his servants "to bind hand and foot the man that had not on his wedding garment, and to cast him out," eis to skotos to exoteron — into the external darkness, the darkness without. Though this is translated "utter darkness," the phrase does not signify how great the darkness is, but where it is to be: namely, outside the dwelling of God's elect.

Gehenna and Tophet

Lastly: there was a place near Jerusalem called the Valley of the Children of Hinnom. In one part of it, called Tophet, the Jews had committed the most grievous idolatry, sacrificing their children to the idol Moloch; there too God had afflicted his enemies with the most grievous punishments; and there Josiah had burned the priests of Moloch upon their own altars, as appears at length in 2 Kings 23. Afterward the place served to receive the filth and garbage carried out of the city, and fires were made there from time to time, to purify the air and take away the stench of rotting flesh. From this abominable place the Jews ever after called the place of the damned by the name Gehenna, or Valley of Hinnom. And this Gehenna is the word that is now usually translated HELL; and from the fires burning there from time to time, we get the notion of everlasting and unquenchable fire.

The Literal Sense of Scripture Concerning Hell

Now, no one interprets Scripture to mean that after the day of judgment the wicked are all to be eternally punished in the Valley of Hinnom; or that they shall rise again only to live forever underground or underwater; or that after the resurrection they shall never see one another again, or never stir from one place to another. It follows, it seems to me, quite necessarily, that what is said about hell fire is spoken metaphorically — and that there is therefore a proper sense to be sought out (for every metaphor has some real ground that can be expressed in proper words), both for the place of hell and for the nature of hellish torment and the tormentors.

Satan and Devil Are Not Proper Names but Titles

First, as for the tormentors: we have their nature and properties exactly and properly delivered in their names — the Enemy, or Satan; the Accuser, or Diabolus; the Destroyer, or Abaddon. These significant names — Satan, Devil, Abaddon — do not set before us any individual person, as proper names do, but only an office or quality. They are therefore appellatives — common nouns, titles — and ought not to have been left untranslated, as they are in the Latin and modern Bibles. Left untranslated, they look like the proper names of demons, and men are the more easily seduced into believing the doctrine of devils — which in those days was the religion of the Gentiles, and contrary to the religion of Moses and of Christ.

And because by the Enemy, the Accuser, and the Destroyer is meant the enemy of those who shall be in the kingdom of God, it follows that if the kingdom of God after the resurrection is on earth (as I have shown from Scripture, in the previous chapter, it seems to be), then the Enemy and his kingdom must be on earth too. So it was, after all, in the time before the Jews deposed God: God's kingdom was in Palestine, and the nations round about were the kingdoms of the Enemy. And consequently, by Satan is meant any earthly enemy of the Church.

The Torments of Hell

The torments of hell are expressed sometimes by "weeping and gnashing of teeth," as in Matthew 8:12; sometimes by "the worm of conscience," as in Isaiah 66:24 and Mark 9:44, 46, 48; sometimes by fire, as in the place just quoted — "where the worm dies not, and the fire is not quenched" — and many places besides; sometimes by "shame and contempt," as in Daniel 12:2: "And many of them that sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake; some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt." All these places designate, metaphorically, a grief and discontent of mind at the sight of that eternal happiness in others which they themselves, through their own unbelief and disobedience, have lost. And because such happiness in others cannot be felt except by comparison with one's own actual miseries, it follows that the damned are to suffer such bodily pains and calamities as fall upon those who not only live under evil and cruel governors, but have for their enemy the eternal King of the saints, God Almighty.

And among these bodily pains is to be reckoned, for every one of the wicked, a SECOND DEATH. For though Scripture is clear about a universal resurrection, we nowhere read that any of the reprobate is promised an eternal life. St. Paul, answering the question of what bodies men shall rise with (1 Corinthians 15:42-43), says that "the body is sown in corruption, and is raised in incorruption; it is sown in dishonor, it is raised in glory; it is sown in weakness, it is raised in power." But glory and power cannot be applied to the bodies of the wicked. Nor can the name of second death be applied to those who can never die more than once. And although in metaphorical speech an everlasting calamitous life may be called an everlasting death, it cannot reasonably be understood as a second death.

The fire prepared for the wicked is an everlasting fire — that is to say, the condition in which no man can be free of torture, of body and mind alike, after the resurrection, shall endure forever. In that sense the fire shall be unquenchable, and the torments everlasting. But it cannot be inferred from this that anyone cast into that fire, or tormented with those torments, shall endure and withstand them in such a way as to be eternally burnt and tortured and yet never be destroyed, never die. And though there are many places that affirm everlasting fire and torments — into which men may be cast, one after another, successively, forever — I find none that affirm there shall be an eternal life in that fire for any individual person. On the contrary, they affirm an everlasting death, which is the second death (Revelation 20:13-14): "For after Death and the Grave shall have delivered up the dead which were in them, and every man be judged according to his works, Death and the Grave shall also be cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death." From which it is evident that there is to be a second death for everyone who shall be condemned at the day of judgment — after which he shall die no more.

The Joys of Eternal Life and Salvation Are the Same Thing; Salvation from Sin and from Misery Are One

The joys of eternal life are all comprehended in Scripture under the name of SALVATION, or being saved. To be saved is to be made secure — either against particular evils, or absolutely, against all evil, including want, sickness, and death itself. And because man was created in an immortal condition, not subject to corruption, and consequently subject to nothing that tends to the dissolution of his nature, and fell from that happiness by the sin of Adam, it follows that to be saved from sin is to be saved from all the evil and calamities that sin has brought upon us. And therefore, in Holy Scripture, remission of sin and salvation from death and misery are the same thing — as appears from the words of our Savior who, having cured a man sick with palsy by saying (Matthew 9:2), "Son, be of good cheer, your sins are forgiven you," and knowing the scribes took it for blasphemy that a man should claim to forgive sins, asked them (verse 5) "whether it were easier to say, Your sins are forgiven you, or, Arise and walk" — signifying that, as far as saving the sick man went, it was all one to say "Your sins are forgiven" and "Arise and walk," and that he used the first form of speech only to show that he had the power to forgive sins. And it is evident in reason, besides: since death and misery were the punishments of sin, the discharge of sin must also be a discharge of death and misery. That is salvation absolute — the salvation the faithful are to enjoy after the day of judgment, by the power and favor of Jesus Christ, who for that reason is called our SAVIOR.

Concerning particular salvations, such as are meant in 1 Samuel 14:39, "as the Lord lives that saves Israel" — that is, saves them from their temporary enemies — and in 2 Samuel 22:4, "You are my Savior, you save me from violence," and in 2 Kings 13:5, "God gave the Israelites a Savior, and so they were delivered from the hand of the Assyrians," and the like, I need say nothing; for there is neither difficulty nor any vested interest to corrupt the interpretation of texts of that kind.

The Place of Eternal Salvation

But concerning the general salvation, because it must take place in the kingdom of heaven, there is great difficulty about the place. On one side: by kingdom — which is an estate ordained by men for their perpetual security against enemies and want — it would seem that this salvation should be on earth. For what salvation sets before us is a glorious reign of our King, by conquest, not a safety by escape. So wherever we look for salvation, we must also look for triumph; and before triumph, victory; and before victory, battle — which can hardly be supposed to happen in heaven. But however good this reasoning may be, I will not trust to it without very clear places of Scripture.

The state of salvation is described at length in Isaiah 33:20-24:

"Look upon Zion, the city of our solemnities; your eyes shall see Jerusalem a quiet habitation, a tabernacle that shall not be taken down; not one of its stakes shall ever be removed, neither shall any of its cords be broken.

But there the glorious Lord will be to us a place of broad rivers and streams, where no galley with oars shall go, neither shall gallant ship pass by.

For the Lord is our judge, the Lord is our lawgiver, the Lord is our king; he will save us.

Your riggings are loosed; they could not strengthen their mast; they could not spread the sail. Then is the prey of a great spoil divided; the lame take the prey.

And the inhabitant shall not say, I am sick; the people that dwell therein shall be forgiven their iniquity."

In these words we have the place from which salvation is to proceed: "Jerusalem, a quiet habitation." Its eternity: "a tabernacle that shall not be taken down," and so on. Its Savior: "the Lord, their judge, their lawgiver, their king — he will save us." The salvation itself: "the Lord shall be to them as a broad moat of swift waters," and so on. The condition of their enemies: their riggings loose, their masts weak, the lame taking their spoils. The condition of the saved: "the inhabitant shall not say, I am sick." And lastly, all of it is comprehended in the forgiveness of sin: "the people that dwell therein shall be forgiven their iniquity." From this it is evident that salvation shall be on earth, at the time when God shall reign in Jerusalem, at the second coming of Christ; and that from Jerusalem shall proceed the salvation of the Gentiles who shall be received into God's kingdom.

This is declared even more expressly by the same prophet (Isaiah 66:20-21): "And they" — that is, the Gentiles who held any Jew in bondage — "shall bring all your brethren, for an offering to the Lord, out of all nations, upon horses, and in chariots, and in litters, and upon mules, and upon swift beasts, to my holy mountain, Jerusalem, says the Lord, as the children of Israel bring an offering in a clean vessel into the house of the Lord. And I will also take of them for priests and for Levites, says the Lord." From this it is manifest that the chief seat of God's kingdom — the place from which the salvation of us who were Gentiles shall proceed — shall be Jerusalem.

The same is confirmed by our Savior in his conversation with the woman of Samaria about the place of God's worship. He tells her (John 4:22) that the Samaritans worship they know not what, but the Jews worship what they know: "For salvation is of the Jews" — ex Judaeis, that is, it begins at the Jews. As if he were saying: you worship God, but you do not know by whom he will save you, as we do, who know it shall be one of the tribe of Judah — a Jew, not a Samaritan. And that is why the woman, not at all beside the point, answered him, "We know the Messiah shall come." So what our Savior says — salvation is from the Jews — is the same as what Paul says (Romans 1:16-17): "The Gospel is the power of God to salvation to every one that believes; to the Jew first, and also to the Greek. For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith to faith" — from the faith of the Jew, to the faith of the Gentile.

In the same sense the prophet Joel, describing the day of judgment (Joel 2:30-31) — that God would "show wonders in heaven and in earth, blood, and fire, and pillars of smoke. The sun shall be turned to darkness, and the moon into blood, before the great and terrible day of the Lord come" — adds at verse 32: "and it shall come to pass, that whoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved. For in Mount Zion, and in Jerusalem shall be salvation." And Obadiah, verse 17, says the same: "Upon Mount Zion shall be deliverance; and there shall be holiness, and the house of Jacob shall possess their possessions" — that is, the possessions of the heathen, which he spells out in the following verses: the Mount of Esau, the land of the Philistines, the fields of Ephraim, of Samaria, Gilead, and the cities of the south; and he concludes with these words: "the kingdom shall be the Lord's."

All these places stand for salvation, and the kingdom of God after the day of judgment, on earth. On the other side, I have found no text that can plausibly be made to prove any ascension of the saints into heaven — that is to say, into any caelum empyreum or other ethereal region — except that it is called the kingdom of heaven. And it may have that name because God, who was King of the Jews, governed them by his commands, sent to Moses by angels from heaven, to bring them back to obedience, and shall send him from there again, to rule both them and all other faithful men, from the day of judgment, everlastingly. Or because the throne of this our great King is in heaven, while the earth is but his footstool. But that the subjects of God should have any place as high as his throne, or higher than his footstool — that hardly seems suitable to the dignity of a king, nor can I find any clear text for it in Holy Scripture.

From what has been said about the kingdom of God and about salvation, it is not hard to interpret what is meant by the WORLD TO COME. There are three worlds mentioned in Scripture: the old world, the present world, and the world to come. Of the first, St. Peter speaks (2 Peter 2:5): "If God spared not the old world, but saved Noah the eighth person, a preacher of righteousness, bringing the flood upon the world of the ungodly," and so on. So the first world ran from Adam to the general flood. Of the present world, our Savior speaks (John 18:36): "My kingdom is not of this world" — for he came only to teach men the way of salvation, and to renew the kingdom of his Father by his doctrine. Of the world to come, St. Peter speaks (2 Peter 3:13): "Nevertheless we, according to his promise, look for new heavens and a new earth." This is that world in which Christ, coming down from heaven in the clouds, with great power and glory, shall send his angels and gather together his elect from the four winds and from the uttermost parts of the earth, and from then on reign over them, under his Father, everlastingly.

Redemption

The salvation of a sinner presupposes a prior REDEMPTION. For whoever is once guilty of sin is liable to the penalty for it, and must pay — or someone else must pay for him — whatever ransom the offended party, who has him in his power, shall require. And since the person offended is Almighty God, in whose power are all things, the ransom to be paid before salvation can be acquired is whatever ransom God has been pleased to require.

By this ransom is not meant a satisfaction for sin equivalent to the offense — something no sinner could ever make for himself, nor any righteous man for another. The damage a man does to another man he can make amends for by restitution or compensation; but sin cannot be taken away by compensation, for that would make the liberty to sin a thing for sale. But sins may be pardoned to the repentant — either freely, or upon such penalty as God is pleased to accept. What God usually accepted in the Old Testament was some sacrifice or offering. To forgive sin is not an act of injustice, even where the punishment has been threatened. Even among men, though the promise of good binds the promiser, threats — that is to say, promises of evil — do not bind them; much less shall they bind God, who is infinitely more merciful than men.

Our Savior Christ, therefore, in redeeming us, did not satisfy for the sins of men in the sense that his death, by its own virtue, could make it unjust in God to punish sinners with eternal death. Rather, he made that sacrifice and offering of himself, at his first coming, which God was pleased to require for the salvation, at his second coming, of all who in the meantime should repent and believe in him. And though this act of our redemption is not always called a sacrifice and offering in Scripture, but sometimes a price, by price we are not to understand anything by whose value he could claim a right to a pardon for us from his offended Father — but only that price which God the Father was pleased, in mercy, to demand.
