Chapter 31: The Kingdom of God by Nature

The Scope of the Chapters That Follow

I have already proved, in everything written so far, the following: that the condition of mere nature — that is, of absolute liberty, the condition of people who are neither sovereigns nor subjects — is anarchy and a condition of war. That the precepts by which men are guided to avoid that condition are the laws of nature. That a commonwealth without sovereign power is just a word without substance, and cannot stand. And that subjects owe their sovereigns simple obedience in everything that does not conflict with the laws of God.

Only one thing is still missing for a complete knowledge of civil duty: knowing what those laws of God are. Without that, a man cannot tell, when the civil power commands him to do something, whether the command is contrary to God's law or not. And so he either offends the Divine Majesty through too much civil obedience, or, out of fear of offending God, breaks the commandments of the commonwealth. To steer clear of both these rocks, it is necessary to know what the divine laws are. And since the knowledge of all law depends on the knowledge of the sovereign power, I will say something in what follows about the KINGDOM OF GOD.

Who Counts as a Subject in the Kingdom of God

"God is King, let the earth rejoice," says the Psalmist (Psalm 96:1). And again, "God is King though the nations be angry; and he that sitteth on the cherubims, though the earth be moved" (Psalm 98:1). Whether men like it or not, they are always subject to the divine power. By denying God's existence or his providence, men may shake off their ease — but not their yoke.

But to call this power of God a "kingdom" — a power that extends not only to man but to beasts, plants, and lifeless bodies — is only a metaphorical use of the word. For only he properly reigns who governs his subjects by his word: by promising rewards to those who obey it, and by threatening punishment to those who do not. So the subjects in the kingdom of God are not inanimate bodies, and not irrational creatures, because they understand no precepts as his. Nor atheists. Nor those who do not believe that God takes any care of the actions of mankind — because these people acknowledge no word as his, and have no hope of his rewards or fear of his threats. Those, then, who believe there is a God who governs the world, who has given precepts and set out rewards and punishments for mankind — those are God's subjects. All the rest are to be understood as enemies.

God's Threefold Word: Reason, Revelation, Prophecy

To rule by words requires that the words be plainly made known; otherwise they are not laws. For it belongs to the nature of law to be sufficiently and clearly promulgated — published in a way that takes away the excuse of ignorance. In the laws of men there is only one kind of promulgation: proclamation by the voice of man. But God declares his laws three ways: by the dictates of natural reason, by revelation, and by the voice of some man to whom he gives credibility with everyone else through the working of miracles.

From this arises a triple word of God — rational, sensible, and prophetic — and to these correspond three kinds of hearing: right reason, supernatural sense, and faith. As for supernatural sense, which consists in revelation or inspiration, no universal laws have ever been given that way, because God does not speak in that manner except to particular persons, and he says different things to different men.

God's Twofold Kingdom: Natural and Prophetic

From the difference between the other two kinds of God's word — the rational and the prophetic — we may attribute to God a twofold kingdom: natural and prophetic. Natural, in which he governs as many of mankind as acknowledge his providence, through the natural dictates of right reason. And prophetic, in which, having chosen out one particular nation (the Jews) as his subjects, he governed them — and them alone — not only by natural reason, but by positive laws, which he gave them through the mouths of his holy prophets. It is the natural kingdom of God I intend to speak of in this chapter.

God's Right to Rule Comes from His Omnipotence

The right of nature by which God reigns over men, and punishes those who break his laws, is derived not from his having created them — as if he required obedience out of gratitude for his benefits — but from his irresistible power.

I have shown earlier how sovereign right arises from pact. To show how the same right can arise from nature, I need only show in what case it is never given up. By nature, all men had a right to all things — which means every man had a right to reign over all the rest. But because this right could not be obtained by force, it concerned the safety of everyone to lay that right aside, and to set up men with sovereign authority, by common consent, to rule and defend them. Yet if there had been any man with irresistible power, there would have been no reason why he should not, by that power, have ruled and defended both himself and them, according to his own discretion.

To those whose power is irresistible, then, dominion over all men attaches naturally, by the sheer excellence of that power. And consequently it is from that power that the kingdom over men, and the right of afflicting men at his pleasure, belongs naturally to God Almighty — not as Creator, not as gracious, but as omnipotent. And though punishment is due for sin only (because by that word we understand affliction for sin), the right of afflicting is not always derived from men's sin, but from God's power.

Sin Is Not the Cause of All Suffering

Why do evil men often prosper, while good men suffer adversity? The ancients disputed this question endlessly, and it is the same as our question here: by what right does God hand out the prosperities and adversities of this life? It is so difficult a question that it has shaken faith in divine providence — not only among ordinary people, but among philosophers, and, what is more, among the saints. "How good," says David, "is the God of Israel to those that are upright in heart; and yet my feet were almost gone, my treadings had well-nigh slipped; for I was grieved at the wicked, when I saw the ungodly in such prosperity." And Job — how earnestly he argues with God over the many afflictions he suffered despite his righteousness!

In Job's case, the question is decided by God himself — and not with arguments drawn from Job's sin, but from his own power. Job's friends reasoned backward from his affliction to his sin; Job defended himself with the knowledge of his own innocence. Then God himself takes up the matter. He justifies the affliction with arguments drawn from his power — such as "Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth?" and the like — and in doing so he both approved Job's innocence and rebuked the erroneous doctrine of his friends.

Our Savior's words about the man born blind agree with this doctrine: "Neither has this man sinned, nor his parents; but that the works of God might be made manifest in him." It is true that Scripture says death entered the world by sin — meaning that if Adam had never sinned, he would never have died, never suffered any separation of his soul from his body. But it does not follow that God could not justly have afflicted him even if he had never sinned — just as God afflicts other living creatures, which cannot sin at all.

The Divine Laws

Having spoken of the right of God's sovereignty as grounded only on nature, we must consider next what the divine laws are — the dictates of natural reason. These laws concern either the natural duties of one man to another, or the honor naturally due to our divine sovereign. The first are the same laws of nature I have already discussed in chapters 14 and 15 of this treatise: equity, justice, mercy, humility, and the rest of the moral virtues. What remains, then, is to consider what precepts natural reason alone — without any other word of God — dictates to men concerning the honor and worship of the Divine Majesty.

What Honor and Worship Are

Honor consists in the inward thought and opinion of another's power and goodness. So to honor God is to think as highly of his power and goodness as we possibly can. The external signs of that opinion, appearing in men's words and actions, are called WORSHIP — which is one part of what the Latins understood by the word cultus.

For cultus properly and consistently means the labor a man spends on something with the purpose of getting some benefit from it. Now, the things we get benefit from are of two kinds. Either they are subject to us, and the profit they yield follows our labor as a natural effect; or they are not subject to us, and answer our labor according to their own wills. In the first sense, the labor spent on the earth is called culture — cultivation — and the education of children is a culture of their minds. In the second sense — where men's wills must be won to our purpose not by force, but by pleasing them — it means as much as courting: winning favor by good offices, by praise, by acknowledging their power, and by whatever else is pleasing to those from whom we hope for some benefit. And this, properly, is worship. In this sense Publicola is understood as a worshipper of the people, and cultus Dei as the worship of God.

The Signs of Honor

From internal honor — the opinion of power and goodness — arise three passions: love, which refers to goodness; and hope and fear, which relate to power. And there are three parts of external worship: praise, magnifying, and blessing. The subject of praise is goodness; the subject of magnifying and blessing is power, and its effect, happiness. Praise and magnifying can be expressed both by words and by actions: by words, when we say a man is good or great; by actions, when we thank him for his generosity and obey his power. The opinion of another's happiness can only be expressed by words.

Natural and Arbitrary Worship

Some signs of honor, both in attributes and in actions, are naturally so: among attributes, "good," "just," "generous," and the like; among actions, prayers, thanks, and obedience. Others are signs of honor by institution or by the custom of men — honorable in some times and places, dishonorable in others, and indifferent in still others. Such are the gestures used in greeting, prayer, and thanksgiving, which different times and places use differently. The first kind is natural worship; the second, arbitrary worship.

Commanded and Free Worship

And arbitrary worship comes in two varieties: sometimes it is commanded, sometimes voluntary. Commanded, when it is what the one being worshipped requires; free, when it is what the worshipper thinks fit. When worship is commanded, the worship lies not in the words or gestures, but in the obedience. But when it is free, the worship consists in the opinion of the onlookers. For if the words or actions by which we intend honor seem ridiculous to them, or insulting, then they are not worship at all — because a sign is not a sign to the one who gives it, but to the one it is made to: that is, to the spectator.

Public and Private Worship

Again, there is public worship and private worship. Public worship is the worship a commonwealth performs as one person. Private worship is what a private person exhibits. Public worship, with respect to the whole commonwealth, is free; but with respect to particular men, it is not. Private worship is free when done in secret; but done in the sight of the multitude, it is never without some restraint — either from the laws or from the opinion of men — and that is contrary to the nature of liberty.

The Purpose of Worship

Among men, the purpose of worship is power. For when a man sees another being worshipped, he supposes him powerful, and is the readier to obey him — which makes his power greater still. But God has no ends. The worship we give him proceeds from our duty, and is directed — as far as our capacity allows — by those rules of honor that reason dictates the weak should perform toward more powerful men: in hope of benefit, in fear of damage, or in thankfulness for good already received from them.

The Attributes of Divine Honor

So that we may know what worship of God the light of nature teaches us, I will begin with his attributes.

First, it is obvious that we ought to attribute existence to him. For no man can have the will to honor something he thinks has no being at all.

Secondly, those philosophers who said that the world, or the soul of the world, was God spoke unworthily of him — in fact, they denied his existence. For by "God" we understand the cause of the world; and to say the world is God is to say it has no cause — that is, that there is no God.

Thirdly, to say the world was not created but is eternal is to deny there is a God — since whatever is eternal has no cause.

Fourthly, those who attribute ease to God (as they imagine they do), and so take from him the care of mankind, take from him his honor. For it removes men's love and fear of him — and that is the root of honor.

Fifthly, in things that signify greatness and power, to say God is finite is not to honor him. For it is no sign of a will to honor God to attribute less to him than we can; and finite is less than we can, because to anything finite it is easy to add more.

Therefore, to attribute shape to him is not honor, for all shape is finite.

Nor to say we conceive, or imagine, or have an idea of him in our mind — for whatever we conceive is finite.

Nor to attribute parts to him, or totality — which are attributes only of finite things.

Nor to say he is in this place or that — for whatever is in a place is bounded and finite.

Nor that he moves, or rests — for both of these attributes assign him a place.

Nor that there is more than one God — because that implies they are all finite, for there cannot be more than one infinite.

Nor should we ascribe to him (except metaphorically — meaning not the passion but its effect) passions that partake of grief, such as repentance, anger, or mercy; or of want, such as appetite, hope, or desire; or any passive faculty at all. For passion is power limited by something else.

And therefore when we ascribe a will to God, it is not to be understood as a man's will — a rational appetite — but as the power by which he effects everything.

Likewise when we attribute to him sight and other acts of sense, or knowledge and understanding — which in us are nothing but a tumult of the mind, stirred up by external things pressing on the organs of a man's body. There is no such thing in God; and since these things depend on natural causes, they cannot be attributed to him.

Whoever will attribute to God nothing but what natural reason warrants must use either negative attributes, such as infinite, eternal, incomprehensible; or superlatives, such as Most High, Most Great, and the like; or indefinite ones, such as good, just, holy, Creator. And he must use them not as though he meant to declare what God is — for that would be to confine him within the limits of our imagination — but to declare how much we admire him, and how ready we would be to obey him. That is a sign of humility, and of a will to honor him as much as we can. For there is only one name that signifies our conception of his nature, and that is I AM; and only one name of his relation to us, and that is God — in which is contained Father, King, and Lord.

Actions That Are Signs of Divine Honor

Concerning the actions of divine worship, the most general precept of reason is this: that they be signs of the intention to honor God.

Such as, first, prayers. For it was not the carvers, when they made images, who were thought to make them gods — it was the people who prayed to them.

Secondly, thanksgiving — which differs from prayer in divine worship only in this, that prayers come before the benefit and thanks come after it. The purpose of both is the same: to acknowledge God as the author of all benefits, past and future alike.

Thirdly, gifts — that is, sacrifices and offerings — are signs of honor, provided they are of the best; for they are thanksgivings.

Fourthly, to swear by none but God is naturally a sign of honor. For it is a confession that God alone knows the heart, and that no man's cleverness or strength can protect him against God's vengeance on the perjured.

Fifthly, it is part of rational worship to speak carefully of God; for it shows fear of him, and fear is a confession of his power. From this it follows that the name of God is not to be used rashly and to no purpose — for that is as much as to use it in vain. And it is to no purpose unless it is by way of an oath, by order of the commonwealth, to make judgments certain; or between commonwealths, to avoid war.

It also follows that disputing about God's nature is contrary to his honor. For in this natural kingdom of God, it is supposed there is no way to know anything except by natural reason — that is, from the principles of natural science. And those principles are so far from teaching us anything about God's nature that they cannot even teach us our own nature, or the nature of the smallest living creature. So when men dispute about the attributes of God from the principles of natural reason, they only dishonor him. For in the attributes we give to God, what matters is not the signification of philosophical truth, but the signification of pious intention — the intention to do him the greatest honor we are able. From neglecting this consideration have come whole volumes of disputation about the nature of God, which tend not to his honor, but to the honor of our own wits and learning — and which are nothing but inconsiderate and vain abuses of his sacred name.

Sixthly, in prayers, thanksgivings, offerings, and sacrifices, natural reason dictates that each one should be, of its kind, the best, and the most significant of honor. For example, prayers and thanksgiving should be made in words and phrases that are not hasty, not flippant, not vulgar, but beautiful and well composed — for otherwise we do not give God as much honor as we can. The heathens were absurd to worship images as gods; but their doing it in verse, and with music of both voice and instruments, was reasonable. So too, that the beasts they sacrificed, and the gifts they offered, and their actions in worshipping were full of submission, and commemorated benefits received — that was according to reason, since it proceeded from an intention to honor him.

Seventhly, reason directs us to worship God not only in secret, but also — and especially — in public, in the sight of men. For without that, the thing most acceptable in honor is lost: leading others to honor him.

Lastly, obedience to his laws — which in this case means the laws of nature — is the greatest worship of all. For just as obedience is more acceptable to God than sacrifice, so to make light of his commandments is the greatest of all insults. And these are the laws of divine worship that natural reason dictates to private men.

Public Worship Must Be Uniform

But since a commonwealth is one person, it ought to exhibit one worship to God. And it does this when it commands that worship to be exhibited by private men publicly. This is PUBLIC WORSHIP, and its defining property is uniformity. For actions done differently by different men cannot be called a public worship. And therefore, where many sorts of worship are allowed, proceeding from the different religions of private men, it cannot be said that there is any public worship — or that the commonwealth has any religion at all.

All Attributes Depend on the Civil Laws

And because words — and consequently the attributes of God — get their signification by the agreement and constitution of men, those attributes are to be held significant of honor that men intend to be so. And whatever may be done by the wills of particular men, where there is no law but reason, may be done by the will of the commonwealth, through civil laws. And because a commonwealth has no will, and makes no laws, except those made by the will of the man or assembly that holds the sovereign power, it follows that the attributes which the sovereign ordains for signs of honor in the worship of God ought to be taken and used as such by private men in their public worship.

But Not All Actions

Not all actions, however, are signs by convention. Some are naturally signs of honor, others of contempt. The latter — the things men are ashamed to do in the sight of those they revere — can never be made part of divine worship by any human power. The former — decent, modest, humble behavior — can never be separated from it. But there is an infinite number of actions and gestures of an indifferent nature; and those of them that the commonwealth ordains to be publicly and universally in use, as signs of honor and part of God's worship, are to be taken and used as such by the subjects. As for what Scripture says — "It is better to obey God than men" — that applies in the kingdom of God by pact, not by nature.

Natural Punishments

Having spoken thus briefly of the natural kingdom of God and his natural laws, I will add to this chapter only a short declaration of his natural punishments.

There is no action of man in this life that is not the beginning of a chain of consequences so long that no human foresight is high enough to see to the end of it. In this chain, pleasing and unpleasing events are linked together — in such a way that whoever does anything for his pleasure must commit himself to suffer all the pains attached to it. These pains are the natural punishments of those actions that begin more harm than good. And so it comes to pass that intemperance is naturally punished with diseases; rashness, with mishaps; injustice, with the violence of enemies; pride, with ruin; cowardice, with oppression; the negligent government of princes, with rebellion; and rebellion, with slaughter. For since punishments follow the breach of laws, natural punishments must naturally follow the breach of the laws of nature — and so they follow them as their natural effects, not arbitrary ones.

The Conclusion of the Second Part

And so much for the constitution, nature, and right of sovereigns, and for the duty of subjects, derived from the principles of natural reason.

Now, when I consider how different this doctrine is from the practice of most of the world — especially of these Western parts, which have received their moral learning from Rome and Athens — and how much depth of moral philosophy is required in those who administer the sovereign power, I am at the point of believing this labor of mine as useless as the Commonwealth of Plato. For Plato too was of the opinion that the disorders of state, and the change of governments by civil war, can never be taken away until sovereigns become philosophers.

But then I consider again: that the science of natural justice is the only science necessary for sovereigns and their principal ministers; that they need not be burdened with the mathematical sciences (as Plato would have them), beyond encouraging the study of them by good laws; and that neither Plato nor any other philosopher so far has put in order, and sufficiently or probably proved, all the theorems of moral doctrine, so that men might learn from them both how to govern and how to obey. And then I recover some hope: that one time or other, this writing of mine may fall into the hands of a sovereign who will consider it for himself — for it is short, and I think clear — without the help of any interested or envious interpreter; and who, by the exercise of entire sovereignty, in protecting the public teaching of it, will convert this truth of speculation into the utility of practice.
