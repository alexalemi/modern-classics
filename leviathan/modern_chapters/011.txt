Chapter 11: The Difference of Manners

What "Manners" Means Here

By MANNERS, I do not mean politeness — how one person should greet another, or whether a man should rinse his mouth or pick his teeth in company, and other points of the small morals. I mean those qualities of mankind that concern our living together in peace and unity.

To see what those qualities are, start here: the happiness of this life does not consist in the repose of a satisfied mind. There is no Finis Ultimus — final goal — and no Summum Bonum — greatest good — of the kind described in the books of the old moral philosophers. A man whose desires have come to an end can no more live than a man whose senses and imagination have come to a standstill. Happiness is a continual progress of desire from one object to another, where getting the first thing is only the road to getting the next.

Why? Because the object of a man's desire is not to enjoy something once, for one instant of time, but to secure forever the way to his future desires. So the voluntary actions and inclinations of all men aim not only at obtaining a contented life, but at making it secure. Men differ only in how they go about it — partly because passions differ from person to person, and partly because each person has different knowledge, or different opinions, about which causes produce the effect he wants.

A Restless Desire for Power, in All Mankind

So in the first place, I put forward as a general inclination of all mankind a perpetual and restless desire of power after power, that ceaseth only in death.

The cause of this is not always that a man hopes for a more intense delight than he has already attained, or that he cannot be content with a moderate power. It is that he cannot secure the power and means to live well that he has right now without acquiring more. That is why kings, whose power is greatest, turn their efforts to securing it — at home by laws, abroad by wars. And when that is done, a new desire takes its place: in some, fame from new conquests; in others, ease and sensual pleasure; in others, admiration, or being flattered for excellence in some art or other ability of the mind.

Competition Breeds Conflict

Competition for riches, honor, command, or any other power inclines men toward contention, enmity, and war — because the way for one competitor to get what he desires is to kill, subdue, supplant, or repel the other. Competition for praise, in particular, inclines men toward reverence for antiquity. Men compete with the living, not the dead — so they credit the dead with more than they deserve, in order to dim the glory of their living rivals.

Love of Ease Makes Men Obey a Common Power

Desire for ease and sensual pleasure disposes men to obey a common power, because such desires make a man give up the protection he might otherwise have to win by his own industry and labor.

So Does Fear of Death or Wounds

Fear of death and wounds disposes men the same way, and for the same reason. On the other hand, needy and hardy men who are not content with their present condition — along with all men ambitious for military command — are inclined to keep the causes of war alive, and to stir up trouble and sedition. There is no military honor except through war, and no better hope of mending a bad game than forcing a new shuffle of the cards.

And So Does Love of Knowledge and the Arts of Peace

Desire for knowledge, and for the arts of peace, inclines men to obey a common power. Such a desire contains a desire for leisure — and therefore for protection by some power other than one's own.

Love of Praise Inspires Praiseworthy Action

Desire for praise disposes men to praiseworthy actions — the kind that please the people whose judgment they value; for when we despise a man, we despise his praise as well. Desire for fame after death does the same. True, after death there is no sense of the praise given us on earth — those joys are either swallowed up in the unspeakable joys of heaven or extinguished in the extreme torments of hell. Yet such fame is not pointless. Men take a present delight in foreseeing it, and in foreseeing the benefit that may pass from it to their descendants. They do not see that benefit now, but they imagine it — and whatever is pleasure to the senses is pleasure in the imagination too.

Hatred, from Benefits Too Great to Repay

To receive from someone we consider our equal greater benefits than we can ever hope to repay disposes us to counterfeit love — and to real, secret hatred. It puts a man in the position of a desperate debtor who avoids the sight of his creditor and quietly wishes him somewhere he will never be seen again. Benefits oblige; obligation is bondage; and bondage to an equal is hateful.

But to receive benefits from someone we acknowledge as our superior inclines us to love, because the obligation is no new humiliation; and cheerful acceptance (which men call gratitude) is such an honor done to the giver that it generally counts as repayment. Receiving benefits from an equal, or even an inferior, also disposes us to love — as long as there is hope of repaying them. For then the receiver intends the obligation as one of mutual aid and service, and from that comes a competition over who shall out-give the other: the noblest and most profitable contest possible, in which the winner is pleased with his victory, and the loser gets his revenge by admitting it.

And from Knowing You Deserve to Be Hated

To have done a man more harm than you can make up for, or are willing to, inclines you to hate the one you have hurt. For you must expect either his revenge or his forgiveness — and both are hateful.

Fear Makes Men Strike First

Fear of oppression disposes a man to strike first, or to seek safety by joining with others. There is no other way a man can secure his life and liberty.

So Does Distrust of One's Own Cleverness

Men who distrust their own cleverness do better in times of tumult and sedition than men who suppose themselves wise or crafty. The crafty love to consult; the others, fearing to be outmaneuvered, strike first. And in sedition, where men are always on the edge of battle, holding together and using every advantage of force is a better strategy than anything cleverness can produce.

Vanity Leads to Empty Undertakings

Vain men — those who delight in imagining themselves gallant without being conscious of any real ability in themselves — are inclined only to show, not to action. For when danger or difficulty appears, they can expect nothing but to have their insufficiency exposed.

Vain men who estimate their ability from the flattery of other men, or from the luck of some past success, without any solid ground of hope from true knowledge of themselves, are inclined to rush in — and then, as danger or difficulty approaches, to retreat if they can. For seeing no way to safety, they would rather risk their honor, which can be patched up with an excuse, than their lives, for which no patch is sufficient.

Ambition Comes from a High Opinion of One's Own Wisdom

Men who hold a strong opinion of their own wisdom in matters of government are disposed to ambition, because without public employment in council or office, the honor of their wisdom is lost. That is why eloquent speakers are inclined to ambition: eloquence looks like wisdom, both to themselves and to others.

Indecision Comes from Overvaluing Small Things

Pusillanimity disposes men to indecision, and so to losing the occasions and best opportunities for action. For when men have deliberated right up to the moment of action, and it is still not clear which course is best, that is a sign that the motives on either side are not very different. To fail to decide then is to lose the occasion by weighing trifles — which is pusillanimity.

Frugality (though a virtue in poor men) makes a man unfit to achieve actions that require the strength of many men at once. For it starves their effort, which has to be nourished and kept vigorous by reward.

Trusting Others, from Not Knowing the Marks of Wisdom and Kindness

Eloquence, joined with flattery, disposes men to trust those who have it — for eloquence looks like wisdom, and flattery looks like kindness. Add military reputation, and men are disposed to attach themselves, and subject themselves, to those who have all three. The first two give them assurance against danger from the man himself; the third gives them assurance against danger from others.

And from Ignorance of Causes

Lack of science — that is, ignorance of causes — disposes a man, or rather forces him, to rely on the advice and authority of others. For every man who cares about the truth must, if he does not rely on his own judgment, rely on the opinion of someone else — someone he thinks wiser than himself, and sees no reason to suspect of deceiving him.

And from Lack of Understanding

Ignorance of what words signify — which is lack of understanding — disposes men to take on trust not only truths they do not know, but errors too, and worse, the nonsense of those they trust. For neither error nor nonsense can be detected without a perfect understanding of words.

From the same source it comes that men give different names to one and the same thing, according to the difference of their own passions. Those who approve of a private opinion call it opinion; those who dislike it call it heresy — and yet heresy means nothing more than private opinion, with a stronger tincture of anger in it.

From the same source it also comes that men cannot distinguish, without study and real understanding, between one action of many men and many actions of one multitude — between, for example, the single action of all the senators of Rome in killing Catiline, and the many actions of a number of senators in killing Caesar. And so they are disposed to take for the action of the people what is really a multitude of actions done by a multitude of men — led on, perhaps, by the persuasion of one.

Clinging to Custom, from Ignorance of the Nature of Right and Wrong

Ignorance of the causes and original constitution of right, equity, law, and justice disposes a man to make custom and example the rule of his actions: to think a thing unjust because it has been the custom to punish it, and just because he can produce an example of it going unpunished and approved — or, as the lawyers (who alone use this false measure of justice) barbarously call it, a precedent. He is like little children, who have no other rule of good and bad manners than the corrections they receive from their parents and teachers. Except that children stay constant to their rule, and grown men do not. Grown strong and stubborn, men appeal from custom to reason and from reason to custom as it serves their turn — abandoning custom when their interest requires it, and setting themselves against reason whenever reason is against them.

That is why the doctrine of right and wrong is perpetually disputed, both by the pen and by the sword — while the doctrine of lines and figures is not. For in that subject, men do not care what the truth is, since it crosses no man's ambition, profit, or lust. I have no doubt that if the proposition that the three angles of a triangle are equal to two angles of a square had been contrary to any man's right of dominion, or to the interest of men who have dominion, that doctrine would have been — if not disputed — then suppressed by the burning of every book of geometry, as far as the man it concerned was able.

Following Private Men, from Ignorance of the Causes of Peace

Ignorance of remote causes disposes men to attribute all events to the causes that are immediate and instrumental — for these are the only causes they perceive. That is why, in every country, men aggrieved by payments to the public discharge their anger on the publicans — the tax farmers, collectors, and other officers of the public revenue — and attach themselves to anyone who finds fault with the public government. Then, having committed themselves beyond hope of justification, they end up attacking the supreme authority itself, for fear of punishment, or for shame at accepting a pardon.

Credulity, from Ignorance of Nature

Ignorance of natural causes disposes a man to credulity — to believing, many times, in impossibilities. For he knows nothing to say they might not be true, being unable to detect the impossibility. And credulity, because men love to be listened to in company, disposes them to lying. So ignorance by itself, without any malice, can make a man both believe lies and tell them — and sometimes invent them too.

Curiosity Comes from Anxiety About the Future

Anxiety about the future disposes men to inquire into the causes of things, because knowing the causes makes men better able to arrange the present to their best advantage.

Natural Religion Comes from the Same Source

Curiosity — the love of knowing causes — draws a man from considering an effect to seeking its cause, and then the cause of that cause, until of necessity he comes at last to this thought: there must be some cause that has no earlier cause, but is eternal. And that is what men call God. So it is impossible to inquire deeply into natural causes without being inclined to believe there is one eternal God — even though no one can have an idea of him in the mind that matches his nature.

Think of a man born blind, who hears others talk of warming themselves by a fire and is then brought to warm himself at one. He can easily conceive, and assure himself, that there is something there which men call fire, and which causes the heat he feels — but he cannot imagine what it is like, or have an idea of it in his mind such as sighted men have. In the same way, from the visible things of this world and their admirable order, a man may conceive that there is a cause of them all, which men call God — and yet have no idea or image of him in his mind.

And those who make little or no inquiry into the natural causes of things are still moved by the fear that grows out of that very ignorance — fear of whatever it is that has the power to do them great good or great harm. That fear inclines them to suppose, and invent for themselves, various kinds of invisible powers; to stand in awe of their own imaginations; to call on those powers in times of distress, and to give them thanks when expected good fortune arrives — making the creatures of their own imagination into their gods. By this means, from the endless variety of imagination, men have created in the world endless sorts of gods. And this fear of invisible things is the natural seed of what everyone calls religion in himself — and superstition in those who worship or fear that power differently than he does.

This seed of religion has been noticed by many. And some of those who noticed it have been inclined to nourish it, dress it up, and shape it into laws — adding to it, out of their own invention, whatever opinions about the causes of future events they thought would best enable them to govern others, and to make the greatest use of their own powers.
