Chapter 15: The Other Laws of Nature

The Third Law of Nature: Justice

From the law of nature that obliges us to transfer to others those rights which, if we kept them, would block the peace of mankind, a third law follows. It is this: that men perform the covenants they make. Without this law, covenants are pointless — nothing but empty words. Every man would still hold his right to everything, and we would still be in the condition of war.

What Justice and Injustice Are

This law of nature is the fountain and origin of JUSTICE. Where no covenant has gone before, no right has been transferred, and every man has a right to everything — so no action can be unjust. But once a covenant is made, breaking it is unjust. The definition of injustice is exactly this, and nothing more: the not performing of a covenant. And whatever is not unjust, is just.

Justice and Property Begin with the Commonwealth

But remember what I said in the last chapter: covenants of mutual trust are invalid wherever either side has reasonable fear that the other will not perform. So although justice originates in the making of covenants, there can be no actual injustice until that fear is removed — and as long as men remain in the natural condition of war, it cannot be removed. Before the words "just" and "unjust" can have any place, there must be some coercive power that compels all men equally to keep their covenants, through the terror of a punishment greater than any benefit they could expect from breaking them. That same power must also secure the property men acquire by mutual contract, as compensation for the universal right they give up. And no such power exists before a commonwealth is erected.

You can draw the same conclusion from the standard definition of justice taught in the universities: "Justice is the constant will of giving to every man his own." Where there is no "own" — no property — there is no injustice. And where no coercive power has been erected — where there is no commonwealth — there is no property, because all men have a right to all things. Therefore where there is no commonwealth, nothing is unjust.

So the nature of justice consists in the keeping of valid covenants. But covenants only become valid when a civil power is constituted that is strong enough to compel men to keep them. And that is also the moment property begins.

Justice Is Not Contrary to Reason

The fool has said in his heart: there is no such thing as justice. Sometimes he says it with his tongue too, and argues for it seriously. His argument goes like this. Every man's survival and contentment are entrusted to his own care. So there can be no reason why a man may not do whatever he thinks will get him there. Therefore making or not making covenants, keeping or not keeping them, is not against reason — so long as it serves his benefit.

Notice what the fool is not denying. He does not deny that covenants exist, that they are sometimes broken and sometimes kept, or that breaking them is called injustice and keeping them justice. What he questions is whether injustice — setting aside the fear of God, for the same fool has said in his heart there is no God — might not sometimes square with the reason that tells every man to pursue his own good. Especially, he says, when the payoff is big enough to put a man beyond the reach not only of other men's scorn and insults, but of their power. The kingdom of God is gotten by violence, he says — but what if it could be gotten by unjust violence? Would it be against reason to take it that way, when it is impossible to be hurt by doing so? And if it is not against reason, it is not against justice — or else justice cannot be approved as good.

From this kind of reasoning, successful wickedness has come to be called virtue. There are men who condemn breaking faith in every other case, yet allow it when the prize is a kingdom. Even the pagans who believed that Saturn was deposed by his own son Jupiter still believed that this same Jupiter was the avenger of injustice. There is something similar in a piece of law in Coke's commentaries on Littleton — the great English law book — where he says that if the rightful heir to the crown stands convicted of treason, the crown shall nevertheless descend to him, and eo instante — at that very instant — the conviction becomes void. From examples like these, a man will be strongly tempted to conclude: when the heir apparent of a kingdom kills the man who holds the throne — even his own father — you may call it injustice or whatever name you please, but it can never be against reason, since all the voluntary actions of men aim at their own benefit, and the most reasonable actions are the ones that best serve their ends.

This reasoning sounds clever. It is nevertheless false.

Here is why. The question is not about mutual promises where neither side has any security of performance — where no civil power stands over the parties — because such promises are not covenants at all. The question arises only where one party has already performed, or where there is a power that can make him perform. Then we ask: is it against reason — that is, against the benefit of the other party — to perform, or not? And I say it is not against reason.

To show this, consider two things. First: when a man does something that, by everything that can be foreseen and reckoned on, tends to his own destruction, the act is not made reasonable or wise just because some accident he could not have expected happens to turn it to his benefit. Lucky is not the same as smart.

Second: in a condition of war, where every man is an enemy to every man for lack of a common power to keep them all in check, no man can hope to defend himself from destruction by his own strength or wit alone. He needs allies — confederates — and in any confederation, each member expects the same defense from the alliance that everyone else does. So a man who declares that he thinks it reasonable to cheat the people who help him can, in reason, expect no means of safety beyond his own single power. The man who breaks his covenant — and thereby declares that he thinks he may reasonably do so — cannot be received into any society that unites for peace and defense, except by the mistake of those who let him in. And once in, he cannot be kept there unless they fail to see the danger of their mistake. A man cannot reasonably count on other people's errors as the means of his security. So if he is abandoned or cast out of society, he perishes; and if he survives in society, it is only by the errors of other men — errors he could neither foresee nor count on. Either way, his policy works against the reason of his own preservation. Everyone who refrains from destroying him does so only out of ignorance of what is good for themselves.

As for the fool's example of gaining the secure and everlasting happiness of heaven by any means whatever — that is frivolous. There is only one imaginable way to gain it, and it is not breaking covenants but keeping them.

And as for the other example, gaining sovereignty by rebellion: it is obvious that even if it succeeds, it cannot reasonably be expected to succeed — the contrary is far more likely. And whoever gains power that way teaches others to gain it from him the same way. So the attempt is against reason. Justice, therefore — that is, the keeping of covenant — is a rule of reason that forbids us to do anything destructive to our life. And consequently it is a law of nature.

Some people go further still. They will not have the law of nature be the set of rules that preserve man's life on earth, but rules for attaining eternal happiness after death — and they think breaking a covenant may sometimes serve that end, and so be just and reasonable. These are the people who think it a work of religious merit to kill, depose, or rebel against the sovereign power set over them by their own consent. But there is no natural knowledge of man's condition after death, much less of any reward then waiting for breach of faith. There is only a belief, grounded on other men's saying that they know it supernaturally — or that they know people, who knew people, who knew other people, who knew it supernaturally. So breach of faith cannot be called a precept of reason or of nature.

Covenants Are Not Cancelled by the Vices of the Person They Are Made With

Others accept the keeping of faith as a law of nature, but make exceptions for certain persons — heretics, say, or people who habitually fail to keep their own covenants. This too is against reason. If some fault in a man were enough to discharge a covenant once made, the same fault should, in reason, have been enough to prevent the making of it.

The Justice of Men and the Justice of Actions

The words "just" and "unjust" mean one thing when applied to men, and another when applied to actions. Applied to men, they signify whether a man's manners — his whole way of life — conform to reason. Applied to actions, they signify whether particular actions conform to reason, regardless of the man's character.

A just man, then, is one who takes all the care he can that his actions be just; an unjust man is one who neglects that care. In our language such men are more often called righteous and unrighteous than just and unjust, but the meaning is the same. A righteous man does not lose that title by one or a few unjust actions that spring from sudden passion or from mistaking things or persons. Nor does an unrighteous man lose his character because of actions he does, or holds back from doing, out of fear — because his will is shaped not by justice but by the apparent benefit of the act.

What gives human actions the flavor of justice is a certain nobility or gallantness of courage — rarely found — by which a man scorns to owe the contentment of his life to fraud or broken promises. This justice of manners is what is meant when justice is called a virtue, and injustice a vice.

The justice of actions, by contrast, does not make men "just" but merely guiltless; and the injustice of actions — which is also called injury — makes them merely guilty.

Justice of Manners and Justice of Actions

Again: injustice of manners is the disposition or readiness to do injury. It is injustice before it ever proceeds to action, and without supposing any individual person wronged. But the injustice of an action — an injury — does suppose an individual person wronged: namely, the person to whom the covenant was made. That is why the injury is often received by one man while the damage lands on another. Suppose a master commands his servant to give money to a stranger, and the servant fails to do it. The injury is done to the master, whom the servant had covenanted to obey; the damage falls on the stranger, to whom the servant had no obligation, and whom he therefore could not injure. So too in commonwealths: private men may forgive one another their debts, but not robberies or other violence that damages them. Withholding a debt is an injury to themselves; robbery and violence are injuries to the person of the commonwealth.

Nothing Done to a Man with His Own Consent Can Be an Injury

Whatever is done to a man in accordance with his own will, signified to the doer, is no injury to him. If the doer had not given away, by some earlier covenant, his original right to do as he pleases, then there is no breach of covenant — and so no injury. And if he had, then the other man's signified will to have the thing done releases that covenant — and so, again, no injury.

Commutative and Distributive Justice

Writers divide the justice of actions into commutative and distributive. The first, they say, consists in arithmetical proportion; the second in geometrical proportion. So they place commutative justice in the equal value of the things contracted for, and distributive justice in distributing equal benefit to men of equal merit — as if it were injustice to sell something for more than you paid for it, or to give a man more than he merits.

This is wrong. The value of everything contracted for is measured by the appetite of the contractors, and so the just value is whatever they are content to give. And merit — apart from the kind created by covenant, where performance on one side merits performance on the other, which falls under commutative justice, not distributive — is not owed by justice at all. It is rewarded by grace only. So this distinction, in the sense in which it is usually expounded, is not right.

To speak properly: commutative justice is the justice of a contractor — the performance of covenant in buying and selling, hiring and renting out, lending and borrowing, exchanging, bartering, and other acts of contract.

And distributive justice is the justice of an arbitrator — the act of defining what is just. If the arbitrator performs the trust placed in him by those who made him arbitrator, he is said to distribute to every man his own. This is indeed just distribution, and may be called (though improperly) distributive justice. More properly it is called equity — which is itself a law of nature, as I will show in its place.

The Fourth Law of Nature: Gratitude

As justice depends on a prior covenant, so gratitude depends on prior grace — that is, on a prior free gift. It is the fourth law of nature, and may be put in this form: that a man who receives benefit from another out of mere grace should endeavor that the giver have no reasonable cause to regret his good will. No man gives except with the intention of some good to himself, because giving is voluntary, and the object of every voluntary act is some good to the doer. If men see that their generosity will be wasted, there will be no beginning of benevolence or trust — and therefore no mutual help, and no reconciliation of one man to another. Men would remain in the condition of war, which is contrary to the first and fundamental law of nature, the one that commands men to seek peace. The breach of this law is called ingratitude, and it has the same relation to grace that injustice has to obligation by covenant.

The Fifth Law: Mutual Accommodation, or Complaisance

A fifth law of nature is COMPLAISANCE — that is: that every man strive to accommodate himself to the rest. To understand this, consider that men differ in their fitness for society, owing to the diversity of their affections — much like stones brought together for building. A stone that is so rough and irregular in shape that it takes more room from the others than it fills itself, and so hard that it cannot easily be smoothed, hinders the building, and the builders throw it away as useless and troublesome. Just so, a man whose roughness of nature drives him to hold on to things that are superfluous to him and necessary to others, and whose stubborn passions cannot be corrected, is to be left out of society, or cast out of it, as a burden to it. Every man is supposed — not only by right but by necessity of nature — to do all he can to obtain what is necessary for his preservation. So whoever sets himself against that, for the sake of things he does not need, is guilty of the war that must follow, and acts contrary to the fundamental law of nature, which commands us to seek peace. Those who observe this law may be called SOCIABLE (the Latins call them commodi — agreeable, accommodating); those who break it: stubborn, unsociable, perverse, intractable.

The Sixth Law: Readiness to Pardon

A sixth law of nature is this: that, given assurance about the future, a man ought to pardon the past offenses of those who repent and desire it. PARDON is nothing but the granting of peace. Granted to those who persist in their hostility, it is not peace but fear. But refused to those who give assurance for the future, it is the sign of an aversion to peace — and therefore contrary to the law of nature.

The Seventh Law: In Revenge, Look Only to the Good to Come

A seventh: that in revenges — that is, in paying back evil for evil — men look not at the greatness of the evil past, but at the greatness of the good to follow. This forbids us to inflict punishment with any other purpose than the correction of the offender or the guidance of others. The law follows from the one before it, which commands pardon upon security for the future. Besides, revenge that pays no regard to example or to future benefit is mere triumphing and glorying in another's hurt, aiming at no end at all — for the end is always something still to come. Glorying to no end is vanity, and contrary to reason; and hurting without reason tends to introduce war, which is against the law of nature. Its common name is cruelty.

The Eighth Law: Against Insult

Because all signs of hatred or contempt provoke men to fight — so much so that most men would rather risk their lives than go unavenged — we may set down in the eighth place this precept for a law of nature: that no man declare hatred or contempt of another, by deed, word, look, or gesture. The breach of this law is commonly called contumely — insult.

The Ninth Law: Against Pride

The question of who is the better man has no place in the condition of mere nature, where, as I showed earlier, all men are equal. The inequality that exists now was introduced by the civil laws. I know that Aristotle, in the first book of his Politics, lays down as a foundation of his doctrine that some men are by nature more worthy to command — meaning the wiser sort, such as he took himself to be on account of his philosophy — and others to serve, meaning those with strong bodies who were not philosophers like him. As if master and servant were introduced not by the consent of men, but by difference of wit! That is against reason, and against experience too. Very few men are so foolish that they would not rather govern themselves than be governed by others. And when the wise-in-their-own-eyes contend by force with those who distrust their own wisdom, the wise do not always win — or often — or almost ever.

If nature has made men equal, then, that equality must be acknowledged. And if nature has made men unequal, the equality must still be admitted — because men who think themselves equal will not enter into conditions of peace except on equal terms. So for the ninth law of nature I set down this: that every man acknowledge another as his equal by nature. The breach of this precept is pride.

The Tenth Law: Against Arrogance

On this law depends another: that at the entrance into conditions of peace, no man require to reserve for himself any right which he is not content to have reserved to every one of the rest. Just as it is necessary for all men who seek peace to lay down certain rights of nature — that is, to give up the liberty of doing whatever they please — so it is necessary for human life to retain some rights: the right to govern their own bodies; to enjoy air, water, movement, roads to travel from place to place; and everything else without which a man cannot live, or cannot live well. If, when peace is being made, men demand for themselves what they would not grant to others, they act contrary to the previous law, which commands the acknowledgment of natural equality, and therefore against the law of nature. The observers of this law are the people we call modest; the breakers, arrogant. The Greeks call the violation of this law pleonexia — the desire for more than one's share.

The Eleventh Law: Equity

Also, if a man is trusted to judge between man and man, it is a precept of the law of nature that he deal equally between them. Without that, the disputes of men can be settled only by war. A judge who plays favorites does everything in his power to scare men away from using judges and arbitrators at all — and so, against the fundamental law of nature, he is a cause of war.

The observance of this law — giving to each man, by equal distribution, what in reason belongs to him — is called EQUITY, and, as I said before, distributive justice. Its violation is called acception of persons — in Greek, prosopolepsia: favoritism, judging by the face rather than the case.

The Twelfth Law: Equal Use of Common Things

From this follows another law: that things which cannot be divided be enjoyed in common, if they can be — without limit, if the quantity of the thing permits; otherwise in proportion to the number of those who have a right to it. Any other arrangement makes the distribution unequal, and contrary to equity.

The Thirteenth Law: Of Lots

But some things can neither be divided nor enjoyed in common. In that case the law of nature that prescribes equity requires that the entire right — or else, if the use is to alternate, the first possession — be determined by lot. For equal distribution belongs to the law of nature, and no other means of equal distribution can be imagined.

The Fourteenth Law: Of Birthright and First Possession

There are two sorts of lots: arbitrary and natural. An arbitrary lot is one agreed on by the competitors. A natural lot is either primogeniture — birthright of the firstborn; the Greek word is kleronomia, which means "given by lot" — or first seizure.

So things that cannot be enjoyed in common or divided ought to be awarded to the first possessor, and in some cases to the firstborn, as acquired by lot.

The Fifteenth Law: Of Mediators

It is also a law of nature that all men who mediate peace be allowed safe conduct. For the law that commands peace as the end commands intercession as the means; and the means to intercession is safe conduct.

The Sixteenth Law: Submission to Arbitration

However willing men may be to observe these laws, questions may still arise about a man's action. First, was it done or not done? Second, if done, was it against the law or not? The first is called a question of fact; the second a question of right. Unless the parties to the question mutually covenant to stand by the decision of another, they are as far from peace as ever. This other person, to whose decision they submit, is called an ARBITRATOR. So it belongs to the law of nature that those in a dispute submit their right to the judgment of an arbitrator.

The Seventeenth Law: No Man Is His Own Judge

Since every man is presumed to do all things for his own benefit, no man is a fit arbitrator in his own cause. And even if he were perfectly fit, equity grants equal benefit to each party — so if one of them is admitted as judge, the other must be admitted too, and the dispute, which is the cause of war, remains. That is against the law of nature.

The Eighteenth Law: No Judge Who Has a Stake in the Outcome

For the same reason, no man ought to be accepted as arbitrator in any cause if the victory of one party visibly brings him greater profit, honor, or pleasure than the victory of the other. He has taken a bribe — an unavoidable bribe, perhaps, but a bribe nonetheless — and no man can be obliged to trust him. So again the dispute, and the condition of war, remain — contrary to the law of nature.

The Nineteenth Law: Of Witnesses

And in a dispute over fact, since the judge has no reason to credit one party more than the other, he must (if there is no other evidence) give credit to a third witness — or a third and a fourth, or more. Otherwise the question stays undecided and is left to force, contrary to the law of nature.

These are the laws of nature that dictate peace as the means of preserving men in multitudes — the ones that concern the doctrine of civil society. There are other things that tend to the destruction of particular men — drunkenness, and all the other forms of intemperance — which may therefore also be counted among the things the law of nature forbids. But they are not necessary to mention here, and not relevant enough to this place.

A Rule for Testing the Laws of Nature Easily

This may seem too subtle a chain of deductions for all men to take notice of — most men being too busy getting food, and the rest too careless to understand. But so that no man can plead ignorance, the laws have been compressed into one easy sum, intelligible to even the humblest capacity. It is this: Do not that to another, which thou wouldest not have done to thyself. This rule shows a man that, to learn the laws of nature, he need do only one thing. When he is weighing the actions of other men against his own, and theirs seem too heavy, let him put their actions into the other pan of the scale and his own into theirs — so that his own passions and self-love add nothing to the weight. Then there is not one of these laws of nature that will not appear to him perfectly reasonable.

The Laws of Nature Always Bind in Conscience — but in Action, Only When It Is Safe

The laws of nature oblige in foro interno — in the internal court, the court of conscience. That is, they bind a man to desire that they take effect. But in foro externo — in the external court, the court of action — they do not always bind. A man who was modest and tractable, and kept all his promises, in a time and place where no one else did, would only make himself prey to others and bring about his own certain ruin — contrary to the foundation of all the laws of nature, which aim at nature's preservation. And on the other side, a man who has security that others will observe those laws toward him, but does not observe them himself, is seeking not peace but war — and consequently the destruction of his nature by violence.

And whatever laws bind in foro interno can be broken not only by an act contrary to the law, but also by an act in accordance with it — in the case where a man believes it to be contrary. For although his action follows the law, his purpose was against the law; and where the obligation is in foro interno, that is a breach.

The Laws of Nature Are Eternal

The laws of nature are immutable and eternal. Injustice, ingratitude, arrogance, pride, iniquity, favoritism, and the rest can never be made lawful. For it can never be that war preserves life and peace destroys it.

And Yet Easy

These same laws are easy to observe, because they oblige only to a desire and an endeavor — I mean an unfeigned and constant endeavor. Since they require nothing but the endeavor, whoever endeavors to fulfill them, fulfills them. And he that fulfills the law is just.

The Science of These Laws Is the True Moral Philosophy

The science of these laws is the true and only moral philosophy. For moral philosophy is nothing other than the science of what is good and evil in the conversation and society of mankind. Good and evil are names that signify our desires and aversions, which differ as men's temperaments, customs, and doctrines differ. Different men disagree not only in their judgments about what is pleasant or unpleasant to taste, smell, hearing, touch, and sight, but about what conforms or fails to conform to reason in the actions of common life. Indeed, the same man at different times differs from himself: at one time he praises — that is, calls good — what at another time he condemns and calls evil. From this arise disputes, controversies, and at last war.

So as long as a man is in the condition of mere nature, which is a condition of war, private appetite is the measure of good and evil. And yet all men agree on this much: that peace is good. And therefore the way or means of peace — which, as I have shown, are justice, gratitude, modesty, equity, mercy, and the rest of the laws of nature — are good too; that is to say, moral virtues, and their contrary vices, evil. Now the science of virtue and vice is moral philosophy; and therefore the true doctrine of the laws of nature is the true moral philosophy.

The writers on moral philosophy acknowledge the same virtues and vices. But they do not see what their goodness consists in, nor that they come to be praised as the means of peaceful, sociable, and comfortable living. So they locate virtue in a moderate middle degree of the passions — as if it were not the cause, but the degree, of daring that made courage; as if it were not the cause, but the size, of a gift that made generosity.

These dictates of reason men usually call by the name of laws — but improperly. They are only conclusions, or theorems, about what conduces to men's preservation and defense; whereas law, properly speaking, is the word of one who by right has command over others. Yet if we consider these same theorems as delivered in the word of God, who by right commands all things — then they are properly called laws.
