Chapter 35: What Scripture Means by "Kingdom of God," "Holy," "Sacred," and "Sacrament"

Theologians Take "Kingdom of God" Metaphorically — But Scripture Takes It Literally

When theologians write about the kingdom of God — especially in sermons and devotional books — they almost always mean one of two things. Usually they mean eternal happiness after this life, up in the highest heaven, which they also call the kingdom of glory. Sometimes they mean the down payment on that happiness — sanctification — which they call the kingdom of grace. What they never mean is a monarchy: that is, the sovereign power of God over actual subjects, acquired by those subjects' own consent. And yet that is the proper meaning of the word kingdom.

I find the opposite. In most places where Scripture uses the phrase, the KINGDOM OF GOD means a kingdom properly so called — a real one, established by the votes of the people of Israel in a special way. They chose God as their king by a covenant made with him, and God, for his part, promised them possession of the land of Canaan. Only rarely is the phrase used metaphorically, and then it means dominion over sin — and that only in the New Testament. The metaphor works because every subject will have that kind of dominion in the kingdom of God, without taking anything away from the sovereign.

From the very creation, God did not only reign over all mankind naturally, by his might. He also had special subjects, whom he commanded by a voice, the way one man speaks to another. That is how he reigned over Adam, commanding him not to eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. When Adam disobeyed — when he tasted the fruit and took it upon himself to play God, judging between good and evil not by his Creator's commandment but by his own sense of things — his punishment was to lose the state of eternal life in which God had first created him. Afterward God punished Adam's descendants for their vices with a universal flood, sparing only eight persons. And in those eight, at that time, the kingdom of God consisted.

Where the Kingdom of God Began

After this, it pleased God to speak to Abraham and to make a covenant with him (Genesis 17:7-8) in these words: "I will establish my covenant between me and thee, and thy seed after thee in their generations, for an everlasting covenant, to be a God to thee, and to thy seed after thee; and I will give unto thee, and to thy seed after thee, the land wherein thou art a stranger, all the land of Canaan, for an everlasting possession." And as a memorial and a token of this covenant, God ordained (verse 11) the sacrament of circumcision.

This is what is called the Old Covenant, or Old Testament. It contains a contract between God and Abraham, by which Abraham binds himself and his descendants, in a special way, to be subject to God's positive law — the law God explicitly lays down. To the moral law he was bound already, as by an oath of allegiance. And though the name of king is not yet given to God here, nor the name of kingdom to Abraham and his seed, the thing itself is the same: an institution, by pact, of God's special sovereignty over the descendants of Abraham. When Moses renewed this same covenant at Mount Sinai, it was expressly called a peculiar kingdom of God over the Jews. And it is of Abraham — not of Moses — that St. Paul says (Romans 4:11) that he is "the father of the faithful." That is, the father of those who are loyal, and do not violate the allegiance they swore to God — sworn then by circumcision, and afterward, in the New Covenant, by baptism.

The Kingdom of God Is, Properly, His Civil Sovereignty Over a Special People, by Pact

This covenant was renewed by Moses at the foot of Mount Sinai (Exodus 19:5), where the Lord commands Moses to speak to the people this way: "If you will obey my voice indeed, and keep my covenant, then ye shall be a peculiar people to me, for all the earth is mine; and ye shall be unto me a sacerdotal kingdom, and an holy nation."

Take that phrase "peculiar people." The Latin Vulgate has peculium de cunctis populis — "a private treasure out of all the peoples." The English translation made at the start of King James's reign has "a peculiar treasure unto me above all nations," and the Geneva French has "the most precious jewel of all nations." But the truest translation is the first one, and St. Paul himself confirms it (Titus 2:14) when, alluding to this very passage, he says that our blessed Savior "gave himself for us, that he might purify us to himself, a peculiar (that is, an extraordinary) people." The Greek word there is periousios, which is commonly set against the word epiousios. Epiousios means ordinary, everyday — as in the Lord's Prayer, where it describes our "daily" bread. Periousios means the opposite: something extra, stored up, enjoyed in a special manner — what the Latins call a peculium, a private treasure. And this reading is confirmed by the reason God gives immediately afterward, when he adds, "For all the earth is mine." It is as if he had said: "All the nations of the world are mine — but not the way you are mine. They are all mine by reason of my power. But you shall be mine by your own consent and covenant." That is something added on top of his ordinary title to all nations.

The same text confirms the point again, in express words: "Ye shall be to me a sacerdotal kingdom, and an holy nation." The Vulgate has regnum sacerdotale — a priestly kingdom — which matches the translation of 1 Peter 2:9, sacerdotium regale, a royal priesthood. It also matches the institution itself, under which no man could enter the Sanctum Sanctorum — the Holy of Holies — which is to say, no man could inquire God's will directly from God himself, except the High Priest alone. The English translation mentioned above, following Geneva, has "a kingdom of priests." Either that means the succession of one High Priest after another, or it contradicts both St. Peter and the actual practice of the high priesthood. For there was never anyone but the High Priest alone whose job it was to inform the people of God's will; and no assembly of priests was ever allowed into the Sanctum Sanctorum.

The title "holy nation" confirms the same thing. For holy means what belongs to God by special right, not by general right. All the earth, as the text says, is God's; but all the earth is not called holy. Only that is holy which is set apart for his special service, as the nation of the Jews was. So this one passage is evidence enough that by the kingdom of God is properly meant a commonwealth — instituted, by the consent of those who were to be its subjects, for their civil government and the regulation of their behavior: not only toward God their king, but toward one another in matters of justice, and toward other nations in both peace and war. It was, properly, a kingdom in which God was king, and in which the High Priest, after the death of Moses, was to be his sole viceroy or lieutenant.

But many other passages clearly prove the same thing. First (1 Samuel 8:7): when the elders of Israel, fed up with the corruption of Samuel's sons, demanded a king, Samuel was displeased and prayed to the Lord. And the Lord answered him: "Hearken unto the voice of the people, for they have not rejected thee, but they have rejected me, that I should not reign over them." From this it is evident that God himself was their king at that time, and that Samuel did not command the people — he only delivered to them what God appointed him to deliver, from time to time.

Again (1 Samuel 12:12), Samuel says to the people: "When ye saw that Nahash, king of the children of Ammon, came against you, ye said unto me, Nay, but a king shall reign over us — when the Lord your God was your king." It is plain that God was their king, and governed the civil state of their commonwealth.

And after the Israelites had rejected God, the prophets foretold his restoration. Isaiah (24:23): "Then the moon shall be confounded, and the sun ashamed, when the Lord of Hosts shall reign in Mount Zion, and in Jerusalem" — where he speaks expressly of God's reign in Zion and Jerusalem; that is, on earth. And Micah (4:7): "And the Lord shall reign over them in Mount Zion" — and Mount Zion is in Jerusalem, on the earth. And Ezekiel (20:33): "As I live, saith the Lord God, surely with a mighty hand, and a stretched out arm, and with fury poured out, I will rule over you"; and (verse 37) "I will cause you to pass under the rod, and I will bring you into the bond of the covenant." That is: I will reign over you, and make you stand to the covenant you made with me through Moses — the covenant you broke in your rebellion against me in the days of Samuel, when you elected another king.

And in the New Testament, the angel Gabriel says of our Savior (Luke 1:32-33): "He shall be great, and be called the Son of the Most High, and the Lord shall give him the throne of his father David; and he shall reign over the house of Jacob for ever; and of his kingdom there shall be no end." This too is a kingdom on earth. It was for claiming this kingdom, as an enemy of Caesar, that he was put to death. The title on his cross was "Jesus of Nazareth, King of the Jews." He was crowned, in mockery, with a crown of thorns. And for proclaiming him, it is said of the disciples (Acts 17:7) "that they did all of them contrary to the decrees of Caesar, saying there was another king, one Jesus."

The kingdom of God, therefore, is a real kingdom, not a metaphorical one — and it is taken that way not only in the Old Testament but in the New. When we say, "For thine is the kingdom, the power, and the glory," we mean God's kingdom by force of our covenant, not by the right of God's power. A kingdom of the second sort God always has, so it would be pointless to pray "Thy kingdom come" — unless we mean the restoration of that kingdom of God which Christ will bring, the kingdom that was interrupted when the Israelites revolted and elected Saul. Nor would it have made sense to announce "The kingdom of heaven is at hand," or to pray "Thy kingdom come," if that kingdom had still been standing.

There are so many other passages confirming this interpretation that it would be a wonder no more notice is taken of it — except that it sheds too much light on the right of Christian kings to govern the church, and certain people have noticed that. That is why, instead of "a sacerdotal kingdom," they translate "a kingdom of priests" — by the same logic they might translate St. Peter's "royal priesthood" into "a priesthood of kings." And where the text has "a peculiar people," they put "a precious jewel" or "treasure" — by which standard a man might as well call a general's special regiment, or his own company, the general's precious jewel, or his treasure.

In short: the kingdom of God is a civil kingdom. It consisted, first, in the obligation of the people of Israel to the laws Moses would bring them from Mount Sinai — and which afterward the High Priest of the day was to deliver to them from before the cherubim in the Sanctum Sanctorum. This kingdom was cast off in the election of Saul. The prophets foretold it would be restored by Christ. Its restoration is what we pray for daily when we say in the Lord's Prayer, "Thy kingdom come." Its rightful claim is what we acknowledge when we add, "For thine is the kingdom, the power, and the glory, for ever and ever, Amen." Its proclamation was the preaching of the apostles; and men are prepared for it by the teachers of the Gospel. To embrace that Gospel — that is, to promise obedience to God's government — is to be in the kingdom of grace, because God has freely (gratis) given to such people the power to be subjects — that is, children — of God hereafter, when Christ comes in majesty to judge the world and actually to govern his own people. And that is what is called the kingdom of glory.

If the kingdom of God (also called the kingdom of heaven, for the gloriousness and astonishing height of that throne) were not a kingdom that God exercised on earth, through his lieutenants or vicars who deliver his commandments to the people — then there would never have been so much contention and war over the question of who it is through whom God speaks to us. No priest would have troubled himself with "spiritual jurisdiction," and no king would have bothered to deny it to him.

From this literal interpretation of the kingdom of God arises the true interpretation of the word HOLY. It is the word which, in God's kingdom, answers to what men in their own kingdoms call public, or the king's.

The king of any country is the public person, the representative of all his subjects. And God, the king of Israel, was the Holy One of Israel. The nation that is subject to one earthly sovereign is the nation of that sovereign — that is, of the public person. So the Jews, who were God's nation, were called (Exodus 19:6) "a holy nation." By holy is always understood either God himself, or that which is God's own property; just as by public is always meant either the person of the commonwealth itself, or something that belongs to the commonwealth in such a way that no private person can claim any property in it.

That is why the Sabbath — God's day — is a holy day; the Temple — God's house — a holy house; sacrifices, tithes, and offerings — God's tribute — holy duties; priests, prophets, and anointed kings under Christ — God's ministers — holy men; the celestial ministering spirits — God's messengers — holy angels; and so on. Wherever the word holy is taken properly, there is always something implied about ownership, acquired by consent. When we say "Hallowed be thy name," we are simply praying to God for grace to keep the first commandment: to have no other gods but him. Mankind is God's nation by right of ownership — but only the Jews were a holy nation. Why? Because they became his property by covenant.

What "Sacred" Means

The word profane is usually used in Scripture to mean the same as common; and so their opposites — holy and proper, one's own — must, in the kingdom of God, mean the same as each other too. Figuratively, it is true, men are also called holy who led such godly lives that they seemed to have abandoned all worldly ambitions and devoted themselves entirely to God. But in the proper sense, that which is made holy by God's appropriating it or setting it apart for his own use is said to be sanctified by God — like the seventh day in the fourth commandment, or like the elect in the New Testament, who were said to be sanctified when they were endowed with the spirit of godliness. And that which is made holy by the dedication of men — given to God so that it may be used only in his public service — is called SACRED, and is said to be consecrated: temples and other houses of public prayer, and their utensils; priests and ministers; victims, offerings, and the physical materials of the sacraments.

Degrees of Holiness

There are degrees of holiness. For of the things set apart for the service of God, some may be set apart again, for a nearer and more special service. The whole nation of the Israelites was a people holy to God; yet within Israel, the tribe of Levi was a holy tribe; among the Levites, the priests were holier still; and among the priests, the High Priest was the most holy. In the same way, the land of Judea was the Holy Land; but the holy city where God was to be worshipped was more holy; the Temple more holy than the city; and the Sanctum Sanctorum more holy than the rest of the Temple.

Sacrament

A SACRAMENT is the separation of some visible thing from common use, and its consecration to God's service, as a sign — either of our admission into the kingdom of God, to be counted among his peculiar people, or of the commemoration of that admission. In the Old Testament, the sign of admission was circumcision; in the New Testament, baptism. The commemoration of it in the Old Testament was the eating, at a fixed yearly time, of the paschal lamb, which reminded the people of the night they were delivered from their bondage in Egypt. In the New Testament it is the celebration of the Lord's Supper, which reminds us of our deliverance from the bondage of sin by our blessed Savior's death on the cross.

The sacraments of admission are used only once, because a person needs to be admitted only once. But because we need to be reminded often of our deliverance and of our allegiance, the sacraments of commemoration must be repeated. These are the principal sacraments — they are, as it were, the solemn oaths we swear of our allegiance. There are other consecrations that may be called sacraments, insofar as the word implies nothing more than consecration to God's service. But insofar as the word implies an oath, or promise of allegiance to God, there were no others in the Old Testament but circumcision and the Passover; nor are there any others in the New Testament but baptism and the Lord's Supper.
