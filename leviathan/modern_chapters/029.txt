Chapter 28: Punishments and Rewards

The Definition of Punishment

A PUNISHMENT is an evil inflicted by public authority on a person who has done — or failed to do — something that the same authority has judged to be a violation of the law, for this purpose: that the wills of men may thereby be better disposed to obedience.

Where the Right to Punish Comes From

Before I draw anything out of this definition, there is a question of great importance that has to be answered first: by what door did the right, or authority, to punish in any case come in?

Here is the puzzle. From everything said before, no man can be supposed bound by covenant not to resist violence. It follows that no man can be understood to have given anyone else a right to lay violent hands on his person. In the making of a commonwealth, every man gives away the right of defending others — but not the right of defending himself. He also obligates himself to assist the one who holds the sovereignty in punishing others — but not in punishing himself. And covenanting to assist the sovereign in hurting another person does not give the sovereign a right to punish, unless the one making the covenant had a right to do that hurt himself. So it is plain: the right which the commonwealth — that is, the man or assembly that represents it — has to punish is not grounded on any concession or gift from the subjects.

But I have also shown earlier that before the institution of the commonwealth, every man had a right to everything, and a right to do whatever he thought necessary for his own preservation — subduing, hurting, or killing any man to that end. And this is the foundation of the right of punishing that is exercised in every commonwealth. The subjects did not give the sovereign that right. Rather, by laying down their own rights, they strengthened him to use his — as he should think fit — for the preservation of them all. The right was not given to him; it was left to him, and left to him alone. And except for the limits set on him by natural law, it remains as entire as it was in the condition of mere nature, in the war of every man against his neighbor.

Private Injuries and Revenges Are Not Punishments

From the definition of punishment, I draw the following conclusions. First: neither private revenge nor injuries done by private men can properly be called punishment, because they do not proceed from public authority.

Nor Is Being Passed Over for Favor

Second: to be neglected and passed over by public favor is not a punishment, because no new evil is thereby inflicted on anyone. The man is simply left in the condition he was in before.

Nor Pain Inflicted Without a Public Hearing

Third: an evil inflicted by public authority without a prior public condemnation is not to be called punishment, but a hostile act — because the deed for which a man is punished must first be judged, by public authority, to be a violation of the law.

Nor Pain Inflicted by a Usurped Power

Fourth: an evil inflicted by usurped power, or by judges without authority from the sovereign, is not punishment but an act of hostility. The acts of a usurped power do not have the condemned person for their author, and therefore they are not acts of public authority.

Nor Pain Inflicted Without Regard to Future Good

Fifth: any evil inflicted without the intention — or even the possibility — of disposing the offender, or other men by his example, to obey the laws, is not punishment but an act of hostility. Without that end in view, no hurt done to anyone deserves the name. Punishment exists only for the correction of the offender or the guidance of others — never for revenge.

Natural Bad Consequences Are Not Punishments

Sixth: nature itself attaches harmful consequences to certain actions — as when a man assaulting another is himself killed or wounded, or when he falls sick from doing some unlawful act. With respect to God, who is the author of nature, such hurt may be said to be inflicted, and so may be called a divine punishment. But it is not contained under the name of punishment with respect to men, because it is not inflicted by the authority of man.

Hurt Less Than the Benefit of the Crime Is Not Punishment

Seventh: if the harm inflicted is less than the benefit or satisfaction that naturally follows from the crime committed, that harm does not fall within the definition. It is the price, or the purchase, of a crime rather than its punishment. Here is why: it is of the nature of punishment to have for its end the disposing of men to obey the law. If the penalty is less than the benefit of breaking the law, it does not achieve that end — it works the opposite effect. The man simply pays for his crime and counts it a bargain.

Where the Penalty Is Fixed by Law, Anything Beyond It Is Not Punishment but Hostility

Eighth: if a punishment is determined and prescribed in the law itself, and after the crime is committed a greater punishment is inflicted, the excess is not punishment but an act of hostility. For the aim of punishment is not revenge, but terror — and the terror of an unknown great punishment is taken away the moment a lesser one is declared. The unexpected addition is no part of the punishment.

But where the law fixes no punishment at all, whatever is inflicted has the nature of punishment. A man who sets about violating a law in which no penalty is specified expects an indeterminate — that is to say, an arbitrary — punishment.

Hurt Inflicted for a Deed Done Before the Law Existed Is Not Punishment

Ninth: harm inflicted for a deed done before there was any law forbidding it is not punishment, but an act of hostility. Before the law, there is no violation of the law. Punishment presupposes a deed judged to have been a violation of the law. Therefore harm inflicted for a deed done before the law was made is not punishment, but an act of hostility.

The Representative of the Commonwealth Cannot Be Punished

Tenth: hurt inflicted on the representative of the commonwealth is not punishment, but an act of hostility. It is of the nature of punishment to be inflicted by public authority — and public authority is the authority of the representative alone.

Hurt Done to Rebels Is Done by Right of War, Not by Way of Punishment

Lastly: harm inflicted on a declared enemy does not fall under the name of punishment at all. Consider why. Enemies either were never subject to the law, and therefore cannot violate it; or, having been subject to it, they profess to be subject no longer, and by consequence deny that they can violate it. So all the harms that can be done to them must be taken as acts of hostility — and in declared hostility, every infliction of evil is lawful.

From this it follows that if a subject deliberately and knowingly denies, by deed or word, the authority of the representative of the commonwealth, he may lawfully be made to suffer whatever the representative wills — whatever penalty may have been previously laid down for treason. In denying his subjection, he denies the punishment the law has ordained, and therefore he suffers as an enemy of the commonwealth: that is, according to the will of the representative. The punishments set down in the law are for subjects, not for enemies — and enemies are exactly what such men are, who, having been subjects by their own act, deliberately revolt and deny the sovereign power.

The first and most general division of punishments is into divine and human. Of divine punishment I shall have occasion to speak in a more convenient place later on.

Human punishments are those inflicted by the command of man, and they are either corporal, or pecuniary, or ignominy, or imprisonment, or exile, or a mixture of these.

Corporal Punishments

Corporal punishment is that which is inflicted on the body directly, according to the intention of the one inflicting it: lashes, or wounds, or the deprivation of bodily pleasures that were lawfully enjoyed before.

Capital Punishment

Of these, some are capital, some less than capital. Capital punishment is the infliction of death — either simply, or with torment. Less than capital are lashes, wounds, chains, and any other bodily pain not in its own nature deadly. For if death follows a punishment but was not in the intention of the one inflicting it, the punishment is not to be counted capital, even though the harm proves fatal through some accident that could not be foreseen. In that case death is not inflicted, but hastened.

Pecuniary punishment consists not only in taking away a sum of money, but also lands or any other goods that are usually bought and sold for money. And here a distinction matters. If the law that ordains such a penalty was made with the design of collecting money from those who break it, then it is not properly a punishment at all, but the price of a privilege — an exemption from the law, which does not absolutely forbid the act, but forbids it only to those who cannot afford to pay. The exception is where the law in question is a law of nature, or part of religion; in that case the payment is not an exemption from the law but a violation of it. Where a law exacts a monetary fine from those who take the name of God in vain, paying the fine is not the price of a license to swear — it is the punishment for breaking a law that admits no dispensation. Likewise, if the law requires a sum of money to be paid to the person who was injured, this is merely compensation for the hurt done to him. It cancels the accusation of the injured party, not the crime of the offender.

Ignominy

Ignominy is the infliction of some evil the commonwealth has made dishonorable, or the deprivation of some good the commonwealth has made honorable. Some things are honorable by nature — the effects of courage, magnanimity, strength, wisdom, and other abilities of body and mind. Other things are made honorable by the commonwealth — badges, titles, offices, or any other distinctive mark of the sovereign's favor. The first kind, though they may fail through nature or accident, cannot be taken away by a law, so the loss of them is not punishment. But the second kind can be taken away by the same public authority that made them honorable, and stripping them is properly punishment: degrading condemned men of their badges, titles, and offices, or declaring them incapable of holding the like in the future.

Imprisonment

Imprisonment is when a man is deprived of liberty by public authority, and it can happen for two different ends. One is the safe custody of a man who stands accused; the other is the inflicting of pain on a man who stands condemned.

The first is not punishment, because no man is supposed to be punished before he has been judicially heard and declared guilty. Therefore whatever hurt a man is made to suffer by bonds or restraint before his case is heard — over and above what is necessary to assure his custody — is against the law of nature. But the second is punishment, because it is an evil inflicted by public authority for something the same authority has judged a violation of the law.

Under the word imprisonment I include every restraint of motion caused by an external obstacle — whether it is a building, which goes by the general name of a prison; or an island, as when men are said to be confined to one; or a place where men are set to forced labor, as in ancient times men were condemned to the quarries and in our times to the galleys; or a chain, or any other such impediment.

Exile

Exile — banishment — is when a man is condemned for a crime to depart from the dominion of the commonwealth, or from a certain part of it, and not to return, either for a fixed time or forever. In its own nature, without other circumstances added, it does not seem to be a punishment at all, but rather an escape — a public command to avoid punishment by running away. Cicero says no such punishment was ever ordained in the city of Rome; he calls exile a refuge for men in danger. For if a banished man is still permitted to enjoy his goods and the income from his lands, the mere change of air is no punishment. Nor does it serve the benefit of the commonwealth for which all punishments are ordained — that is, the forming of men's wills toward observing the law. Often it works to the commonwealth's damage. A banished man is a lawful enemy of the commonwealth that banished him, being no longer a member of it. But if he is also stripped of his lands or goods, then the punishment lies not in the exile, but is to be counted among pecuniary punishments.

Punishing Innocent Subjects Is Against the Law of Nature

All punishments of innocent subjects, whether great or small, are against the law of nature. Punishment is only for violation of the law; therefore there can be no punishment of the innocent.

To punish an innocent subject is a violation, first, of that law of nature which forbids all men, in their revenges, to look at anything but some future good — for no good can come to the commonwealth by punishing the innocent. Second, of the law that forbids ingratitude: since all sovereign power is originally given by the consent of every one of the subjects so that they may be protected as long as they are obedient, the punishment of the innocent is a returning of evil for good. And third, of the law that commands equity — that is, the equal distribution of justice — which is plainly not observed in punishing the innocent.

But Harm Done to Innocents in War Is Another Matter

But inflicting any evil whatsoever on an innocent man who is not a subject — if it is done for the benefit of the commonwealth and without violating any previous covenant — is no breach of the law of nature. All men who are not subjects are either enemies, or else they have ceased to be enemies through some prior covenant. Against enemies whom the commonwealth judges capable of doing it harm, it is lawful by the original right of nature to make war. And in war the sword does not judge; the victor makes no distinction between guilty and innocent as to what is past, and shows no other mercy than what serves the good of his own people.

On this ground, vengeance against subjects who deliberately deny the authority of the established commonwealth is lawfully extended not only to the fathers, but to the third and fourth generation not yet in being — and consequently innocent of the deed for which they are afflicted. The nature of this offense consists in the renouncing of subjection, which is a relapse into the condition of war, commonly called rebellion. Those who offend in this way suffer not as subjects, but as enemies. For rebellion is simply war renewed.

Reward Is Either Salary or Grace

REWARD is either by gift or by contract. When by contract, it is called salary or wages — benefit owed for service performed or promised. When by gift, it is benefit proceeding from the grace of those who bestow it, to encourage men to serve them, or to enable men to do so.

So when the sovereign of a commonwealth attaches a salary to a public office, the man who receives it is bound in justice to perform that office. Otherwise — when there is no salary — he is bound only in honor, to acknowledgment and an effort to repay the favor. It is true that men have no lawful remedy when they are commanded to abandon their private business and serve the public without reward or salary. But they are not bound to such service by the law of nature, nor by the institution of the commonwealth, unless the service cannot be done any other way — because it is presumed the sovereign may make use of all their means. So much is this so that the most common soldier may demand the wages of his service in war as a debt.

Benefits Bestowed Out of Fear Are Not Rewards

The benefits a sovereign bestows on a subject out of fear of some power and ability that subject has to hurt the commonwealth are not properly rewards. They are not salaries, because in this case no contract is supposed — every man is already obliged not to do the commonwealth disservice. Nor are they graces, because they are extorted by fear, which ought never to touch the sovereign power. They are rather sacrifices, which the sovereign — considered in his natural person, not in the person of the commonwealth — makes to appease the discontent of someone he thinks more powerful than himself. And they encourage not obedience, but the opposite: the continuation and escalation of further extortion.

Salaries Fixed and Variable

Some salaries are fixed and proceed from the public treasury; others are uncertain and variable, proceeding from the execution of the office for which the salary is established. The second kind is in some cases harmful to the commonwealth — as in the administration of justice. For where the income of judges and the officers of a court arises from the multitude of cases brought before them, two inconveniences must follow. One is the nourishing of lawsuits, for the more suits, the greater the income. The other, which depends on the first, is contention over jurisdiction — each court drawing to itself as many cases as it can. In offices of execution these inconveniences do not arise, because such officers cannot increase their own employment by any effort of their own.

And this shall suffice for the nature of punishment and reward — which are, as it were, the nerves and tendons that move the limbs and joints of a commonwealth.

So far I have set forth the nature of man, whose pride and other passions have compelled him to submit himself to government, together with the great power of his governor — whom I compared to the Leviathan, taking that comparison from the last two verses of the forty-first chapter of Job, where God, having set forth the great power of the Leviathan, calls him King of the Proud. "There is nothing," says he, "on earth to be compared with him. He is made so as not to be afraid. He sees every high thing below him, and is king of all the children of pride." (Job 41:33-34)

But because he is mortal and subject to decay, as all other earthly creatures are; and because there is something in heaven — though not on earth — that he must stand in fear of, and whose laws he ought to obey: I shall in the next chapters speak of his diseases and the causes of his mortality, and of what laws of nature he is bound to obey.
