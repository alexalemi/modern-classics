Chapter 23: The Public Ministers of Sovereign Power

In the last chapter I spoke of the similar parts of a commonwealth — the systems and bodies within it. In this one I will speak of its organic parts: the organs and joints of the artificial man, which are its public ministers.

Who Counts as a Public Minister

A PUBLIC MINISTER is a person whom the sovereign — whether a monarch or an assembly — employs in some business, with authority to represent, in that employment, the person of the commonwealth.

Now, every man or assembly that holds sovereignty represents two persons — or, as the more common phrase puts it, has two capacities. One is natural, the other political. A monarch bears not only the person of the commonwealth but also the person of a man; a sovereign assembly bears not only the person of the commonwealth but also the person of the assembly itself.

This distinction matters, because those who serve the sovereign in his natural capacity are not public ministers. Only those who serve him in the administration of the public business are. So the ushers, sergeants, and other officers who wait on an assembly for no purpose except the convenience of the men assembled — in an aristocracy or a democracy — are not public ministers. Neither are the stewards, chamberlains, treasurers, or any other officers of a monarch's household. They serve the man, not the state.

Ministers for General Administration

Among public ministers, some are given charge of a general administration — either of the whole dominion or of a part of it.

Of the whole: as when the predecessor of an infant king commits the entire administration of the kingdom, during the boy's minority, to a Protector or Regent. In that case every subject is obliged to obey — as far as the ordinances the Protector makes and the commands he gives are issued in the king's name and are not inconsistent with the king's sovereign power.

Of a part, or province: as when a monarch or a sovereign assembly gives the general charge of some territory to a governor, lieutenant, prefect, or viceroy. Here too, everyone in that province is obliged to obey everything he does in the name of the sovereign, so long as it is not incompatible with the sovereign's right.

For such Protectors, viceroys, and governors have no right of their own at all — only what depends on the sovereign's will. And no commission given to them can ever be interpreted as a declaration of intent to transfer the sovereignty itself, unless it says so in express and unmistakable words. This kind of public minister resembles the nerves and tendons that move the various limbs of a natural body.

Ministers for Special Administration: First, the Economy

Other ministers have a special administration — charge of some particular business, either at home or abroad.

At home, first, there is the economy of the commonwealth. Those who have authority concerning the treasury — to collect, receive, pay out, or audit the tributes, taxes, rents, fines, and whatever else counts as public revenue — are public ministers. Ministers, because they serve the representative person and can do nothing against his command or without his authority. Public, because they serve him in his political capacity.

Second, those who have authority concerning the military — the custody of arms, forts, and ports; the levying, paying, or commanding of soldiers; or providing anything necessary for war by land or sea — are public ministers. But a soldier without command, though he fights for the commonwealth, does not on that account represent its person — because there is no one for him to represent it to. Everyone who holds command represents the commonwealth only to those he commands.

Ministers for the Instruction of the People

Those who have authority to teach the people their duty to the sovereign power — or to license others to teach it — and to instruct them in the knowledge of what is just and unjust, so as to make them more fit to live in godliness and in peace among themselves, and to resist the public enemy, are also public ministers. Ministers, because they do this not by their own authority but by another's. Public, because they do it — or should do it — by no authority but the sovereign's.

Only the monarch, or the sovereign assembly, has immediate authority from God to teach and instruct the people. No one but the sovereign receives his power Dei Gratia simply — that is, from the favor of God alone. Everyone else receives his from the favor and providence of God and of his sovereign: in a monarchy, Dei Gratia et Regis — "by the grace of God and the king" — or Dei Providentia et Voluntate Regis — "by God's providence and the king's will."

Ministers for Judicature

Those who are given jurisdiction are also public ministers. In their seats of justice they represent the person of the sovereign, and their sentence is his sentence. For, as I have already shown, all judicature is essentially attached to the sovereignty; every other judge is therefore only a minister of him, or them, that holds the sovereign power.

Controversies come in two kinds — controversies of fact and controversies of law — and so judgments do too: some of fact, some of law. Consequently, in the same controversy there may be two judges, one of fact and one of law.

In both kinds of controversy, a dispute may arise between the party being judged and the judge himself. Since both are subjects of the sovereign, equity requires that this dispute be judged by men agreed on by the consent of both — for no man can be a judge in his own cause. But the sovereign is already agreed on as judge by them both, so the sovereign must either hear the cause and decide it himself, or appoint as judge someone both parties agree on.

This agreement can be understood to happen in several ways. First, if the defendant is allowed to object to any judges whose interest makes him suspect them — as for the complainant, he has already chosen his own judge by bringing the case — then the judges he does not object to are judges he himself has agreed on. Second, if he appeals to some other judge, he can appeal no further; his appeal is his choice. Third, if he appeals to the sovereign himself, and the sovereign — in person, or through delegates the parties agree on — gives sentence, that sentence is final. For the defendant has been judged by his own judges; that is to say, by himself.

Having considered these properties of just and rational judicature, I cannot resist pointing out the excellent constitution of the courts of justice established in England for both Common Pleas and Public Pleas. By Common Pleas I mean those where both complainant and defendant are subjects; by Public Pleas — also called Pleas of the Crown — those where the complainant is the sovereign.

There were two orders of men: Lords and Commons. The Lords had this privilege: in all capital crimes, their judges were none but Lords — as many of them as chose to be present. Since this was always acknowledged as a privilege of favor, their judges were never anyone but those they had themselves desired. And in all controversies, every subject — and the Lords too, in civil cases — had for judges men of the county where the matter in dispute lay. Against these the accused could make his objections, until at last twelve men were agreed on without objection, and by those twelve he was judged. So, having his own judges, the party could allege nothing against the finality of the sentence.

These public persons, with authority from the sovereign power either to instruct or to judge the people, are members of the commonwealth that may fitly be compared to the organs of voice in a natural body.

Ministers for Execution

Public ministers also include all those who have authority from the sovereign to carry out the execution of judgments given; to publish the sovereign's commands; to suppress riots; to apprehend and imprison criminals; and to do other acts that tend to preserve the peace. Every act they do by such authority is the act of the commonwealth, and their service answers to that of the hands in a natural body.

Public ministers abroad are those who represent the person of their own sovereign to foreign states: ambassadors, messengers, agents, and heralds, sent by public authority and on public business.

But those sent only by the authority of some private faction of a troubled state, even if they are received, are neither public nor private ministers of the commonwealth — because none of their actions have the commonwealth for author. Likewise, an ambassador sent from one prince to another to offer congratulations or condolences, or to attend a ceremony, is a private person: the authority may be public, but the business is private, belonging to the prince in his natural capacity.

And if a man is sent secretly into another country to scout out its plans and its strength — a spy — then although both the authority and the business are public, he is only a private minister, because there is no one abroad who can take notice of any person in him except his own. Yet he is still a minister of the commonwealth, and may be compared to an eye in the natural body. And those appointed to receive the petitions and other information of the people — who are, so to speak, the public ear — are public ministers, and represent their sovereign in that office.

Counselors with No Employment but Advice Are Not Public Ministers

A counselor — or a council of state, if we consider it as having no authority of judicature or command, but only the job of giving advice to the sovereign when asked, or offering it unasked — is not a public person. The reason: the advice is addressed to the sovereign alone, and the sovereign's person cannot be represented to him by someone else in his own presence.

But a body of counselors is never without some other authority, either of judicature or of immediate administration. In a monarchy, they represent the monarch when delivering his commands to the public ministers. In a democracy, the council or senate presents the result of its deliberations to the people as a council — but when it appoints judges, or hears cases, or gives audience to ambassadors, it acts in the quality of a minister of the people. And in an aristocracy, the council of state is the sovereign assembly itself, and gives counsel to no one but itself.
