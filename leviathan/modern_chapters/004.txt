Chapter 4: Of Speech

The Origin of Speech

The invention of printing, clever as it was, is no great achievement compared with the invention of letters. Who first discovered the use of letters, nobody knows. The man said to have brought them into Greece was Cadmus, son of Agenor, king of Phoenicia. A profitable invention it was — for preserving the memory of time past, and for connecting humankind, scattered as it is across so many distant regions of the earth. And a difficult one too, since it required careful observation of the various motions of the tongue, palate, lips, and the other organs of speech, in order to create that many distinct characters to record them.

But the noblest and most profitable invention of all was SPEECH itself — names and the ways of connecting them — by which people register their thoughts, recall them after they have passed, and declare them to one another for mutual benefit and conversation. Without speech, there would have been among human beings no commonwealth, no society, no contract, no peace — no more than among lions, bears, and wolves.

The first author of speech was God himself, who instructed Adam how to name the creatures presented to his sight. Scripture goes no further than that on the subject. But that much was enough to set Adam on the path of adding more names, as experience and the use of things gave him occasion, and of joining them together, bit by bit, so as to make himself understood. In time he could have gathered as much language as he found a use for — though hardly as rich a vocabulary as an orator or philosopher needs. I find nothing in Scripture, directly or by implication, suggesting that Adam was taught the names of all figures, numbers, measures, colors, sounds, imaginings, and relations — much less the names of words and speech themselves, like general, special, affirmative, negative, interrogative, optative, infinitive, all of which are useful — and least of all words like entity, intentionality, quiddity, and the other weighty terms of the Scholastic schools.

All this language, gathered and enlarged by Adam and his descendants, was lost again at the Tower of Babel, when God struck every man, for his rebellion, with forgetfulness of his former language. Forced by this to scatter into different parts of the world, people necessarily developed, little by little, the diversity of tongues we have now — in whatever ways need, the mother of all inventions, taught them. And over the course of time, languages everywhere grew richer.

The Uses of Speech

The general use of speech is to transfer our mental discourse into verbal discourse — to turn the train of our thoughts into a train of words. We do this for two benefits.

The first is to register the consequences of our thoughts. Thoughts tend to slip out of memory and put us to fresh labor; but they can be recalled again by the words we marked them with. So the first use of names is to serve as MARKS, or notes of remembrance.

The second comes when many people use the same words: then, by the connection and order of those words, they can signal to one another what they conceive or think about each matter, and also what they desire, fear, or feel. Used this way, words are called SIGNS.

The special uses of speech are these. First, to record what we discover by thinking to be the cause of anything, present or past, and what effects present or past things may produce — which, in sum, is the acquiring of arts and sciences. Second, to show others the knowledge we have attained — which is to counsel and teach one another. Third, to make our wills and purposes known to others, so that we may have one another's mutual help. Fourth, to please and delight ourselves and others by playing with our words, innocently, for pleasure or ornament.

The Abuses of Speech

To these four uses there correspond four abuses. First, when people register their thoughts wrongly because the meanings of their words keep shifting — so they record, as their conceptions, things they never actually conceived, and deceive themselves. Second, when they use words metaphorically — that is, in some sense other than the one the words were established for — and thereby deceive others. Third, when by words they declare something to be their will which is not. Fourth, when they use words to wound one another. Nature has armed living creatures — some with teeth, some with horns, some with hands — to hurt an enemy; so it is simply an abuse of speech to wound someone with the tongue. The one exception is a person we are obligated to govern; and then it is not wounding, but correcting and improving.

The way speech serves the remembrance of causes and effects consists in the imposing of names, and in the connecting of them.

Proper Names, Common Names, and Universals

Of names, some are PROPER, singular to one thing only — Peter, John, this man, this tree. And some are COMMON to many things — man, horse, tree. Each of these, though only one name, is nevertheless the name of many different particular things; and with respect to all of them taken together, it is called a UNIVERSAL. There is nothing in the world universal except names; for every one of the things named is individual and singular.

One universal name is imposed on many things because of their similarity in some quality or other feature. And whereas a proper name brings to mind one thing only, a universal recalls any one of those many.

Among universal names, some are of wider and some of narrower extent — the larger ones containing the smaller — and some are of equal extent, each containing the other. For example, the name body has a wider signification than the word man, and contains it; while the names man and rational are of equal extent, each comprehending the other. And we should notice here that by a "name" we do not always mean a single word, as in grammar; sometimes a name is a roundabout phrase of many words. All these words — he that in his actions observes the laws of his country — make up just one name, equivalent to the single word just.

By this imposing of names, some of wider and some of narrower signification, we turn the reckoning of consequences among things imagined in the mind into a reckoning of consequences among names. Take, for example, a man with no use of speech at all — someone born and remaining entirely deaf and mute. If you set a triangle before his eyes, with two right angles beside it (like the corners of a square), he may, by working it through, compare them and find that the three angles of that triangle equal the two right angles standing next to it. But show him another triangle, different in shape from the first, and he cannot know without fresh labor whether its three angles also equal the same.

A man who has the use of words is in a different position. When he observes that this equality of angles followed not from the length of the sides, nor from anything else particular to his triangle, but only from the sides being straight and the angles being three — and that this was all he meant by calling it a triangle — he will boldly conclude, universally, that this equality of angles holds in all triangles whatsoever, and will register his discovery in these general terms: every triangle has its three angles equal to two right angles. And so a consequence found in one particular case comes to be registered and remembered as a universal rule. It frees our mental reckoning from time and place; it spares us all labor of the mind except the first; and it makes what was found true here and now, true in all times and places.

Nowhere is the use of words in registering our thoughts so obvious as in numbering. A born fool who could never learn by heart the order of the number words — one, two, three — may notice every stroke of the clock, and nod along with it, or say one, one, one, but he can never know what hour it strikes. And it seems there was a time when those number names were not in use, and people had to apply the fingers of one or both hands to whatever they wanted to keep count of. That, it appears, is why our number words run to ten in every nation — and in some only to five — before beginning again. And someone who can count to ten, if he recites the numbers out of order, will lose himself and not know when he is done; much less will he be able to add and subtract and perform the other operations of arithmetic. So without words there is no possibility of reckoning numbers — much less of reckoning magnitudes, speeds, forces, and the other things whose calculation is necessary for humankind's existence and well-being.

When two names are joined together into a consequence, or affirmation — for instance, a man is a living creature, or if he is a man, he is a living creature — then if the second name, living creature, signifies everything the first name, man, signifies, the affirmation is TRUE; otherwise it is FALSE. For true and false are attributes of speech, not of things. Where there is no speech, there is neither truth nor falsehood. There may be ERROR — as when we expect something that will not happen, or suspect something that never did — but in neither case can a person be charged with untruth.

Since truth, then, consists in the right ordering of names in our affirmations, anyone who seeks precise truth must remember what every name he uses stands for, and place it accordingly. Otherwise he will find himself entangled in words like a bird in lime twigs: the more he struggles, the more stuck he becomes. That is why in geometry — the only science it has pleased God to bestow on mankind so far — men begin by settling the meanings of their words. This settling of meanings they call DEFINITIONS, and they place them at the beginning of their reckoning.

From this it is clear how necessary it is, for anyone who aspires to true knowledge, to examine the definitions of earlier authors — and either to correct them where they are carelessly set down, or to make his own. For the errors of definitions multiply as the reckoning proceeds, and lead men into absurdities which they eventually see but cannot escape without reckoning anew from the beginning, where the foundation of their errors lies. This is what happens to people who trust books: they do as those who add up many small sums into a larger one without checking whether the small sums were added correctly in the first place. When at last the error becomes visible, and they never think to distrust their starting points, they have no idea how to clear themselves. They waste their time fluttering over their books — like birds that came in by the chimney and, finding themselves shut in a room, flutter against the false light of a glass window, lacking the wit to consider how they got in.

So the first use of speech lies in the right definition of names, which is the acquisition of science. And the first abuse lies in wrong definitions, or in none at all; from this come all false and senseless doctrines. These make the men who take their instruction from the authority of books, rather than from their own thinking, as far below the condition of ordinary ignorant men as men equipped with true science are above it. For between true science and erroneous doctrine, ignorance sits in the middle. Natural sense and imagination are not subject to absurdity. Nature itself cannot err. And as people grow richer in language, they become wiser — or madder — than ordinary. Nor is it possible, without letters, for anyone to become either exceptionally wise or (unless his memory is damaged by disease or some defect of the organs) exceptionally foolish. For words are wise men's counters — they use them to calculate with, nothing more. But words are the money of fools, who value them by the authority of an Aristotle, a Cicero, a Thomas Aquinas, or any other doctor whatsoever — who was, after all, only a man.

What Can Be Named

Subject to names is whatever can enter into, or be considered in, an account — whatever can be added to something else to make a sum, or subtracted from it to leave a remainder. The Romans called accounts of money rationes, and accounting ratiocinatio; and what we call items in our bills and account books, they called nomina — that is, names. From this, it seems, they extended the word ratio to the faculty of reckoning in everything else. The Greeks have just one word, logos, for both speech and reason — not because they thought there was no speech without reason, but because there is no reasoning without speech. And the act of reasoning they called syllogism, which means summing up the consequences of one statement with another.

Because the same things can enter into an account for different features, their names are variously twisted and varied to show that diversity. This diversity of names can be reduced to four general headings.

First, a thing may enter into account as matter, or body — as living, sensible, rational, hot, cold, moved, at rest. With all such names, the word matter or body is understood; all these are names of matter.

Second, a thing may enter into account, or be considered, for some accident or quality we conceive to be in it — for being moved, for being so long, for being hot, and so on. Then, from the name of the thing itself, by a small change or twist, we make a name for the accident we are considering: for living we put into the account life; for moved, motion; for hot, heat; for long, length; and the like. All such names are names of the accidents and properties by which one body is distinguished from another. These are called ABSTRACT names — because they are severed, not from matter, but from the account of matter.

Third, we bring into account the properties of our own bodies, by which we make distinctions of another kind. When something is seen by us, we reckon not the thing itself but the sight, the color, the idea of it in the imagination. When something is heard, we reckon not the thing but only the hearing, or the sound, which is our imagination or conception of it through the ear. Such names are names of fancies — of our own imaginings.

Fourth, we bring into account, consider, and give names to names themselves, and to forms of speech. General, universal, special, equivocal are names of names. And affirmation, interrogation, command, narration, syllogism, sermon, oration, and many other such words are names of speeches.

Positive Names

And this is the whole variety of names POSITIVE — names put down to mark something that exists in nature, or that the human mind can feign: bodies that are, or may be conceived to be; or the properties of bodies that are, or may be feigned to be; or words and speech.

Negative Names and Their Uses

There are also other names, called NEGATIVE, which are notes signifying that a given word is not the name of the thing in question — words like nothing, no man, infinite, unteachable, three lacking four, and the like. These are nevertheless useful in reckoning, or in correcting a reckoning, and they call our past thoughts to mind, even though they are not the names of any thing — because they make us refuse to accept names that are not rightly used.

Meaningless Words

All other names are nothing but insignificant sounds, and they come in two sorts. The first sort: words that are new, and whose meaning has never been explained by any definition. The Scholastics and puzzled philosophers have coined these in abundance.

The second sort: when men weld together two names whose significations are contradictory and inconsistent — like the name an incorporeal body, or (which comes to the same thing) an incorporeal substance, and a great many more. For whenever an affirmation is false, the two names it is composed of, put together and made into one, signify nothing at all. For example, if it is a false affirmation to say a quadrangle is round, then the phrase round quadrangle signifies nothing; it is a mere sound. Likewise, if it is false to say that virtue can be poured, or blown up and down, then the phrases in-poured virtue and in-blown virtue are as absurd and meaningless as a round quadrangle. And so you will rarely meet a senseless, insignificant word that is not built out of some Latin or Greek names. A Frenchman seldom hears our Savior called by the name Parole, but often by the name Verbe — yet Verbe and Parole differ in nothing except that one is Latin and the other French.

Understanding

When a man, on hearing any speech, has the very thoughts which the words of that speech and their connection were established and arranged to signify, then he is said to UNDERSTAND it — understanding being nothing else but conception caused by speech. And therefore, if speech is peculiar to man (and as far as I know, it is), then understanding is peculiar to him as well. It follows that of absurd and false affirmations — when they are universal — there can be no understanding at all; though many people think they understand such statements, when all they are really doing is repeating the words softly to themselves, or turning them over in their minds.

What kinds of speech signify the desires, aversions, and passions of the human mind, and how those speeches are used and abused, I will discuss after I have dealt with the passions.

Unstable Names

The names of things that affect us — that is, things that please and displease us — have, in everyday conversation, an INCONSTANT signification, because not everyone is affected the same way by the same thing, nor is the same person affected the same way at all times. Since all names are imposed to signify our conceptions, and all our feelings are themselves conceptions, then when we conceive the same things differently, we can hardly avoid naming them differently. Even though the nature of the thing we conceive is the same, the diversity of our reception of it — owing to our different bodily constitutions and our prejudices of opinion — gives everything a tint of our different passions.

In reasoning, therefore, a man must be careful of words which, beyond signifying what we imagine of the thing's nature, also carry the disposition and interest of the speaker. The names of virtues and vices are like this. One man calls wisdom what another calls fear; one calls cruelty what another calls justice; one calls extravagance what another calls generosity; one calls seriousness what another calls stupidity; and so on. Names like these can never be true grounds for any reasoning. Neither can metaphors and figures of speech — but those are less dangerous, because they openly profess their inconstancy. The others do not.
