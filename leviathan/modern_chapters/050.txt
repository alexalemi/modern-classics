Chapter 44: Spiritual Darkness from Misinterpretation of Scripture
(Part 2 of 2)


Other Texts Abused to Defend the Pope's Power

Besides Bellarmine's arguments, there are some other texts pressed into service to prove the Pope's power over civil sovereigns. For instance: the two swords that Christ and his apostles had among them are said to be the spiritual sword and the temporal sword, both supposedly given to St. Peter by Christ. And the two great lights in the sky? The greater one signifies the Pope, they say, and the lesser one the king.

By that method of reading, one might just as well conclude from the first verse of the Bible that "heaven" means the Pope and "earth" means the king. This is not arguing from Scripture. It is wanton insult flung at princes — a fashion that came in after the Popes had grown so secure in their greatness that they could treat all Christian kings with contempt. Treading on the necks of emperors, they mocked both the emperors and the Scripture at once, using the words of Psalm 91: "Thou shalt tread upon the lion and the adder; the young lion and the dragon thou shalt trample under thy feet."


Consecration in Scripture Used No Exorcisms

As for the rites of consecration: these depend, for the most part, on the discretion and judgment of the governors of the Church, not on Scripture. But even so, those governors are bound by one obvious standard — the nature of the action itself. The ceremonies, words, and gestures must be decent and meaningful, or at least appropriate to what is being done.

Look at how it was actually done in Scripture. When Moses consecrated the tabernacle, the altar, and their vessels (Exodus 40), he anointed them with the oil God had commanded to be made for that purpose — and they were holy. Nothing was exorcised. No phantasms were driven away. The same Moses — the civil sovereign of Israel — when he consecrated Aaron the high priest and his sons, washed them with water (not exorcised water), put their garments on them, and anointed them with oil. And they were sanctified to minister to the Lord in the priest's office. It was a simple, decent cleansing and adorning of them before he presented them to God to be his servants.

When King Solomon — the civil sovereign of Israel — consecrated the temple he had built (2 Kings 8), he stood before the whole congregation of Israel. He blessed them. He gave thanks to God for putting it into his father's heart to build the temple, and for giving him the grace to finish it. Then he prayed: first, that God would accept that house, though it was not remotely adequate to his infinite greatness; second, that God would hear the prayers of his servants who prayed in it, or — if they were far away — toward it. And last, he offered a sacrifice of peace offerings, and the house was dedicated.

Notice what was missing. No procession — the king stood still in his place. No exorcised water. No Asperges me — "sprinkle me," words lifted from a psalm and applied where they do not belong — nor any other irrelevant recycling of words spoken on some other occasion. Just a decent, rational speech, exactly suited to the occasion of presenting God with his newly built house.

We never read that St. John exorcised the water of the Jordan, or that Philip exorcised the river where he baptized the eunuch. Nor did any pastor in the apostles' time take his own spittle, put it to the nose of the person being baptized, and say In odorem suavitatis — "for a sweet savor unto the Lord." No human authority can justify that ceremony: not the spittle, which is unclean; not the application of that Scripture, which is frivolous.


The Soul's Immortality Comes from Grace, Not Nature

Now to the foundation under all of this. Some maintain that the soul, separated from the body, lives eternally — and not only the souls of the elect, by special grace, by the restoration of the eternal life Adam lost through sin and our Savior bought back for the faithful by the sacrifice of himself. No: they claim the souls of the reprobate live forever too, as a property that naturally belongs to the essence of mankind, requiring no grace of God beyond what is given universally to everyone.

There are various passages that at first glance seem to support this. But when I compare them with the passage from Job 14 that I cited back in chapter 38, they seem to me far more open to a different interpretation than the words of Job are.

Take them in order. First, the words of Solomon (Ecclesiastes 12:7): "Then shall the dust return to dust, as it was, and the spirit shall return to God that gave it." If no other text stood directly against it, this could perfectly well mean that God alone knows — and man does not — what becomes of a man's spirit when he dies. And the same Solomon, in the same book (Ecclesiastes 3:20-21), delivers the same thought in exactly that sense: "All go (man and beast) to the same place; all are of the dust, and all turn to dust again; who knoweth that the spirit of man goeth upward, and the spirit of the beast goeth downward to the earth?" That is: nobody knows but God. And it is hardly an unusual way of speaking — we still say of things we do not understand, "God knows what" and "God knows where."

Then there is Genesis 5:24: "Enoch walked with God, and he was not; for God took him." The epistle to the Hebrews expounds it: "He was translated, that he should not die; and was not found, because God had translated him. For before his translation, he had this testimony, that he pleased God." Notice that this passage argues for the immortality of the body just as much as of the soul. And what it actually proves is that this translation was peculiar to those who please God — not common to them and the wicked alike — and that it depended on grace, not on nature.

But on the other side, what interpretation can we possibly give — other than the literal one — to Solomon's words in Ecclesiastes 3:19? "That which befalleth the sons of men, befalleth beasts, even one thing befalleth them; as the one dieth, so doth the other; yea, they have all one breath (one spirit); so that a man hath no preeminence above a beast, for all is vanity." Taken literally, there is no natural immortality of the soul here — and yet nothing that contradicts the eternal life the elect shall enjoy by grace. And again (Ecclesiastes 4:3): "Better is he that hath not yet been, than both they" — better, that is, than those who live or have lived. If the souls of all who have ever lived were immortal, that would be a hard saying, for then having an immortal soul would be worse than having no soul at all. And again (Ecclesiastes 9:5): "The living know they shall die, but the dead know not any thing" — that is, naturally, and before the resurrection of the body.

Another passage that seems to support a natural immortality of the soul is where our Savior says that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob are living. But this is said of the promise of God, and of their certainty of rising again — not of a life then actual. It is in the same sense that God told Adam that on the day he ate the forbidden fruit he would certainly die: from that moment forward Adam was a dead man by sentence, though not by execution until almost a thousand years later. So Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob were alive by promise when Christ spoke, but they are not actually alive until the resurrection. And the story of Dives and Lazarus makes nothing against this, if we take it — as it is — for a parable.

But there are other places in the New Testament where an immortality seems to be attributed directly to the wicked. It is evident that they shall all rise to judgment. And it is said in many places that they shall go into "everlasting fire, everlasting torments, everlasting punishments," and that "the worm of conscience never dieth." All of this gets bundled into the phrase "everlasting death" — which is then ordinarily interpreted as everlasting life in torments. And yet I can find nowhere in Scripture that any man shall live in torments everlastingly.

It also seems hard to say that God — the Father of mercies; who does in heaven and earth all that he wills; who has the hearts of all men at his disposal; who works in men both the doing and the willing; without whose free gift a man has neither any inclination to good nor any repentance of evil — should punish men's transgressions without any end of time, and with every extremity of torture that men can imagine, and more. So we must consider what "everlasting fire" and the other phrases like it actually mean.

I have shown already that the kingdom of God by Christ begins at the day of judgment. That on that day the faithful shall rise again with glorious and spiritual bodies, and be his subjects in that kingdom, which shall be eternal. That they shall neither marry nor be given in marriage, nor eat and drink as they did in their natural bodies, but live forever in their individual persons, with no need for the eternity-of-the-species that comes by reproduction. That the reprobate also shall rise again, to receive punishment for their sins. And that those of the elect still alive in their earthly bodies on that day shall have their bodies suddenly changed and made spiritual and immortal.

But here is what Scripture nowhere says: that the bodies of the reprobate — who make up the kingdom of Satan — shall also be glorious or spiritual bodies; or that they shall be like the angels of God, neither eating, nor drinking, nor reproducing; or that their life shall be eternal in their individual persons, the way the life of every faithful man is, or the way Adam's life would have been had he not sinned. There is no passage of Scripture to prove any of it — except those passages about eternal torments, which can be interpreted otherwise.

From this we may infer the symmetry of the whole matter. As the elect, after the resurrection, shall be restored to the state Adam was in before he sinned, so the reprobate shall be in the state Adam and his posterity were in after the sin was committed — with this difference: God promised a Redeemer to Adam, and to those of his seed who should trust in him and repent, but not to those who die in their sins, as the reprobate do.


What "Eternal Torments" Means

With all that on the table, the texts that mention eternal fire, eternal torments, or the worm that never dies do not contradict the doctrine of a second and everlasting death, taking the word "death" in its proper and natural sense.

The fire or torments prepared for the wicked — in Gehenna, in Tophet, or wherever — may continue forever. And there may never be a shortage of wicked men to be tormented in them. But that does not mean every wicked man, or any one wicked man, is tormented eternally. For the wicked, being left in the state they were in after Adam's sin, may at the resurrection live as they lived before: marry, give in marriage, have gross and corruptible bodies as all mankind now has — and consequently may reproduce perpetually after the resurrection, as they did before. There is no passage of Scripture against it.

For when St. Paul speaks of the resurrection (1 Corinthians 15), he means only the resurrection to eternal life, not the resurrection to punishment. Of the first he says that the body is "sown in corruption, raised in incorruption; sown in dishonor, raised in honor; sown in weakness, raised in power; sown a natural body, raised a spiritual body." Nothing of the sort can be said of the bodies of those who rise to punishment.

The key text is Luke 20:34-36 — a fertile text. "The children of this world marry, and are given in marriage; but they that shall be counted worthy to obtain that world, and the resurrection from the dead, neither marry, nor are given in marriage: neither can they die any more; for they are equal to the angels, and are the children of God, being the children of the resurrection."

Read it closely. The children of this world — those left in the state Adam left them in — shall marry and be given in marriage; that is, they shall be corruptible and generate one generation after another. That is an immortality of the kind, but not of the persons of men. They are not counted worthy to obtain the next world and an absolute resurrection from the dead; they get only a short time as lodgers in that world, and only in order to receive the punishment their defiance has earned. The elect are the only children of the resurrection — that is, the sole heirs of eternal life. They alone can die no more. It is they who are equal to the angels and are the children of God — not the reprobate.

For the reprobate there remains, after the resurrection, a second and eternal death. Between their resurrection and their second and eternal death there is only a time of punishment and torment — a fire kept burning by the succession of sinners coming into it, lasting as long as mankind endures by propagation, which is eternally.


The Texts Alleged for Purgatory, Answered

On this doctrine of the natural eternity of separated souls is founded, as I said, the doctrine of purgatory. For if eternal life comes by grace only, then there is no life but the life of the body, and no immortality until the resurrection — and purgatory has nowhere to exist and no one to hold.

So let us examine the texts Bellarmine alleges for purgatory, starting with those from the canonical Scripture of the Old Testament.

First, the fasting of David for Saul and Jonathan (2 Kings 1:12), and again for the death of Abner (2 Samuel 3:35). This fasting of David, Bellarmine says, was to obtain something for them at God's hands after their death — because when David fasted to procure the recovery of his own child, he called for food the moment he learned the child was dead. The argument runs: the soul has an existence separate from the body; nothing can be obtained by fasting for souls already in heaven or in hell; therefore there must be some souls of dead men that are neither in heaven nor in hell; therefore they must be in some third place — which must be purgatory.

That is hard straining. He has wrested these passages into a proof of purgatory when the real explanation is plain. The ceremonies of mourning and fasting, when used for the death of men whose life was of no profit to the mourners, are used out of honor to their persons. When done for the death of men by whose life the mourners benefited, they proceed from the mourners' own particular loss. So David honored Saul and Abner with his fasting; and at the death of his own child, he comforted himself by taking his ordinary food.

As for the other places he alleges out of the Old Testament, there is not so much as a show or shadow of proof in them. His method is simple: he brings in every text containing the word "anger," or "fire," or "burning," or "purging," or "cleansing," provided any of the Fathers ever rhetorically applied it in a sermon to the doctrine of purgatory — a doctrine already believed before the text was consulted. Take the first verse of Psalm 37: "O Lord, rebuke me not in thy wrath, nor chasten me in thy hot displeasure." What would this have to do with purgatory, if Augustine had not assigned the "wrath" to the fire of hell and the "displeasure" to the fire of purgatory? And what does Psalm 66:12 have to do with purgatory — "We went through fire and water, and thou broughtest us to a moist place"? These and other texts like them — which the doctors of those times reached for to decorate or stretch out their sermons and commentaries — have been hauled to their purposes by sheer force of wit.


The New Testament Passages for Purgatory, Answered

But Bellarmine alleges other places, from the New Testament, that are not so easy to answer.

The first is Matthew 12:32: "Whosoever speaketh a word against the Son of man, it shall be forgiven him; but whosoever speaketh against the Holy Ghost, it shall not be forgiven him, neither in this world, nor in the world to come." Here he insists that "the world to come" is purgatory — a place where some sins may be forgiven that were not forgiven in this world.

But it is manifest that there are only three worlds. One, from the creation to the flood, destroyed by water, and called in Scripture the old world. Another, from the flood to the day of judgment, which is the present world, and shall be destroyed by fire. And the third, from the day of judgment forward, everlasting, which is called the world to come — and in which, by everyone's agreement, there shall be no purgatory. So "the world to come" and purgatory are inconsistent. The very text he builds on excludes the thing he builds.

What, then, can our Savior's words mean? I confess they are very hard to reconcile with all the doctrines now unanimously received. And it is no shame to confess that the depth of Scripture is too great to be sounded by the shortness of human understanding. Nevertheless, I can propose, for the consideration of more learned divines, what the text itself suggests.

First: since to speak against the Holy Ghost — the third person of the Trinity — is to speak against the Church, in which the Holy Ghost resides, it seems the comparison is between the gentleness of our Savior in bearing with offenses done to him while he was on earth, and the severity of the pastors after him toward those who would deny their authority, which came from the Holy Ghost. As if he had said: You that deny my power — even you that shall crucify me — shall be pardoned by me as often as you turn to me in repentance. But if you deny the power of those who teach you hereafter by virtue of the Holy Ghost, they shall be inexorable. They shall not forgive you. They shall persecute you in this world and leave you without absolution (though you turn to me, unless you turn to them as well), consigned — as far as lies in their power — to the punishments of the world to come. On this reading, the words may be taken as a prophecy, a prediction of the times — and the history of the Christian Church has borne it out.

Or, if that is not the meaning (for I am not dogmatic about such difficult places), perhaps there may be room left, after the resurrection, for the repentance of some sinners. There is another passage that seems to agree with this. Considering the words of St. Paul (1 Corinthians 15:29) — "What shall they do which are baptized for the dead, if the dead rise not at all? why also are they baptized for the dead?" — a man may plausibly infer, as some have, that in St. Paul's time there was a custom of receiving baptism for the dead: standing in for the persons of one's deceased friends — much as believers today are sureties and undertakers for the faith of infants who are not yet capable of believing — and undertaking that they should be ready to obey and receive our Savior as their king at his coming again. If so, then the forgiveness of sins in the world to come has no need of a purgatory.

But there is so much paradox in both of these interpretations that I do not trust them. I propose them to those thoroughly versed in the Scripture, to inquire whether some clearer passage contradicts them. This much, however, I do see in evident Scripture: there is neither the word "purgatory" nor the thing itself, not in this text nor in any other. Nor is there anything that can prove a necessity of a place for the soul without the body — not for the soul of Lazarus during the four days he was dead, nor for the souls the Roman Church claims are being tormented in purgatory right now. For God, who could give life to a piece of clay, has the same power to give life again to a dead man, and to renew his lifeless, rotten carcass into a glorious, spiritual, and immortal body.

Another place is 1 Corinthians 3, where it is said that those who build stubble, hay, and the like on the true foundation shall see their work perish, but "they themselves shall be saved; yet so as by fire." This fire, Bellarmine will have to be the fire of purgatory. But the words, as I have said before, are an allusion to Zechariah 13:9: "I will bring the third part through the fire, and refine them as silver is refined, and will try them as gold is tried" — which is spoken of the coming of the Messiah in power and glory, that is, at the day of judgment and the conflagration of the present world. In that fire the elect shall not be consumed but refined: they shall shed their erroneous doctrines and traditions, have them singed off, as it were, and afterward call on the name of the true God. In the same way the Apostle says of those who hold the foundation — Jesus is the Christ — but build erroneous doctrines on it, that they shall not be consumed in the fire that renews the world, but shall pass through it to salvation: yet in such a way as to see and abandon their former errors. The builders are the pastors; the foundation, that Jesus is the Christ; the stubble and hay, the false conclusions drawn from it through ignorance or frailty; the gold, silver, and precious stones are their true doctrines; and the refining or purging is the abandoning of their errors. In all of this there is no shadow of support for the burning of incorporeal — that is to say, impassible — souls: souls incapable of suffering anything at all.


How "Baptism for the Dead" Should Be Understood

A third place is that of 1 Corinthians 15, already mentioned, concerning baptism for the dead. From it Bellarmine concludes, first, that prayers for the dead are not unprofitable, and from that, that there is a fire of purgatory. Neither conclusion follows.

Of the many interpretations of the word "baptism," he ranks first the one that takes baptism (metaphorically) as a baptism of penance — so that men are "baptized" when they fast, and pray, and give alms; and thus baptism for the dead and prayer for the dead are the same thing. But this is a metaphor without one single example, either in Scripture or in any other use of language — and it clashes with the harmony and drift of Scripture besides. The word "baptism" is used (Mark 10:38; Luke 12:59) for being dipped in one's own blood, as Christ was on the cross and as most of the apostles were in bearing witness to him. But it is hard to say that prayer, fasting, and alms bear any resemblance to dipping. The word is also used (Matthew 3:11) — and this might seem to do something for purgatory — for a purging with fire. But it is evident that the fire and purging mentioned there are the same the prophet Zechariah speaks of (Zechariah 13:9): "I will bring the third part through the fire, and will refine them," and so on. And St. Peter after him (1 Peter 1:7): "That the trial of your faith, which is much more precious than of gold that perisheth, though it be tried with fire, might be found unto praise, and honour, and glory at the appearing of Jesus Christ." And St. Paul (1 Corinthians 3:13): "The fire shall try every man's work, of what sort it is." But St. Peter and St. Paul speak of the fire that shall come at the second appearing of Christ, and the prophet Zechariah of the day of judgment. So the place in St. Matthew may be interpreted the same way — and then there is no necessity for the fire of purgatory.

The other interpretation of baptism for the dead is the one I mentioned above, which Bellarmine ranks second in probability — and from it he likewise infers the usefulness of prayer for the dead. For if, after the resurrection, those who never heard of Christ or never believed in him may be received into Christ's kingdom, then it is not in vain for their friends to pray for them after their death, until they should rise. Grant all of it. Grant that God, at the prayers of the faithful, may convert to himself some of those who never heard Christ preached and so cannot have rejected him; grant that men's charity in that point cannot be blamed. It still concludes nothing for purgatory. Because to rise from death to life is one thing; to rise from purgatory to life is quite another — that would be a rising from life to life, from a life in torments to a life in joy.

A fourth place is Matthew 5:25: "Agree with thine adversary quickly, whilest thou art in the way with him, lest at any time the adversary deliver thee to the officer, and thou be cast into prison. Verily I say unto thee, thou shalt by no means come out thence, till thou hast paid the uttermost farthing." In this allegory, the offender is the sinner; both the adversary and the judge are God; the way is this life; the prison is the grave; the officer is death. And from that prison the sinner shall not rise again to eternal life, but to a second death — unless the uttermost farthing is paid, or Christ pays it for him by his passion, which is a full ransom for every kind of sin, lesser sins and greater crimes alike, both made equally forgivable by the passion of Christ. No purgatory: a grave, and a ransom.

The fifth place is Matthew 5:22: "Whosoever is angry with his brother without a cause, shall be guilty in judgment. And whosoever shall say to his brother, Racha" — an Aramaic insult, roughly "worthless" — "shall be guilty in the council. But whosoever shall say, Thou fool, shall be guilty to hell fire." From these words Bellarmine infers three sorts of sins and three sorts of punishments, with only the last punished by hellfire — and consequently, that after this life there is punishment of lesser sins in purgatory.

There is no shadow of support for that inference in any interpretation ever given of these words. Shall there be, after this life, a system of separate courts of justice, like the ones the Jews had in our Savior's time to hear and determine different sorts of crimes — the judges here, the council there? Shall not all judgment belong to Christ and his apostles? To understand the text, we must not read it in isolation but together with the words before and after it. In this chapter our Savior is interpreting the law of Moses, which the Jews thought they had fulfilled so long as they had not violated its grammatical sense — however thoroughly they had violated the intent of the Legislator. They thought the sixth commandment was broken only by killing a man, and the seventh only when a man lay with a woman not his wife. So our Savior tells them: the inward anger of a man against his brother, if it is without just cause, is homicide. You have heard the law of Moses — "Thou shalt not kill," and "Whosoever shall kill shall be condemned before the judges," or before the session of the seventy. But I say to you: to be angry with one's brother without cause, or to call him Racha, or fool, is homicide — and shall be punished at the day of judgment, at the session of Christ and his apostles, with hellfire.

So the words were never meant to distinguish between different crimes, different courts, and different punishments. They were meant to rebuke the Jews' way of ranking sin against sin — drawn not from the difference of the will in obeying God, but from the difference of their temporal courts — and to show them that whoever has the will to hurt his brother, even if the effect goes no further than an insult, or never shows at all, shall be cast into hellfire by the judges and the session, which at the day of judgment shall be one and the same court, not different ones. With all this considered, what can be drawn from this text to support purgatory, I cannot imagine.

The sixth place is Luke 16:9: "Make ye friends of the unrighteous Mammon, that when ye fail, they may receive you into everlasting tabernacles." This he alleges to prove the invocation of departed saints. But the sense is plain: we should use our riches to make friends of the poor, and thereby obtain their prayers while they live. "He that giveth to the poor, lendeth to the Lord."

The seventh is Luke 23:42: "Lord, remember me when thou comest into thy kingdom." Therefore, says Bellarmine, there is remission of sins after this life. But the inference does not hold. Our Savior forgave him then and there; and at his coming again in glory, he will remember to raise him again to eternal life.

The eighth is Acts 2:24, where St. Peter says of Christ "that God had raised him up, and loosed the pains of death, because it was not possible he should be holden of it." This he interprets as a descent of Christ into purgatory, to loose some souls there from their torments. But it is manifest that it was Christ who was loosed; it was Christ who could not be held by death, or the grave — not the souls in purgatory. And if what Beza says in his notes on this place is well observed — that instead of "pains" the word should be "bands" — then there is no further reason to go hunting for purgatory in this text at all.
