Chapter 14: The First and Second Laws of Nature, and Contracts


What the Right of Nature Is

The RIGHT OF NATURE, which writers commonly call jus naturale — the natural right — is the liberty each man has to use his own power, as he himself wills, for the preservation of his own nature; that is to say, of his own life. And consequently, it is the liberty of doing anything which, in his own judgment and reason, he conceives to be the aptest means to that end.


What Liberty Is

By LIBERTY is understood, according to the proper meaning of the word, the absence of external impediments. Such impediments may often take away part of a man's power to do what he would; but they cannot prevent him from using the power left to him according to what his judgment and reason dictate.


What a Law of Nature Is

A LAW OF NATURE (lex naturalis — natural law) is a precept, or general rule, discovered by reason, which forbids a man to do anything that is destructive of his life, or that takes away his means of preserving it; and forbids him to neglect anything by which he thinks his life may best be preserved.

Those who write on this subject tend to confuse jus and lex — right and law. But the two ought to be distinguished. A RIGHT consists in the liberty to do something or to hold back from it; whereas a LAW determines the matter and binds you to one or the other. So law and right differ as much as obligation and liberty — which, in one and the same matter, cannot coexist.


By Nature, Every Man Has a Right to Everything

Now, the condition of man, as I declared in the previous chapter, is a condition of war of every one against every one. In that condition, every man is governed by his own reason, and there is nothing he might use that could not be a help to him in preserving his life against his enemies. It follows that in such a condition, every man has a right to everything — even to one another's body. And therefore, as long as this natural right of every man to everything endures, no man — however strong or wise he may be — can have any security of living out the time that nature ordinarily allows men to live.


The Fundamental Law of Nature

And consequently it is a precept, a general rule of reason: that every man ought to seek peace, as far as he has hope of obtaining it; and when he cannot obtain it, that he may seek and use all the helps and advantages of war.

The first branch of this rule contains the first and fundamental law of nature, which is: seek peace, and follow it. The second branch contains the sum of the right of nature, which is: by all means we can, defend ourselves.


The Second Law of Nature

From this fundamental law of nature, which commands men to seek peace, this second law is derived: that a man be willing, when others are willing too, to lay down this right to all things — as far as he thinks it necessary for peace and for his own defense — and be contented with as much liberty against other men as he would allow other men against himself.

For as long as every man holds on to this right of doing anything he likes, all men remain in the condition of war. But if other men will not lay down their right along with him, then there is no reason for anyone to strip himself of his own. That would be to offer himself up as prey — which no man is bound to do — rather than to dispose himself to peace.

This is that law of the Gospel: whatever you require that others should do to you, do that to them. And it is that law of all men: quod tibi fieri non vis, alteri ne feceris — what you do not want done to you, do not do to another.


What It Means to Lay Down a Right

To lay down a man's right to anything is to strip himself of the liberty of hindering another from the benefit of that other man's own right to the same thing. For he who renounces or passes away his right does not give any other man a right which that man did not have before — because there is nothing to which every man did not already have a right by nature. He merely stands out of the way, so that the other may enjoy his own original right without hindrance from him — though not without hindrance from everyone else. So the benefit that comes to one man from another man's giving up his right is simply this much: fewer obstacles to the use of the right he had all along.


Renouncing and Transferring a Right; Obligation, Duty, Injustice

A right is laid aside either by simply renouncing it, or by transferring it to another. By simply RENOUNCING it, when the man does not care who receives the benefit of it. By TRANSFERRING it, when he intends the benefit for some particular person or persons.

And when a man has in either way abandoned or granted away his right, he is then said to be OBLIGED, or BOUND, not to hinder those to whom the right was granted or abandoned from enjoying the benefit of it. He ought not — it is his DUTY not — to make void that voluntary act of his own. And such hindrance is INJUSTICE, and INJURY, as being sine jure — without right — the right having been renounced or transferred beforehand.

So injury, or injustice, in the controversies of the world is somewhat like what the Scholastics, in their disputations, call absurdity. For just as it is called an absurdity to contradict what one maintained at the beginning, so in the world it is called injustice and injury to voluntarily undo what one had voluntarily done from the beginning.

The way a man either simply renounces or transfers his right is by a declaration, or signification — some voluntary and sufficient sign or signs — that he does so renounce or transfer, or has done so, to the one who accepts it. These signs are either words only, or actions only, or — as happens most often — both words and actions. And these same signs are the BONDS by which men are bound and obliged. Bonds that have their strength not from their own nature — for nothing is more easily broken than a man's word — but from fear of some evil consequence upon breaking them.


Not All Rights Can Be Given Away

Whenever a man transfers his right or renounces it, he does so either in exchange for some right reciprocally transferred to himself, or for some other good he hopes to gain by it. For it is a voluntary act, and the object of every man's voluntary acts is some good to himself.

And therefore there are some rights which no man can be understood — by any words or other signs — to have abandoned or transferred. First, a man cannot lay down the right of resisting those who assault him by force to take away his life; because he cannot be understood to aim at any good to himself by doing so. The same may be said of wounds, and chains, and imprisonment — both because there is no benefit in enduring such things, as there is in patiently allowing another to be wounded or imprisoned, and also because when a man sees men proceed against him by violence, he cannot tell whether they intend his death or not.

And lastly, the motive and end for which this renouncing and transferring of right is introduced is nothing else but the security of a man's person — his life, and his means of preserving life in a way that does not make him weary of it. And therefore, if a man by words or other signs seems to strip himself of the very end for which those signs were intended, he is not to be understood as if he meant it, or as if it were his will. He is to be understood as ignorant of how such words and actions would be interpreted.


What a Contract Is

The mutual transferring of right is what men call CONTRACT.

There is a difference between transferring the right to a thing and transferring — that is, delivering — the thing itself. For the thing may be delivered together with the transfer of the right, as in buying and selling with cash in hand, or in the exchange of goods or lands; or it may be delivered some time afterward.


What a Covenant Is

Again, one of the contractors may deliver the thing contracted for on his part, and leave the other to perform his part at some fixed time afterward, trusting him in the meantime. In that case, the contract on the trusting side is called a PACT, or COVENANT. Or both parties may contract now to perform later. In these cases, the one who is to perform in the time to come, being trusted, is said — when he performs — to be keeping his promise, or keeping faith; and if he voluntarily fails to perform, that is a violation of faith.


Free Gift

When the transferring of right is not mutual, but one party transfers in the hope of gaining friendship or service from another, or from that person's friends; or in hope of gaining a reputation for charity or magnanimity; or to free his mind from the pain of compassion; or in hope of reward in heaven — this is not contract, but GIFT, FREE GIFT, GRACE. These words all signify one and the same thing.


Express Signs of Contract

Signs of contract are either express or by inference. Express signs are words spoken with understanding of what they signify. Such words are either of the present or past tense — "I give," "I grant," "I have given," "I have granted," "I will that this be yours" — or of the future: "I will give," "I will grant." Words of the future are called a promise.


Signs of Contract by Inference

Signs by inference are sometimes the consequence of words, sometimes the consequence of silence, sometimes the consequence of actions, sometimes the consequence of refraining from an action. In general, a sign by inference of any contract is whatever sufficiently demonstrates the will of the contractor.


A Free Gift Passes Only by Words of the Present or Past

Words alone, if they are of the time to come and contain a bare promise, are an insufficient sign of a free gift, and therefore not binding. For if they are of the time to come — "Tomorrow I will give" — they are a sign that I have not given yet, and consequently that my right is not transferred, but remains mine until I transfer it by some other act. But if the words are of the present or past — "I have given," or "I do give, to be delivered tomorrow" — then my tomorrow's right is given away today, and that by the force of the words alone, even with no other evidence of my will.

There is a great difference in meaning between these two phrases: volo hoc tuum esse cras and cras dabo — that is, between "I will that this be yours tomorrow" and "I will give it to you tomorrow." The phrase "I will" in the first expression signifies an act of the will in the present; in the second, it signifies a promise of an act of the will to come. So the first words, being of the present, transfer a future right; the second, being of the future, transfer nothing.

But if there are other signs of the will to transfer a right besides words, then even though the gift is free, the right may pass by words of the future. For instance, if a man sets a prize for whoever comes first to the end of a race, the gift is free; and though the words are of the future, the right passes. For if he did not want his words understood that way, he should not have let the runners run.


In Contracts, Words of Past, Present, and Future All Bind

In contracts, the right passes not only where the words are of the present or past, but also where they are of the future. For all contract is mutual transfer, or exchange, of right; and therefore he who promises only — because he has already received the benefit for which he promises — is to be understood as intending the right to pass. For unless he had been content to have his words so understood, the other party would not have performed his part first. For this reason, in buying and selling and other acts of contract, a promise is equivalent to a covenant, and is therefore binding.


What Merit Is

He that performs first, in the case of a contract, is said to MERIT what he is to receive by the other's performance; and he has it as his due. Also, when a prize is offered to many, to be given only to the winner, or money is thrown into a crowd to be enjoyed by those who catch it — though this is a free gift, yet to win it, or to catch it, is to merit it, and to have it as DUE. For the right is transferred in the offering of the prize and in the throwing down of the money; it is simply not determined to whom until the outcome of the contest decides it.

But between these two sorts of merit there is this difference: in contract, I merit by virtue of my own power and the contractor's need; but in the case of free gift, I am enabled to merit only by the generosity of the giver. In contract, I merit that the contractor should part with his right; in the case of gift, I do not merit that the giver should part with his right, but only that when he has parted with it, it should be mine rather than another's.

And this, I think, is the meaning of that distinction of the Scholastics between meritum congrui and meritum condigni — merit by fitness and merit by worthiness. God Almighty has promised paradise to those men (blinkered as they are by carnal desires) who can walk through this world according to the precepts and limits he has prescribed; so they say that he who walks that way shall merit paradise ex congruo — by fitness. But because no man can demand a right to it by his own righteousness, or by any other power in himself, but only by the free grace of God, they say no man can merit paradise ex condigno — by worthiness. This, I say, I think is the meaning of that distinction. But since these disputers do not agree on the meaning of their own technical terms any longer than it serves their purposes, I will not affirm anything about what they mean. I say only this: when a gift is given indefinitely, as a prize to be contended for, he that wins it merits it, and may claim the prize as due.


When Covenants of Mutual Trust Are Invalid

If a covenant is made in which neither party performs immediately, but they trust one another, then in the condition of mere nature — which is a condition of war of every man against every man — upon any reasonable suspicion, it is void. But if there is a common power set over them both, with right and force sufficient to compel performance, it is not void.

For he that performs first has no assurance the other will perform afterward; because the bonds of words are too weak to bridle men's ambition, greed, anger, and other passions without the fear of some coercive power — and in the condition of mere nature, where all men are equal and each is judge of the justness of his own fears, no such power can possibly be supposed. And therefore he who performs first only betrays himself to his enemy — contrary to the right, which he can never abandon, of defending his life and his means of living.

But in a civil state, where a power is set up to constrain those who would otherwise violate their faith, that fear is no longer reasonable. And for that reason, he who by the covenant is to perform first is obliged to do so.

The cause of fear that makes such a covenant invalid must always be something arising after the covenant is made — some new fact, or other sign of the will not to perform. Otherwise it cannot make the covenant void. For whatever could not stop a man from promising ought not to be admitted as a hindrance to performing.


The Right to the End Contains the Right to the Means

He that transfers any right transfers, as far as lies in his power, the means of enjoying it. He that sells land is understood to transfer the grass and whatever grows upon it; nor can he that sells a mill divert the stream that drives it. And they that give a man the right of government in sovereignty are understood to give him the right of levying money to maintain soldiers, and of appointing magistrates for the administration of justice.


No Covenants with Animals

To make a covenant with brute beasts is impossible. Since they do not understand our speech, they do not understand or accept any transfer of right, nor can they transfer any right to another; and without mutual acceptance, there is no covenant.


None with God, Except by Special Revelation

To make a covenant with God is impossible, except through the mediation of those God speaks to — either by supernatural revelation, or by his lieutenants who govern under him and in his name. For otherwise we cannot know whether our covenants are accepted or not. And therefore those who vow anything contrary to any law of nature vow in vain, since it would be unjust to pay such a vow. And if the thing vowed is commanded by the law of nature, then it is not the vow that binds them, but the law.


Covenants Only Concern the Possible and the Future

The matter, or subject, of a covenant is always something that falls under deliberation — for to covenant is an act of the will, that is to say, an act, and the last act, of deliberating. It is therefore always understood to be something to come, and something judged possible for the one covenanting to perform.

And therefore, to promise what is known to be impossible is no covenant. But if something thought possible beforehand proves impossible afterward, the covenant is valid and binding — though not to the thing itself, yet to its value; or if that too is impossible, then to the honest, unfeigned endeavor of performing as much as is possible. For to more than that, no man can be obliged.


How Covenants Are Dissolved

Men are freed of their covenants in two ways: by performing, or by being forgiven. For performance is the natural end of obligation; and forgiveness is the restoring of liberty, being a transfer back of that right in which the obligation consisted.


Covenants Extorted by Fear Are Valid

Covenants entered into out of fear, in the condition of mere nature, are binding. For example, if I covenant to pay a ransom, or perform a service for my life, to an enemy, I am bound by it. For it is a contract in which one party receives the benefit of life, and the other is to receive money or service for it; and consequently, where no other law forbids the performance — as none does in the condition of mere nature — the covenant is valid.

Therefore prisoners of war, if trusted with the payment of their ransom, are obliged to pay it. And if a weaker prince makes a disadvantageous peace with a stronger one out of fear, he is bound to keep it — unless, as was said before, some new and just cause of fear arises to renew the war. And even within commonwealths, if I am forced to buy my life from a thief by promising him money, I am bound to pay it, until the civil law discharges me. For whatever I may lawfully do without obligation, I may lawfully covenant to do through fear; and what I lawfully covenant, I cannot lawfully break.


An Earlier Covenant Voids a Later One

A former covenant makes a later one void. For a man that has passed away his right to one man today does not have it to pass to another man tomorrow; and therefore the later promise passes no right, and is null.


A Covenant Not to Defend Yourself Is Void

A covenant not to defend myself from force by force is always void. For, as I have shown before, no man can transfer or lay down his right to save himself from death, wounds, and imprisonment — the avoiding of which is the only purpose of laying down any right at all. Therefore the promise of not resisting force transfers no right in any covenant, and is not binding. For though a man may covenant, "Unless I do such-and-such, kill me," he cannot covenant, "Unless I do such-and-such, I will not resist you when you come to kill me." For man by nature chooses the lesser evil — the danger of death in resisting — rather than the greater, which is certain and present death in not resisting.

And all men grant this to be true, as shown by the fact that they lead criminals to execution and to prison with armed guards, even though those criminals consented to the very law by which they are condemned.


No Man Is Obliged to Accuse Himself

A covenant to accuse oneself, without assurance of pardon, is likewise invalid. For in the condition of nature, where every man is judge, there is no place for accusation; and in the civil state, accusation is followed by punishment — which, being force, a man is not obliged not to resist.

The same is true of accusing those whose condemnation would plunge a man into misery — a father, a wife, a benefactor. For the testimony of such an accuser, if not willingly given, is presumed to be corrupted by nature, and therefore not to be received; and where a man's testimony is not to be credited, he is not bound to give it.

Accusations extracted by torture, likewise, are not to be counted as testimony. For torture is to be used only as a means of conjecture — a glimmer of light in the further examination and search for truth. What a man confesses in that case tends toward the relief of the one being tortured, not toward informing the torturers, and therefore ought not to have the credit of sufficient testimony. For whether he delivers himself by a true accusation or a false one, he does it by the right of preserving his own life.


The Purpose and Form of an Oath

The force of words being, as I have noted before, too weak to hold men to the performance of their covenants, there are in man's nature only two imaginable helps to strengthen it. These are either a fear of the consequence of breaking their word, or a glory, a pride, in appearing not to need to break it. This second is a generosity too rarely found to be counted on — especially among the pursuers of wealth, command, or sensual pleasure, which is to say the greatest part of mankind. The passion to be relied upon is fear.

Fear has two very general objects: one is the power of invisible spirits; the other is the power of the men one would offend by breaking faith. Of these two, though the first is the greater power, the fear of the second is commonly the greater fear. The fear of the first is, in every man, his own religion — which has a place in the nature of man before civil society exists. The second does not; at least, not place enough to keep men to their promises, because in the condition of mere nature, the inequality of power is revealed only by the outcome of battle.

So before the time of civil society, or during its interruption by war, there is nothing that can strengthen a covenant of peace against the temptations of greed, ambition, lust, or any other strong desire, except the fear of that invisible power which every man worships as God and fears as an avenger of his treachery. All that can be done, therefore, between two men not subject to civil power, is to put one another to swear by the God each fears. This swearing, or OATH, is a form of speech, added to a promise, by which the one who promises signifies that unless he performs, he renounces the mercy of his God, or calls down God's vengeance on himself. Such was the heathen form: "Let Jupiter kill me, as I kill this beast." So is our form: "I shall do thus and thus, so help me God." And each man adds the rites and ceremonies of his own religion, so that the fear of breaking faith may be the greater.


No Oath Except by God

From this it appears that an oath taken according to any other form or rite than that of the man who swears it is in vain, and is no oath; and that there is no swearing by anything which the swearer does not believe to be God. For though men have sometimes sworn by their kings, out of fear or flattery, they meant it to be understood that they were attributing divine honor to them. And swearing by God unnecessarily is merely profaning his name; while swearing by other things, as men do in everyday talk, is not swearing at all, but an impious habit picked up from too much vehemence in talking.


An Oath Adds Nothing to the Obligation

It appears also that the oath adds nothing to the obligation. For a covenant, if lawful, binds in the sight of God without the oath just as much as with it; and if unlawful, it does not bind at all — even though it is confirmed with an oath.
