Chapter 45: Demonology and Other Relics of Pagan Religion
(Part 1 of 2)

Where Demonology Came From

When light strikes the organs of sight — whether it comes straight from a luminous body, or bounces off an opaque one, or bends as it passes through something transparent — it produces, in any living creature God has equipped with such organs, an imagination of the object the light came from. That imagination is what we call sight. And it does not feel like a mere imagination; it feels like the body itself, out there, outside us. It is exactly like what happens when a man presses hard on his own eye: a light appears outside him and in front of him, a light no one else can see — because there is in fact nothing out there at all, only a motion in the inner organs, pressing outward by resistance, that makes him think there is. And when that motion continues after the object that caused it is gone, we call it imagination, and memory, and — in sleep, or when the organs are badly disordered by sickness or violence — a dream. I covered all of this briefly back in Chapters 2 and 3.

The ancient pretenders to natural knowledge never discovered this nature of sight; still less did ordinary people, who do not bother with things so remote from daily use. So it was hard for men to think of those images in the imagination and the senses as anything other than things really existing outside us. Some — because the images vanish away, they know not where or how — insist they are absolutely incorporeal, that is, immaterial: forms without matter; color and shape without any colored or shaped body; able to put on airy bodies, like a garment, whenever they wish to make themselves visible to our bodily eyes. Others say they are bodies, and living creatures, but made of air or some other finer, more ethereal matter, which condenses when they want to be seen. But both camps agree on one general name for them: DEMONS. As if the dead people they dreamed of were not inhabitants of their own brains, but of the air, or of heaven, or hell — not phantasms, but ghosts. They had exactly as much reason for this as a man would have for saying he saw his own ghost in a mirror, or the ghosts of the stars in a river, or for calling the ordinary appearance of the sun — about a foot wide, as it looks — the demon, or ghost, of that great sun that lights the whole visible world.

And having invented these demons, men feared them as things of unknown — which is to say, unlimited — power to do them good or harm. That fear gave the governors of the pagan commonwealths their opening: they regulated it by establishing DEMONOLOGY (a field in which the poets, as the principal priests of pagan religion, were specially employed and revered), and they aimed it at the public peace, and at the obedience of subjects necessary to that peace. They sorted the demons into good ones and evil ones — the good as a spur to keep men observing the laws, the evil as reins to hold them back from breaking them.

What the Ancients' Demons Were

What sort of things they were that got the name of demons appears partly in the genealogy of their gods written by Hesiod, one of the most ancient of the Greek poets, and partly in other histories — some of which I noted earlier, in Chapter 12 of this book.

How the Doctrine Spread

The Greeks, through their colonies and conquests, carried their language and writings into Asia, Egypt, and Italy — and with them, by necessary consequence, their demonology, or (as St. Paul calls it) their "doctrines of devils." By that route the contagion reached the Jews as well, both those of Judaea and those of Alexandria and the other places where they were scattered. But the Jews did not apply the name "demon," as the Greeks did, to good spirits and evil ones alike. They reserved it for the evil only. The good demons they called the Spirit of God, and they considered anyone such a spirit entered to be a prophet. In short: anything extraordinary, if good, they credited to the Spirit of God; if evil, to some demon — but always a kakodaimon, an evil demon, that is, a devil.

And so they used the word "demoniacs" — people possessed by the devil — for those we would call madmen or lunatics; or for those who had the falling sickness — epilepsy; or for anyone who said something that they, for lack of understanding, thought absurd. Of a notoriously unclean person they would say he had an unclean spirit; of a mute man, that he had a dumb devil. Of John the Baptist they said, because of his extraordinary fasting, that he had a devil (Matthew 11:18). And of our Savior, because he said that whoever keeps his sayings shall not see death in aeternum — forever — they said, "Now we know you have a devil; Abraham is dead, and the prophets are dead" (John 8:52). And again, because he said they were trying to kill him, the people answered, "You have a devil; who is trying to kill you?" (John 7:20). All of which makes it plain that the Jews held the same opinion about phantasms that the Greeks did — namely, that they were not phantasms at all, not idols of the brain, but real things, independent of the imagination.

Why Our Savior Did Not Correct It

If this doctrine is false, someone may ask, why did our Savior not contradict it and teach the contrary? Indeed, why does he on various occasions use forms of speech that seem to confirm it?

To this I answer: first, where Christ says "a spirit has not flesh and bone," he shows that spirits exist, but he does not deny that they are bodies. And where St. Paul says "we shall rise spiritual bodies," he acknowledges the nature of spirits — but as bodily spirits. That is not hard to understand. Air and many other things are bodies, though they are not flesh and bone or any other gross body the eye can pick out.

But when our Savior speaks to the devil and commands him to come out of a man — if by "the devil" is meant a disease, such as frenzy or lunacy, or else a corporeal spirit, isn't the speech improper? Can a disease hear? And can there be a corporeal spirit in a body of flesh and bone that is already full of vital and animal spirits? Doesn't it follow that there must be spirits that neither have bodies nor are mere imaginations?

To the first objection I answer that addressing a command to the madness or lunacy he is curing is no more improper than his rebuking of the fever, or of the wind and the sea — for those do not hear either. Nor is it more improper than God's command to the light, to the firmament, to the sun and stars, when he commanded them to be — for they could not hear before they existed. Those speeches are not improper, because they signify the power of God's word. And so it is no more improper to command madness or lunacy — under the name of devils, which is how everyone then commonly understood those conditions — to depart out of a man's body. To the second objection, concerning spirits being incorporeal: I have not yet found any place in Scripture from which it can be gathered that any man was ever possessed by any corporeal spirit except his own — the one by which his body is naturally moved.

Scripture Does Not Teach That Spirits Are Incorporeal

Immediately after the Holy Spirit descended on him in the form of a dove, our Savior is said by St. Matthew (Matthew 4:1) to have been "led up by the Spirit into the wilderness"; and the same event is told by St. Luke (Luke 4:1) in these words: "Jesus, being full of the Holy Spirit, was led in the Spirit into the wilderness." It is evident that "the Spirit" there means the Holy Spirit. And this cannot be interpreted as a possession — for Christ and the Holy Spirit are one and the same substance, and one substance cannot be "possessed" by itself.

In the verses that follow, he is said to have been "taken up by the devil into the holy city, and set upon a pinnacle of the temple." Shall we conclude from this that he was possessed by the devil, or carried there by force? And again, he was "carried from there by the devil onto an exceedingly high mountain, who showed him from it all the kingdoms of the world." Here we are not to believe he was either possessed or forced by the devil — and no mountain is high enough, taken literally, to show a man one whole hemisphere, let alone all the kingdoms of the world. What, then, can this passage mean, except that he went into the wilderness of his own accord, and that this carrying of him up and down — from the wilderness to the city, and from there onto a mountain — was a vision? St. Luke's phrasing agrees with this: he says Jesus was led into the wilderness not by, but in, the Spirit; while concerning the mountain and the pinnacle of the temple he speaks as St. Matthew does. All of which suits the nature of a vision.

Again, where St. Luke says of Judas Iscariot that "Satan entered into him," and that he thereupon went and conferred with the chief priests and captains about how to betray Christ to them — it may be answered that by the entering of Satan (that is, the Enemy) is meant his hostile and traitorous intention of selling his Lord and Master. For just as "the Holy Spirit" in Scripture frequently means the graces and good inclinations the Holy Spirit gives, so "the entering of Satan" may mean the wicked thoughts and designs of the adversaries of Christ and his disciples. It is hard to say the devil entered Judas before he had any such hostile design; and it is pointless to say he was first Christ's enemy in his heart and that the devil only entered him afterward. The entering of Satan and his wicked purpose were one and the same thing.

But if there is no immaterial spirit, and no possession of men's bodies by any corporeal spirit, the question comes back again: why did our Savior and his apostles not teach the people so, in words so clear that no one could doubt it any longer? My answer is that questions like these are more curious than necessary for a Christian man's salvation. One might as well ask why Christ, who could have given faith, piety, and every moral virtue to all men, gave them to some only and not to all; or why he left the search for natural causes and the sciences to the natural reason and industry of men, instead of revealing them supernaturally to everyone, or to anyone; and many other such questions. And yet probable and pious reasons can be offered. When God brought the Israelites into the Promised Land, he did not secure them in it by subduing all the nations around them; he left many of them there, as thorns in their sides, to awaken the Israelites' piety and industry from time to time. In the same way our Savior, in conducting us toward his heavenly kingdom, did not clear away every difficulty in natural questions; he left them standing, to exercise our industry and reason. The whole scope of his preaching was to show us the plain and direct way to salvation — namely, belief in this article: that he was the Christ, the Son of the living God, sent into the world to sacrifice himself for our sins, and at his coming again to reign gloriously over his elect and save them from their enemies eternally. The opinion that spirits or phantasms can possess people is no obstacle on that road — though for some it is an occasion to wander off the road and follow their own inventions.

If we demand that Scripture settle every question that might be raised to trouble us in performing God's commands, we may as well complain of Moses for not having set down the time when such spirits were created, alongside the creation of the earth and sea and of men and beasts. To conclude: I find in Scripture that there are angels and spirits, good and evil. I do not find that they are incorporeal, like the apparitions men see in the dark, or in a dream, or in a vision — the things the Latins called spectra, and took for demons. I find that there are corporeal spirits, though subtle and invisible; but not that any man's body was ever possessed or inhabited by them. And I find that the bodies of the saints shall be of that kind — spiritual bodies, as St. Paul calls them.

The Power of Casting Out Devils Is Not What It Was in the Early Church

Nevertheless, the contrary doctrine — that there are incorporeal spirits — has prevailed in the Church so far that the practice of exorcism (that is, the ejection of devils by conjuration) is built upon it; and though it is rarely and faintly practiced now, it has not yet been given up entirely. That there were many demoniacs in the early Church and few madmen and other such singular diseases, whereas in our times we hear of and see many madmen and few demoniacs, comes not from any change in nature, but from a change in names.

But how it came about that the apostles, and for a time the pastors of the Church after them, cured those singular diseases, which now they are not seen to do; and likewise why it is not in the power of every true believer now to do everything the faithful did then — that is, as we read (Mark 16:17), in Christ's name to cast out devils, to speak with new tongues, to take up serpents, to drink deadly poison without harm, and to cure the sick by the laying on of their hands, all with no other words than "in the name of Jesus" — that is another question. And the probable answer is this: those extraordinary gifts were given to the Church for no longer than the time men trusted wholly in Christ and looked for their happiness only in his kingdom to come. When instead they began to seek authority and riches, and to trust their own cunning to win a kingdom of this world, these supernatural gifts of God were taken from them again.

Another Relic of Paganism: Image Worship — Left in the Church, Not Brought Into It

Another relic of paganism is the worship of images. It was instituted neither by Moses in the Old Testament nor by Christ in the New; nor was it imported from the pagans — it was simply left among them, still in place, after they had given their names to Christ. Before our Savior preached, the general religion of the pagans was to worship as gods the appearances that remain in the brain from the impression of external bodies on the organs of sense — the things commonly called ideas, idols, phantasms, conceits. These are representations of the external bodies that cause them, and they have no more reality in them than the things that seem to stand before us in a dream. That is why St. Paul says, "We know that an idol is nothing." Not that he thought an image of metal, stone, or wood was nothing — but that the thing men honored or feared in the image, and held for a god, was a mere figment, with no place, no habitation, no motion, no existence at all, except in the motions of the brain.

The worship of these figments with divine honor is what Scripture calls idolatry, and rebellion against God. For God was King of the Jews, and his lieutenant was first Moses and afterward the High Priest. If the people had been permitted to worship and pray to images — which are representations of their own imaginations — they would have had no further dependence on the true God, of whom there can be no likeness, nor on his prime ministers, Moses and the High Priests. Every man would have governed himself according to his own appetite, to the utter overthrow of the commonwealth and their own destruction for want of union. And therefore the first law of God was that they should not take for gods alienos deos — the gods of other nations — but only that one true God who deigned to commune with Moses, and through him to give them laws and directions for their peace and for their salvation from their enemies. And the second law was that they should not make for themselves any image of their own invention to worship. For it is the same deposing of a king to submit to another king, whether he is set up by a neighboring nation or by ourselves.

Answering Texts That Seem to Support Images

The places of Scripture claimed to support setting up images to worship them — or setting them up at all in the places where God is worshipped — are these. First, two examples: the cherubim over the Ark of God, and the bronze serpent. Second, certain texts in which we are commanded to worship certain creatures for their relation to God — as, to worship his footstool. And last, certain other texts said to authorize a religious honoring of holy things. But before I examine the force of these passages to prove what is claimed, I must first explain what is to be understood by worshipping, and what by images and idols.

What Worship Is

I have already shown, earlier in this book, that to honor is to value highly the power of a person, and that such value is measured by comparing him with others. But because nothing can be compared with God in power, we do not honor him — we dishonor him — by any valuation less than infinite. Honor, properly speaking, is by its own nature secret, internal, in the heart. But the inward thoughts of men, when they appear outwardly in words and actions, are the signs of our honoring; and these go by the name of worship — in Latin, cultus. Therefore, to pray to someone, to swear by him, to obey him, to be diligent and attentive in serving him — in short, all words and actions that show fear of offending or desire to please — is worship, whether those words and actions are sincere or feigned. And because they appear as signs of honoring, they are ordinarily also called honor.

The Difference Between Divine and Civil Worship

The worship we offer to those we believe to be merely men — kings, and men in authority — is civil worship. But the worship we offer to what we think to be God, whatever the words, ceremonies, gestures, or other actions may be, is divine worship. To fall prostrate before a king, by a man who thinks him only a man, is merely civil worship. But a man who so much as takes off his hat in church, because he thinks it the house of God, worships with divine worship.

Those who try to ground the distinction between divine and civil worship not in the intention of the worshipper, but in the Greek words douleia and latreia, deceive themselves. There were two sorts of servants. One sort were those absolutely in the power of their masters — slaves taken in war, and their children — whose bodies were not their own, whose lives depended on their masters' will so completely as to be forfeited at the least disobedience, and who were bought and sold like beasts. These were called douloi, properly "slaves," and their service douleia. The other sort were those who served voluntarily, for hire or in hope of benefit from their masters. These were called thetes — domestic servants — to whose service the masters had no further right than was contained in the covenants made between them. These two kinds of servants have this much in common: their labor is appointed for them by another, whether they serve as a slave or as a voluntary servant. And the word latris is the general name for both, meaning anyone who works for another, slave or volunteer. So latreia signifies service in general, while douleia signifies the service of bondmen only, and the condition of slavery. And in Scripture, both words are used for our service of God interchangeably: douleia, because we are God's slaves; latreia, because we serve him. And in every kind of service is contained not only obedience but also worship — that is, the actions, gestures, and words that signify honor.

What an Image Is; Phantasms

An IMAGE, in the strictest sense of the word, is the resemblance of something visible. In this sense, the phantastical forms, apparitions, or seemings of visible bodies to the sight are nothing but images: the appearance of a man or some other thing in the water, by reflection or refraction; or of the sun or the stars seen directly through the air. These appearances are nothing real in the things seen, nor in the place where they seem to be. Their sizes and shapes are not the same as the object's, but change with the variation of the organs of sight, or with lenses. They are often present in our imagination and in our dreams when the object is absent, or changed into other colors and shapes — things that depend only on the imagination. These are the images that were originally and most properly called ideas and IDOLS, words derived from the language of the Greeks, in which eido means "to see." They are also called PHANTASMS, which in the same language means "apparitions." And it is from these images that one of the faculties of man's nature is called the imagination. From all this it is plain that there neither is, nor can be, any image of a thing that is invisible.

It is equally evident that there can be no image of a thing that is infinite. All the images and phantasms made by the impression of visible things have a shape; but shape is a quantity bounded in every direction. Therefore there can be no image of God, nor of the soul of man, nor of spirits — only of visible bodies, that is, bodies that have light in themselves or are lit by bodies that do.

Fictions; Material Images

A man can, of course, imagine shapes he never saw — making up a figure out of the parts of various creatures, as the poets make their centaurs, chimeras, and other monsters never seen. And he can give matter to those shapes too, and make them in wood, clay, or metal. These are also called images — not for their resemblance to any corporeal thing, but for their resemblance to some phantastical inhabitant of the brain of their maker. But between these idols as they exist originally in the brain, and as they are painted, carved, molded, or cast in matter, there is a likeness of the one to the other; and for that likeness, the material body made by art may be said to be the image of the phantastical idol made by nature.

But in a broader use of the word, "image" also includes any representation of one thing by another. So an earthly sovereign may be called the image of God, and an inferior magistrate the image of an earthly sovereign. And in the idolatry of the pagans, there was often little regard for whether the material idol resembled the idol in their imagination — yet it was still called its image. An unhewn stone has been set up for Neptune, along with various other shapes far different from the shapes men conceived of their gods. And at this day we see many images of the Virgin Mary and other saints, unlike one another and corresponding to no one man's imagination, that nevertheless serve well enough for the purpose they were set up for — which was no more than to represent, by their names alone, the persons mentioned in the history; to which every man applies a mental image of his own making, or none at all. And so an image, in the largest sense, is either the resemblance, or the representation, of something visible — or both together, as it most often happens.
