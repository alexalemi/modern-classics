Chapter 21: The Liberty of Subjects

What Liberty Is

Liberty, or FREEDOM, properly means the absence of opposition — and by opposition I mean external impediments to motion. The word applies just as well to irrational and inanimate creatures as to rational ones. Whatever is tied down or hemmed in, so that it can move only within a certain space, and that space is fixed by the opposition of some external body — we say it does not have the liberty to go further.

The same goes for all living creatures while they are imprisoned or restrained by walls or chains. The same goes for water while it is held in by banks or vessels, when otherwise it would spread itself into a larger space. We say they are not at liberty to move as they would without those external impediments.

But when the obstacle to motion lies in the constitution of the thing itself, we do not say it lacks the liberty to move; we say it lacks the power. A stone lying still, or a man fastened to his bed by sickness, is not unfree. He is unable.

What It Means to Be Free

According to this proper and generally accepted meaning of the word, A FREE MAN is he that, in those things which by his strength and wit he is able to do, is not hindered from doing what he has a will to do.

But when the words "free" and "liberty" are applied to anything but bodies, they are being misused. What is not subject to motion is not subject to impediment. When we say, for example, "the way is free," we do not mean any liberty of the road, but of the people who walk it without being stopped. When we say a gift is free, we do not mean any liberty of the gift, but of the giver, who was not bound by any law or covenant to give it. When we speak freely, it is not the liberty of the voice or the pronunciation, but of the man, whom no law has obliged to speak otherwise than he did.

And finally, from the phrase "free will" no liberty of the will, desire, or inclination can be inferred — only the liberty of the man, which consists in this: that he finds no stop in doing what he has the will, desire, or inclination to do.

Fear and Liberty Are Consistent

Fear and liberty go together perfectly well. When a man throws his goods into the sea for fear the ship will sink, he does it entirely willingly, and he could refuse to do it if he wanted. It is therefore the action of a man who was free. So too a man sometimes pays his debt only for fear of prison; since nobody stopped him from keeping the money, that was the action of a man at liberty. And in general, all the actions men do in commonwealths out of fear of the law are actions the doers had the liberty not to do.

Liberty and Necessity Are Consistent

Liberty and necessity go together too. Watch the water: it has not only the liberty but the necessity of flowing down its channel. The same is true of the actions men do voluntarily. Because they proceed from the man's will, they proceed from liberty. And yet because every act of a man's will, and every desire and inclination, proceeds from some cause, and that cause from another cause, in a continual chain whose first link is in the hand of God — the first of all causes — they proceed from necessity. To anyone who could see the connection of all those causes, the necessity of all men's voluntary actions would be plainly visible.

And so God, who sees and arranges all things, sees also that the liberty of man in doing what he will is accompanied by the necessity of doing what God wills — no more and no less. Men may do many things that God does not command, and of which He is therefore not the author; but they can have no passion or appetite for anything unless God's will is the cause of that appetite. If His will did not guarantee the necessity of man's will, and consequently of everything that depends on man's will, then the liberty of men would contradict and obstruct the omnipotence and liberty of God. And that is enough, for our present purpose, about natural liberty — the only thing that is properly called liberty.

Artificial Chains: The Civil Laws

But just as men, to attain peace and preserve themselves by it, have made an artificial man, which we call a commonwealth, so they have also made artificial chains, called civil laws. By their mutual covenants, they have fastened these chains at one end to the lips of the man or assembly to whom they have given the sovereign power, and at the other end to their own ears. These bonds are weak by their own nature; yet they can be made to hold — not because they are hard to break, but because breaking them is dangerous.

The Liberty of Subjects Lies in What the Laws Leave Unregulated

It is only in relation to these bonds that I am now to speak of the liberty of subjects. No commonwealth in the world has rules regulating all the actions and words of men — such a thing is impossible. It follows necessarily that in every kind of action the laws have passed over in silence, men have the liberty to do what their own reason suggests is most profitable to themselves.

For if we take liberty in its proper sense — bodily liberty, freedom from chains and prison — it would be quite absurd for men to clamor, as they do, for a liberty they so obviously enjoy. And if we take liberty to mean exemption from the laws, it is no less absurd for men to demand, as they do, the very liberty by which all other men may become masters of their lives. Yet absurd as it is, that is exactly what they demand — not realizing that laws have no power to protect them without a sword in the hands of a man, or men, to see those laws carried out.

The liberty of a subject, therefore, lies only in those things which the sovereign, in regulating men's actions, has left unregulated: the liberty to buy and sell and otherwise make contracts with one another; to choose where they live, what they eat, and how they make their living; to raise their children as they think fit; and so on.

The Subject's Liberty Is Consistent with the Sovereign's Unlimited Power

Still, we must not imagine that this kind of liberty abolishes or limits the sovereign's power of life and death. For I have already shown that nothing the sovereign representative does to a subject, on any pretext whatsoever, can properly be called injustice or injury — because every subject is the author of every act the sovereign does. The sovereign never lacks the right to anything, except insofar as he himself is a subject of God, and bound by that to observe the laws of nature.

And so it can happen, and often does happen in commonwealths, that a subject is put to death by command of the sovereign power, and yet neither party wrongs the other — as when Jephthah caused his daughter to be sacrificed. In that case and others like it, the one who dies had the liberty to do the action for which he is nevertheless put to death without injury.

The same holds for a sovereign prince who puts an innocent subject to death. The action is against the law of nature, because it is contrary to equity — as the killing of Uriah by David was. Yet it was not an injury to Uriah, but to God. Not to Uriah, because Uriah himself had given David the right to do as he pleased. But to God, because David was God's subject, and the law of nature forbade him all iniquity. David himself, when he repented of the deed, plainly confirmed this distinction, saying, "Against you only have I sinned."

In the same way, when the people of Athens banished the most powerful men of their commonwealth for ten years at a time, they did not think they were committing any injustice — and yet they never even asked what crime the man had committed, only what harm he might do. Indeed, they would command the banishment of they knew not whom. Every citizen brought his oyster shell into the marketplace with the name of the man he wanted banished written on it, without actually accusing him of anything. Sometimes they banished an Aristides, precisely because of his reputation for justice; sometimes a vulgar comedian like Hyperbolus, simply as a joke. And yet no one can say that the sovereign people of Athens lacked the right to banish them, or that an Athenian lacked the liberty to joke, or to be just.

The Liberty the Ancient Writers Praise Is the Liberty of Commonwealths, Not of Private Men

The liberty that is mentioned so often and so honorably in the histories and philosophy of the ancient Greeks and Romans — and in the writings and talk of those who have gotten all their political learning from them — is not the liberty of particular men. It is the liberty of the commonwealth. And it is exactly the same liberty every man would have if there were no civil laws and no commonwealth at all. Its effects are the same, too.

For just as among masterless men there is perpetual war of every man against his neighbor — no inheritance to pass to a son or expect from a father, no property in goods or lands, no security, but a full and absolute liberty in every particular man — so in states and commonwealths that do not depend on one another, every commonwealth (not every man) has an absolute liberty to do whatever it judges — that is, whatever the man or assembly that represents it judges — most conducive to its benefit. But along with that, commonwealths live in the condition of perpetual war, forever on the edge of battle, with their frontiers armed and cannons planted against their neighbors all around.

The Athenians and the Romans were free — that is, they were free commonwealths. Not that any particular man had the liberty to resist his own representative, but that their representative had the liberty to resist or invade other peoples. To this day, the word LIBERTAS — liberty — is written in huge letters on the turrets of the city of Lucca. Yet no man can conclude from this that a particular man has any more liberty there, or any more exemption from service to the commonwealth, than he would have in Constantinople. Whether a commonwealth is a monarchy or a popular state, the freedom is still the same.

But it is an easy thing for men to be deceived by the attractive name of liberty, and — for lack of the judgment to tell the difference — to mistake for their private inheritance and birthright what is in fact the right of the public only. And when that same error is backed by the authority of men famous for their writings on the subject, it is no wonder it produces sedition and the overthrow of governments.

In these western parts of the world, we have been taught to take our opinions about the founding and the rights of commonwealths from Aristotle, Cicero, and other Greeks and Romans. Living under popular states, these men did not derive those rights from the principles of nature. They copied them into their books from the practice of their own commonwealths, which were popular — just as grammarians describe the rules of language from the speech of their time, or the rules of poetry from the poems of Homer and Virgil.

And because the Athenians were taught (to keep them from wanting to change their government) that they were free men, and that everyone living under a monarchy was a slave, Aristotle wrote it down in his Politics (book 6, chapter 2): "In democracy, liberty is to be supposed: for it is commonly held that no man is free in any other government." And like Aristotle, so Cicero and other writers grounded their civil doctrine on the opinions of the Romans, who were taught to hate monarchy — at first by the men who, having deposed their sovereign, divided the sovereignty of Rome among themselves, and afterward by their successors.

By reading these Greek and Latin authors, men have acquired from childhood a habit — under a false show of liberty — of favoring uprisings, of insolently second-guessing the actions of their sovereigns, and then of second-guessing those second-guessers, with so much spilling of blood that I think I can truly say: nothing was ever so dearly bought as these western parts have bought the learning of the Greek and Latin tongues.

How to Measure the True Liberty of a Subject

Now we come to the particulars of the true liberty of a subject — that is, the things a subject may refuse to do, without injustice, even when the sovereign commands them. To find them, we must consider what rights we give away when we make a commonwealth — or, which is the same thing, what liberty we deny ourselves by owning all the actions, without exception, of the man or assembly we make our sovereign.

For our obligation and our liberty both lie in the act of our submission, and must therefore be deduced from it. No man has any obligation that does not arise from some act of his own, for all men equally are by nature free. And since such deductions must be drawn either from the express words "I authorize all his actions," or from the intention of the man who submits himself to the sovereign's power (an intention which is to be understood from the purpose for which he submits), the obligation and liberty of the subject must be derived either from those words (or their equivalent), or else from the purpose for which sovereignty was instituted: namely, peace among the subjects themselves, and defense against a common enemy.

Subjects May Defend Their Own Bodies, Even Against Those Who Lawfully Attack Them

First, then. Sovereignty by agreement is created by covenant of every one to every one; sovereignty by conquest, by covenant of the vanquished to the victor, or of child to parent. So it is plain that every subject retains liberty in all those things whose right cannot be transferred by covenant. I showed in chapter 14 that covenants not to defend one's own body are void. Therefore:

They Are Not Bound to Hurt Themselves

If the sovereign commands a man — even one justly condemned — to kill, wound, or maim himself; or not to resist those who attack him; or to go without food, air, medicine, or anything else he cannot live without; that man has the liberty to disobey.

If a man is interrogated by the sovereign, or by the sovereign's authority, about a crime he himself committed, he is not bound to confess it without assurance of a pardon — because, as I showed in the same chapter, no man can be obliged by covenant to accuse himself.

Again, the consent of a subject to sovereign power is contained in these words: "I authorize, or take upon me, all his actions." There is no restriction at all in those words of the subject's own former natural liberty. By permitting the sovereign to kill me, I am not bound to kill myself when he commands it. It is one thing to say, "Kill me, or my fellow, if you please"; it is quite another to say, "I will kill myself, or my fellow." It follows, therefore, that:

No man is bound by the words themselves either to kill himself or to kill any other man. Consequently, whatever obligation a man may sometimes have, on the sovereign's command, to carry out a dangerous or dishonorable office, depends not on the words of our submission but on the intention behind it — which is to be understood from its purpose. So the rule is this: when our refusal to obey would frustrate the purpose for which sovereignty was established, then there is no liberty to refuse. Otherwise, there is.

Nor to Fight in War, Unless They Volunteer

On this ground, a man commanded as a soldier to fight against the enemy may in many cases refuse without injustice — even though his sovereign has right enough to punish his refusal with death. For instance, when he provides a capable soldier to take his place: in that case he has not deserted the service of the commonwealth. And allowance must be made for natural timidity — not only in women, of whom no such dangerous duty is expected, but also in men of womanish courage. When armies fight, there is running away on one side or both; yet when men do it not out of treachery but out of fear, they are judged to have acted not unjustly, but dishonorably. For the same reason, avoiding battle is not injustice — it is cowardice.

But the man who enrolls himself as a soldier, or takes enlistment money, throws away the excuse of a timid nature. He is obliged not only to go into battle but also not to run from it without his captain's leave. And when the defense of the commonwealth requires, all at once, the help of everyone able to bear arms, then everyone is obliged — because otherwise the founding of the commonwealth, which they lack the purpose or the courage to preserve, was for nothing.

No man has the liberty to resist the sword of the commonwealth in defense of another man, guilty or innocent — because such a liberty strips the sovereign of the means of protecting us, and so destroys the very essence of government. But suppose a great many men together have already resisted the sovereign power unjustly, or committed some capital crime for which every one of them expects death. Do they then have the liberty to band together and assist and defend one another? Certainly they do. For they are only defending their lives — which the guilty man may do just as well as the innocent. There was indeed injustice in the first breach of their duty; but their taking up arms afterward, though it is done to maintain what they have done, is no new unjust act. And if it is done only to defend their persons, it is not unjust at all. The offer of a pardon, however, takes away the plea of self-defense from those it is offered to, and makes it unlawful for them to keep assisting or defending the rest.

The Greatest Liberty of Subjects Depends on the Silence of the Law

As for other liberties, they depend on the silence of the law. Where the sovereign has prescribed no rule, the subject has the liberty to act or to hold back, at his own discretion. This kind of liberty is therefore greater in some places and lesser in others, greater in some times and lesser in others, according to what those who hold the sovereignty think most convenient. For example, there was a time in England when a man could enter his own land by force and throw out anyone wrongfully occupying it. But in later times, that liberty of forcible entry was taken away by a statute made by the king in Parliament. And in some places of the world men have the liberty of many wives; in other places, no such liberty is allowed.

If a subject has a dispute with his sovereign — about a debt, or the right of possession of lands or goods, or about any service required of him, or about any penalty, bodily or financial, grounded on a previous law — he has the same liberty to sue for his right as if it were a dispute with another subject, and before judges appointed by the sovereign. For since the sovereign is making his demand by force of a previous law, and not by virtue of his power, he thereby declares that he requires no more than what that law shows to be due. The lawsuit is therefore not contrary to the will of the sovereign, and consequently the subject has the liberty to demand a hearing of his case, and a sentence according to that law. But if the sovereign demands or takes anything on the claim of his power, then no legal action lies. For everything done by him by virtue of his power is done by the authority of every subject — and so a man who brings an action against the sovereign brings it against himself.

If a monarch or sovereign assembly grants a liberty to all or any of his subjects, and while that grant stands he is unable to provide for their safety, the grant is void — unless he directly renounces the sovereignty or transfers it to another. For he could have openly and in plain terms renounced or transferred it, if that had been his will, and he did not. So we must understand that it was not his will, and that the grant came from ignorance of the conflict between such a liberty and the sovereign power. The sovereignty is therefore still retained, and with it all the powers necessary to exercise it: the power of war and peace, of the courts, of appointing officers and counselors, of levying money, and the rest named in chapter 18.

When Subjects Are Released from Obedience to Their Sovereign

The obligation of subjects to the sovereign is understood to last as long, and no longer, than the power lasts by which he is able to protect them. For the right men have by nature to protect themselves, when no one else can protect them, can be given up by no covenant.

The sovereignty is the soul of the commonwealth; once it has departed from the body, the limbs no longer receive their motion from it. The end of obedience is protection — and wherever a man sees protection, whether in his own sword or in another's, nature directs his obedience to it, and his effort to maintain it. And though sovereignty, in the intention of those who make it, is immortal, it is by its own nature not only subject to violent death by foreign war, but carries within it, from the very moment of its founding, through the ignorance and passions of men, many seeds of a natural mortality by internal discord.

Captivity

If a subject is taken prisoner in war, or his person or his means of living are within the guards of the enemy, and his life and bodily liberty are given back to him on condition that he become subject to the victor, he has the liberty to accept the condition. And having accepted it, he is the subject of the one who took him — because he had no other way to preserve himself. The case is the same if he is detained on the same terms in a foreign country. But if a man is held in prison or in chains, or is not trusted with the liberty of his own body, he cannot be understood to be bound by covenant to subjection. He may therefore escape, if he can, by any means whatsoever.

If the Sovereign Renounces the Government for Himself and His Heirs

If a monarch gives up the sovereignty both for himself and for his heirs, his subjects return to the absolute liberty of nature. For though nature may declare who his sons are and who his nearest kin are, it depends on his own will (as was said in the previous chapter) who shall be his heir. If he will have no heir, there is no sovereignty and no subjection. The case is the same if he dies without known relatives and without declaring an heir — for then no heir can be known, and consequently no subjection is due.

Banishment

If the sovereign banishes his subject, then during the banishment he is not a subject. But a man sent abroad on a mission, or given leave to travel, is still a subject — though by contract between sovereigns, not by virtue of the covenant of subjection. For whoever enters another's dominion is subject to all its laws, unless he has a privilege by the friendship of the sovereigns, or by special license.

If the Sovereign Becomes Subject to Another

If a monarch defeated in war surrenders himself as subject to the victor, his subjects are released from their former obligation and become obliged to the victor. But if he is held prisoner, or does not have the liberty of his own body, he is not understood to have given away the right of sovereignty. His subjects are therefore obliged to obey the magistrates he previously appointed, who govern not in their own name but in his. For since his right remains, the only question is one of administration — that is, of the magistrates and officers. And if he has no means of naming them, he is presumed to approve those he himself appointed before.
