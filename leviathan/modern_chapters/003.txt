Chapter 3: The Train of Thoughts

By a train of thoughts — what I call the "consequence" of one thought from another — I mean the way thoughts follow each other in succession. To distinguish it from discourse in words, I call it MENTAL DISCOURSE: a conversation the mind has with itself.

When a person thinks about anything at all, the next thought is not as random as it seems. Thoughts do not just follow one another in any order whatsoever. Remember: we have no imagination that did not first come to us, in whole or in part, through the senses. In the same way, we never pass from one imagination to another unless the same transition happened before in our senses.

Here is the reason. All imaginations are motions inside us — leftovers of the motions made during sensation. And motions that followed one another during sensation stay linked together afterward. So when the first motion comes back and takes over, the second follows it, the way matter in motion hangs together — the way water on a smooth table is drawn in whichever direction your finger guides any one part of it.

But here is the catch: in sensation, the same perceived thing is sometimes followed by one thing and sometimes by another. So over time it becomes impossible to be certain what we will imagine next. Only one thing is certain: it will be something that followed the same thought before, at one time or another.

The Unguided Train of Thoughts

This train of thoughts, this mental discourse, comes in two kinds.

The first is unguided — without design, without steadiness. In this kind there is no passionate thought to govern and direct the thoughts that follow, no desire steering them toward some goal. The thoughts simply wander, and seem irrelevant to one another, as in a dream. These are typically the thoughts of people who are not only alone but also free of any pressing concern — though even then their thoughts are as busy as ever, just without harmony. It is like the sound an out-of-tune lute would make for anyone who strummed it, or a tuned one in the hands of someone who cannot play.

And yet even in this wild ranging of the mind, you can often trace its path and see how one thought depends on another. Here is an example. In a conversation about our present civil war, what could seem more out of place than the question someone actually asked: what was the value of a Roman penny? Yet to me the connection was perfectly clear. The thought of the war led to the thought of handing the King over to his enemies. That thought brought in the thought of the handing over of Christ. And that brought the thought of the thirty pieces of silver, the price of that betrayal — from which the malicious question followed easily enough. And all of this happened in an instant, for thought is quick.

The Regulated Train of Thoughts

The second kind is steadier, because it is regulated by some desire and design. The impression made by things we desire or fear is strong and lasting — or, if it fades for a while, it comes back quickly. Sometimes it is so strong that it interrupts and breaks our sleep.

From desire arises the thought of some means we have seen produce the thing we are aiming at. From that comes the thought of a means to that means, and so on, until we arrive at some starting point within our own power. And because the goal makes such a strong impression, it keeps coming back to mind — so if our thoughts begin to wander, they are quickly pulled back onto the path. One of the Seven Sages of Greece noticed this, and it led him to give people a precept that is now worn out with use: Respice finem — "look to the end." That is, in everything you do, keep your eye fixed on what you want, because that is what directs all your thoughts along the way to getting it.

The regulated train of thoughts is itself of two kinds. In the first, we imagine an effect and hunt for the causes or means that produce it. This kind is common to both humans and animals. In the second, we imagine some thing — anything at all — and hunt for all the possible effects it could produce. In other words, we imagine what we could do with it once we have it. Of this second kind I have never seen any sign except in human beings. It is a kind of curiosity that hardly belongs to the nature of any creature whose only passions are bodily ones, like hunger, thirst, lust, and anger.

In short, when the discourse of the mind is governed by design, it is nothing but seeking — the faculty of invention, which the Romans called sagacitas and solertia: a hunting out of the causes of some present or past effect, or of the effects of some present or past cause.

Remembrance

Sometimes a person looks for something he has lost. From the place and time where he first missed it, his mind runs backward, from place to place and time to time, to find where and when he last had it — that is, to find some definite, limited time and place from which to begin a methodical search. Then, from there, his thoughts run over those same places and times to discover what action or occasion might have made him lose it. This is what we call REMEMBRANCE, or calling to mind. The Romans called it reminiscentia — as if it were a re-counting, a going back over the ledger of our former actions.

Sometimes a person knows the definite area within which he must search, and then his thoughts sweep over every part of it — the way one would sweep a room to find a jewel, or the way a spaniel ranges across a field until it picks up a scent, or the way someone runs through the alphabet to start a rhyme.

Prudence

Sometimes a person wants to know the outcome of an action. Then he thinks of some similar action in the past and the outcomes that followed it, one after another, assuming that similar actions will be followed by similar outcomes. Someone who wants to foresee what will become of a criminal runs back over what he has seen follow the same crime before, in this order of thoughts: the crime, the officer, the prison, the judge, and the gallows. This kind of thinking is called foresight, and PRUDENCE, or providence — and sometimes wisdom. Though, since it is so hard to observe every circumstance, such guesswork is very prone to error. Still, this much is certain: the more experience of past things one person has compared to another, the more prudent he is, and the less often his expectations fail him.

Only the present exists in nature. Past things exist only in memory. And things to come have no existence at all — the future is nothing but a fiction of the mind, made by applying the consequences of past actions to present ones. The person with the most experience does this with the most confidence, but never with enough confidence to count as certainty. And though we call it prudence when the outcome matches our expectation, in its own nature it is only presumption. For genuine foresight of things to come — providence — belongs only to the one by whose will those things are to come. From him alone, and supernaturally, comes prophecy. The best natural prophet is the best guesser; and the best guesser is the one most practiced and studied in the matters he guesses at, for he has the most signs to guess by.

Signs

A SIGN is the prior event taken as evidence of the one that follows — or, in reverse, the following event taken as evidence of the prior one — whenever similar sequences have been observed before. And the more often they have been observed, the less uncertain the sign is. That is why the person with the most experience in any kind of business has the most signs by which to guess at the future, and so is the most prudent — so much more prudent than a newcomer to that business that no advantage of natural, off-the-cuff cleverness can make up the difference. Though perhaps many young men think otherwise.

Nevertheless, it is not prudence that separates man from beast. There are animals that, at a year old, observe more and pursue their own good more prudently than a ten-year-old child can.

Guessing About the Past

Just as prudence is a presumption about the future, drawn from the experience of the past, there is also a presumption about past things, drawn from other things that are likewise past. Someone who has seen the stages by which a flourishing state slid first into civil war and then into ruin will, at the sight of any other state's ruins, guess that the same kind of war and the same downward course happened there too. But this guess carries almost the same uncertainty as guesses about the future, since both rest on nothing but experience.

I can think of no other act of the human mind that is planted in us so naturally that it requires nothing for its exercise except being born human and living with the use of the five senses. The other faculties, which I will discuss shortly and which seem to belong to humans alone, are acquired and improved by study and effort. Most people learn them through instruction and discipline, and they all flow from the invention of words and speech. Beyond sense, thoughts, and the train of thoughts, the mind of man has no other motion at all — though with the help of speech and method, these same faculties can be raised to such a height that they distinguish humans from every other living creature.

Whatever we imagine is finite. Therefore there is no idea or conception of anything we call infinite. No one can hold in his mind an image of infinite size, or conceive the ends and bounds of the thing so named — we have no conception of the thing itself, only of our own inability. So the name of God is used not to make us conceive him — for he is incomprehensible, and his greatness and power are beyond conceiving — but so that we may honor him.

Also, because (as I said before) whatever we conceive was first perceived by sense, all at once or in parts, no one can have a thought representing anything that is not subject to sense. It follows that no one can conceive anything except as being in some place; as having some definite size; and as divisible into parts. Nor can anyone conceive that a thing is entirely in this place and entirely in another place at the same time, or that two or more things can occupy one and the same place at once. None of these things ever has been or ever can be presented to the senses. They are absurd phrases, accepted on trust — with no meaning at all — from deceived philosophers, and from Scholastics who are deceived, or deceiving.
