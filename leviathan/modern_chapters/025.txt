Chapter 25: On Counsel

What Counsel Is

Nothing shows more plainly how misleading it is to judge the nature of things by the ordinary, inconstant use of words than the confusion between counsel and command. The trouble comes from grammar: both are spoken in the imperative mood. "Do this" is what a man says when he commands — but it is also what he says when he gives counsel, and when he exhorts. And yet few people fail to see that these are very different things, or fail to tell them apart, once they notice who is speaking, to whom the speech is directed, and on what occasion.

But when readers meet these phrases in books, and either cannot or will not stop to consider the circumstances, they sometimes mistake the precepts of counselors for the precepts of commanders, and sometimes the reverse — whichever reading best suits the conclusion they want to draw or the action they want to approve. To prevent those mistakes, and to give the terms commanding, counseling, and exhorting their proper and distinct meanings, I define them as follows.

The Difference Between Command and Counsel

COMMAND is when a man says "Do this" or "Do not do this" without expecting any reason for it beyond the will of the one who says it. From this it follows plainly that he who commands is aiming at his own benefit. The only reason behind his command is his own will — and the proper object of every man's will is some good to himself.

COUNSEL is when a man says "Do this" or "Do not do this" and draws his reasons from the benefit that the action will bring to the person he is speaking to. From this it is evident that he who gives counsel claims to be aiming — whatever he actually intends — at the good of the one he advises.

So here is the first great difference between counsel and command: command is directed to the benefit of the one who gives it; counsel, to the benefit of the one who receives it.

From this comes a second difference. A man may be obliged to do what he is commanded — as when he has covenanted to obey. But he can never be obliged to do what he is counseled, because the harm of ignoring counsel falls on himself alone. And if he should covenant to follow it, then the counsel has changed its nature: it has become a command.

A third difference: no man can claim a right to be another man's counselor. He cannot claim that being heard benefits himself — that is not what counsel is for — and to demand the right to counsel someone is really to announce a desire to learn that person's plans, or to gain some other good for oneself, which (as I said before) is the proper object of every man's will.

One more thing belongs to the nature of counsel: whoever asks for it cannot, in fairness, accuse or punish the counsel he receives, whatever it turns out to be. To ask counsel of another man is to give him permission to advise whatever he thinks best. So a man who gives counsel to his sovereign — whether a monarch or an assembly — when the sovereign asks for it, cannot in fairness be punished for it, whether his advice agrees with the opinion of the majority or not, so long as it speaks to the question under debate. Indeed, if the sense of an assembly could be known before the debate was finished, the assembly should neither ask for nor accept any further counsel — for the sense of the assembly is the resolution of the debate and the end of all deliberation. The general rule is this: whoever demands counsel is the author of it, and therefore cannot punish it; and what the sovereign cannot do, no one else can. But if one subject counsels another to do something against the law, that counsel is punishable by the commonwealth — whether it came from bad intention or from mere ignorance — because ignorance of the law is no excuse where every man is bound to know the laws he lives under.

What Exhortation and Dehortation Are

EXHORTATION and DEHORTATION are counsel delivered with visible signs that the giver passionately wants it followed — or, to put it more briefly, counsel vehemently pressed. (Exhortation urges you toward an action; dehortation urges you away from it.) A man who exhorts does not carefully work out the consequences of what he advises, or bind himself to the rigor of true reasoning. He spurs the man he counsels toward action — just as the man who dehorts scares him away from it. So such speakers pitch their reasons to the common passions and opinions of their audience, and they reach for similes, metaphors, examples, and the other tools of oratory to persuade their hearers that following the advice would be useful, honorable, or just.

Several things follow from this. First: exhortation and dehortation are directed to the good of the speaker, not of the one being advised — which is contrary to the duty of a counselor, who by the very definition of counsel ought to regard not his own benefit but the benefit of the man he advises. And that the exhorter is serving his own benefit is plain enough from his long and vehement urging, and from the artful, calculated style of it. Nobody asked him for any of that. Since it springs from his own motives, it is directed chiefly to his own advantage — and only accidentally, or not at all, to the good of the person being counseled.

Second: exhortation and dehortation have a use only where a man is speaking to a crowd. When speech is addressed to one person, that person can interrupt and examine the reasons more rigorously than a multitude can — a multitude is simply too many people to enter into dispute and dialogue with someone addressing them all at once.

Third: those who exhort and dehort when they have been asked for counsel are corrupt counselors — bribed, as it were, by their own interest. However good their advice may happen to be, the man who gives it this way is no more a good counselor than a judge who hands down a just sentence for a bribe is a just judge. But where a man may lawfully command — a father in his family, a general in his army — his exhortations and dehortations are not only lawful but necessary and praiseworthy. Only then they are no longer counsels; they are commands. And when such commands order harsh and unpleasant labor, necessity sometimes — and humanity always — requires that they be sweetened in the delivery: framed with encouragement, in the tone and phrasing of counsel rather than the harsher language of command.

We can take examples of the difference between command and counsel from the forms of speech that express them in Holy Scripture. "Have no other gods but me. Make no graven image. Do not take God's name in vain. Keep the Sabbath holy. Honor your parents. Do not kill. Do not steal" — these are commands, because the reason we are to obey them is drawn from the will of God our King, whom we are obliged to obey. But these words — "Sell all you have, give it to the poor, and follow me" — are counsel, because the reason for doing so is drawn from our own benefit: namely, that we shall have "treasure in heaven." Again, "Go into the village across from you, and you will find a donkey tied, and her colt; untie her and bring her to me" is a command, for the reason behind the act is drawn from the will of the Master. But "Repent, and be baptized in the name of Jesus" is counsel — because doing so brings no benefit to God Almighty, who will still be King however we rebel, but only to ourselves, who have no other way of escaping the punishment hanging over us for our sins.

What Makes a Good or Bad Counselor

We have now derived the difference between counsel and command from the nature of counsel itself — which consists in laying out the benefit or harm that may come to the person counseled, through the necessary or probable consequences of the proposed action. From that same nature we can also derive the difference between fit and unfit counselors.

Experience is nothing but the memory of consequences observed in similar actions before, and counsel is nothing but the speech by which that experience is made known to another. So the virtues and defects of counsel are the same as the intellectual virtues and defects. To the person of a commonwealth, counselors serve in the place of memory and mental discourse. But here the resemblance between the commonwealth and a natural man breaks down in one respect of great importance. A natural man receives his experience from the natural objects of sense, which act on him without any passion or interest of their own. Whereas those who give counsel to the representative person of a commonwealth may have — and often do have — private aims and passions of their own, which render their counsels always suspect and many times unfaithful. So we may set down as the first condition of a good counselor: that his ends and interests not be inconsistent with the ends and interests of the one he counsels.

Second. The office of a counselor, when an action comes under deliberation, is to make its consequences plain — to inform the one he counsels truly and clearly. So he ought to deliver his advice in the form of speech that makes the truth appear most evident: with the firmest reasoning, in language exact and proper, and as briefly as the evidence allows. Therefore rash and unproven inferences (such as those fetched only from examples or from the authority of books — which are witnesses of fact or of opinion, not arguments about what is good or evil), obscure, confused, and ambiguous expressions, and all metaphorical speech designed to stir up passion, are repugnant to the office of a counselor. Such reasoning and such language are useful only for deceiving a man, or for steering him toward someone else's ends instead of his own.

Third. The ability to counsel comes from experience and long study, and no man can be presumed to have experience in everything that the administration of a great commonwealth requires. So no man is presumed to be a good counselor except in business he has not only handled extensively but has also thought long and hard about. The business of a commonwealth is to keep the people safe at home and defend them against foreign invasion. That requires great knowledge of human nature, of the rights of government, and of the nature of equity, law, justice, and honor — none of which can be gained without study. It also requires knowledge of the strength, resources, and geography of one's own country and its neighbors, and of the inclinations and designs of every nation that might do them harm — and that is not gained without much experience. Not just the whole of these subjects, but every single particular of them, demands the age and observation of a man in years, and more than ordinary study. The kind of wit required for counsel, as I have said before, is judgment; and men differ in judgment according to their different educations — some trained in one kind of study or business, some in another. Where infallible rules exist for doing a thing — as in machines and buildings, where the rules of geometry govern — all the experience in the world cannot equal the counsel of the man who has learned or discovered the rule. Where there is no such rule, the man with the most experience in that particular kind of business has the best judgment of it, and is the best counselor.

Fourth. To counsel a commonwealth in any business that touches another commonwealth, it is necessary to know the intelligence reports and dispatches that come from there, and all the records of treaties and other transactions of state between the two — which no one can do except those whom the representative sees fit to admit. From which we may see that people who have not been called to counsel have no good counsel to push forward in such cases.

Fifth. Supposing the number of counselors equal, a man is better counseled by hearing them separately than in an assembly — and for many reasons.

First, hearing them apart, you get the advice of every man. In an assembly, many of them deliver their advice with an aye or a no, or with their hands or feet, moved not by their own judgment but by the eloquence of another — or by fear of displeasing some speaker, or the whole assembly, by contradicting — or by fear of appearing slower-witted than those who applauded the contrary opinion.

Second, in any assembly of many, there cannot fail to be some whose interests run contrary to the public's. Those interests make them passionate; passion makes them eloquent; and eloquence draws others into the same advice. The passions of men, which are moderate when separate — like the heat of a single ember — when assembled are like many embers that inflame one another (especially when they fan each other with orations), to the point of setting the commonwealth on fire under the pretense of counseling it.

Third, hearing every man apart, you can examine — when there is need — the truth or probability of his reasons, and the grounds of the advice he gives, by frequent interruptions and objections. That cannot be done in an assembly, where on every difficult question a man is more likely to be stunned and dazzled by the variety of talk about it than informed of the course he ought to take. Besides, no assembly of many is ever called together for advice without someone in it ambitious to be thought eloquent and learned in politics — someone who gives his advice with an eye not to the business at hand but to the applause for his motley oration, stitched together from the multicolored threads and shreds of other authors. That is, at best, an irrelevance that eats up the time of serious consultation — and in the private way of counseling apart, it is easily avoided.

Fourth, in deliberations that ought to be kept secret — and public business gives many occasions for those — the counsels of many, and especially of assemblies, are dangerous. That is why great assemblies are forced to hand such affairs over to smaller numbers, and to the persons most experienced and most trusted.

To conclude: who approves of taking counsel from a great assembly of counselors so heartily that he would wish for, or even accept, their services when the question is marrying off his children, disposing of his lands, governing his household, or managing his private estate — especially if some among them do not wish him well?

A man who does his business with the help of many prudent counselors, consulting each one separately in his own area of expertise, does it best — like a man playing tennis with skilled partners, each placed at his proper station. He does next best who uses his own judgment alone, like a man who plays with no partner at all. But the man who is carried up and down to his business by an organized council — one that cannot move except by a plurality of agreeing opinions, and whose decisions are commonly slowed (out of envy or self-interest) by the dissenting faction — does it worst of all. He is like a man carried to the ball by players who may be good, but who carry him in a wheelbarrow or some other contraption: heavy in itself, dragged back by the clashing judgments and efforts of those pushing it, and all the more so the more hands are set to it — and worst of all when one or more among them wants him to lose.

It is true that many eyes see more than one. But that does not apply to many counselors — only to the case where the final decision rests with one man. Otherwise, because many eyes see the same thing along different lines of sight, and are apt to glance sideways toward their private benefit, those who do not wish to miss their mark, though they look about with two eyes, never aim with more than one. And so no great popular commonwealth was ever held together except by a foreign enemy that united it, or by the reputation of some one eminent man among them, or by the secret counsel of a few, or by the mutual fear of evenly matched factions — never by the open consultations of the assembly. As for very small commonwealths, popular or monarchical, no human wisdom can hold them up any longer than the jealousy of their powerful neighbors lasts.
