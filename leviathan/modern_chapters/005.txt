Chapter 5: Of Reason and Science

What Reason Is

When a man reasons, he is doing nothing but conceiving a sum total from the addition of parts, or conceiving a remainder from the subtraction of one sum from another. If this is done with words, it is conceiving the consequence from the names of all the parts to the name of the whole; or from the names of the whole and one part, to the name of the other part. In some fields, such as numbers, men name other operations besides adding and subtracting — multiplying and dividing — but these are really the same thing. For multiplication is just the adding together of equal things, and division is just the subtracting of one thing as many times as we can.

These operations are not confined to numbers. They apply to anything at all that can be added together or taken one out of another. Arithmeticians teach how to add and subtract in numbers. Geometricians teach the same in lines, figures (solid and flat), angles, proportions, times, degrees of speed, force, power, and the like. Logicians teach the same in consequences of words: adding together two names to make an affirmation; two affirmations to make a syllogism; many syllogisms to make a demonstration; and from the sum, or conclusion of a syllogism, they subtract one proposition to find the other. Writers on politics add together agreements to find men's duties; and lawyers add laws and facts to find what is right and wrong in the actions of private persons. In sum: wherever there is room for addition and subtraction, there is room for reason. And where these have no place, reason has nothing at all to do.

Reason Defined

Out of all this we may define — that is to say, determine — what is meant by the word reason when we count it among the faculties of the mind. REASON, in this sense, is nothing but reckoning — that is, adding and subtracting — the consequences of general names agreed upon for the marking and signifying of our thoughts. I say marking them when we reckon by ourselves, and signifying when we demonstrate or prove our reckonings to other men.

Where Right Reason Is Found

In arithmetic, beginners must make mistakes, and even the professors themselves may often err and total things up wrongly. So too in every other subject of reasoning: the ablest, most attentive, and most practiced men may deceive themselves and draw false conclusions. Not that reason itself is not always right reason — it is, just as arithmetic is a certain and infallible art. But no one man's reason, and no group of men's reason, supplies the certainty — any more than an account is correctly totaled just because a great many people have unanimously approved it.

So when there is a dispute over an account, the parties must, by their own agreement, set up as right reason the reason of some arbitrator or judge, whose decision they will both accept. Otherwise their dispute must either come to blows or remain unsettled, for want of a right reason established by nature. And so it is in every debate of every kind. When men who think themselves wiser than everyone else clamor and demand "right reason" as judge, yet really want nothing more than for matters to be decided by no reason but their own, it is as intolerable in human society as it is in a card game, after trump has been declared, to insist on using as trump, on every hand, whatever suit you happen to hold the most of. For that is exactly what such men do: they want every passion of theirs, as it comes to hold sway in them, to be taken for right reason — and in their own disputes, no less. They betray their lack of right reason by the very claim they lay to it.

The Use of Reason

The use and end of reason is not to find the sum and truth of one or a few consequences far removed from the first definitions and settled meanings of names. It is to begin at those definitions and proceed from one consequence to the next. There can be no certainty about the final conclusion without certainty about all the affirmations and negations on which it was grounded and from which it was inferred.

Think of the head of a household settling his accounts. He adds the sums of all his bills of expense into one total — but never checks how each bill was added up by the people who submitted it, or what he is actually paying for. He gains no more by this than if he had accepted the whole account in one lump, trusting in the skill and honesty of every accountant. It is the same in reasoning about anything else: a man who takes up conclusions on the trust of authors, and does not work them out from the first items in every reckoning — which are the meanings of names settled by definitions — loses his labor. He does not know anything. He only believes.

Of Error and Absurdity

A man can reckon without words in particular cases — as when, at the sight of some one thing, we guess what probably came before it or what is likely to follow it. If what he thought likely to follow does not follow, or what he thought likely to have come before did not come before, this is called error, and even the most prudent men are subject to it.

But when we reason in words of general signification and land on a general inference that is false, then — though it is commonly called error — it is in fact an ABSURDITY, or senseless speech. For error is only a deception, a presuming that something has happened or will happen which, though it did not happen or will not, was at least not impossible. But when we make a general assertion, unless it is a true one, its very possibility is inconceivable. And words by which we conceive nothing but the sound are the ones we call absurd, insignificant, and nonsense. So if a man talked to me of a round quadrangle, or the accidents of bread in cheese, or immaterial substances, or a free subject, a free will, or anything "free" other than free from being hindered by opposition — I would not say he was in error. I would say his words were without meaning. That is to say: absurd.

I said before, in the second chapter, that man excels all other animals in this faculty: whenever he conceives anything whatsoever, he is inclined to inquire into its consequences, and what effects he could produce with it. Now I add another degree of that same excellence: by words, he can reduce the consequences he finds to general rules, called theorems or aphorisms. That is, he can reason — reckon — not only in numbers, but in everything else that can be added to or subtracted from something.

But this privilege is offset by another: the privilege of absurdity, to which no living creature is subject but man alone. And of men, none are more subject to it than those who profess philosophy. What Cicero says of them somewhere is entirely true: there is nothing so absurd that it cannot be found in the books of philosophers. The reason is obvious. Not one of them begins his reasoning from the definitions, or explanations, of the names he is going to use — a method that has been used only in geometry, whose conclusions have thereby been made indisputable.

The Causes of Absurdity

The first cause of absurd conclusions I ascribe to the lack of method: they do not begin their reasoning from definitions — that is, from settled meanings of their words — as if they could cast up an account without knowing the value of the number words one, two, and three.

Now, all bodies enter into our reckoning under various considerations, which I mentioned in the previous chapter; and since these considerations carry different names, various absurdities arise from confusing them and connecting their names into assertions where they do not fit. And therefore:

The second cause of absurd assertions I ascribe to giving the names of bodies to accidents, or of accidents to bodies. This is what people do when they say faith is "infused" or "inspired" — when nothing can be poured or breathed into anything but a body — or that extension is body, or that phantasms are spirits, and so on.

The third, to giving the names of the accidents of bodies outside us to the accidents of our own bodies — as they do who say the color is in the body, or the sound is in the air, and so on.

The fourth, to giving the names of bodies to names or speeches — as they do who say that there are things universal, or that a living creature is a genus, or "a general thing," and so on.

The fifth, to giving the names of accidents to names and speeches — as they do who say the nature of a thing is in its definition, or a man's command is his will, and the like.

The sixth, to the use of metaphors, tropes, and other rhetorical figures in place of proper words. It is perfectly acceptable in common speech to say, for example, the road goes, or leads, here or there, or the proverb says this or that — even though roads cannot go and proverbs cannot speak. But in reckoning, in the search for truth, such expressions cannot be admitted.

The seventh, to names that signify nothing, picked up and learned by rote from the Scholastics: "hypostatical," "transubstantiate," "consubstantiate," "eternal-now," and the rest of the Schoolmen's cant.

A man who can avoid these things will not easily fall into any absurdity — unless it be through the sheer length of a calculation, where he may forget what came before. For all men by nature reason alike, and reason well, when they have good principles. Who is so stupid as both to make a mistake in geometry and to persist in it after someone else has pointed out his error?

Science

From all this it appears that reason is not, like sense and memory, born with us; nor acquired by experience alone, as prudence is. It is attained by hard work: first, in the apt imposing of names; and second, in acquiring a good and orderly method of proceeding from the elements, which are names, to assertions made by connecting one name to another; and from there to syllogisms, which are the connections of one assertion to another — until we arrive at a knowledge of all the consequences of names belonging to the subject at hand. And that is what men call SCIENCE.

Whereas sense and memory are only knowledge of fact — of a thing past and unchangeable — science is the knowledge of consequences, of the dependence of one fact upon another. By it, from what we can do right now, we know how to do something else when we choose, or a similar thing at another time. Because once we see how a thing comes about, from what causes, and in what manner, then whenever similar causes come into our power, we see how to make them produce similar effects.

Children, then, are not endowed with reason at all until they have attained the use of speech. They are called reasonable creatures because of the evident possibility of their having the use of reason in time to come. And most men, though they have the use of reasoning a little way — as in counting to some degree — get little use out of it in everyday life. There they govern themselves, some better, some worse, according to their differences of experience, quickness of memory, and inclination toward various ends — but most of all according to good or bad fortune, and one another's mistakes. As for science — certain rules for their actions — they are so far from it that they do not even know what it is. Geometry they have taken for sorcery. And as for the other sciences, those who have never been taught their beginnings, and made some progress in them so as to see how they are acquired and generated, are in this respect like children who, having no idea where babies come from, are made to believe by the women that their brothers and sisters were not born but found in the garden.

Even so, those who have no science are in a better and nobler condition with their natural prudence than men who, by reasoning badly, or by trusting those who reason wrong, land on false and absurd general rules. For ignorance of causes and of rules does not lead men as far astray as relying on false rules — taking for causes of what they aim at things which are not causes of it at all, but rather causes of the contrary.

To conclude: the light of human minds is perspicuous words — but first trimmed and purged of ambiguity by exact definitions. Reason is the pace; the increase of science, the road; and the benefit of mankind, the destination. And on the contrary, metaphors and senseless, ambiguous words are like ignes fatui — will-o'-the-wisps. Reasoning upon them is wandering among innumerable absurdities; and their end is contention, and sedition, or contempt.

Prudence and Sapience, with Their Difference

As much experience is prudence, so much science is sapience. We usually have only the one name, wisdom, for them both; but the Romans always distinguished between prudentia and sapientia, ascribing the former to experience, the latter to science. To make the difference appear more clearly, suppose one man endowed with an excellent natural skill and dexterity in handling his weapons; and another who has added to that dexterity an acquired science of where he can strike his adversary, or be struck by him, in every possible posture or guard. The ability of the first would be to the ability of the second as prudence is to sapience: both useful, but the second infallible. But those who, trusting only to the authority of books, follow the blind blindly, are like the man who trusts the false rules of a fencing master and presumptuously ventures against an adversary who either kills him or disgraces him.

The Signs of Science

Of the signs of science, some are certain and infallible; some, uncertain. Certain, when the man who claims the science of anything can teach it — that is, demonstrate its truth clearly to another. Uncertain, when only some particular outcomes match his claim, and on many occasions things turn out as he says they must. The signs of prudence are all uncertain; for it is impossible to observe by experience, and remember, every circumstance that might change the outcome.

But in any business where a man has no infallible science to proceed by, to abandon his own natural judgment and be guided by general maxims read in authors — maxims subject to many exceptions — is a sign of folly, and is generally scorned under the name of pedantry. Even among those very men who, in the councils of the commonwealth, love to show off their reading in politics and history, very few do the same in their domestic affairs, where their own particular interest is at stake. They have prudence enough for their private business. But in public, they care more about the reputation of their own cleverness than about the success of anyone else's business.
