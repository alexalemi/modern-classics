Chapter 42: Of Ecclesiastical Power
(Part 5 of 5)

The last passage is Hebrews 13:17: "Obey your leaders, and submit yourselves to them, for they watch for your souls, as those that must give account." Here too the obedience intended is a following of their counsel. For the reason for our obedience is not drawn from the will and command of our pastors, but from our own benefit — it is the salvation of our souls they watch for, not the exaltation of their own power and authority. If this verse meant that everything pastors teach is law, then not only the Pope but every pastor in his parish would have legislative power. Again: if men were bound to obey their pastors, they would have no power to examine their commands. And what then shall we say to St. John, who tells us (1 John 4:1) "not to believe every spirit, but to test the spirits, whether they are of God, because many false prophets have gone out into the world"?

It is plain, therefore, that we may dispute the doctrine of our pastors — but no man can dispute a law. The commands of civil sovereigns are granted on all sides to be laws. And if anyone besides the sovereign could make a law, then all commonwealth — and with it all peace and justice — must cease; which is contrary to all laws, both divine and human. Nothing, therefore, can be drawn from these or any other passages of Scripture to prove that the decrees of the Pope are laws anywhere he does not also hold the civil sovereignty.

The Question of Superiority Between the Pope and the Other Bishops

The last point he tries to prove is this: "that our Savior Christ committed ecclesiastical jurisdiction immediately to none but the Pope." Notice that here he is not handling the question of supremacy between the Pope and Christian kings, but between the Pope and the other bishops.

First, he says it is agreed that the jurisdiction of bishops is, at least in general, de jure divino — by divine right. For this he cites St. Paul (Ephesians 4:11), where he says that Christ, after his ascension into heaven, "gave gifts to men: some apostles, some prophets, and some evangelists, and some pastors, and some teachers." From this he infers that bishops do indeed hold their jurisdiction in God's right — but he will not grant that they have it immediately from God. It must come, he says, derived through the Pope.

But if a man can be said to hold his jurisdiction by divine right and yet not immediately, then what lawful jurisdiction, even a merely civil one, is there in a Christian commonwealth that is not also by divine right? Christian kings have their civil power from God immediately, and the magistrates under a king exercise their several offices by virtue of his commission. What those magistrates do is no less de jure divino mediato — by divine right at one remove — than what bishops do by virtue of the Pope's ordination. All lawful power is from God: immediately in the supreme governor, and mediately in those who hold authority under him. So he must either grant that every constable in the state holds his office by the right of God, or else admit that no bishop holds his office that way — except the Pope himself.

But this whole dispute — whether Christ left jurisdiction to the Pope only, or to the other bishops as well — is, outside the territories where the Pope holds the civil sovereignty, a contention de lana caprina — a quarrel over goat's wool; that is, over nothing. For where they are not sovereigns, none of them has any jurisdiction at all. Jurisdiction is the power of hearing and deciding cases between man and man; and it can belong to no one but the man who has the power to prescribe the rules of right and wrong — that is, to make laws — and to compel men with the sword of justice to obey his decisions, whether pronounced by himself or by the judges he appoints. And no one can lawfully do that but the civil sovereign.

So when he cites Luke 6 — that our Savior called his disciples together and chose twelve of them, whom he named apostles — he proves only that Christ elected them (all except Matthias, Paul, and Barnabas) and gave them power and command to preach. Not to judge cases between man and man; for that is a power Christ refused to take upon himself, saying, "Who made me a judge or a divider over you?" — and in another place, "My kingdom is not of this world." And he that has no power to hear and decide cases between man and man cannot be said to have any jurisdiction at all. None of this prevents our Savior from having given them power to preach and baptize in all parts of the world — supposing they were not forbidden by their own lawful sovereign. For Christ himself, and his apostles, have in many places expressly commanded us to be obedient to our own sovereigns in all things.

The arguments by which he would prove that bishops receive their jurisdiction from the Pope are therefore all in vain, since the Pope, in the dominions of other princes, has no jurisdiction himself. Yet because those same arguments prove the contrary — that all bishops, when they have jurisdiction at all, receive it from their civil sovereigns — I will not omit to run through them.

The first is from Numbers 11, where Moses, unable to bear alone the whole burden of administering the affairs of the people of Israel, was commanded by God to choose seventy elders; and God took part of the spirit of Moses and put it upon those seventy elders. This is to be understood not as God weakening the spirit of Moses — that would not have eased him at all — but as showing that the elders all had their authority derived from him. In this, Bellarmine interprets the passage truly and honestly. But since Moses held the entire sovereignty in the commonwealth of the Jews, what the passage plainly signifies is that they had their authority from the civil sovereign. So this passage proves that bishops in every Christian commonwealth have their authority from the civil sovereign — and from the Pope in his own territories only, not in the territories of any other state.

The second argument is from the nature of monarchy, in which all authority resides in one man, and in others only by derivation from him; and the government of the Church, he says, is monarchical. But this too works in favor of Christian monarchs. For they really are monarchs of their own people — that is, of their own church, since the church is the same thing as a Christian people. Whereas the power of the Pope, even if he were St. Peter himself, is not monarchy at all; it has nothing in it of the archical — ruling — or the cratical — commanding — but only of the didactical — teaching. For God accepts not a forced, but a willing obedience.

The third is that the See of St. Peter is called by St. Cyprian the Head, the Source, the Root, and the Sun, from which the authority of bishops is derived. But by the law of nature — which is a better principle of right and wrong than the word of any doctor who is but a man — the civil sovereign in every commonwealth is the head, the source, the root, and the sun from which all jurisdiction is derived. Therefore the jurisdiction of bishops is derived from the civil sovereign.

The fourth is taken from the inequality of bishops' jurisdictions. For if God (says he) had given jurisdiction to bishops immediately, he would have given them equality of jurisdiction along with equality of order. But we see that some are bishops of only one town, some of a hundred towns, and some of many whole provinces — differences that were never determined by any command of God. Their jurisdiction, therefore, is not from God but from man; one has a greater share and another a lesser, as it pleases the prince of the Church. Now this argument would have served his purpose — if he had first proved that the Pope holds a universal jurisdiction over all Christians. But that has not been proved. And it is notoriously well known that the Pope's large jurisdiction was given him by those who had it to give — namely, the emperors of Rome. (The Patriarch of Constantinople, on the very same title — being bishop of the capital city of the empire and seat of the emperor — claimed to be the Pope's equal.) It follows that all other bishops have their jurisdiction from the sovereigns of the places where they exercise it. And just as, for that reason, they do not hold their authority by divine right, neither does the Pope hold his by divine right — except only where he is also the civil sovereign.

His fifth argument is this: "If bishops have their jurisdiction immediately from God, the Pope could not take it from them, for he can do nothing contrary to God's ordination." This consequence is good, and well proved. "But," says he, "the Pope can do this, and has done it." This too is granted — provided he does it in his own dominions, or in the dominions of any other prince who has given him that power; but not universally, in right of the papacy. For that power belongs to every Christian sovereign within the bounds of his own empire, and is inseparable from the sovereignty. Before the people of Israel, by God's command to Samuel, set a king over themselves after the manner of the other nations, the high priest held the civil government, and none but he could make or depose an inferior priest. But afterward that power was in the king — as can be proved by this very argument of Bellarmine's. For if the priest (high priest or any other) had his jurisdiction immediately from God, then the king could not take it from him, "for he could do nothing contrary to God's ordinance." Yet it is certain that King Solomon deprived Abiathar the high priest of his office (1 Kings 2:26) and placed Zadok in his room (verse 35). Kings, therefore, may in the same way ordain and depose bishops as they see fit for the good governing of their subjects.

His sixth argument is this: if bishops have their jurisdiction de jure divino — that is, immediately from God — then those who maintain it should produce some word of God to prove it; but they can produce none. The argument is good; I have nothing to say against it. But it is an equally good argument to prove that the Pope himself has no jurisdiction in the dominion of any other prince.

Lastly, he brings as an argument the testimony of two Popes, Innocent and Leo. And I do not doubt that he might, with just as good reason, have cited the testimony of nearly every Pope since St. Peter. Considering the love of power naturally planted in mankind, whoever was made Pope would be tempted to uphold the same opinion. Nevertheless, all of them would only be doing what Innocent and Leo did: bearing witness on their own behalf — and therefore their witness is no good.

The Pope's Temporal Power

In his fifth book he has four conclusions. The first is "that the Pope is not lord of all the world." The second, "that the Pope is not lord of all the Christian world." The third, "that the Pope, outside his own territory, has no temporal jurisdiction DIRECTLY." These three conclusions are easily granted. The fourth is "that the Pope has, in the dominions of other princes, the supreme temporal power INDIRECTLY." This is denied — unless by "indirectly" he means that he has gotten it by indirect means; in which case that too is granted. But that is not what he means. When he says the Pope has it indirectly, he means that such temporal jurisdiction belongs to him by right, but that this right is merely a consequence of his pastoral authority, which he could not exercise without it. Therefore (so the doctrine runs) to the pastoral power — which he calls spiritual — the supreme civil power is necessarily annexed; and the Pope thereby has the right to change kingdoms, giving them to one and taking them from another, whenever he thinks it conduces to the salvation of souls.

Before I come to consider the arguments by which he would prove this doctrine, it will not be amiss to lay open its consequences — so that the princes and states who hold the civil sovereignty in their several commonwealths may consider for themselves whether it is convenient for them, and conducive to the good of their subjects, of whom they must give an account at the day of judgment, to admit it.

When it is said that the Pope does not hold the supreme civil power in the territories of other states directly, we are to understand that he does not claim it the way other civil sovereigns do — from the original submission of those who are to be governed. For it is evident, and has already been sufficiently demonstrated in this treatise, that the right of all sovereigns is derived originally from the consent of every one of those who are to be governed: whether those who choose a sovereign do it for their common defense against an enemy, as when they agree among themselves to appoint a man or an assembly of men to protect them, or whether they do it to save their lives, by submission to a conquering enemy. So when the Pope disclaims the supreme civil power over other states "directly," all he denies is that his right comes to him by that route. He does not, for all that, cease to claim it by another route — namely (without the consent of those who are to be governed), by a right given him by God in his elevation to the papacy; and this he calls "indirect." But by whatever route he pretends to it, the power is the same. And if it is granted to be his right, he may depose princes and states as often as it is necessary for the salvation of souls — that is, as often as he wants. For he also claims the sole power to judge whether something is for the salvation of men's souls or not.

And this is the doctrine not only that Bellarmine here, and many other doctors, teach in their sermons and books, but that some councils have decreed, and that Popes have, when occasion served them, put into practice. For the Fourth Lateran Council, held under Pope Innocent III, has this canon (in the third chapter, De Haereticis — on heretics): "If a king, at the Pope's admonition, does not purge his kingdom of heretics, and, being excommunicated for the same, does not make satisfaction within a year, his subjects are absolved of their obedience." And the practice of this doctrine has been seen on various occasions: in the deposing of Chilperic, King of France; in the transfer of the Roman Empire to Charlemagne; in the oppression of John, King of England; in the transferring of the Kingdom of Navarre; and, in recent years, in the League against Henry III of France — and in many more instances.

I think there are few princes who do not consider this unjust and inconvenient. But I wish they would all resolve to be either kings or subjects. Men cannot serve two masters. Princes ought therefore to relieve their people, either by holding the reins of government wholly in their own hands, or by delivering them wholly into the hands of the Pope — so that the men who are willing to be obedient may be protected in their obedience. For this distinction of temporal and spiritual power is but words. Power is as really divided, and as dangerously for all purposes, by sharing it with an indirect power as with a direct one. But now to his arguments.

The first is this: "The civil power is subject to the spiritual. Therefore he that has the supreme spiritual power has the right to command temporal princes, and to dispose of their temporal goods in order to the spiritual end."

As for this distinction of temporal and spiritual, let us consider in what sense it can intelligibly be said that the temporal, or civil, power is subject to the spiritual. There are only two ways those words can be made into sense. When we say one power is subject to another power, we mean either that the holder of the one is subject to the holder of the other, or that the one power stands to the other as means to an end. For we cannot understand that one power has power over another power, or that one power can have right or command over another power. Subjection, command, right, and power are properties not of powers, but of persons. One power may be subordinate to another the way the art of a saddle-maker is subordinate to the art of a rider. Suppose we grant, then, that civil government is ordained as a means to bring us to spiritual happiness. It still does not follow that if a king has the civil power and the Pope the spiritual, the king is therefore bound to obey the Pope — any more than every saddle-maker is bound to obey every rider. Just as the subordination of an art does not imply the subjection of its practitioner, so the subordination of a government does not imply the subjection of the governor.

So when he says the civil power is subject to the spiritual, his meaning is that the civil sovereign is subject to the spiritual sovereign. And then the argument stands thus: "The civil sovereign is subject to the spiritual; therefore the spiritual prince may command temporal princes." The conclusion is the very thing he was supposed to prove as his premise.

To prove it, he first offers this reason: "Kings and Popes, clergy and laity, make but one commonwealth — that is, but one Church. And in all bodies the members depend on one another. But things spiritual do not depend on things temporal. Therefore things temporal depend on things spiritual, and so are subject to them." In this argument there are two gross errors.

One is the claim that all Christian kings, Popes, clergy, and all other Christian men make but one commonwealth. For it is evident that France is one commonwealth, Spain another, and Venice a third, and so on. These consist of Christians, and so they are several bodies of Christians — that is to say, several churches. And their several sovereigns represent them, which is what makes them capable of commanding and obeying, of doing and suffering, like a natural man. No general or universal church has that capacity until it has a representative — which it does not have on earth. If it had one, there is no doubt that all Christendom would be one commonwealth, whose sovereign would be that representative, in things spiritual and temporal alike. And the Pope, to make himself this representative, lacks three things our Savior has not given him: to command, to judge, and to punish — beyond the punishment of excommunication, which is only to withdraw from those who will not learn from him. For even if the Pope were Christ's only vicar, he cannot exercise his government until our Savior's second coming. And even then it is not the Pope, but St. Peter himself, together with the other apostles, who are to be judges of the world.

The other error in this first argument is his saying that the members of every commonwealth depend on one another, like the members of a natural body. It is true that they cohere together; but they depend only on the sovereign, who is the soul of the commonwealth. When the sovereign fails, the commonwealth dissolves into civil war — no one man so much as cohering with another, for want of a common dependence on a known sovereign — just as the members of a natural body dissolve into earth for want of a soul to hold them together. So there is nothing in this comparison from which to infer a dependence of the laity on the clergy, or of the temporal officers on the spiritual — only the dependence of both on the civil sovereign. And the sovereign ought indeed to direct his civil commands toward the salvation of souls; but he is not, for all that, subject to anyone but God himself. So now you see the labored fallacy of the first argument: it is built to deceive men who do not distinguish between the subordination of actions as means toward an end, and the subjection of persons to one another in the administration of those means. For the means to every end are determined by nature, or by God himself supernaturally; but the power to make men use the means is, in every nation, resigned (by the law of nature, which forbids men to violate the faith they have given) to the civil sovereign.

His second argument is this: "Every commonwealth — because it is supposed to be perfect and sufficient in itself — may command any other commonwealth not subject to it, and force it to change the administration of its government, and even depose its prince and set another in his place, if it cannot otherwise defend itself against the injuries that prince attempts against it. Much more, then, may a spiritual commonwealth command a temporal one to change the administration of its government, and may depose princes and institute others, when it cannot otherwise defend the spiritual good."

That a commonwealth, to defend itself against injuries, may lawfully do everything he says here is very true, and has already been sufficiently demonstrated earlier in this book. And if it were also true that there now exists in this world a spiritual commonwealth distinct from the civil commonwealth, then its prince might, upon injury done to him, or for want of security against injury to come, repair and secure himself by war — which is, in sum, deposing, killing, subduing, or doing any act of hostility. But by the same reasoning, it would be no less lawful for a civil sovereign, upon like injuries done or feared, to make war upon the spiritual sovereign — which, I believe, is more than Cardinal Bellarmine intended to have inferred from his own proposition.

But there is no spiritual commonwealth in this world. For a spiritual commonwealth is the same thing as the kingdom of Christ — which Christ himself says is not of this world, but shall be in the next world, at the resurrection, when those who have lived justly and believed that he was the Christ shall rise (though they died natural bodies) as spiritual bodies. Then it is that our Savior shall judge the world, conquer his adversaries, and make a spiritual commonwealth. In the meantime, seeing there are no men on earth whose bodies are spiritual, there can be no spiritual commonwealth among men who are still in the flesh — unless we call the preachers, who have a commission to teach and to prepare men for their reception into the kingdom of Christ at the resurrection, a commonwealth; and I have proved that they are none.

The third argument is this: "It is not lawful for Christians to tolerate an infidel or heretical king, if he tries to draw them into his heresy or unbelief. But it belongs to the Pope to judge whether a king is drawing his subjects to heresy or not. Therefore the Pope has the right to determine whether a prince is to be deposed or not deposed."

To this I answer that both assertions are false. For Christians — and men of whatever religion — who do not tolerate their king, whatever law he makes, even a law concerning religion, violate their faith, contrary to the divine law, both natural and positive. Nor is there any judge of heresy among subjects but their own civil sovereign. For heresy is nothing else but a private opinion, obstinately maintained, contrary to the opinion which the public person — that is, the representative of the commonwealth — has commanded to be taught. From which it is plain that an opinion publicly appointed to be taught cannot be heresy, and that the sovereign princes who authorize such opinions are not heretics. Heretics are none but private men who stubbornly defend some doctrine prohibited by their lawful sovereigns.

To prove that Christians are not to tolerate infidel or heretical kings, he cites a passage in Deuteronomy 17 where God forbids the Jews, when they set a king over themselves, to choose a foreigner; and from this he infers that it is unlawful for a Christian to choose a king who is not a Christian. And it is true that a Christian — that is, a man who has already pledged himself to receive our Savior, when he comes, as his king — would tempt God too far by choosing as his king in this world one whom he knows will try, by terror and by persuasion, to make him violate his faith. But, says he, it is the same danger to choose a non-Christian for king and then not to depose him once he is chosen. To this I say: the question is not about the danger of not deposing him; it is about the justice of deposing him. To choose him may, in some cases, be unjust. But to depose him, once he is chosen, is in no case just. For it is always a violation of faith, and consequently against the law of nature, which is the eternal law of God. Nor do we read that any such doctrine was accounted Christian in the time of the apostles, or in the time of the Roman emperors, until the Popes gained the civil sovereignty of Rome.

To this he has replied that the Christians of old did not depose Nero, or Diocletian, or Julian, or Valens (an Arian), for this one reason only: that they lacked the temporal forces. Perhaps so. But did our Savior — who, had he called for them, might have had twelve legions of immortal, invulnerable angels to assist him — lack the forces to depose Caesar, or at least Pilate, who unjustly, finding no fault in him, delivered him to the Jews to be crucified? Or if the apostles lacked temporal forces to depose Nero, was it therefore necessary for them, in their epistles to the newly made Christians, to teach them (as they did) to obey the powers constituted over them — of whom Nero, at that time, was one — and to obey them not for fear of their wrath, but for conscience's sake? Shall we say they not only obeyed, but also taught what they did not mean, for want of strength? It is not for want of strength, then, but for conscience's sake, that Christians are to tolerate their heathen princes, or princes who authorize the teaching of an error (for I cannot call anyone whose doctrine is the public doctrine a heretic).

And whereas, in support of the Pope's temporal power, he further alleges that St. Paul (1 Corinthians 6) appointed judges under the heathen princes of those times — judges not ordained by those princes — this is not true. St. Paul only advises them to take some of their brethren to settle their differences as arbitrators, rather than to go to law with one another before the heathen judges: a wholesome precept, full of charity, fit to be practiced in the best Christian commonwealths too. And as for the danger that may arise to religion from subjects tolerating a heathen or an erring prince — that is a point of which a subject is no competent judge. Or if he is, then the Pope's own temporal subjects may likewise judge of the Pope's doctrine. For every Christian prince, as I have proved before, is no less supreme pastor of his own subjects than the Pope is of his.

The fourth argument is taken from the baptism of kings: that in being made Christians, they submit their scepters to Christ, and promise to keep and defend the Christian faith. This is true; for Christian kings are no more than Christ's subjects. But they may, for all that, be the Pope's fellows. For they are supreme pastors of their own subjects; and the Pope is no more than king, and pastor, even in Rome itself.

The fifth argument is drawn from the words spoken by our Savior, "Feed my sheep" — by which, he says, was given all the power necessary for a pastor: the power to chase away wolves, such as heretics; the power to shut up rams, if they are mad or butt the other sheep with their horns, such as evil (though Christian) kings; and the power to give the flock convenient food. From this he infers that St. Peter had these three powers given him by Christ.

To this I answer that the last of these powers is nothing more than the power — or rather the command — to teach. As for the first, the power to chase away wolves, that is, heretics: the passage he quotes is Matthew 7:15, "Beware of false prophets, which come to you in sheep's clothing, but inwardly are ravening wolves." But heretics are not false prophets — they are not prophets at all. And even granting that heretics are the wolves meant there, the apostles were not commanded to kill them, or, if they were kings, to depose them — but to beware of them, flee from them, and avoid them. Nor was that counsel to beware of false prophets given to St. Peter, or to any of the apostles, but to the crowd of Jews who followed him up the mountain — men for the most part not yet converted. So if the passage confers a power of chasing away kings, it was given not only to private men, but to men who were not even Christians.

And as to the power of separating and shutting up furious rams — by which he means Christian kings who refuse to submit themselves to the Roman pastor — our Savior refused to take that power upon himself in this world, and advised letting the wheat and the weeds grow up together until the day of judgment. Much less did he give it to St. Peter; nor can St. Peter give it to the Popes. St. Peter, and all other pastors, are told to treat those Christians who disobey the Church — that is, who disobey the Christian sovereign — as heathen men and tax collectors. And since no one claims for the Pope any authority over heathen princes, they ought to claim none over princes who are to be treated as heathen.

But from the power to teach alone, he infers also a coercive power in the Pope over kings. The pastor, says he, must give his flock convenient food; therefore the Pope may, and ought to, compel kings to do their duty. From which it follows that the Pope, as pastor of Christian men, is King of Kings — which all Christian kings ought indeed either to confess, or else take upon themselves the supreme pastoral charge, every one in his own dominion.

His sixth and last argument is from examples. To which I answer, first, that examples prove nothing; and second, that the examples he cites do not establish so much as a probability of right. The act of Jehoiada in killing Athaliah (2 Kings 11) was done either by the authority of King Joash, or else it was a horrible crime in the high priest — who, ever after the election of King Saul, was a mere subject. The act of St. Ambrose in excommunicating the Emperor Theodosius (if it is true that he did so) was a capital crime. And as for the Popes — Gregory I, Gregory II, Zachary, and Leo III — their judgments are void, as judgments given in their own cause; and the acts done by them in conformity with this doctrine are the greatest crimes (especially that of Zachary) that human nature is capable of.

And this is as much as I have to say of ecclesiastical power. I would have been briefer, and spared the examination of these arguments of Bellarmine's, if they had been his as a private man — and not as the champion of the papacy against all other Christian princes and states.
