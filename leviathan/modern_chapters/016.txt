Chapter 16: Persons, Authors, and Things Personated


What a Person Is

A PERSON is one whose words or actions are considered either as his own, or as representing the words or actions of another man — or of any other thing to which they are attributed, whether truly or by fiction.


Natural and Artificial Persons

When the words and actions are considered as his own, he is called a NATURAL PERSON. When they are considered as representing the words and actions of another, he is a feigned or ARTIFICIAL PERSON.


Where the Word "Person" Comes From

The word "person" is Latin. Where we use it, the Greeks had prosopon, which means the face — just as persona in Latin means the disguise, the outward appearance of a man, counterfeited on the stage; and sometimes, more particularly, the part of the costume that disguises the face: a mask or visor. From the stage, the word was carried over to any representer of speech and action, in courtrooms as well as theaters. A person, then, is the same thing as an actor — both on the stage and in everyday life. To PERSONATE is to act, or to represent, either yourself or someone else; and he who acts another is said to bear his person, or to act in his name. Cicero uses the word in this sense when he says, "Unus sustineo tres personas: mei, adversarii, et judicis — I bear three persons: my own, my adversary's, and the judge's." A man in this role goes by different names on different occasions: a representer or representative, a lieutenant, a vicar, an attorney, a deputy, a procurator, an actor, and the like.


Actor, Author, Authority

Among artificial persons, some have their words and actions owned by those they represent. In that case the person is the ACTOR — the one who acts — and the one who owns his words and actions is the AUTHOR; and the actor acts by authority. For the same relation that, when we speak of goods and possessions, is called ownership (in Latin dominus, in Greek kurios — owner, master), is called authorship when we speak of actions. And just as the right of possession is called dominion, so the right of doing any action is called AUTHORITY. By authority, then, is always understood a right of doing some act; and done by authority means done by commission or license from the one whose right it is.


Covenants Made by Authority Bind the Author

From this it follows that when the actor makes a covenant by authority, he binds the author by it, no less than if the author had made it himself, and no less subjects him to all its consequences. So everything said earlier (in chapter 14) about the nature of covenants between man and man in their natural capacity is true also when covenants are made by their actors, representers, or procurators who have authority from them — as far as their commission extends, but no further.

And therefore anyone who makes a covenant with the actor, or representer, without knowing what authority he actually has, does so at his own peril. For no man is obliged by a covenant of which he is not the author — nor, consequently, by a covenant made against, or beyond, the authority he gave.


But Not the Actor

When the actor does something against the law of nature by command of the author, and he is obliged by a former covenant to obey him, then it is not he but the author who breaks the law of nature. For though the action is against the law of nature, it is not his action. On the contrary: to refuse to do it would be against the law of nature that forbids breaking covenants.


The Authority Must Be Shown

And he who makes a covenant with the author through the mediation of the actor, not knowing what authority the actor has but taking him at his word, is no longer obliged if that authority is not made evident to him on demand. For a covenant made with the author is not valid without the author's own counter-assurance. But if the man making the covenant knew in advance that he could expect no assurance beyond the actor's word, then the covenant is valid — because in that case the actor makes himself the author. And so, just as the covenant obliges the author and not the actor when the authority is evident, it obliges the actor alone when the authority is faked; for then there is no author but himself.


Inanimate Things Can Be Personated

There are few things that cannot be represented by fiction. Inanimate things — a church, a hospital, a bridge — may be personated by a rector, a master, or an overseer. But inanimate things cannot be authors, and therefore cannot give authority to their actors. Still, the actors may have authority to maintain such things, given to them by their owners or governors. And for this reason, such things cannot be personated until there is some state of civil government.


Those Without the Use of Reason

Likewise children, fools, and madmen who have no use of reason may be personated by guardians or curators. But they can be authors, during that time, of no action done by them — not unless, when they recover the use of reason, they judge that action reasonable. Yet during the period of folly, whoever has the right of governing them may give authority to the guardian. But this, again, has no place except in a civil state; for before such a state exists, there is no dominion of persons.


False Gods

An idol, a mere figment of the brain, may be personated — as the gods of the heathen were. Officers appointed by the state personated them; and the gods held possessions, goods, and rights which men from time to time dedicated and consecrated to them. But idols cannot be authors, for an idol is nothing. The authority came from the state. And therefore, before the introduction of civil government, the gods of the heathen could not be personated.


The True God

The true God may be personated. And he was: first, by Moses, who governed the Israelites — they were not his people, but God's people — not in his own name, with "Hoc dicit Moses — Thus says Moses," but in God's name, with "Hoc dicit Dominus — Thus says the Lord." Secondly, by the Son of man, his own Son, our Blessed Savior Jesus Christ, who came to bring back the Jews and bring all nations into the kingdom of his Father — not as from himself, but as one sent from his Father. And thirdly, by the Holy Ghost, the Comforter, speaking and working in the Apostles — which Holy Ghost was a Comforter who came not of himself, but was sent by them both, and proceeded from them both.


How a Multitude of Men Becomes One Person

A multitude of men is made one person when they are represented by one man, or one person — provided it is done with the consent of every single individual in that multitude. For it is the unity of the representer, not the unity of the represented, that makes the person one. And it is the representer who bears the person — and only one person. Unity in a multitude cannot be understood in any other way.


Every One of Them Is an Author

And because the multitude is naturally not one but many, its members cannot be understood as one author, but as many authors, of everything their representative says or does in their name. Every man gives their common representer authority from himself in particular, and owns all the actions the representer performs — if they give him authority without limit. Otherwise, when they limit him in what he shall represent them in, and how far, none of them owns more than what they gave him commission to act. Without that one representer, there is no "one action of many men" — only the many actions of one multitude.


A Representative May Be Many Men, Made One by Majority of Voices

If the representative consists of many men, the voice of the greater number must be taken as the voice of them all. For if the lesser number pronounces, say, in the affirmative, and the greater in the negative, there will be more than enough negatives to cancel the affirmatives; and so the surplus of negatives, standing uncontradicted, is the only voice the representative has.


Representatives with an Even Number Are Often Useless

A representative of an even number — especially when the number is not large, so that the contradictory voices are frequently equal — is for that reason frequently mute and incapable of action. Yet in some cases, contradictory voices equal in number can still settle a question. In condemning or absolving, for instance, an equal split of votes absolves precisely by failing to condemn; but it does not, on the contrary, condemn by failing to absolve. For when a case has been heard, not to condemn is to absolve; but to say that not absolving is condemning is not true. The same holds in deliberating whether to carry out an execution at once or to defer it to another time: when the voices are equal, not decreeing execution is a decree of delay.


The Negative Voice

Or suppose the number is odd — three or more, whether men or assemblies — and each one has, by a negative voice, the authority to cancel the effect of all the affirmative voices of the rest. This number is no representative at all. Because of the diversity of men's opinions and interests, it becomes — often, and in matters of the greatest consequence — a mute person, unfit for many things, and above all unfit for the government of a multitude, especially in time of war.

Of authors there are two sorts. The first is the author simply so called, whom I defined above: the one who owns the action of another simply. The second is the one who owns an action or covenant of another conditionally — that is, he undertakes to do it himself if the other does not do it at, or before, a certain time. These conditional authors are generally called SURETIES — in Latin, fidejussores and sponsores; for debt in particular, praedes; and for appearance before a judge or magistrate, vades.
