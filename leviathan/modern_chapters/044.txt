Chapter 42: Of Ecclesiastical Power
(Part 2 of 5)

Who Can Be Excommunicated

For a man to be liable to excommunication at all, several conditions have to be met. First, he must be a member of some community — that is, of some lawful assembly, that is, of some Christian church that has the power to judge the matter for which he is to be excommunicated. Where there is no community, there can be no excommunication; and where there is no power to judge, there can be no power to pass sentence.

From this it follows that one church cannot excommunicate another. Either the two churches have equal power to excommunicate each other — in which case excommunication is not discipline, not an act of authority, but schism and the breakdown of charity — or one church is subordinate to the other in such a way that the two speak with a single voice, in which case they are really one church, and the part that gets excommunicated is no longer a church at all, but a scattered crowd of individual persons.

And because the sentence of excommunication amounts to advice not to keep company with the excommunicated person, not even to eat with him, the sentence has no effect if the one excommunicated is a sovereign prince or assembly. All subjects are bound by the law of nature to be in the company and presence of their own sovereign whenever he requires it. They cannot lawfully expel him from any place in his own dominion, sacred or ordinary; they cannot leave his dominion without his permission; and least of all, if he invites them to that honor, can they refuse to eat with him. As for other princes and states: since they are not parts of one and the same congregation, they need no sentence to keep them away from the excommunicated state. The very institution of a commonwealth, which unites many men into one community, also separates one community from another. So excommunication is not needed for keeping kings and states apart; it has no effect beyond what lies in the nature of politics itself — unless its effect is to goad princes into making war on one another.

Nor does the excommunication of a Christian subject have any effect, so long as he obeys the laws of his own sovereign, whether that sovereign is Christian or heathen. For if he believes that "Jesus is the Christ, he has the Spirit of God" (1 John 4:1), "and God dwells in him, and he in God" (1 John 4:15). But a man who has the Spirit of God, who dwells in God, in whom God dwells, can receive no harm from the excommunication of men. Therefore, whoever believes Jesus to be the Christ is free from all the dangers threatened against the excommunicated. And whoever does not believe it is no Christian in the first place. So a true and sincere Christian is not liable to excommunication. Neither is a professed Christian, until his hypocrisy shows in his behavior — that is, until his conduct goes against the law of his sovereign, which is the rule of behavior, and which Christ and his apostles have commanded us to obey. The church cannot judge a man's morals except by his external actions, and external actions can never be unlawful except when they break the law of the commonwealth.

If a man's father, or mother, or master is excommunicated, the children are still not forbidden to keep them company or to eat with them. To forbid that would, in most cases, be to oblige them not to eat at all, since they have no other way to get food — and it would authorize them to disobey their parents and masters, contrary to the teaching of the apostles.

In sum: the power of excommunication cannot be stretched any further than the purpose for which the apostles and pastors of the church hold their commission from our Savior — which is not to rule by command and coercion, but to teach and to direct men on the road to salvation in the world to come. A master of any science may give up on a student who stubbornly refuses to practice his rules; but he cannot accuse the student of injustice, because the student was never bound to obey him. In the same way, a teacher of Christian doctrine may abandon disciples who stubbornly persist in an unchristian life; but he cannot say they have wronged him, because they are not obliged to obey him. To a teacher who complains that way, one may apply God's answer to Samuel in a similar case: "They have not rejected thee, but me" (1 Samuel 8).

Excommunication, then, when it lacks the backing of the civil power — as it does whenever a Christian state or prince is excommunicated by some foreign authority — is without effect, and consequently ought to be without terror. The name fulmen excommunicationis — the thunderbolt of excommunication — came from a fantasy of the Bishop of Rome who first used it: that he was King of Kings, just as the pagans made Jupiter king of the gods and gave him, in their poems and pictures, a thunderbolt to subdue and punish any giants who dared deny his power. That fantasy rested on two errors. The first: that the kingdom of Christ is of this world — contrary to our Savior's own words, "My kingdom is not of this world." The second: that the Pope is Christ's vicar not only over his own subjects, but over all the Christians of the world — for which there is no basis in Scripture, and the contrary will be proved in its proper place.

Who Interpreted the Scriptures Before Civil Sovereigns Became Christian

When St. Paul came to Thessalonica, where there was a synagogue of the Jews, he, "as his manner was, went in unto them, and three Sabbath days reasoned with them out of the Scriptures, opening and alleging that Christ must needs have suffered and risen again from the dead, and that this Jesus whom he preached was the Christ" (Acts 17:2-3). The Scriptures mentioned here were the Scriptures of the Jews — the Old Testament. The men to whom he had to prove that Jesus was the Christ, risen from the dead, were also Jews, and they already believed those Scriptures were the word of God. The result: some of them believed (verse 4), and some did not (verse 5).

Why is that? They all believed the Scripture. Why did they not all believe alike — some accepting, others rejecting, the interpretation of St. Paul who cited it, each man interpreting it for himself? Here is the reason. St. Paul came to them with no legal commission, in the manner of a man who would not command but persuade. And persuade he must, either by miracles, as Moses did with the Israelites in Egypt, so that they might see his authority in God's works; or by reasoning from the Scripture they already accepted, so that they might see the truth of his doctrine in God's word. But whoever persuades by reasoning from written principles makes the person he is speaking to the judge — both of what those principles mean, and of how strong his inferences from them are. If these Jews of Thessalonica were not the judges of what St. Paul alleged out of Scripture, who else was? If St. Paul was the judge, why did he need to quote any passages at all to prove his doctrine? It would have been enough to say: I find it so in Scripture — that is, in your laws — of which I am the interpreter, sent by Christ. Therefore there was no interpreter of Scripture whose interpretation the Jews of Thessalonica were bound to accept. Every one of them was free to believe or not believe, according to whether the citations seemed to him to fit, or not to fit, the meaning of the passages cited.

And this holds generally, in every case in the world: whoever offers a proof makes the person he addresses the judge of that proof. As for the Jews in particular, they were bound by express words (Deuteronomy 17) to accept the resolution of all hard questions from the priests and judges of Israel then in office. But that applies to the Jews who were not yet converted.

For the conversion of the Gentiles, there was no point in citing Scriptures they did not believe. So the apostles labored by reason to refute their idolatry; and that done, they worked to persuade them to faith in Christ by their testimony of his life and resurrection. So at that stage there could not yet be any controversy about the authority to interpret Scripture — since no man, while still an unbeliever, was obliged to follow anyone's interpretation of any Scripture except his own sovereign's interpretation of the laws of his country.

Now consider the conversion itself, and ask what there was in it that could create such an obligation. Men were converted to nothing other than belief in what the apostles preached. And the apostles preached nothing but this: that Jesus was the Christ — that is, the King who was to save them and reign over them eternally in the world to come — and consequently that he was not dead but risen from the dead, gone up into heaven, and coming again one day to judge the world (which would also rise again to be judged), and to reward every man according to his works. None of them preached that he himself, or any other apostle, was an interpreter of Scripture whose interpretation everyone who became a Christian ought to take for law. To interpret the laws is part of administering a present kingdom — and the apostles had no present kingdom. They prayed, as all pastors have prayed ever since, "Thy kingdom come"; and they urged their converts to obey the pagan princes they then lived under.

The New Testament had not yet been published as one body. Each evangelist was the interpreter of his own gospel; each apostle, of his own epistle. And as for the Old Testament, our Savior himself said to the Jews, "Search the Scriptures; for in them ye think ye have eternal life, and they are they that testify of me" (John 5:39). If he had not meant for them to interpret the Scriptures themselves, he would not have told them to draw from them the proof that he was the Christ. He would either have interpreted the Scriptures himself, or referred them to the interpretation of the priests.

When a difficulty arose, the apostles and elders of the church assembled and settled what should be preached and taught, and how they should interpret the Scriptures to the people — but they did not take from the people the liberty to read the Scriptures and interpret them for themselves. The apostles sent various letters to the churches, and other writings for their instruction — which would have been pointless if the people had not been allowed to interpret them, that is, to consider what they meant. And as it was in the apostles' time, so it had to remain until there were pastors who could authorize an interpreter whose interpretation would generally be accepted as final. And that could not happen until kings were pastors, or pastors kings.

The Power to Make Scripture Law

There are two senses in which a writing may be called canonical. Canon means a rule; and a rule is a precept by which a man is guided and directed in any action whatsoever. Such precepts, even when given by a teacher to his student, or by a counselor to his friend, with no power to compel obedience, are still canons — because they are rules. But when they are given by someone whom the receiver is bound to obey, then those canons are not only rules but laws. The question here, therefore, is about the power to make the Scriptures — which are the rules of Christian faith — into laws.

The Ten Commandments

The first part of Scripture to become law was the Ten Commandments, written on two tablets of stone, delivered by God himself to Moses, and made known to the people by Moses. Before that time there was no written law of God. Having not yet chosen any people to be his peculiar kingdom, God had given men no law but the law of nature — that is, the precepts of natural reason, written in every man's own heart.

Of these two tablets, the first contains the law of sovereignty:

1. That they should not obey or honor the gods of other nations — "Non habebis Deos alienos coram me," that is, "Thou shalt not have for gods the gods that other nations worship, but only me." By this they were forbidden to obey or honor, as their king and governor, any god other than the one who spoke to them then through Moses, and afterward through the High Priest.

2. That they should not make any image to represent him. That is, they were not to pick out for themselves, in heaven or on earth, any representative of their own imagining, but were to obey Moses and Aaron, whom God had appointed to that office.

3. That they should not take the name of God in vain. That is, they should not speak rashly of their King, nor dispute his right, nor question the commissions of Moses and Aaron, his lieutenants.

4. That every seventh day they should abstain from their ordinary labor and spend that time doing him public honor.

The second tablet contains the duty of one man toward another: to honor parents; not to kill; not to commit adultery; not to steal; not to corrupt judgment by false witness; and finally, not even to plan in their hearts any injury to one another.

The question now is: who gave these written tablets the binding force of laws? There is no doubt that they were made laws by God himself. But a law does not bind anyone — is not law to anyone — except those who acknowledge it as the act of the sovereign. So how could the people of Israel, who were forbidden to approach the mountain to hear what God said to Moses, be obliged to obey all the laws Moses set before them? Some of those laws were indeed laws of nature — all of the second tablet — and so were to be acknowledged as God's laws not by the Israelites alone, but by all people. But for the laws peculiar to the Israelites, those of the first tablet, the question stands — except for this: immediately after the laws were proposed, the people had bound themselves to obey Moses, in these words: "Speak thou to us, and we will hear thee; but let not God speak to us, lest we die" (Exodus 20:19). It was therefore Moses alone at that time — and after him the High Priest, whom God (through Moses) declared should administer this his peculiar kingdom — who had the power on earth to make that short Scripture, the Ten Commandments, law in the commonwealth of Israel. But Moses, and Aaron, and the succeeding High Priests were the civil sovereigns. So up to this point, the canonizing of Scripture — the making of Scripture into law — belonged to the civil sovereign.

The Judicial and Levitical Law

The judicial law — the laws God prescribed to the magistrates of Israel as the rule for administering justice, and for the sentences and judgments they were to pronounce in disputes between man and man — and the Levitical law — the rule God prescribed concerning the rites and ceremonies of the priests and Levites — were all delivered to the people by Moses only; and so they too became laws by virtue of that same promise of obedience to Moses. Whether those laws were written down at the time, or simply dictated to the people by Moses by word of mouth after his forty days with God on the mountain, the text does not say. But they were all positive laws, equivalent to holy Scripture, and made canonical by Moses, the civil sovereign.

The Second Law

After the Israelites had come into the plains of Moab opposite Jericho, ready to enter the promised land, Moses added various other laws to the earlier ones. These are therefore called Deuteronomy — that is, the second laws. They are, as it is written, "the words of a covenant which the Lord commanded Moses to make with the children of Israel, besides the covenant which he made with them in Horeb" (Deuteronomy 29:1). For after explaining the earlier laws at the beginning of the book of Deuteronomy, he adds others, beginning at chapter 12 and continuing to the end of chapter 26 of the same book. This law the people were commanded to write upon great plastered stones at their crossing of the Jordan (Deuteronomy 27:1). This law was also written by Moses himself in a book and delivered into the hands of the priests and the elders of Israel (Deuteronomy 31:9), with the command (verse 26) that it be placed at the side of the Ark — for in the Ark itself there was nothing but the Ten Commandments. This was the law Moses commanded the kings of Israel to keep a copy of (Deuteronomy 17:18). And this is the law which, after being lost for a long time, was found again in the Temple in the days of Josiah, and by his authority received as the law of God. But note: both Moses at the writing of it, and Josiah at the recovery of it, held the civil sovereignty. So thus far, the power of making Scripture canonical lay with the civil sovereign.

Besides this book of the Law, no other book was received among the Jews as the law of God from the time of Moses until after the Captivity. The prophets, with few exceptions, lived during the Captivity itself, and the rest only a little before it; and so far were their prophecies from being generally received as laws that the prophets themselves were persecuted — partly by false prophets, and partly by kings whom those false prophets had seduced. And even this book, which Josiah confirmed as the law of God, was lost — along with all the history of the works of God — in the Captivity and the sack of Jerusalem, as appears from 2 Esdras 14:21: "Thy Law is burnt; therefore no man knoweth the things that are done of thee, or the works that shall begin." And before the Captivity, between the time the Law was first lost (which Scripture does not mention, but which was probably the time of Rehoboam, when Shishak king of Egypt carried off the treasures of the Temple — 1 Kings 14:26) and the time of Josiah, when it was found again, the Jews had no written word of God at all. They governed themselves by their own discretion, or by the direction of whoever each of them took for a prophet.

When the Old Testament Became Canon

From all this we may infer that the Scriptures of the Old Testament as we have them today were not canonical — were not a law to the Jews — until the renewal of their covenant with God at their return from the Captivity, and the restoration of their commonwealth under Esdras. From that time forward they were counted the law of the Jews, and as such were translated into Greek by seventy elders of Judaea, placed in the library of Ptolemy at Alexandria, and approved as the word of God. Now since Esdras was the High Priest, and the High Priest was their civil sovereign, it is plain that the Scriptures were never made laws except by the sovereign civil power.

The New Testament Began to Be Canon Under Christian Sovereigns

From the writings of the Fathers who lived before the Christian religion was received and authorized by the Emperor Constantine, we can see that the books we now have of the New Testament were held by the Christians of that age — except a few, whose small numbers led the rest to be called the Catholic Church, and the others heretics — to be the dictates of the Holy Spirit, and consequently the canon, or rule, of faith. Such was the reverence and esteem they had for their teachers — and in general, the reverence disciples bear toward their first masters, in every kind of doctrine they receive from them, is no small thing. So there is no doubt that when St. Paul wrote to the churches he had converted, or any other apostle or disciple of Christ wrote to those who had already embraced Christ, the recipients received those writings as the true Christian doctrine. But in that age it was not the power and authority of the teacher that made men receive the writings — it was the faith of the hearer. It was not the apostles who made their own writings canonical; every convert made them canonical to himself.

But the question here is not what any Christian made a law or canon to himself — which he might reject again by the same right he received it. The question is what was made a canon to them in such a way that they could not, without injustice, act contrary to it. That the New Testament should be canonical in this sense — a law — in any place where the law of the commonwealth had not made it so, is contrary to the very nature of a law. For a law, as has already been shown, is the command of that man or assembly to whom we have given sovereign authority to make such rules for the direction of our actions as he thinks fit, and to punish us when we do anything contrary to them. When any other man offers us rules the sovereign ruler has not prescribed, those rules are nothing but counsel and advice — which the person counseled may refuse to follow without injustice, whether the advice is good or bad; and which, when it is contrary to the laws already established, he cannot follow without injustice, no matter how good he thinks it is.

I say he cannot in that case follow it in his actions, nor in his conversation with other men — though he may, without blame, believe his private teachers, wish he had the liberty to practice their advice, and wish it were publicly received as law. For internal faith is by its own nature invisible, and consequently exempt from all human jurisdiction; but the words and actions that proceed from it, when they are breaches of our civil obedience, are injustice before both God and man.

Since, then, our Savior has denied that his kingdom is in this world; since he has said he came not to judge but to save the world — he has not subjected us to any laws other than the laws of the commonwealth. That is: the Jews to the law of Moses (which he says he came not to destroy but to fulfill — Matthew 5), other nations to the laws of their various sovereigns, and all men to the laws of nature. The keeping of these laws both he himself and his apostles recommended to us in their teaching as a necessary condition of being admitted by him, at the last day, into his eternal kingdom, where there will be protection and life everlasting. Since, then, our Savior and his apostles left us no new laws to bind us in this world, but new doctrine to prepare us for the next, the books of the New Testament — which contain that doctrine — were not, until obedience to them was commanded by those God has given power on earth to be legislators, obligatory canons, that is, laws. They were only good and safe advice for the direction of sinners on the road to salvation, which every man might take or refuse at his own peril, without injustice.

Consider, too, the commission our Savior Christ gave to his apostles and disciples: to proclaim his kingdom — not present, but to come; to teach all nations; to baptize those who believed; to enter the houses of those who received them; and where they were not received, to shake the dust off their feet against that house — but not to call down fire from heaven to destroy them, and not to compel them to obedience by the sword. In all of this there is nothing of power; only persuasion. He sent them out as sheep among wolves, not as kings to their subjects. They had no commission to make laws, but to obey, and to teach obedience to, laws already made. Consequently they could not make their writings obligatory canons without the help of the sovereign civil power. And therefore the Scripture of the New Testament is law only where the lawful civil power has made it law. And there, the king or sovereign makes it a law to himself as well — by which he subjects himself not to the teacher or apostle who converted him, but to God himself and his Son Jesus Christ, as immediately as the apostles did.

Could Church Councils Make Scripture Law?

What might seem to give the New Testament the force of law, for those who had embraced Christian doctrine in the times and places of persecution, is the decrees the Christians made among themselves in their synods. We read the formula of the council of the apostles, the elders, and the whole church (Acts 15:28), in this style: "It seemed good to the Holy Ghost, and to us, to lay upon you no greater burden than these necessary things," and so on — a style that seems to signify a power to lay a burden on those who had received their doctrine. And "to lay a burden on someone" sounds like the same thing as "to oblige" — so the acts of that council, one might think, were laws to the Christians of that time.

Nevertheless, they were no more laws than these other precepts are: "Repent"; "Be baptized"; "Keep the commandments"; "Believe the gospel"; "Come unto me"; "Sell all that thou hast, give it to the poor"; "Follow me." These are not commands but invitations — callings of men to Christianity — like that of Isaiah 55:1: "Ho, every man that thirsteth, come ye to the waters; come, and buy wine and milk without money."

For first, the apostles' power was no different from our Savior's own: to invite men to embrace the kingdom of God — a kingdom which they themselves acknowledged to be not present, but to come. And those who have no kingdom can make no laws. And second, if the acts of their council were laws, then they could not be disobeyed without sin. But we read nowhere that those who rejected the doctrine of Christ sinned in doing so — only that they died in their sins; that is, that their sins against the laws they already owed obedience to were not pardoned. And those laws were the laws of nature and the civil laws of the state, to which every Christian man had submitted himself by pact. The "burden" the apostles could lay on those they had converted is therefore not to be understood as laws, but as conditions — conditions proposed to those who sought salvation, which they might accept or refuse at their own peril, without committing any new sin, though not without the hazard of being condemned and shut out of the kingdom of God for their sins past. That is why St. John does not say of unbelievers that the wrath of God shall come upon them, but that "the wrath of God remaineth upon them"; not that they shall be condemned, but that "they are condemned already" (John 3:36, 3:18). And it is impossible to conceive that the benefit of faith is the remission of sins unless we also conceive that the damage of unbelief is the retention of those same sins.

But then, someone may ask, what was the point of the apostles, and the church's pastors after them, meeting together to agree on what doctrine should be taught, both for faith and for conduct, if no one was obliged to observe their decrees? The answer: the apostles and elders of that council were obliged — by their very entrance into it — to teach the doctrine concluded and decreed there, so far as no prior law they owed obedience to stood against it. But all other Christians were not obliged to observe what they taught. For though the members of the council could deliberate about what each of them should teach, they could not deliberate about what others should do — unless their assembly had legislative power, which none can have but civil sovereigns. For though God is the sovereign of all the world, we are not bound to take for his law whatever any man propounds in his name, nor anything contrary to the civil law, which God has expressly commanded us to obey.

Since, then, the acts of the council of the apostles were not laws at the time, but counsels, much less are the acts of any other doctors or councils since then laws — if those councils assembled without the authority of the civil sovereign. Consequently, the books of the New Testament, though the most perfect rules of Christian doctrine, could not be made laws by any authority other than that of kings or sovereign assemblies.

The first council that made the Scriptures we now have into the canon is not extant. The collection attributed to the first Bishop of Rome after St. Peter is open to question: though the canonical books are listed there, the words "Sint vobis omnibus Clericis et Laicis Libri venerandi" — "Let these books be venerated by all of you, clergy and laity" — contain a distinction between clergy and laity that was not in use so near St. Peter's time. The first surviving council that settled the canonical Scripture is that of Laodicea (canon 59), which forbids the reading of any other books in the churches. But that is a mandate addressed not to every Christian, but only to those who had the authority to read anything publicly in church — that is, to churchmen only.

Who Appointed Church Officers in the Time of the Apostles

Among church officers in the time of the apostles, some were magisterial, some ministerial. Magisterial were the offices of preaching the gospel of the kingdom of God to unbelievers; of administering the sacraments and divine service; and of teaching the rules of faith and conduct to the converted. Ministerial was the office of deacons — those appointed to manage the worldly necessities of the church, in the days when Christians lived on a common fund of money raised from the voluntary contributions of the faithful.

Among the magisterial officers, the first and principal were the apostles. At first there were only twelve, chosen and appointed by our Savior himself; and their office was not only to preach, teach, and baptize, but also to be martyrs — witnesses of our Savior's resurrection. This testimony was the specific, essential mark distinguishing the apostleship from every other church office. To be an apostle, a man had either to have seen our Savior after his resurrection, or to have kept company with him before it and seen his works and the other evidence of his divinity, so that he could count as a sufficient witness. That is why, at the election of a new apostle to replace Judas Iscariot, St. Peter says: "Of these men that have companied with us, all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out among us, beginning from the baptism of John unto that same day that he was taken up from us, must one be ordained to be a witness with us of his resurrection" (Acts 1:21-22). That word "must" implies a necessary property of an apostle: to have kept company with the first and original apostles during the time our Savior showed himself in the flesh.

Matthias: Made an Apostle by the Congregation

The first apostle not appointed by Christ during his time on earth was Matthias, and he was chosen in this manner. About a hundred and twenty Christians were assembled in Jerusalem (Acts 1:15). They nominated two men, Joseph the Just and Matthias (verse 23), and had lots drawn; "and the lot fell upon Matthias, and he was numbered with the apostles" (verse 26). So here we see that the ordination of this apostle was the act of the congregation — not of St. Peter, nor of the eleven, except as members of the assembly.

Paul and Barnabas: Made Apostles by the Church of Antioch

After Matthias, no other apostles were ever ordained except Paul and Barnabas, and that was done (as we read in Acts 13:1-3) in this manner: "There were in the church that was at Antioch certain prophets and teachers; as Barnabas, and Simeon that was called Niger, and Lucius of Cyrene, and Manaen, which had been brought up with Herod the Tetrarch, and Saul. As they ministered unto the Lord, and fasted, the Holy Ghost said, 'Separate me Barnabas and Saul for the work whereunto I have called them.' And when they had fasted and prayed, and laid their hands on them, they sent them away."

From this it is clear that though they were called by the Holy Spirit, their calling was declared to them, and their mission authorized, by the particular church of Antioch. That this calling was to the apostleship is shown by the fact that both of them are called apostles (Acts 14:14). And that it was by virtue of this act of the church of Antioch that they were apostles, St. Paul declares plainly (Romans 1:1), for he uses the very word the Holy Spirit used at his calling: he styles himself "an apostle separated unto the gospel of God," alluding to the words of the Holy Spirit, "Separate me Barnabas and Saul."

But since the work of an apostle was to be a witness of the resurrection of Christ, someone may ask how St. Paul — who never kept company with our Savior before his passion — could know that he had risen. The answer is easy: our Savior himself appeared to him on the road to Damascus, from heaven, after his ascension, "and chose him for a vessel to bear his name before the Gentiles, and kings, and the children of Israel." Having seen the Lord after his passion, Paul was a competent witness of his resurrection. As for Barnabas, he was a disciple before the passion. It is therefore evident that Paul and Barnabas were apostles — and yet they were chosen and authorized not by the first apostles alone, but by the church of Antioch, just as Matthias was chosen and authorized by the church of Jerusalem.
