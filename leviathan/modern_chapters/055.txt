Chapter 47: Of the Benefit That Comes from This Darkness, and Who Collects It

Whoever Benefits from a Deed Is Presumed to Be Its Author

Cicero makes honorable mention of one of the Cassii, a stern judge among the Romans, for a custom he kept in criminal cases. When the testimony of the witnesses was not sufficient, he would ask the accusers: Cui bono? — who benefits? What profit, honor, or other satisfaction did the accused gain, or expect to gain, from the deed? For among all presumptions, none points to the author of an action so plainly as the benefit that comes of it.

By that same rule I intend, in this place, to examine who it can be that has kept the people of this part of Christendom possessed for so long by these doctrines — doctrines contrary to the peaceful societies of mankind.

The Church of Rome First Taught That the Church on Earth Is the Kingdom of God

Begin with the first error: that the present church, still militant here on earth, is the kingdom of God — meaning the kingdom of glory, the Promised Land itself, and not the kingdom of grace, which is only a promise of the land. To this error are attached the following worldly benefits.

First, the pastors and teachers of the church are entitled by it, as God's public ministers, to a right of governing the church — and consequently, since the church and the commonwealth are the same persons, to be rectors and governors of the commonwealth. It was by this title that the Pope prevailed upon the subjects of all Christian princes to believe that to disobey him was to disobey Christ himself, and, in every quarrel between him and other princes, to abandon their lawful sovereigns — charmed by the words "spiritual power." Which is, in effect, a universal monarchy over all Christendom.

For the popes were first invested with the right of being supreme teachers of Christian doctrine by Christian emperors, and under them, within the limits of the Roman Empire — as they themselves acknowledge — under the title of Pontifex Maximus, the chief priest of Rome, an officer subject to the civil state. But after the empire was divided and dissolved, it was not hard to press upon a people already subject to them another title: the right of St. Peter — not only to keep their pretended power intact, but to extend it over those same Christian provinces, even though they were no longer united in the empire of Rome.

This benefit of a universal monarchy — considering how much men desire to rule — is presumption enough that the popes who claimed it, and who enjoyed it for a long time, were the authors of the doctrine by which it was obtained: namely, that the church now on earth is the kingdom of Christ. For grant that, and it must follow that Christ has some lieutenant among us, through whom we are to be told what his commandments are.

After certain churches renounced this universal power of the Pope, you would reasonably expect the civil sovereigns in all those churches to have recovered the portion of it that had been their own right, in their own hands, all along — before they so carelessly let it go. And in England that is, in effect, what happened — except that the men through whom the kings administered the government of religion, by insisting that they held their employment by God's own right, seemed to claim, if not supremacy, then at least independence from the civil power. And they only seemed to claim it, since they acknowledged the King's right to strip them of the exercise of their functions whenever he pleased.

And the Presbyterians Kept Teaching It

But in those places where the presbytery took over that office, many other doctrines of the Church of Rome were indeed forbidden to be taught — yet this one doctrine, that the kingdom of Christ has already come, and that it began at the resurrection of our Savior, was retained. But cui bono? What profit did they expect from it? The same profit the popes expected: sovereign power over the people.

For what does it mean for men to excommunicate their lawful king, if not to bar him from every place of God's public service in his own kingdom — and to resist him with force when he tries with force to correct them? And what does it mean to excommunicate any person without authority from the civil sovereign, if not to take away his lawful liberty — that is, to usurp an unlawful power over their brethren? The authors of this darkness in religion, therefore, are the Roman and the Presbyterian clergy.

Infallibility

Under this same head I place all the doctrines that serve them to keep possession of this spiritual sovereignty once it has been gotten. First, that the Pope, in his public capacity, cannot err. For who is there that believes this to be true, and will not readily obey him in whatever he commands?

The Subjection of Bishops

Secondly, that all other bishops, in whatever commonwealth they live, hold their right neither immediately from God nor mediately from their civil sovereigns, but from the Pope. This is a doctrine by which every Christian commonwealth comes to contain many powerful men — for that is what bishops are — who depend on the Pope and owe obedience to him, even though he is a foreign prince. By this means he is able, as he has done many times, to raise a civil war against any state that will not submit to be governed according to his pleasure and interest.

Exemption of the Clergy

Thirdly, the exemption of these bishops, and of all other priests, and of all monks and friars, from the power of the civil laws. By this means a great part of every commonwealth enjoys the benefit of the laws and the protection of the civil state, yet pays no share of the public expense, and is not liable, as other subjects are, to the penalties due for its crimes. Consequently these men stand in fear of no one but the Pope, and adhere to him alone, to uphold his universal monarchy.

The Names "Sacerdotes" and "Sacrifices"

Fourthly, giving their priests the name of sacerdotes — that is, sacrificers — when in the New Testament they are nothing more than presbyters, that is, elders. "Sacrificer" was the title of the civil sovereign and his public ministers among the Jews, in the days when God was their king. Likewise, making the Lord's Supper a sacrifice serves to make the people believe the Pope has the same power over all Christians that Moses and Aaron had over the Jews — that is to say, all power, both civil and ecclesiastical, just as the High Priest had then.

Making Marriage a Sacrament

Fifthly, teaching that matrimony is a sacrament gives the clergy the judging of which marriages are lawful — and thereby of which children are legitimate — and consequently of the right of succession to hereditary kingdoms.

The Celibacy of Priests

Sixthly, the denial of marriage to priests serves to secure this power of the Pope over kings. For if a king is a priest, he cannot marry and pass his kingdom to his posterity. And if he is not a priest, then the Pope claims this ecclesiastical authority over him, and over his people.

Auricular Confession

Seventhly, from auricular confession — confession whispered privately into the priest's ear — they obtain, for the security of their power, better intelligence of the designs of princes and great persons in the civil state than those princes can ever have of the designs of the ecclesiastical state.

Canonizing Saints and Declaring Martyrs

Eighthly, by the canonization of saints, and by declaring who are martyrs, they secure their power by leading simple men into obstinacy against the laws and commands of their civil sovereigns — obstinacy even to the death — whenever the Pope's excommunication declares those sovereigns heretics or enemies of the church; that is, as they interpret it, enemies of the Pope.

Transubstantiation, Penance, Absolution

Ninthly, they secure the same power by what they ascribe to every priest: the power of making Christ; the power of ordaining penance; and the power of remitting and retaining sins.

Purgatory, Indulgences, External Works

Tenthly, by the doctrine of purgatory, of justification by external works, and of indulgences, the clergy is enriched.

Demonology and Exorcism

Eleventhly, by their demonology, and the use of exorcism, and the other things belonging to it, they keep the people — or think they keep them — in greater awe of their power.

Scholastic Theology

Lastly, the metaphysics, ethics, and politics of Aristotle, and the frivolous distinctions, barbarous terms, and obscure language of the Scholastics, taught in the universities — all of which were erected and regulated by the Pope's authority — serve them to keep these errors from being detected, and to make men mistake the ignis fatuus — the fool's fire, the will-o'-the-wisp — of vain philosophy for the light of the Gospel.

Who the Authors of Spiritual Darkness Are

If these did not suffice, other dark doctrines of theirs could be added, whose profit plainly flows toward the same ends: toward setting up an unlawful power over the lawful sovereigns of Christian people; or toward sustaining that power once it is set up; or toward the worldly riches, honor, and authority of those who sustain it. And therefore, by the rule of cui bono stated above, we may justly pronounce the authors of all this spiritual darkness: the Pope, and the Roman clergy, and all those besides who labor to settle in the minds of men this erroneous doctrine — that the church now on earth is the kingdom of God spoken of in the Old and New Testament.

But the emperors, and the other Christian sovereigns, under whose government these errors and the like encroachments of churchmen upon their office first crept in — to the disturbance of their possessions and of the peace of their subjects — are not blameless either. Though they suffered it through lack of foresight of the consequences, and lack of insight into the designs of their teachers, they may nevertheless be counted accessories to their own damage, and to the public's. For without their authority, no seditious doctrine could have been publicly preached in the first place. I say they could have stopped it at the beginning.

But once the people were possessed by those spiritual men, no human remedy remained that anyone could invent. As for the remedies God provides — and he never fails, in his good time, to destroy all the machinations of men against the truth — for those we must wait upon his good pleasure. Many times he allows the prosperity of his enemies, together with their ambition, to grow to such a height that its very violence opens the eyes that the caution of their predecessors had sealed shut, and makes men, by grasping too much, lose their hold on everything — as Peter's net was broken by the struggling of too great a multitude of fish. Whereas the impatience of those who tried to resist such encroachment before their subjects' eyes were opened only increased the power they were resisting.

I do not, therefore, blame the Emperor Frederick for holding the stirrup for our countryman Pope Adrian. Such was the disposition of his subjects at the time that if he had not done it, he was not likely to have succeeded in the empire. But I do blame those who, in the beginning, when their power was still whole, allowed such doctrines to be forged in the universities of their own dominions — and so held the stirrup for all the succeeding popes, while they mounted into the thrones of all Christian sovereigns, to ride and tire both them and their people at their pleasure.

But as the inventions of men are woven, so also they are unraveled: the path is the same, but the order is reversed. The web begins at the first elements of power — wisdom, humility, sincerity, and the other virtues of the apostles, whom the people they converted obeyed out of reverence, not obligation. Their consciences were free, and their words and actions were subject to none but the civil power.

Afterwards, as the flocks of Christ increased, the presbyters assembled to consider what they should teach; and by binding themselves to teach nothing against the decrees of their assemblies, they made it be thought that the people were bound to follow their doctrine too — and when anyone refused, they refused to keep him company. That was what excommunication then meant: the man was shunned not as an infidel, but as disobedient. And this was the first knot upon Christian liberty.

Then, as the number of presbyters grew, the presbyters of the chief city or province got themselves authority over the parish presbyters, and appropriated to themselves the name of bishops. And this was the second knot on Christian liberty.

Lastly, the Bishop of Rome, on the strength of the imperial city, took upon himself an authority over all the other bishops of the empire — partly by the will of the emperors themselves and by the title of Pontifex Maximus, and at last, when the emperors had grown weak, by the privileges of St. Peter. And this was the third and last knot, and the whole synthesis and construction of the pontifical power.

And therefore the analysis — the untying — proceeds by the same path, but beginning with the knot that was tied last. We can watch it happen in the dissolution of the praeterpolitical church government in England — the church government that stood outside, and above, the civil order.

First, the power of the popes was dissolved totally by Queen Elizabeth; and the bishops, who before had exercised their functions in the right of the Pope, afterwards exercised them in the right of the Queen and her successors — though by keeping the phrase jure divino — "by divine right" — they were thought to claim them by immediate right from God. And so the first knot was untied: the one that had been tied last.

After this, the Presbyterians in England lately obtained the putting down of episcopacy. And so the second knot was dissolved.

And almost at the same time, the power was taken from the Presbyterians too. And so we are reduced to the independency of the primitive Christians: to follow Paul, or Cephas, or Apollos, every man as he likes best. Which — if it comes without contention, and without measuring the doctrine of Christ by our affection for the person of his minister (the fault the Apostle rebuked in the Corinthians) — is perhaps the best arrangement of all. First, because there ought to be no power over the consciences of men but the power of the Word itself, working faith in every one of us — not always according to the purpose of those who plant and water, but according to the purpose of God himself, who gives the increase. And secondly, because it is unreasonable for those who teach that there is danger in every little error to demand that a man endowed with reason of his own follow the reason of some other man, or the majority vote of many other men — which is little better than staking his salvation on a game of cross and pile: a coin toss.

Nor should those teachers be displeased at this loss of their ancient authority. For no one should know better than they that power is preserved by the same virtues by which it is acquired — by wisdom, humility, clearness of doctrine, and sincerity of life. Not by suppressing the natural sciences and the morality of natural reason. Not by obscure language. Not by claiming more knowledge than they let appear. Not by pious frauds. Nor by the other faults that, in the pastors of God's church, are not merely faults but scandals — stumbling blocks apt to make men trip, one time or another, into throwing off their authority altogether.

Comparing the Papacy with the Kingdom of Fairies

But after this doctrine — "that the church now militant is the kingdom of God spoken of in the Old and New Testament" — was received in the world, the ambition and campaigning for the offices that belong to it, and especially for that great office of being Christ's lieutenant, and the pomp of those who obtained the principal public charges in it, became by degrees so obvious that men lost the inward reverence due to the pastoral function. So much so that the wisest men, among those who had any power in the civil state, needed nothing but the authority of their princes to refuse them any further obedience.

For from the time the Bishop of Rome got himself acknowledged as universal bishop, by pretense of succession to St. Peter, their whole hierarchy — their kingdom of darkness — may be compared, not unfittingly, to the kingdom of fairies: that is, to the old wives' tales of England about ghosts and spirits, and the tricks they play in the night. And if a man considers the origin of this great ecclesiastical dominion, he will easily perceive that the Papacy is no other than the ghost of the deceased Roman Empire, sitting crowned upon the grave thereof. For so the Papacy started up, all of a sudden, out of the ruins of that heathen power.

The language they use, too, both in their churches and in their public acts, is Latin — which is not the common speech of any nation now in the world. What is it but the ghost of the old Roman language?

The fairies, in whatever nation they keep company, have but one universal king, whom some of our poets call King Oberon — but whom Scripture calls Beelzebub, prince of demons. The churchmen likewise, in whatever dominions they are found, acknowledge but one universal king: the Pope.

The churchmen are spiritual men and ghostly fathers. The fairies are spirits and ghosts. Fairies and ghosts inhabit darkness, solitudes, and graves. The churchmen walk in obscurity of doctrine, in monasteries, churches, and churchyards.

The churchmen have their cathedral churches, which, in whatever town they are erected, have the power — by virtue of holy water, and certain charms called exorcisms — to make those towns cities, that is to say, seats of empire. The fairies also have their enchanted castles, and certain gigantic ghosts that lord it over all the regions round about them.

The fairies cannot be seized and brought to answer for the harm they do. So also the churchmen vanish away from the tribunals of civil justice.

The churchmen take from young men the use of reason, by certain charms compounded of metaphysics, and miracles, and traditions, and abused Scripture — after which they are good for nothing else but to execute what the churchmen command them. The fairies likewise are said to take young children out of their cradles and change them into natural fools — which the common people therefore call elves, and which are prone to mischief.

In what shop or workshop the fairies make their enchantments, the old wives have not determined. But the workshops of the clergy are well enough known: they are the universities, which received their discipline from papal authority.

When the fairies are displeased with somebody, they are said to send their elves to pinch him. The churchmen, when they are displeased with any civil state, likewise make their elves — that is, their superstitious, enchanted subjects — pinch their princes, by preaching sedition; or they use one prince, enchanted with promises, to pinch another.

The fairies do not marry; but there are among them incubi, which nevertheless couple with flesh and blood. The priests also do not marry.

The churchmen skim the cream of the land, by the donations of ignorant men who stand in awe of them, and by tithes. So also it is in the fable of the fairies, that they slip into the dairies and feast upon the cream, which they skim from the milk.

What kind of money is current in the kingdom of fairies, the story does not record. But the churchmen, in what they take in, accept the same money we do — though when they have any payment to make, they pay it in canonizations, indulgences, and masses.

To these and other such resemblances between the Papacy and the kingdom of fairies, one more may be added: that just as the fairies have no existence except in the fancies of ignorant people, rising out of the traditions of old wives or old poets, so the spiritual power of the Pope — outside the bounds of his own civil dominion — consists only in the fear that seduced people stand in of his excommunication, upon hearing false miracles, false traditions, and false interpretations of Scripture.

It was not, therefore, a very difficult matter for Henry VIII to cast them out by his exorcism, nor for Queen Elizabeth by hers. But who knows whether this spirit of Rome — now gone out, and walking by missions through the dry places of China, Japan, and the Indies, which yield him little fruit — may not return? Or rather, whether an assembly of spirits worse than he may not enter and inhabit this clean-swept house, and make the end of it worse than the beginning? For the Roman clergy are not the only ones who claim that the kingdom of God is of this world, and who claim, on that ground, a power in it distinct from the power of the civil state.

And this is all I had a design to say concerning the doctrine of POLITICS. When I have reviewed it, I shall willingly expose it to the censure of my country.
