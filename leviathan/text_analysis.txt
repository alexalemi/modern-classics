TEXT ANALYSIS AND TRANSLATION STRATEGY
Leviathan, or The Matter, Forme, & Power of a Common-Wealth Ecclesiasticall
and Civill — Thomas Hobbes, 1651

================================================================================
1. WHAT THIS BOOK IS
================================================================================

Leviathan is the founding masterpiece of modern political philosophy, written
during the chaos of the English Civil War. Hobbes builds, from the ground up,
an argument for why human beings need government: starting from physics and
psychology (Part I: Of Man), he derives the social contract and the necessity
of a sovereign power (Part II: Of Common-wealth), then spends the second half
of the book (Parts III and IV) arguing that Scripture, rightly interpreted,
supports his political conclusions, and attacking what he sees as the
superstitions and power-grabs of organized churches.

The four parts:
  I.   OF MAN (Intro, ch. 1-16) — perception, language, reason, the passions,
       the state of nature, the laws of nature, persons and representation.
  II.  OF COMMON-WEALTH (ch. 17-31) — the social contract, the rights and
       duties of sovereigns, kinds of government, law, crime, punishment,
       and what destroys states.
  III. OF A CHRISTIAN COMMON-WEALTH (ch. 32-43) — biblical interpretation:
       what Scripture actually says about spirits, miracles, heaven, hell,
       prophecy, and church authority. Hobbes' goal: show that no church can
       claim authority over the civil sovereign.
  IV.  OF THE KINGDOME OF DARKNESSE (ch. 44-47) — a polemic against
       "spiritual darkness": misread scripture, pagan leftovers, and vain
       philosophy (especially Aristotle as filtered through Scholasticism),
       and an analysis of who benefits from keeping people in the dark.
  Plus a "Review and Conclusion" addressing the political crisis of 1651:
  when may a citizen submit to a new government?

This is a work of serious historical and philosophical importance. The
discussions of war, rebellion, punishment, and religious authority are
17th-century political theory and must be rendered faithfully as such — they
are the intellectual foundation of modern ideas about the state.

================================================================================
2. HOBBES' STYLE — WHAT MAKES HIS VOICE HIS
================================================================================

- GEOMETRIC METHOD. Hobbes idolized Euclid. He defines terms, then builds
  chains of reasoning from those definitions. Definitions are load-bearing:
  when Hobbes defines POWER, HONOUR, COVENANT, LIBERTY, etc., that exact
  word choice matters for the next 30 chapters. Keep definitions crisp and
  visually prominent.
- THE MASTER METAPHOR. The state is an "Artificial Man" — the Leviathan —
  with sovereignty as its soul, magistrates as joints, reward and punishment
  as nerves, money as blood, colonies as children, civil war as death.
  Hobbes also loves mechanical metaphors (the heart a spring, nerves strings,
  joints wheels). Preserve and even savor these images.
- DRY, CUTTING WIT. Hobbes is funny — bluntly, scornfully funny, especially
  about Scholastic philosophers ("insignificant speech"), university
  pedantry, and clerical ambition. The modern version should keep the
  knife-edge: confident, plain-spoken, occasionally sardonic. Think of a
  brilliant, slightly intimidating professor who has zero patience for
  nonsense but genuinely wants you to follow the argument.
- LONG PERIODIC SENTENCES. Sentences routinely run 80-120 words with nested
  parentheticals. Break them into 2-4 modern sentences, but keep the logical
  connectives explicit — Hobbes' "For... And therefore... From whence..."
  chains ARE the argument. Use "Because," "So," "That's why," "And that
  means" to keep the chain visible.
- LISTS AND TAXONOMIES. Hobbes constantly enumerates (kinds of madness,
  kinds of honor, causes of crime). Render these as clean lists or tight
  sequences; don't flatten them into mush.

================================================================================
3. FORMAT CONVENTIONS IN THE SOURCE FILES
================================================================================

- The Gutenberg text renders Hobbes' MARGIN NOTES as short standalone
  title-case lines (e.g., "From Equality Proceeds Diffidence", "From
  Diffidence Warre"). TREAT THESE AS SECTION SUBHEADINGS: keep them, on
  their own line, modernized (e.g., "Equality Breeds Mistrust" /
  "Mistrust Breeds War"). They are the book's built-in outline and make
  the modern text dramatically more navigable.
- Some files are PARTS of one long chapter, marked "(PART n OF k)" under
  the chapter heading. Translate the part completely; do NOT write an
  introduction or conclusion for the chapter as a whole — just render the
  text of that part. Part boundaries fall at paragraph breaks.
- Scripture citations like (Job 41.24) or (Exod. 20.19) stay as-is in
  modern citation style: (Job 41:24), (Exodus 20:19).
- Hobbes' erratic capitalization (Common-wealth, Soveraigne, NATURE) was
  emphasis. Use normal modern capitalization; for genuinely load-bearing
  defined terms on first definition, capitals or emphasis are fine
  (e.g., "This is what I'll call the RIGHT OF NATURE...").

================================================================================
4. VOCABULARY MAP (use consistently in every chapter)
================================================================================

  Common-wealth            -> commonwealth (keep this word — it's his term of
                              art; gloss once early as "the state")
  Soveraigne / Soveraignty -> sovereign / sovereignty (keep; gloss early as
                              "the supreme power, whether a king or assembly")
  Covenant                 -> covenant (keep, gloss as "binding agreement");
                              "contract" where Hobbes himself uses contract
  Right of Nature          -> right of nature
  Law of Nature (Lex Naturalis) -> law of nature
  State/Condition of Meer Nature -> the state of nature
  Diffidence               -> distrust / mistrust
  Felicity                 -> happiness
  Glory / Vain-glory       -> glory / vanity (pride in ch. 13's three causes:
                              competition, distrust, glory — keep "glory")
  Fancy                    -> imagination (but "Fancy" = wit in ch. 8 context:
                              "creative wit / quickness of imagination")
  Trayne of Imaginations   -> train of thought / chain of thoughts
  Endeavour (technical, ch. 6) -> "endeavor — the tiny internal beginnings
                              of motion" (keep his technical term, gloss it)
  Appetite / Aversion      -> desire / aversion
  Contemne                 -> despise, disregard
  Lucre                    -> profit, gain
  Praeter-naturall         -> supernatural
  Worship (Cultus)         -> worship
  Person / Author / Actor  -> keep all three (ch. 16 terms of art; gloss:
                              actor = the one who acts, author = the one
                              whose words/actions they are)
  By Institution / By Acquisition -> by agreement / by conquest (give the
                              original term once, then use modern gloss)
  Dominion Paternall/Despoticall -> parental dominion / dominion by conquest
  Mixt Government          -> mixed government
  Publique Minister        -> public minister/official
  Counsell vs Command      -> counsel (advice) vs command (keep distinction
                              sharp; it's the point of ch. 25)
  Civill Law               -> civil law (the law of the commonwealth)
  Aequity                  -> equity, fairness
  Daemon / Daemonology     -> demon / demonology
  Ghostly men              -> clergy ("ghostly" = spiritual; keep Hobbes'
                              jab where he puns on it)
  Schoole-men / the Schools-> the Scholastics / medieval university
                              philosophers
  Kingdome of Darknesse    -> Kingdom of Darkness (his title-metaphor; keep)
  Bishop of Rome           -> Pope / Bishop of Rome (match his usage; he
                              alternates deliberately)

================================================================================
5. FAMOUS PASSAGES — PRESERVE NEARLY VERBATIM
================================================================================

These lines are quoted everywhere; modern readers should meet them in close
to their original form (lightly modernized spelling only), ideally with the
flow arranged so they land as the punchline they are:

- "the life of man, solitary, poore, nasty, brutish, and short" (ch. 13)
  -> "...and the life of man: solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."
- "Warre of every man against every man" / "Bellum omnium contra omnes"
  (ch. 13) -> "a war of every man against every man"
- "Force, and Fraud, are in warre the two Cardinall vertues" (ch. 13)
- "Covenants, without the Sword, are but Words" (ch. 17)
- "that great LEVIATHAN, or rather (to speake more reverently) that Mortall
  God" (ch. 17)
- "The Papacy is no other, than the Ghost of the deceased Romane Empire,
  sitting crowned upon the grave thereof" (ch. 47)
- The Introduction's opening: "Nature... is by the art of man... imitated,
  that it can make an Artificial Animal" — keep the watch/spring/wheels
  imagery intact.

================================================================================
6. CONTENT THAT NEEDS CAREFUL, SCHOLARLY HANDLING
================================================================================

- POLITICAL VIOLENCE & REBELLION. Hobbes analyzes war, rebellion, conquest,
  punishment (including capital punishment), and assassination as a
  political theorist writing amid an actual civil war. Render these
  discussions faithfully and completely, in the measured voice of a scholar
  explaining a historical argument. Never sensationalize; never bowdlerize.
  The argument is consistently AGAINST rebellion and FOR civil peace —
  Hobbes' entire project is to prevent civil war. Keeping that framing
  explicit (as Hobbes does) is both accurate and appropriate.
- RELIGION. Parts III-IV contain aggressive criticism of Catholicism,
  Presbyterianism, and Scholastic theology, plus deflationary readings of
  miracles, demons, hell, and inspiration. Translate Hobbes' arguments as
  HIS arguments, in his voice — the modern text says what Hobbes says,
  vividly, without the translator endorsing or softening it. These chapters
  are landmark documents in the history of biblical criticism and
  church-state theory.
- DATED SCIENCE & ETHNOGRAPHY. Hobbes' physiology (sense as "pressure,"
  animal spirits) and his reference to "the savage people in many places
  of America" reflect 1651. Render his meaning accurately; for the America
  passage, translate respectfully and precisely ("indigenous peoples of
  the Americas, as Hobbes understood reports of them") without editorial
  intrusion — at most a graceful in-line signal that this is his
  17th-century view.
- MISOGYNY/PATRIARCHY in ch. 20 (dominion over children) is actually
  surprisingly egalitarian for 1651 — translate precisely; don't import
  assumptions either way.

================================================================================
7. VOICE TARGET — SAMPLE TRANSFORMATION
================================================================================

Original (ch. 13):
  "Hereby it is manifest, that during the time men live without a common
  Power to keep them all in awe, they are in that condition which is
  called Warre; and such a warre, as is of every man, against every man."

Modern target:
  "This makes one thing clear: whenever people live without a common power
  to keep them all in check, they are in the condition called war — and
  not just any war, but a war of every man against every man."

Original (ch. 5):
  "For words are wise mens counters, they do but reckon by them: but they
  are the mony of fooles"

Modern target:
  "Words are wise men's counters — they use them to calculate with, nothing
  more. But words are the money of fools."

Register: eighth grade to high school reading level. Short paragraphs.
Contractions welcome. Rhetorical questions welcome where Hobbes implies
them. Never dumbed down: keep ALL the content, every argument, every
example, every biblical citation. "Complete, not literal."

================================================================================
8. PER-CHAPTER NOTES PIPELINE
================================================================================

Each translation produces "next chapter notes." Seed notes for chapter 000:
- Establish the translator's standing conventions: margin notes as
  subheadings, defined terms emphasized at first definition, scripture
  citations modernized, Hobbes' first-person ("I") preserved throughout.
- The Introduction's four-part roadmap ("First... Secondly... Thirdly...
  Lastly") should be rendered as a clean preview of the book's structure.
- The Epistle Dedicatory (to Francis Godolphin) opens file 000: keep it
  short, warm, and slightly formal — it's a letter, not a chapter.
