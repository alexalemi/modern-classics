## The Decameron: A Modern Retelling

**The Plague and the Escape**

It was 1348, and Florence, a vibrant city in the heart of Italy, was facing a nightmare. The Black Death, a terrifying plague that had ravaged the East, was now spreading through Europe, leaving a trail of death and despair in its wake.  No human wisdom, foresight, or prayer could stop its advance. 

It began with swollen glands in the groin and armpits, some as big as apples, others like eggs.  These were a sure sign of death.  Then came black or purple boils, appearing on arms, thighs, and spreading across the body.  Few survived. Most succumbed within three days, often without fever or any other obvious symptoms.

The plague was merciless.  Just being near someone who was sick, or even touching their clothes or belongings, was enough to spread the disease.  It was so contagious that even animals, like hogs, would die after sniffing at the rags of plague victims.

Fear gripped the city.  People isolated themselves, convinced that moderation and a healthy lifestyle would save them.  Others went wild, believing that drinking, partying, and indulging in every pleasure imaginable would be a shield against the plague.  There was no real understanding of the disease, and no cure.

The laws of the land, both human and divine, were practically non-existent.  Government officials were too busy trying to survive to enforce the rules.  Fear and despair ruled, and people abandoned their families, their homes, and their belongings to escape the plague.  Neighbors stopped talking to each other, and even parents refused to care for their own children.

The sick were left alone to die, except for the occasional charity of friends and the greed of servants, who demanded exorbitant wages for their services.  Many perished in the streets, and countless bodies lay rotting in houses, abandoned by their families.  

Neighbors, more driven by fear than compassion, dragged the dead from their homes and left them on the street.  The graveyards overflowed, and mass graves were dug, overflowing with bodies that were quickly buried in layers, like cargo on a ship.  The stench of death hung heavy in the air.

Even the countryside was not spared.  Farmers and their families died in their fields and homes, untended and uncared for.  The countryside was abandoned, with livestock roaming freely and crops left to rot.  

The plague was relentless.  Between March and July, over 100,000 people perished within the walls of Florence, leaving a city of deserted homes and empty streets.  

**The Ladies and the Young Men**

In the midst of this devastation, a group of seven young women sought refuge in the venerable church of Santa Maria Novella.  They were all intelligent, well-born, and beautiful, united by friendship and a shared sense of loss.  The oldest, Pampinea, spoke first:

"My dear ladies, we are in mortal danger, and it's time we took action.  We have to flee this city, this plague-ridden wasteland.  We're women, and we have a right to save our own lives. We have the means to escape, so let's go."

Her words struck a chord with the others.  They were ready to leave the city, but Filomena, the most discreet of the group, spoke up:

"I agree with Pampinea, but we can't just run off blindly.  We need to plan, to be careful, to make sure we're safe and avoid trouble. We need a leader."

Elisa, who was admired for her beauty and spirit, echoed Filomena's concerns:

"You are right, Filomena.  We need a man to guide us. But where can we find one? All the men we know are either dead or have already fled." 

As they spoke, three young men entered the church.  They were Pamfilo, Filostrato, and Dioneo, all handsome, charming, and full of life.  They were there to visit their beloveds, who happened to be among the seven women.

Pampinea, recognizing the opportunity, approached the young men and with a cheerful smile, proposed a plan:

"Gentlemen, we ladies are fleeing the plague and seek a safe haven.  We are asking for your protection, for your guidance, for your company on our journey."

The young men were taken aback, but seeing the sincerity in their eyes, they readily agreed.  And so, on Wednesday morning, the seven ladies and the three young men, accompanied by their servants, left Florence, fleeing the plague and heading towards the country, towards safety, towards a new beginning.  

They arrived at a beautiful palace on a hill, a haven of peace and beauty, full of gardens, fountains, and cool, fresh air.  They were finally safe, free from the plague and the city's despair.  

**The First Story**

Pampinea, elected queen for the first day, declared that they would spend their time in joyous activities and ordered that no news from the outside world, except good news, would be permitted.  After enjoying the garden and a delicious meal, they settled down to tell stories, the first of which was told by Pamfilo.

"It is fitting to begin with the name of God," Pamfilo declared, "because He is the maker of all things, and it is by His grace alone that we survive in these troubled times."

He then told the story of Master Ciappelletto, a man who, in life, was a notorious liar, cheat, and rogue, a blasphemer of God and the saints.  But when he was dying, he realized the error of his ways and decided to fake a confession, confessing to sins he never committed, painting himself as a devout and holy man.  The priest, believing his confession to be sincere, absolved him and hailed him as a saint.  He was buried in the church, and the people came to pray to him, believing him to be blessed by God.

"This story," Pamfilo concluded, "is a testament to the infinite mercy of God, who listens to our prayers even when they are misdirected.  He cares more for our intentions than for our ignorance.  Let us remember that, in these times of plague and sorrow, God is our only hope."

The ladies applauded Pamfilo's story, acknowledging its message of hope and faith. 

This concludes the retelling of Day 1 of The Decameron, through the lens of a modern audience.  The story continues as the seven ladies and three young men continue their journey into the countryside, seeking refuge and solace from the plague.
