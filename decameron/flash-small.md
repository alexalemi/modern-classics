## The Decameron: A Modern Retelling

**The Pandemic and The Flight**

It was 1348, and Florence, the jewel of Italy, was gripped by a terrible plague. It wasn't just a bad flu, but a terrifying nightmare, striking people down with brutal swiftness. No doctor, no medicine, no prayer could stop it.  

The city was a ghost town, with death lurking on every corner. Bodies piled up in the streets, and families fled in terror. Fear and despair hung heavy in the air.

But within the walls of the venerable Santa Maria Novella church, a group of young people found solace. Seven women – Pampinea, the eldest and wisest, Fiammetta, Filomena, Emilia, Lauretta, Neifile, and Elisa, all beautiful and bright – gathered for a moment of peace amidst the chaos. 

Pampinea, recognizing the fear that gripped them all, suggested they escape the city, find a safe haven in the countryside, and spend their time in quiet reflection and joyful diversion. Her plan resonated with the others, but Filomena, ever the pragmatist, cautioned them against being too impulsive. 

“We need guidance, ladies. We need someone to help us stay true to our purpose,” she declared.

Their discussion was interrupted by the arrival of three young men – Pamfilo, Filostrato, and Dioneo, all charming and witty. They were drawn to the church by their love for the women, and Pampinea, seizing the opportunity, invited them to join their escape.

The group, seeking refuge from the plague, found a beautiful palace on a hill overlooking the countryside, full of gardens, fountains, and rooms painted with joyous scenes. As they settled in, Dioneo, ever the jester, questioned their plan.

"Ladies, what are we doing with our cares? Mine were left at the city gates," he quipped.

"Dioneo, we're here to enjoy life, to laugh and sing, to forget the terrors," Pampinea responded, banishing her own worries.

But, seeking to ensure their time together would be fruitful, Pampinea proposed they elect a leader for each day, one from each sex, to guide their activities.  They decided to spend their days in leisure, taking walks in the countryside, dancing, making music, and – most importantly – telling stories. 

And so, with Pampinea as their first queen, the storytellers began their ten days of tales, each day dedicated to a new theme.

**Day One: Stories of Divine Intervention**

Pamfilo, the first storyteller, began with a tale of *Master Ciappelletto*, a man who was wicked to his core, yet, through a masterful performance of repentance on his deathbed, fooled a holy friar and ended up being revered as a saint. 

“It’s a testament to God’s mercy,” Pamfilo concluded, “that even a man who lived a life of sin could find salvation through genuine remorse.”

Neifile followed with a story of *Abraham the Jew*, who, convinced by his friend Jehannot de Chevigné to convert to Christianity, went to Rome to observe the clergy. Witnessing their corruption and debauchery, he returned to Paris, convinced Christianity was the true faith. 

"Even in the midst of the clergy’s shortcomings, God’s true light shines through,” Neifile affirmed.

Filomena then shared the story of *Melchizedek the Jew*, who, faced with a cunning question from Saladin, skillfully used a parable to avoid a trap, ultimately demonstrating the power of wisdom to outsmart evil. 

Dioneo, ever seeking amusement, followed with a tale of a monk who, caught in a compromising position, cleverly turned the tables on his abbot, exposing the hypocrisy of the religious orders.

Fiammetta, the woman who captivated Boccaccio, related the story of *The Marchioness of Monferrato*, who with a deftly chosen banquet of hens, fended off the unwanted advances of the king of France. 

Emilia shared a tale of a gullible man, tricked by a cunning friar, who, mistaking greed for piety, ended up paying the friar handsomely, a tale that sparked laughter amongst the ladies.

Filostrato, the lovesick one, recounted a story of *Bergamino*, a witty minstrel who, with a clever parable, skillfully confronted the stinginess of Messer Cane della Scala, a nobleman renowned for his generosity. 

Lauretta, the learned one, spoke of *Guglielmo Borsiere*, the minstrel who, with a brilliant retort, managed to shame Messer Ermino Avarizia, a man so miserly he would rather wear rags than spend a penny on himself.

Elisa, with a bittersweet tale, told of the king of Cyprus, who, roused from his complacency by a Gascon lady, transformed himself from a fearful ruler into a valiant and just king. 

Pampinea, ending the day’s stories, spoke of *Master Alberto of Bologna*, a witty physician who, with a clever comparison between leeks and lovers, put a lady to shame for her attempts to humiliate him.

**Day Two: Stories of Fortunate Escapes**

Filomena, as queen of the day, declared that their stories would focus on those who, after facing terrible hardships, found joy and prosperity beyond their wildest dreams.

Neifile began with a tale of *Martellino*, a prankster who, pretending to be a cripple, sought healing from the supposed saint, Arrigo. His deception was exposed, and he faced a furious mob, only to be saved from their wrath by cunningly claiming he was a thief.

Filostrato, having heard Neifile's tale, shared a story of *Rinaldo d'Asti*, a merchant robbed by highwaymen, who found shelter and solace with a beautiful widow, and ultimately regained his fortune through a twist of fate.

Pampinea continued with a tale of *three brothers*, who, spending their inheritance recklessly, ended up in poverty and prison. Their nephew, Alessandro, who had also squandered their fortune in England, returned to Italy, where he encountered a disguised princess, who, falling in love with him, married him and restored his uncles to their former wealth.

Lauretta then spoke of *Landolfo Ruffolo*, a merchant who, after losing his fortune, became a pirate and plundered many Turkish ships, only to be captured by the Genoese and shipwrecked.  In a miraculous turn of events, he found himself clinging to a chest filled with jewels, which, he later discovered, would make him richer than ever before.

Fiammetta, still enthralled by the power of love, shared the tale of *Andreuccio of Perugia*, who, traveling to Naples to buy horses, found himself the victim of a cunning con-woman. But, through a series of strange and unlikely events, he not only escaped the clutches of the cunning lady but also found himself in possession of a valuable ruby.

Emilia, the compassionate one, spoke of *Madam Beritola*, who, after the death of her husband and the loss of her children during a pirate attack, found herself alone and grief-stricken on a deserted island. There, she raised two kids, finding solace in their company.  Through a series of coincidences, she was rescued by a kind gentleman, and her children, who had been enslaved, were found and reunited.  In a joyous ending, her eldest son, who had fallen in love with his rescuer’s daughter, was freed from prison and married her, restoring his family to their former status.

The day’s stories ended with Pamfilo, who recounted the tale of *The Soldan of Babylon*, who, seeking to honor the King of Algarve, sent his daughter, Alatiel, to marry him.  Alatiel’s ship was struck by a storm, and she endured a series of harrowing adventures, ending up in the hands of nine men in different parts of the world. But, through a series of surprising twists and turns, she was returned to her father and ultimately married the King of Algarve, as was first intended.

Elisa then spoke of *The Count of Antwerp*, who was falsely accused of treason and exiled, leaving his two children in England.  Years later, he returned in disguise, found his children, and, after many harrowing adventures, was eventually proven innocent and restored to his rightful place. 

Filostrato, his turn now to tell, shared a tale of *Bernabo of Genoa*, who, tricked by a scheming con man, lost his fortune and ordered his innocent wife to be killed. She escaped and ended up serving the Soldan, disguised as a man, where she discovered the one responsible for her husband’s misfortune and ultimately ensured he was punished.

Finally, Dioneo, the last storyteller of the day, told the story of *Paganino of Monaco*, a pirate who stole away the wife of Messer Ricciardo di Chinzica.  Ricciardo, after finding his wife, sought to bargain with Paganino for her return, only to find his wife was content to remain with Paganino. 

**Day Three: Stories of the Unexpected**

Neifile, as queen, announced that their stories would focus on the theme of those who achieved success through persistence, shrewdness, or a stroke of good luck. 

Filostrato began with a tale of *Masetto*, a simple man, who, pretending to be mute, found employment as a gardener in a convent.  Unable to resist the temptation of the nuns, he  ended up having sex with them all, ultimately revealing his secret and exploiting his position to his advantage.  

Pampinea, following Filostrato, shared the story of *a horsekeeper*, who, sleeping with the wife of King Agilulf, was discovered by the king, who cut off a lock of his hair to identify him. The horsekeeper, however, cleverly cut off the hair of all the other servants, so the king couldn’t identify him.  

Filomena then told of *a lady*, who,  enamoured with a young man, persuaded a friar to rebuke him on her behalf.  The friar, believing the lady was chaste, warned the young man off, only to be tricked again by the lady, who, playing the role of a virtuous woman, got the friar to help her procure a meeting with her lover.

Dioneo, with his characteristically bawdy wit, told the story of *Ricciardo*, who, having given his palfrey to Messer Francesco Vergellesi in exchange for a night with his wife, tricked the man by pretending to be speaking to his wife, but actually talking to himself.  He succeeded in convincing her to meet him at a bathhouse, where, thinking to meet her husband, she found herself with Ricciardo.

Fiammetta, captivated by the cleverness of the lady in the previous story, continued with a tale of *Ricciardo Minutolo*, who, in love with Catella, skillfully tricked her by feigning indifference, and, with the help of a bathhouse keeper, managed to spend the night with her, leading her to believe she was with her husband.

Emilia, the sentimental one, shared the story of *Tedaldo Elisei*, who, having been betrayed by his mistress, left Florence. Years later, returning in disguise, he reconciled his mistress’s husband, who had been wrongly convicted of murdering him, with his brothers, and ultimately resumed his relationship with his beloved.

Lauretta, the learned one, recounted the story of *Ferondo*, who, having been drugged by the abbot of his monastery, was buried as dead.  While Ferondo was imprisoned in a vault, the abbot spent several months sleeping with Ferondo’s wife.  When Ferondo came to his senses, he was told he was in purgatory. Ultimately, the abbot, fearing his affair would be discovered, caused Ferondo to be “resurrected” and sent back to his wife.

Elisa, with a tale of *Gillette de Narbonne*, a woman who, healing the king of France of a fistula, demanded the hand of Bertrand de Roussillon in marriage, spoke of a woman who, after being married to a man she didn’t love, cleverly tricked him into believing she was another woman, and in the end, convinced him to love and cherish her.  

Finally, Dioneo, seeking to end the day’s stories on a humorous note, told the story of *Alibech*, a young woman from Capsa in Barbary, who, inspired by the teachings of Christianity, went into the desert to become a hermit. She met Rustico, a monk, who, unable to resist her charms, persuaded her to help him “put the devil back into hell.”  This act, of course, involved sex, which Alibech came to enjoy so much, that she began to believe serving God was the most pleasurable thing in the world.

**Day Four: Stories of Love Lost**

Filostrato, the melancholy king, announced that their stories would now focus on those whose love ended in tragedy.

Fiammetta, the queen of the day, recounted the tale of *Tancred, Prince of Salerno*, who, upon learning of his daughter Ghismonda’s affair with Guiscardo, had the young man murdered and his heart sent to his daughter.  In a moment of despair, Ghismonda consumed the poisoned heart, taking her own life. 

Pampinea, following Fiammetta, told the story of *Fra Alberto*, a friar who, posing as the angel Gabriel, seduced a lady.  His scheme was discovered and, fearing his reputation would be ruined, he jumped out of her window and fled into the night.

Emilia, the empathetic one, spoke of *three young men*, each in love with a sister, who ran away together to Crete.  In a series of betrayals and murders fueled by jealousy, the lovers were all killed, and only the youngest sister, with her remaining lover, escaped, only to die in poverty.

Lauretta shared the story of *Lisabetta*, whose brothers murdered her lover, Lorenzo.  Haunted by his ghost, she dug up his head and planted it in a pot of basil, over which she wept until she died of grief.

Elisa told a tale of *Andrevuola*, who, in love with Gabriotto, told him of a dream she had.  Gabriotto, believing it was a premonition of his death, died suddenly in her arms.  She was accused of his death and, while being held by the authorities, refused to betray Gabriotto’s memory.  Ultimately, she was released, but in despair, decided to become a nun.

Filostrato, in turn, shared a story of *Simona*, who, having lost her lover Pasquino in a garden, was accused of poisoning him.  She was taken by the authorities and, thinking to prove her innocence by showing the judge how her lover died, rubbed a sage leaf against her teeth and also died.

Dioneo, with his penchant for the bizarre, told a story of *Girolamo*, who, in love with Salvestra, left to go to Paris.  Upon his return, he discovered she was married and died by her side, in despair.  Upon seeing this, Salvestra also died of grief.

Finally, the king, with a tale of *Sir Guillaume de Roussillon*, who, consumed by jealousy, murdered his wife’s lover, Sir Guillaume de Guardestaing, and, in a cruel twist of fate, had his wife eat the heart of her murdered lover, ended the day’s stories with a chilling illustration of the dark side of passion.

**Day Five: Stories of Love Regained**

Fiammetta, as queen, declared that their stories would focus on those who, after a series of hardships and misfortunes, found love and happiness.

Pamfilo, the first storyteller, recounted the tale of *Cimon*, a simpleton who, seeing Iphigenia asleep in a meadow, was struck by her beauty and, transforming himself through the power of love, became a scholar and a gentleman.  He then kidnapped Iphigenia and, after a tempestuous journey, found himself in Crete, where he married her and lived happily. 

Emilia, seeking to counter the tragic themes of the previous day, shared a story of *Costanza*, who, having lost her lover Martuccio Gomito at sea, embarked on a lonely voyage, only to find him alive in Tunis.  There, Martuccio had won the favor of the king and, after revealing herself to him, she was married and returned with him to Lipari.

Elisa told a story of *Pietro Boccamazza*, who, while fleeing with his beloved, Agnolella, was captured by thieves.  Agnolella escaped, only to find herself in a castle where, by chance, she was reunited with Pietro, who had escaped from the thieves and, through a series of adventures, found his way to the castle. They were married and lived happily in Rome.

Filostrato, still yearning for love, shared a story of *Ricciardo Manardi*, who, while visiting Messer Lizio da Valbona, fell in love with Lizio’s daughter, Caterina.  Ricciardo, unable to confess his feelings, cunningly persuaded Caterina to sleep in the gallery outside her father's room, where he secretly joined her.  His plan was discovered by Lizio, but he, seeing Ricciardo as a good match for his daughter, ultimately allowed them to marry.  

Neifile, the compassionate one, recounted a story of *Guidotto da Cremona*, who, upon his death, left his daughter to the care of Giacomino da Pavia.  Two young men fell in love with the girl, only to discover, after a brawl, that she was the sister of one of them, and was ultimately given to the other to wife.

The king, seeking to lighten the atmosphere, shared the story of *Gianni di Procida*, who was condemned to be burned along with his beloved, who had been given to King Frederick of Sicily.  He was saved from death by Ruggieri dell' Oria, who recognized him and secured his release.  

Pampinea, continuing the theme of love found, spoke of *Teodoro*, who, having gotten his mistress, Violante, pregnant, was sentenced to be hanged.  He was saved by his father, who, revealing his true identity, helped him marry Violante, proving that even the most desperate situations can lead to unexpected happiness.

Lauretta, the learned one, then spoke of *Nastagio degli Onesti*, who was in love with a cruel and heartless lady.  In a haunting scene, he witnessed a spectral hunt where a nobleman chased a damsel through the forest and, upon catching her, killed her and had dogs devour her heart.  He invited his beloved, along with her family, to a banquet, where she witnessed the same gruesome scene, leading her to accept Nastagio's proposal.

Finally, Dioneo, with his characteristic bawdy wit, shared the story of *Federigo degli Alberighi*, who, in love with a lady named Giovanna, squandered his fortune on her.  When Giovanna came to visit him, he, finding himself with nothing left to offer, slaughtered his beloved falcon and served it to her for dinner.  Giovanna, touched by his sacrifice, married him and restored his fortune.

**Day Six: Stories of Sharp Wit**

Elisa, as queen, declared that their stories would focus on clever retorts and wise advice, and how these were used to escape danger and shame.

Filomena began with the tale of *Madam Oretta*, who, while being carried on horseback by a gentleman, found his rambling story so excruciatingly boring that she told him to set her down.

Pampinea, with a story of *Cisti the baker*,  demonstrated how even a simple man, through wit and a touch of cleverness, could shame a nobleman who was trying to take advantage of him.

Lauretta, the sharp-tongued one, recounted the story of *Madam Nonna de' Pulci*, who, with a swift and witty retort, put a bishop in his place after he made an inappropriate comment about her. 

Filostrato then shared a story of *Chichibio*, a cook who, when confronted by his master for missing a leg from a roasted crane, cleverly lied, claiming cranes only have one leg, and, through a timely observation, cleverly tricked his master into believing him.

Neifile, the compassionate one, told the story of *Michele Scalza*, who, with a clever and humorous argument, convinced a group of young men that the Cadgers of Florence were the best gentlemen in the world. 

Emilia, the sentimental one, spoke of *Madam Filippa*, who, after being caught in adultery with her lover, used a brilliant defense,  arguing that, since she had already fulfilled her husband’s needs, she was free to dispose of the “remainder,” which ultimately led to a change in the law.

Elisa then recounted a story of *a gentleman*, who, when his niece complained about seeing disagreeable people, told her, “Never look at yourself in the mirror, then.”

The king, seeking to participate in the day’s theme, told the story of *Guido Cavalcanti*, who, when confronted by a group of gentlemen, used a clever turn of phrase to rebuke them without offending them. 

Dioneo, with a tale of *Fra Cipolla*, a friar who promised to show his parishioners a feather of the Angel Gabriel, but, upon finding coals in place of the feather, used a brilliant display of ingenuity and storytelling to convince them the coals were from the fire that roasted St. Lawrence, ended the day’s tales with a hilarious display of cleverness and trickery.

**Day Seven: Stories of Deceit**

Dioneo, as king, declared that their stories would focus on the tricks played by men and women upon one another, for love or self-preservation.

Emilia, ever insightful, began with a tale of *Gianni Lotteringhi*, a simple man, who, convinced by his wife that there was a ghost knocking on their door, joined her in a bizarre exorcism, during which he unwittingly helped her secret lover escape.  

Filostrato, enjoying the themes of the day, shared the story of *Peronella*, who,  when her husband unexpectedly returned home, hid her lover in a vat, and, when the husband said he’d sold the vat, claimed she’d already sold it to a man who was currently inside, prompting her husband to scrape the vat clean, allowing her lover to escape.

Neifile then told the story of *Fra Rinaldo*, who, caught in a compromising position with his gossip, tricked her husband into believing he was performing an exorcism.

Lauretta continued with the tale of *Tofano*, a jealous husband, who, having been tricked into believing he’d caught his wife in the act, locked her out of doors.  The clever lady feigned to jump into a well, then, when her husband ran to rescue her, slipped into the house and locked him out, taunting him from the window.

Elisa, with a story of *a jealous husband*, who, disguised as a priest, confessed his wife, demonstrated how, after his wife said she was in love with a priest who came to her every night, he secretly watched for the priest, only to find the lady’s actual lover had entered the house through the roof and was in the bed with her.

Filomena shared the story of *Madam Isabella*, who, while being visited by a man she didn’t love, managed to trick her husband into thinking the man was a stranger, and by a stroke of luck, her husband ended up escorting her lover home.

The king, seeking to outdo the others with his tale, shared a story of *Lodovico*, who, smitten with the beauty of Madam Beatrice, cunningly became one of her husband’s servants.  He then confessed his love for her, and with the help of his clever mistress, he managed to trick his master into a confrontation, which resulted in a beating for the master, leaving the lovers free to enjoy one another.

Dioneo, always seeking laughter, told a story of *a jealous husband*, who, suspecting his wife, had her tie a string to her great toe so she could alert her lover when he arrived.  The husband, having discovered the scheme, hid himself and waited for the lover to arrive, only to find his wife had cleverly replaced herself with another woman.  He ended up beating and disfiguring the wrong woman.

Finally, Pampinea, the wisest of the storytellers, shared the story of *Lydia*, a woman who loved Pyrrhus, one of her husband Nicostratus’s servants.  Pyrrhus, not believing the lady’s affections, demanded she do three things to prove her love: kill his hawk, give him a lock of his master’s beard, and a tooth.  Lydia, with the help of her chamberwoman, Lusca, managed to accomplish all of these tasks, while convincing Nicostratus that he had been the victim of illusions, all while Nicostratus was still present, leaving the lovers free to indulge in their secret passion.

**Day Eight: Stories of Clever Tricks**

Dioneo, as king of the day, announced that their stories would focus on the clever tricks played by men and women on one another. 

Emilia began with a story of *Gulfardo*, who, having been asked by Madam Ambruogia, the wife of Guasparruolo Cagastraccio, for two hundred gold florins in exchange for her favors, borrowed the money from her husband, and then, with the help of his friend,  pretended to return the money to Ambruogia, causing her to confess to having received it from him.

Filostrato, following Emilia, told the story of *Fra Rinaldo*, a friar who, having slept with his gossip, cleverly tricked her husband into believing he was performing an exorcism.  

Neifile then recounted the tale of *Calandrino*, who, convinced by his friends Bruno and Buffalmacco that there was a magical stone that made one invisible, went searching for it in the Mugnone river.  He returned home, laden with stones, only to be tricked again by his friends, who pelted him with stones along the way, ending with a beating he delivered upon his wife.

Lauretta, relishing the absurd nature of the stories, continued with a story of *The Rector of Fiesole*, who, unable to win the affections of a widow lady, tricked her into allowing him to spend the night with her by having her maidservant pretend to be her.

Elisa then shared a tale of *Bruno and Buffalmacco*, who stole a pig from Calandrino, and, with a series of clever tricks involving a poisoned “ordeal” using ginger boluses,  convinced Calandrino to pay them to keep the matter a secret.  

Filomena, the compassionate one, shared a story of *Messer Forese da Rabatta* and *Master Giotto*, who, while returning from Mugello, met during a rainstorm, and, with witty retorts, playfully teased each other about their misfortunes.

The king, with a story of *Michele Scalza*, a cunning man who, with a humorous argument, convinced a group of young men that the Cadgers of Florence were the most noble people in the world, ended the day with a reminder of the power of wit and humor.

Finally, Dioneo, always willing to share a humorous tale, spoke of *Madam Filippa*, who, after being caught in adultery with her lover, used a clever and bold defense to convince the authorities to alter the law.

**Day Nine: Stories of Love, Loss, and New Beginnings**

Emilia, as queen, declared that the day’s stories would be told freely, each storyteller choosing a theme to their liking.

Filomena, with a story of *Madam Francesca*, who, being courted by two men, used a clever scheme involving a recently buried corpse to rid herself of their unwelcome advances, began the day’s stories.

Pampinea, following Filomena, told the story of *an abbess*, who, upon receiving word that one of her nuns was with a man in her cell, went to confront her.  In the dark, she mistakenly put on the priest’s breeches instead of her coif.  The nun, recognizing this, called out, “Madam, tie up your coif and then say what you will to me,” effectively exposing the abbess’s indiscretion and ultimately leading to a change in the rules of the convent.

Lauretta, the learned one, shared a story of *Master Simone*, a physician, who, convinced by Bruno and Buffalmacco that he was pregnant,  agreed to a series of ridiculous “treatments,” including drinking a special potion, to be cured, and gave his friends money to buy capons to help with the “cure.”

Elisa then recounted the tale of *Cecco Fortarrigo*, who gambled away all his money and his master’s clothes, and, with a clever ploy, had his master arrested by country folk,  allowing him to escape with his master’s clothes and horse.

Filostrato, the lovesick one, shared the story of *Calandrino*, who, in love with a young woman, was tricked by Bruno and Buffalmacco into believing a talisman he had given him would make the woman fall in love with him.  Calandrino attempted to use the talisman, only to be found by his wife and beaten mercilessly.

The king, eager to participate in the day’s stories, recounted a tale of *two young men* who, having made a pact to return from the afterlife to tell each other what life was like, found themselves before Solomon, who, seeking to teach them wisdom, simply told one to “love” and the other to “get him to Goosebridge.”  Ultimately, through a humorous encounter with a mule, the man seeking guidance about his wife discovered that the path to a happy marriage is often paved with a little firmness and discipline.

Dioneo, the jester, told a story of *two gentlemen* who, both in love with the same woman, tricked her into meeting each of them at the same time, and, in a bizarre turn of events, ended up sharing her and her husband, with both couples enjoying a very unusual arrangement.

Finally, Pampinea, the wisest of the storytellers, spoke of *Master Simone*, who, desiring to join a secret society, was tricked by Bruno and Buffalmacco, who, posing as members of the society, arranged for him to be dumped into a trench filled with ordure.

**Day Ten: Stories of Nobility and Magnificence**

Pamfilo, as king, declared that the day’s stories would focus on acts of generosity and magnificence in matters of love and life.

Neifile, the first storyteller, shared a tale of *a knight*, who, after serving the king of Spain and feeling unrewarded, made a mock complaint about the king’s lack of generosity.  The king, seeking to prove his loyalty,  arranged for the knight to choose between two chests, one filled with jewels and the other with dirt.  The knight, with an air of certainty, chose the chest full of dirt, and the king, after revealing the contents of the other chest, gave him the chest full of dirt, as a sign of his respect.

Filostrato then recounted the story of *Ghino di Tacco*, a notorious bandit, who captured the Abbot of Cluny,  and,  after treating him with utmost care and curing him of a stomach ailment,  released him. The abbot, returning to the Pope,  interceded on Ghino’s behalf, securing him a high position within the Hospitallers.

Pampinea, with a story of *Mithridanes*, who, envious of Nathan’s generosity, planned to murder him, but ended up becoming his friend after he was discovered by Nathan in a most unlikely way, spoke of the power of unexpected kindness to change hearts.

Lauretta, the learned one, told the story of *Messer Gentile de’ Carisendi*, a nobleman who, hearing that his love, Madam Catalina, was dead, went to visit her tomb.  There, he found her alive, brought her back to his home, and, after restoring her to health, presented her to her husband, demonstrating the true nature of love and magnanimity.

Elisa then recounted the story of *Madam Dianora*, who, demanding a garden that would bloom in January, challenged her lover, Messer Ansaldo, to prove his love.  Ansaldo, with the help of a nigromancer, created such a garden, but, upon learning of her husband’s generosity in granting Dianora the right to choose between keeping her promise to Ansaldo or remaining faithful to her husband,  Ansaldo forgave her, and the nigromancer, in turn, forgave Ansaldo for not paying him.  

The king, with a story of *King Charles the Old*, who, despite his age,  fell in love with two young women.  Recognizing the folly of his passion, he married the two sisters to two noble gentlemen, demonstrating that true magnanimity lies in self-control.

Dioneo, with a tale of *King Pedro of Arragon*, who, after learning of Lisa’s love for him,  married her to a noble gentleman, and, in a gesture of chivalry, proclaimed himself her knight, ended the day with a story that showcased the power of kindness and compassion to inspire love and loyalty.

Finally, Fiammetta, the beloved of Boccaccio, shared the tale of *Sophronia*, who, while intended for Gisippus, ended up marrying Titus Quintius Fulvus.  Gisippus, in a remarkable show of friendship,  concealed his own love for Sophronia and ultimately convinced Titus to take her for his wife.  When Gisippus was falsely accused of murder, Titus, to save his friend,  claimed responsibility for the crime.  In the end, both men were proven innocent, and Titus, after sharing his wealth and his sister with his friend, lived a long and happy life with Sophronia.

**The End of The Decameron**

With the completion of the tenth day, the ten days of storytelling came
to an end.  The seven ladies and three young men, having escaped the
plague and rediscovering the joys of life, returned to Florence, enriched
not just by their adventures, but by the power of their stories,
a testament to the enduring power of human connection, wit, and resilience.
