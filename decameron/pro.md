## The Decameron: Tales from a Time of Plague

**Introduction**

Friends, it does you good to be kind to those who are suffering. This is especially true for those of us who have needed comfort in the past and found it in the generosity of others. I, for one, know this to be true. From a young age, I was completely consumed by a passionate love – a love that, perhaps, was far too grand for someone of my humble standing. Although discreet friends who knew of this love commended me for it, it was, nonetheless, a heavy burden to bear.  It was not the cruelty of my beloved that caused this burden, but the overwhelming power of my own unruly desire, which often led me to experience more pain than necessary.

In this time of deep sorrow, I found solace in the conversation and advice of a dear friend. His words offered me such relief that I believe they truly saved me from succumbing to despair. But, as is the nature of all things under heaven, my love eventually faded, leaving behind only a gentle warmth – the kind of warmth that comes to those who navigate the deep waters of love cautiously. While the pain of unrequited love has disappeared, the memory of the kindness shown to me by those who witnessed my suffering remains strong and, I believe, will only be erased by death itself. 

Because showing gratitude is among the most important of virtues, I have resolved to offer some relief to those in need, as a way of repaying the kindness I received.  Although I know my help may seem small, I believe it is best offered where the need is greatest. 

And who would argue that love-sick ladies need comfort more than men? They, burdened by fear and modesty, keep the fires of their love hidden in their hearts.  These fires burn far stronger than any outward flame, as those who have experienced such love know all too well. Confined by the demands of fathers, mothers, brothers, and husbands, ladies spend much of their time within the walls of their homes, sitting idle and lost in thought. If their minds become burdened with longing, there is little to relieve them, especially as they are far less equipped than men to endure such suffering. 

It is different for men in love. If melancholy weighs on their hearts, they have many ways of distracting themselves: traveling, seeing new sights, hunting, fishing, riding, gambling, or conducting business. Each of these pursuits can, to some extent, occupy their minds and divert them from painful thoughts, at least for a time. Afterwards, they will find solace, or at least, the burden of their longing will have lessened.

Therefore, wishing to correct this imbalance – this lack of support for those who need it most – I intend to provide some comfort and amusement for lovesick ladies. For those who are not in love, a needle, spindle, and thread will suffice.  But for those whose hearts are heavy with unrequited desire, I offer one hundred tales of love and fortune, told over ten days by a group of seven ladies and three young men, gathered together during the recent, devastating plague. Interspersed among these stories are songs sung by the ladies for their entertainment. 

Within these tales, you will find love stories, both happy and sad, as well as other surprising events that have happened throughout history. The ladies who read them may find comfort in the stories themselves and may also glean valuable advice on how to avoid that which should be avoided and embrace that which should be embraced. It is my hope that reading these tales will bring an end to heartache. 

If my hope is fulfilled, let the readers give thanks to Love, who, by releasing me from his chains, has allowed me to dedicate myself to their pleasure.


**Day One: Tales of Wit and Resourcefulness**

**How the Company Came to Be**

It was the year 1348, and the Black Death had reached the beautiful city of Florence, surpassing in its elegance all other Italian cities. This terrible plague, sent by God as punishment for our sins or perhaps brought about by the movement of the stars, had already ravaged the East, killing countless people. Now, it had spread relentlessly westward. No amount of human wisdom or foresight could stop its advance. Officials attempted to cleanse the city of impurities, and the sick were forbidden from entering, but the plague continued to spread. Prayers and processions led by the devout offered no protection. With the arrival of spring, the plague revealed its horrifying effects. 

Unlike in the East, where bleeding from the nose signaled imminent death, the plague in Florence began with swellings in the groin or armpits. These swellings, called “gavoccioli,” grew to the size of apples or eggs, and soon spread to other parts of the body.  After a time, these buboes transformed into black or purplish blotches that appeared on the arms, thighs, and eventually all over the body, sometimes in large patches, sometimes in dense clusters. Like the gavoccioli, these blotches were a sure sign of death. 

No doctor could offer any advice or medicine that seemed to help.  Either the disease was too strong, or the physicians were too ignorant. Besides the true doctors, there were now countless men and women, with no medical training, claiming to understand the plague.  But no matter what treatments they tried, very few people recovered; almost everyone died within three days of the appearance of the symptoms, some sooner, some later, and usually without fever or other complications.

This pestilence was even more terrifying because it was so contagious. Not only could it be spread through direct contact with the sick, but simply touching their clothing or anything they had used could transmit the disease. 

I, myself, witnessed a truly astonishing event that I would never have believed, had I not seen it with my own eyes.  One day, the rags of a poor man who had died of the plague were thrown into the street. Two pigs came upon them and began rooting through the rags with their snouts. After taking the rags into their mouths and chewing them, the pigs began to whirl around, as if they had been poisoned, and within a short time, they fell dead upon the rags.

The events I have described, along with many others far more frightening, filled the hearts of those who were not yet sick with terror. Almost everyone became convinced that the only way to survive was to avoid the sick and anything that belonged to them.

Some people believed that living moderately, avoiding excesses, and keeping company only with those who were healthy would protect them from the disease. So, they formed groups and shut themselves up in houses where no one had been sick. They enjoyed delicious food and fine wine in moderation, entertained themselves with music and games, and avoided all contact with the outside world, refusing even to listen to any news of death or illness. 

Others, believing just the opposite, maintained that drinking, celebrating, singing, and indulging in every possible pleasure – while avoiding contact with the sick as much as possible – was the surest remedy.  They roamed the streets night and day, going from one tavern to another, drinking without restraint.  They entered the homes of others freely, helping themselves to whatever they desired.  People had abandoned all care for their possessions, believing they were going to die anyway, so most houses were open to anyone.  

Still others took a middle course.  They did not restrict their diets or indulge in excessive drinking and partying.  Instead, they satisfied their appetites in moderation.  They did not shut themselves away, but went about the city carrying flowers, herbs, and spices to their noses, believing that strong scents would protect their brains from the stench of rotting bodies that filled the air.  

Many others, holding a more extreme view, were convinced that there was no better or more certain way to escape the plague than to flee from it.  So, thinking only of themselves, countless men and women abandoned their homes, their families, and their possessions, going to live in the country, either on their own estates or on those of others. They seemed to believe that God’s wrath, directed at punishing mankind, would not strike them in the countryside, or that the plague would destroy the city entirely, leaving no one alive.  But while not all of these people died, neither did they all survive. Many of those who fled, regardless of their beliefs about how to avoid the disease, became sick and languished, abandoned and alone, because they themselves had set the example of abandoning those who were ill. 

Within the city, almost no one cared for their neighbors or their friends.  Families rarely visited each other and only spoke from a distance. This terrible suffering had terrified people so much that brothers abandoned brothers, uncles abandoned nephews, and sisters abandoned sisters. Husbands even abandoned their wives. And what is more incredible and almost impossible to believe is that mothers and fathers abandoned their own children, as if they were not their own flesh and blood. 

Because of this abandonment, countless sick men and women were left without anyone to care for them. They received no help unless they had a charitable friend (which was rare) or could afford to pay a servant exorbitant wages.  Even then, it was difficult to find servants.  The few who were willing were mostly simple-minded and unaccustomed to caring for the sick. They performed only basic tasks like bringing the sick what they needed and noting when they died.  In doing so, many of these servants perished, along with their wages. 

The abandonment of the sick by their families and the lack of servants led to a custom that had previously been unknown:  No woman, no matter how beautiful or noble, hesitated to allow a man, no matter his age or attractiveness, to care for her when she was ill. If necessary, she would reveal to him every part of her body, as freely as she would to a woman. 

Another custom arose from this terrible situation: Because the consecrated burial grounds were overflowing with the dead who were brought from the city day and night, it was impossible to give everyone their own grave, as was the custom. Instead, after all the plots were filled, vast trenches were dug in the churchyards.  Bodies were stacked in these trenches, in layers, like cargo in a ship, until they reached the top, where they were covered with a thin layer of dirt. 

During this time of calamity, the countryside surrounding the city was also struck by the plague. In villages and fields, poor farmers and their families died without the help of doctors or servants, like beasts rather than men.  

Back within the city walls, the great plague and the lack of care for the sick led to the deaths of well over one hundred thousand people between March and July. Entire noble families perished, leaving their palaces and grand homes empty.   

**The Company Forms**

One Tuesday morning, during this dark time, seven young ladies happened to gather for a prayer service at the church of Santa Maria Novella. There were few people in the church that day.  The young ladies, all friends or neighbors, were dressed in mourning, as befitted the time.  None of them was older than twenty-eight or younger than eighteen. Each was beautiful, well-mannered, and possessed of a quick wit. 

Their names were Pampinea, Fiammetta, Filomena, Emilia, Lauretta, Neifile, and Elisa. After praying together in a corner of the church, they began to talk about the times.  

Pampinea, the oldest of the group, began, “My dear ladies, I’m sure you, like me, have realized that using one’s rights to protect oneself harms no one.  It is our right, as it is the right of all living things, to do whatever we can to preserve our own lives.  

Look at us this morning – we are all dressed in mourning, as if we need to remind ourselves of the dead, or to see how many bodies are carried in for burial. We come here to hear if the friars, whose numbers have dwindled, are still chanting their offices at the appointed hours. If we leave this place, we see nothing but the sick and dying, carried through the streets. We hear nothing but news of death and suffering, and those who have not been taken by the plague seem to have abandoned all sense of morality, thinking only of indulging their appetites.  They break the law, knowing that there is no one left to enforce it. 

Even those who are shut away in monasteries have given themselves over to earthly pleasures, breaking their vows and indulging in sensual delights. They seem to believe that what is permitted for others is also permitted for them.

If this is the state of things, my friends, why do we remain here?  Why aren’t we doing more to protect ourselves? Do we think we are worth less than others?  Are we somehow less vulnerable to the plague?  How foolish we are if this is what we think! Just consider the many young men and women, healthy and strong, who have been taken by this cruel plague. 

We must not, out of stubbornness or indifference, allow ourselves to fall into this same fate, when we have the means to escape. It seems to me that it would be best for us to leave the city, as many have done before us, and go to live quietly in the country. Each of us has more than enough beautiful estates to choose from. In doing so, we will avoid the immoral behavior we see all around us, and instead, enjoy simple pleasures.  We will hear the songs of birds, see the hills and plains covered in green, and watch the fields of wheat wave like the sea. We will see countless varieties of trees, and the open sky, which, although it may be angered against us, still shows us its eternal beauties. The air is fresh in the countryside, and there is greater abundance of all that is needed for life. 

And let’s be honest – we are not abandoning anyone by leaving the city.  It would be more accurate to say that we have been abandoned, as our families, either dead or fleeing from death, have left us here alone, as if we were not their own.  

There can be no blame in following this advice, but if we do not, we risk sorrow, pain, and perhaps even death.  Therefore, if it pleases you, let us gather our maidservants and whatever we need and go to live in the country, moving from one estate to another, enjoying the pleasures of the season, until this plague has passed. I remind you that we are not forbidden to leave, just as others are not forbidden to remain and live in dishonor.”

The other ladies not only agreed with Pampinea’s suggestion, but were eager to start making plans. 

“Hold on,” said Filomena, who was known for her wisdom.  “There’s no need to rush off immediately.  Remember that we are women, and none of us is so young that she doesn’t understand how unreasonable women are when left to their own devices.  We are fickle, stubborn, suspicious, and easily frightened.  Without someone to guide us, I am afraid our company will quickly dissolve, and we will not bring ourselves honor. We need to find some men to accompany us.”

“Of course,” answered Elisa, “we all know that men are the heads of women, and that few of our endeavors come to a good end without a man’s guidance.  But how will we find them? Most of our relatives are dead, and those who remain are scattered all over the countryside, fleeing from the plague, just as we are. It wouldn’t be right to invite strangers, because if we are truly going to protect ourselves, we must avoid any scandal or harm.”

As the ladies were talking, three young men entered the church. They were not so young that the youngest among them was less than twenty-five.  Neither the devastation of the plague, the loss of their relatives, nor fear for their own lives could dampen the fires of love that burned in their hearts. Their names were Pamfilo, Filostrato, and Dioneo, and each was handsome, well-bred, and looking for the company of his beloved, each of whom, as it happened, was among the seven ladies gathered in the church.  Some of the other ladies were also related to one or another of the young men.  

The ladies noticed the young men as soon as they entered, and Pampinea, smiling, said, “Look, it seems fortune is smiling upon our endeavor. She has sent us worthy and intelligent young men who will gladly be our guides and protectors, if we allow them.”

Neifile, whose face flushed because one of the young men was her beloved, replied, “For God’s sake, Pampinea, be careful what you say!  I admit that these are good men, and I believe they would be more than capable of providing protection and companionship, not only for us, but for ladies far more noble and beautiful. But they are all in love with some of us here, and I’m afraid that if we take them with us, people will gossip, even if we do nothing to deserve it.”

“What does it matter what people say?” replied Filomena.  “As long as I live honestly and my conscience is clear, I don’t care who speaks against me.  God and truth will be my protectors. If the young men are willing to come, we should agree with Pampinea that fortune favors our venture.”

Her companions, convinced by her certainty, agreed that the young men should be invited to join them. Without further discussion, Pampinea, who was related to one of them, approached the three men and with a cheerful face, explained the plan and asked if they would be willing to accompany the ladies to the country, as brothers.  The young men, at first, thought the ladies were joking, but when they realized they were serious, they readily agreed, and all began to make preparations to leave. 

On Wednesday morning, before dawn, they gathered their belongings, sent servants ahead to a pre-arranged destination, and left Florence.  They had not traveled more than two miles outside the city walls before they arrived at their destination.  

The property was situated on a gentle hill, just off the main road. It was covered in trees and flowering shrubs and overlooked fields of wheat. Atop the hill stood a palace with a large courtyard, balconies, drawing rooms, and bedrooms, each more beautiful than the last. The walls of the rooms were decorated with cheerful paintings. Surrounding the palace were lawns and gardens, filled with wells of cool water and cellars stocked with fine wine, more suited, perhaps, to those who enjoyed a good drink than to ladies of virtue.  The ladies and gentlemen were delighted to find the house swept clean, the beds made, and bowls of flowers in every room.  

They had barely settled in before Dioneo, who was known for his good humor and quick wit, said, “Ladies, it seems your intelligence, rather than our own foresight, has led us here. I don’t know what you intend to do with your worries, but I left mine behind at the city gate. So, either prepare to laugh, sing, and enjoy yourselves (within the bounds of decency, of course), or allow me to go back and retrieve my anxieties and stay in the plague-ridden city.”

Pampinea, who seemed to have left her own worries behind, replied, “You’re right, Dioneo. We must live cheerfully, and there’s no better reason to have fled from that city of sorrows than to do just that. But things done without moderation rarely last. I, therefore, propose that we choose someone to act as our leader.  This leader will be responsible for ensuring we continue to enjoy ourselves, and we will obey him or her as our ruler.  To avoid jealousy and ensure that everyone has a chance to rule, I propose that each of us be given this responsibility, as well as the pleasure of being in charge, for one day.  Let us all vote on who will be our first leader. After that, each ruler, before evening, will choose his or her successor.  Each leader will decide where we live and how we spend our time during their reign.”

Her companions readily agreed with Pampinea’s plan and unanimously voted to make her their first ruler.  Then, Filomena, remembering the honor associated with laurel leaves, ran to a laurel tree, gathered some branches, and wove a beautiful wreath, which she placed upon Pampinea’s head as a sign of her authority.  

Queen Pampinea then called for silence, summoned all the servants, and gave them their instructions: “To set a good example for you all, so that our company will continue in order and good humor, I declare that Parmeno, Dioneo’s servant, shall be our steward.  He will be responsible for overseeing the household and preparing the drawing room for our use.  Sirisco, Pamfilo’s servant, will be our purveyor and treasurer. He will follow Parmeno’s instructions.  Tindaro will attend to Filostrato and the other gentlemen when the other servants are busy with their duties. My maid, Misia, and Filomena’s maid, Licisca, will work in the kitchen and diligently prepare whatever food Parmeno instructs them to make. Lauretta’s Chimera and Fiammetta’s Stratilia will oversee the ladies’ chambers and ensure that the places where we gather are kept clean.  And I command that all of you, if you value my favor, bring us no news from the outside world except that which is happy.”

After giving these orders, Queen Pampinea declared, “We are surrounded by gardens, meadows, and other delightful places. Let’s all go and enjoy ourselves.  When you hear the church bells ring at nine in the morning, return here, so we can dine in the cool of the day.”

The company, dismissed by their new queen, went strolling through the gardens. Young men and beautiful ladies walked together, weaving garlands of leaves and singing love songs. After spending the morning enjoying themselves, they returned to the house, where they found Parmeno had already set the tables with fine white cloths, silver goblets, and vases filled with flowers. The servants had also placed flowers and rushes on the floor.  

After washing their hands, the ladies and gentlemen sat down at the table, following Parmeno’s arrangement.  Delicious food and fine wine were served, and the servants quietly attended to their needs.  Everyone was pleased with the elegance and orderliness of the meal and they ate with good appetites, engaging in cheerful conversation.

When the table was cleared, the queen called for musical instruments.  All the ladies knew how to dance, as did the young men, and some were also talented musicians and singers. So, at the queen’s request, Dioneo took up a lute and Fiammetta a viol and began to play a slow dance. The queen and the ladies, along with two of the young men, sent the servants away to eat and began to dance. After dancing, they sang cheerful songs and enjoyed each other’s company until it was time to take their afternoon naps.  

At three in the afternoon, the church bells rang, and the queen, rising from her nap, woke the others, saying that sleeping too much during the day was harmful to one’s health.  Together they walked to a nearby meadow, where the grass was long and green and provided shade from the sun.  There, enjoying the gentle breeze, they sat in a circle.

“As you can see,” said the queen, “the sun is high, and the heat is intense. We hear nothing but the chirping of crickets.  It would be foolish to go anywhere else right now.  This meadow is cool and beautiful, and we have chess and backgammon to play if you wish. But I propose that we spend this hot part of the day, not in games, which can be frustrating for the players and not always entertaining for those who are watching, but in telling stories.  By the time each of us has told a tale, the sun will have set, and the air will be cooler. Then, we can go out and enjoy ourselves. If you agree with me, let us do this.  If not, let us each do whatever we wish until evening.”

The ladies and gentlemen all agreed that telling stories would be an excellent way to pass the time.  

“Then,” said the queen, “on this first day, let us each tell a story on whatever subject is most pleasing to us.”  Turning to Pamfilo, who sat at her right hand, she asked him to begin.  He readily agreed and, with everyone listening attentively, began his tale.  

**The First Story: A Wicked Man Becomes a Saint**

Pamfilo began, “It is always fitting to begin any endeavor by invoking the holy name of the Creator of all things. I will therefore begin with one of His miracles.

It is well known that all earthly things are subject to change, decay, and death.  Our lives are filled with hardship, sorrow, and dangers, which we could not endure without God’s grace and protection.  This grace does not come to us because we deserve it, but because of God’s own goodness and the prayers of those who lived virtuous lives and are now in His eternal presence. It is to these holy souls that we offer our prayers, believing them to be powerful advocates for us before the throne of God.  We often do not know the true nature of these souls to whom we pray, but God, who knows all, sees the purity of our intentions and hears our prayers, even when we mistakenly invoke the intercession of someone who is not worthy. 

The truth of this will become clear in the story I am about to tell you – clear, I say, not because we can know the true judgment of God, but because of the judgment of men.

There was once a very wealthy French merchant named Musciatto Franzesi. After becoming a knight, he had to travel to Tuscany with Lord Charles of Valois, the brother of the King of France, at the request of Pope Boniface.  Because his business affairs had become tangled, as often happens with merchants, he had to entrust his various enterprises to others. However, he struggled to find someone capable of collecting the money he was owed by certain men from Burgundy.  He knew these Burgundians to be liars, troublemakers, and scoundrels and couldn’t think of anyone dishonest and ruthless enough to deal with them.

After considering the matter at length, he remembered a man named Master Ciappelletto of Prato, who often visited his home in Paris.  Ciappelletto was a small, fastidious man, whom the French, not knowing the meaning of his name, called “Ciappelletto,” which in their language means “little hat.”  This became the name by which he was commonly known. 

Now, this Ciappelletto was a notary who was ashamed to produce any document that was not fraudulent.  He would forge any legal document requested of him, and would rather give such documents away for free than receive a fair payment. He was also a skilled liar, and because oaths were highly valued in those times, he made a good living by falsely swearing to tell the truth in legal disputes. He delighted in stirring up trouble and spreading rumors, turning friends and family against each other, and was happiest when he saw the harm caused by his lies and schemes. If he was asked to commit murder or any other evil act, he would do so willingly. He cursed God and the saints constantly, even over the smallest thing, and was known for his bad temper.  He never went to church and mocked the sacraments, preferring taverns and other wicked places.  He loved women as much as a dog loves a stick and took pleasure in unnatural sins. He robbed and cheated without conscience, ate and drank to excess, and was a notorious gambler and cheat at dice. What more can I say?  He was perhaps the most wicked man ever born.  

For a long time, Ciappelletto was protected by the power and influence of Messer Musciatto, who had often shielded him from both private citizens, whom he frequently harmed, and the law, which he constantly broke.

So, thinking that Ciappelletto was just the sort of person needed to deal with the wicked Burgundians, Messer Musciatto summoned him. 

“Master Ciappelletto,” he said, “I am leaving France soon.  Among my other affairs, I need to collect money owed to me by certain men from Burgundy.  They are deceivers, and I can’t think of anyone better suited to deal with them than you, especially since you have nothing else to do at present.  If you agree, I will secure for you the protection of the court and give you a generous share of whatever you collect.”

Ciappelletto, who was unemployed and lacked money, saw that his benefactor and protector was leaving France and realized that he had no choice but to agree.  So, he accepted the offer. 

Messer Musciatto soon departed, and Ciappelletto, armed with his benefactor’s power of attorney and letters of recommendation from the king, traveled to Burgundy, where he was unknown. There, contrary to his nature, he began to collect his payments, acting with courtesy and diplomacy, as if he was saving his cruelty and violence for a last resort.

While working in Burgundy, he lodged in the home of two Florentine brothers who were moneylenders and who treated him with great respect, for Messer Musciatto’s sake. During his stay, he fell ill.  The brothers, concerned for his well-being, summoned doctors and servants, and provided everything he needed to recover.  But their efforts were useless. The doctors said that Ciappelletto, old and accustomed to a life of excess, was weakening daily and would surely die.

One day, while discussing Ciappelletto’s illness near the room where he lay, the brothers said, “What are we to do with this man?  Our association with him is going to cause us trouble. We can’t send him away, sick as he is. It would be dishonorable, and people would think we are fools to have taken him in, cared for him, and paid for his medical treatments, only to send him away to die, especially since he has done nothing to offend us. But he is so wicked, he will never agree to confess his sins or receive the last rites.  And if he dies without confession, no church will accept his body. He will be tossed into a ditch like a dog. And even if he confesses, his sins are so numerous and horrific that no priest will be willing or able to absolve him.  Without absolution, his body will still be cast into a ditch. When the people of the city see this, they will surely riot. They already dislike us for our business, which they consider unfair and unethical.  They will surely say, “These Lombard dogs, whom the church refuses to bury, should be allowed to stay here no longer!” They will rush to our homes, steal our belongings, and perhaps even kill us. No matter what happens, if this man dies, we are doomed.”

Ciappelletto, who had excellent hearing, despite his illness, heard the brothers talking and called them to him.  

“I won’t have you worried about me,” he said. “I heard what you just said, and I know this would be your fate, if things happened as you expect. But I have a plan.  Find me the holiest, most worthy friar you can, and leave the rest to me. I will take care of you and me, and you will be pleased with the result.”

The brothers, although skeptical, went to a nearby monastery and asked for a learned and holy friar to hear the confession of a sick Lombard man at their house. The monks provided them with a wise, elderly friar who was a scholar of holy scripture and revered by the people of the city, and the brothers brought him home.

The friar entered Ciappelletto’s sickroom and sat by his side. He began by gently comforting him and then asked him how long it had been since his last confession.

Ciappelletto, who had never confessed in his life, answered, “Father, I usually confess once a week, sometimes more.  But since I became ill eight days ago, the pain has been so great that I haven’t been able to confess.”

“My son, you have done well,” replied the friar, “and you must continue to do so. I can see, because you confess so often, that I will have little to hear or to ask you.”

“Don’t say that, Father,” answered Ciappelletto.  “I never confess so much or so often that I don’t want to make a general confession of all my sins, from the day I was born to the present.  So, Father, I beg you, question me thoroughly, as if I had never confessed before. And don’t hold back because I am ill, for I would much rather suffer discomfort in my body than act in a way that might harm my soul, which my Savior redeemed with his precious blood.”

The holy man was pleased by these words, believing them to signal a virtuous heart. He commended Ciappelletto for his devout habits and began his questioning.

“Tell me, my son, have you ever sinned against God by lusting after women?”

“Father,” answered Ciappelletto, sighing, “I’m ashamed to tell you the truth, because I am afraid of appearing to boast.”

“Speak freely,” said the friar. “You can never sin by telling the truth, whether in confession or at any other time.”

“Then, since you assure me of this, I will tell you. I am a virgin, just as I came from my mother’s womb.”

“God bless you, my son,” exclaimed the friar. “You have done well. You are even more deserving, because, as a layman, you have more opportunity to sin than we who live according to a strict rule.”

“Have you ever offended God by the sin of gluttony?,” asked the friar. 

Ciappelletto answered, with a sigh, that he had many times. “Although,” he said, “besides the holy Lenten fasts, which I always observe, I fast on bread and water at least three days every week, I have often found myself (especially when I have been working hard, praying or making a pilgrimage) drinking water with as much gusto as others drink wine.  And many times, I have longed for simple salads of herbs, and have taken more pleasure in eating them than I believe is suitable for someone who fasts out of devotion, as I do.”

“My son,” said the friar, “these are natural and very minor sins.  Don’t burden your conscience with them.  Everyone, no matter how devout, finds food delicious after a fast and drink refreshing after hard work.”

“Don’t say that to comfort me, Father,” said Ciappelletto.  “You must know that things done for God should be done sincerely and with a pure heart.  Anyone who does otherwise sins.”

The friar, delighted, replied, “I’m glad you understand this, and I am very pleased with your clear and virtuous conscience. Now, tell me, have you ever sinned by being greedy, desiring more than was appropriate, or withholding what you should not have withheld?”

“Father, please don’t judge me because I’m staying in the home of these moneylenders,” replied Ciappelletto. “They have nothing to do with my sins. I am here to admonish them, to try to convince them to turn from their wicked ways, and I believe I might have been able to do so, if God had not seen fit to make me ill.  You should know that I inherited a great deal of wealth from my father. After his death, I gave most of it to the poor, keeping only enough to support myself and assist Christ’s poor.  I engaged in a little business, and it’s true that I desired to earn a profit, but I always shared what I earned with God’s poor. I spent half of my earnings on what I needed and gave the other half to the poor. My Creator has so blessed me that my business has grown, and my affairs have continued to improve.”

“You have done well,” said the friar.  “But tell me, have you been often angered?”

“Oh, Father,” cried Ciappelletto, “that I must tell you I have very often been!  Who could keep himself from anger, when he sees people constantly doing evil things?  They ignore God’s commandments and have no fear of His judgment.  Every day, I would rather be dead than alive, as I see young men pursuing vanities, hear them curse and swear, watch them frequent taverns instead of churches, and follow the ways of the world instead of God.”

“My son, this is righteous anger,” said the friar, “and I can assign you no penance for it. But tell me, has your anger ever led you to kill anyone or to speak against or harm another?”

“Oh, Father,” replied the sick man, “you seem to be a man of God. How can you speak such words?  If I had ever even thought of doing any of the things you have described, do you think God would have spared me for so long? These are the actions of wicked men, whom I cannot even bear to look upon. When I see them, I say only, “God have mercy on you.”

The friar then asked, “Now tell me, my son (God bless you), have you ever lied in court or spoken against another person, or taken something that did not belong to you, without the owner’s permission?”

“Yes, Father, it’s true,” replied Ciappelletto. “I have spoken against others. There was a neighbor of mine, who beat his wife so mercilessly that I once complained of him to her relatives.  I could not bear to witness such cruelty.”

“You say you were a businessman. Did you ever cheat anyone, as merchants sometimes do?”

“Yes, Father,” answered Ciappelletto. “I can’t recall who, except perhaps one time, a man came to pay me money he owed for some cloth. I threw the money into a box without counting it.  Later, I realized he had overpaid me by four farthings. Because I didn’t see him again and had kept the money for a year, hoping to return it, I gave it away as alms.”

“This was a small matter, and you were right to do as you did,” said the friar.
