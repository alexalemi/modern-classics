1. SENTENCE RESTRUCTURING: Smith's opening argument about property and inequality contains some of his most powerful long sentences. Preserved the force while breaking into clearer units.

This is one of Smith's most famous and provocative statements — kept nearly verbatim. Often quoted by both critics and supporters of capitalism.

4. LEGAL HISTORY: Simplified Smith's discussion of English court evolution (King's Bench, Exchequer, Chancery) while keeping the key insight — that competition between courts improved the quality of justice. The writ of ejectment explanation is preserved as a vivid example.

5. HOMER REFERENCE: Kept the Agamemnon/Achilles example — it's a perfect Smith illustration, grounding abstract theory in a concrete (and famous) literary reference.

6. FRENCH COURTS: Kept the detail about Toulouse judges earning less than a footman — another classic Smith touch showing how concrete data illuminates abstract principles.

7. TONE: This chapter is more philosophical than the defense chapter. Smith is building a theory of how government emerges from human nature and inequality. The tone is scholarly but accessible, with flashes of his characteristic moral clarity.
