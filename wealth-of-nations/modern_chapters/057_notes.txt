1. SENTENCE RESTRUCTURING: Smith's detailed descriptions of trading company regulations, their charter terms, and their financial histories contain very dense, legalistic prose. Broke into clearer paragraphs and sentences while preserving all the important details.

Translated "negroes" as "enslaved people" to reflect modern understanding while preserving Smith's meaning. Smith is not endorsing slavery — he condemns it elsewhere — but here he's simply describing what these companies traded in.

4. PROPER NAMES AND COMPANIES: Kept all company names (Turkey Company, Hudson's Bay Company, Royal African Company, South Sea Company, East India Company). Kept key figures (Sir Josiah Child, Mr. Anderson, Mr. Dobbs, Riquet).

5. SMITH'S KEY INSIGHT: The principal-agent problem — "the directors of such companies, however, manage other people's money rather than their own" — is one of Smith's most prescient observations. It anticipates centuries of corporate governance theory. Preserved prominently.

6. TONE: Smith is devastating in his assessment of joint stock companies — systematically documenting their failures through concrete examples. His dry wit shines through in details like the committee shipping bricks from England to West Africa, the Hudson's Bay Company's "forts" (scare quotes in his text), and the South Sea Company's one profitable voyage out of ten.

7. Removed all superscript endnote reference numbers.
