Major changes in this chapter:

1. SENTENCE RESTRUCTURING: Smith's complex sentences with multiple subordinate clauses were broken into shorter, clearer sentences. For example, the opening sentence about manufacturing workmanship was split to separate the causes (better machinery, skill, division of labor) from the effects (less labor needed, compensating for higher labor costs).

2. VOCABULARY MODERNIZATION: "manufactures" -> "manufactured goods" in the title and where it means products; "the manufacturing workmanship" -> "manufacturing labor"; "rude materials" -> "raw materials"; "toys" -> "small metal goods and trinkets" (18th-century "toys" meant small manufactured items); "artificer" -> "craftsman"; "superfine cloth" -> "finest cloth"; "dearness" -> "high cost"; "conveniencies and luxuries" -> "comforts and luxuries"; "corn" -> "grain."

3. REMOVED SUPERSCRIPT REFERENCES: Stripped all editorial endnote numbers (644-650, etc.).

4. PRESERVED SMITH'S HISTORICAL EXAMPLES: Kept all the specific price comparisons (the twenty-pound watch vs. twenty-shilling watch, the cloth prices from Henry VII and Edward IV's reigns, the Queen Elizabeth stocking anecdote) while making the monetary comparisons clearer.

5. CLARIFIED PRICE COMPARISONS: Smith's method of comparing historical prices -- converting to grain equivalents, then to modern money -- is presented step by step so readers can follow the logic.

6. PRESERVED SMITH'S VOICE: Maintained his first-person observations ("I consider all claims of this kind somewhat uncertain," "as far as I know"), his methodical building of evidence, and his attention to historical detail.

7. SUMPTUARY LAWS: Briefly explained what these laws were (laws capping luxury spending) rather than leaving the term unexplained.
