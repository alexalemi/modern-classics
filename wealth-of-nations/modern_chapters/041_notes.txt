Major changes in this chapter:

1. "Bounty" consistently translated as "subsidy" (the modern equivalent) throughout, while noting the original term. Smith's chapter title "Of Bounties" becomes "Of Export Subsidies."

2. The central economic argument -- that the grain subsidy raises nominal but not real prices -- is preserved carefully. Smith's crucial distinction: grain is the "regulating commodity" that determines the real value of everything else, so you can't raise its real value through price manipulation. This is one of his most important theoretical contributions.

3. The detailed herring fishery arithmetic is preserved fully -- Smith's devastating cost-per-barrel calculations showing that government subsidies sometimes cost more per barrel than the herrings themselves are worth. The 1759 figure (each barrel costing the government 113 pounds in subsidies) is particularly absurd and effective.

4. "Corn" -> "grain" throughout. "Stock" -> "capital." "Dear" -> "expensive." "Liberal" -> "generous."

5. Removed all superscript reference numbers (955-998).

6. Smith's bitter observation about who really benefits from the subsidy (grain merchants, not farmers) is preserved with its full ironic force.

7. The passage about manufacturers intimidating parliament is echoed here -- landowners imitating manufacturers but without manufacturers' keen understanding of self-interest.

8. The distinction between subsidies on production vs. export is preserved -- Smith notes that production subsidies would be more logical but merchants don't want them because they'd lower domestic prices.

9. Smith's personal aside about manufacturers he personally knew funding their own export subsidies to double domestic prices -- a great concrete example -- preserved.

10. The Spain/Portugal dam metaphor is one of Smith's most vivid analogies -- preserved nearly verbatim.
</explanation>
