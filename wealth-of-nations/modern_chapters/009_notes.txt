Major changes in this chapter:

1. VOCABULARY: "stock" → "capital" throughout (this chapter's title itself is "Of the Profits of Stock" but Smith means capital); "dear" → "expensive"; "conveniencies" → "comforts"; "corn" → "grain"; "scarce" (adverb) → "hardly/almost never"; "neat profit" → "net or clear profit"; "per centum" → "percent"; "opulence" → "wealth"; "mandarines" → "petty officials"; "engrossing" → "controlling/monopolizing"; "manufactures" → "industries/manufacturing"; "Muhammadan" → "Muslim"; "undertaker" → "contractor"; "barbarous nations" → "peoples" (in the Roman context).

2. REMOVED SUPERSCRIPT REFERENCES: Removed all editor's endnote numbers (310-340).

3. KEY ARGUMENTS PRESERVED:
   - The inverse relationship between wages and profits (capital growth raises wages but lowers profits)
   - Interest rates as a proxy for profit rates
   - The historical trajectory of English interest rate legislation from Henry VIII through Queen Anne
   - Scotland vs. England vs. France vs. Holland comparisons
   - The new colony argument (why both wages and profits are high simultaneously in colonies)
   - China's institutional constraints on wealth
   - Bengal as an example of an economy in decline with extreme profit rates
   - The devastating final paragraph about compound interest effects of high profits vs. simple interest effects of high wages

4. SMITH'S WIT PRESERVED: The chapter ends with one of Smith's sharpest observations -- that merchants complain endlessly about the effects of high wages on prices while saying nothing about the effects of their own high profits. This is preserved with full force.

5. HISTORICAL REFERENCES: Clarified references to specific statutes and reigns while keeping the historical detail. Brutus lending at 48% in Cyprus (from Cicero's letters) is a vivid example preserved in full.

6. THE HOLLAND PASSAGE: Smith's charming observation that in Holland it's unfashionable not to be in business, comparing an idle person among businesspeople to a civilian in a military camp, is preserved.

7. "MONEY MAKES MONEY": Preserved this proverb and Smith's gloss on it -- that the great difficulty is getting the first bit of capital.
