<explanation>
Major changes in this chapter:

1. TITLE MODIFICATION: Changed "Of the Nature, Accumulation, and Employment of Stock" to "On the Nature, Accumulation, and Employment of Capital." Smith's "stock" in this context means capital -- the goods and money accumulated to fund production. Since he's using "stock" in its broadest sense (encompassing all three divisions he'll discuss in the next chapter), I used "Capital" in the title for clarity, matching the vocabulary established in earlier chapters.

2. SENTENCE RESTRUCTURING: Smith's long, periodic sentences were broken into shorter ones. For example, his opening sentence about primitive society (which runs to about 50 words with multiple clauses) was restructured into clearer, shorter statements. His description of the weaver was similarly simplified.

3. VOCABULARY MODERNIZATION: "rude state of society" -> "primitive society"; "stock should be accumulated or stored up" -> "stockpile of goods"; "peculiar business" -> "specialized work"; "hut begins to go to ruin" -> "shelter starts falling apart"; "rude state of things" -> "primitive state"; "the operations of each workman are gradually reduced to a greater degree of simplicity" -> "each worker's tasks are reduced to greater and greater simplicity."

4. PRESERVED SMITH'S STRUCTURE: Smith uses this introduction to set up the logical framework: accumulation must precede division of labor, and the two reinforce each other. This circular logic is clearly preserved.

5. PRESERVED THE WEAVER EXAMPLE: Smith's concrete illustration of a weaver who needs stored-up capital before he can specialize is kept intact as a vivid example.

6. SIMPLIFIED THE CHAPTER OUTLINE: Smith's roadmap of Book II's five chapters is presented clearly and directly.

7. REMOVED SUPERSCRIPT REFERENCES: Stripped editorial endnote numbers (669-670).

8. SECOND-PERSON ADDRESS: Used "you" in places where Smith uses the impersonal "a man" or "every man" to make the abstract argument more immediate and engaging for modern readers.
</explanation>

- This is a crucial definitional chapter where Smith establishes his taxonomy of capital
- Key distinction: Smith divides all "stock" into three categories:
  1. Stock reserved for immediate consumption (not capital -- yields no revenue)
  2. Fixed capital (yields revenue without changing hands -- machines, buildings, land improvements, skills)
  3. Circulating capital (yields revenue by changing hands -- money, provisions, raw materials, finished goods in transit)
- "Stock" in this chapter has a broader meaning than "capital" -- it includes all accumulated goods. Use "stock" when Smith means total accumulated goods/wealth, and "capital" only for the portion that yields revenue
