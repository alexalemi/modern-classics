1. SENTENCE RESTRUCTURING: This chapter contains some of the longest and most complex passages in the entire book — particularly the East India Company financial history, with its nested dates, amounts, percentages, and legal arrangements. Broke into much shorter, clearer sentences while preserving all the essential financial and institutional detail.

2. VOCABULARY: "copartnery" → "private partnership"; "stock-jobbing" → "stock-market schemes"; "nett" → "net"; "adventurers" → "traders/investors"; "engross" → "dominate/monopolize"; "sovereign" → "ruler/government."

3. KEY PASSAGES PRESERVED:
   - "No other rulers ever were, or ever could be, so perfectly indifferent about the happiness or misery of their subjects" — one of Smith's most devastating judgments on corporate governance.
   - "Consumption is the sole end and purpose of all production" (from ch. 051) — echoed here in the concluding arguments about monopoly.
   - The passage about foreign trade as "commercial warfare" requiring constant vigilance that corporate directors can't provide.

4. EAST INDIA COMPANY: Smith's detailed account of the EIC's financial and political evolution is one of the most important narratives in the book — it's his primary evidence for the failure of corporate imperialism. Preserved all the key events (Dupleix, Hyder Ali, the 1773 reforms) while making the financial narrative clearer.

5. FOUR SUITABLE TRADES: Smith's conclusion — that joint stock companies work only for banking, insurance, canals, and water supply — is presented as a clear numbered list. This is a remarkably prescient piece of institutional analysis.

6. TONE: Smith is at his most scathing in this chapter. His contempt for the East India Company's misgovernment — "appointing the plunderers of India" — is barely controlled. Preserved this intensity while making it accessible.

7. Removed all superscript endnote reference numbers throughout.

8. SENSITIVE CONTENT: References to the slave trade (Asiento contract) translated clearly. References to colonial violence and exploitation preserved faithfully — Smith is critical of all of it.
