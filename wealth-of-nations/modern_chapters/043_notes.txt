Major changes in this chapter:

1. Completed the four-branch analysis of the grain trade: importer, exporter, carrier. Each branch's contribution (or not) to the domestic market is clearly laid out.

2. The famous comparison of grain laws to religious laws is preserved verbatim -- "People feel so personally invested in what relates to their livelihood in this world, or to their happiness in the next" -- one of Smith's most striking observations.

3. Smith's crucial passage about individual effort -- "The natural effort of every individual to better his own condition, when allowed to operate with freedom and security, is so powerful a force..." -- is preserved carefully. This is one of the thematic pillars of the entire book, parallel to the invisible hand passage.

4. The closing reference to the laws of Solon is preserved, along with Smith's characteristically balanced verdict: the new law is imperfect but the best that current political reality permits.

5. "Corn" -> "grain" throughout. "Stock" -> "capital." "Bounty" -> "subsidy." "Dear" -> "expensive."

6. Removed all superscript references (1012-1037).

7. Smith's argument that Britain's prosperity comes from security of property and individual liberty, not from trade regulations, is preserved with full force -- it directly echoes the invisible hand passage.

8. The distinction between the national debt and prosperity ("The national debt has most assuredly not been the cause of it") is a wonderfully dry aside -- preserved.

9. The detailed discussion of specific price thresholds under the new and old grain laws is simplified for clarity while preserving all the key numbers.

10. Smith's moral argument about export bans ("preventing a farmer from selling his goods at all times in the best market is clearly a sacrifice of the ordinary principles of justice") is preserved -- connecting his economic analysis to natural rights philosophy.

11. "The present disturbances" is not referenced in this section, but "the present king" refers to George III and his recent grain law reform.
