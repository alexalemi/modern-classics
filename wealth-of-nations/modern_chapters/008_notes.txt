Major changes in this chapter:

1. VOCABULARY: "stock" → "capital"; "conveniencies" → "comforts"; "dear" → "expensive"; "cheap years" retained where the meaning of "years of low food prices" is clear from context; "artificers" → "skilled workers"; "corn" → "grain"; "menial servants" → "domestic servants"; "manufactures" (noun, meaning factories) → "manufacturing industries" or "industries"; "emoluments" → "compensation"; "fewel" → "fuel"; "pecuniary" → "money"; "scarce" (adverb) → "hardly/barely"; "œconomy" → "management/economy"; "tailles" explained as "tax official."

2. REMOVED SUPERSCRIPT REFERENCES: Removed all editor's endnote numbers (262-309).

3. MAJOR PASSAGES PRESERVED:
   - The master-worker power imbalance passage is one of Smith's most powerful and is preserved in full, including his observation that employers' combinations are "the usual, and one may say, the natural state of things which nobody ever hears of."
   - The North America vs. England wage comparison, with all specific wages preserved.
   - The China passage, including the grim details about Canton's boat-dwelling families and infanticide, is preserved completely -- it's essential to Smith's argument about stagnant economies.
   - The Bengal/East India Company critique is preserved as Smith's devastating comparison of constitutional government vs. corporate domination.
   - "No society can surely be flourishing and happy, of which the far greater part of the members are poor and miserable" -- preserved as one of Smith's most quotable lines.
   - The carriage/walking analogy about Scottish wages is preserved.

4. SENTENCE RESTRUCTURING: This chapter contains some of Smith's longest and most complex sentences, particularly in the passages about worker combinations and the statistical analysis of cheap vs. expensive years. These were broken into shorter, clearer units while maintaining the argumentative flow.

5. SENSITIVE CONTENT: The China passage includes descriptions of extreme poverty and infanticide. These are presented faithfully as Smith wrote them -- they're central to his economic argument about what happens in stagnant economies, not gratuitous detail.

6. SMITH'S VOICE: Preserved his first-person observations throughout ("I have been frequently told," "I believe," "I have frequently heard it represented"), his moral outrage at worker exploitation, and his dry wit (the observation about soldiers being paid by the piece).
