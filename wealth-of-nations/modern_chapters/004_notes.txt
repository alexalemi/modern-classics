Major changes in this chapter:

1. DIAMOND-WATER PARADOX: Preserved this famous passage with maximum clarity. Gave "value in use" and "value in exchange" their own paragraph, then the water/diamond example its own paragraph for emphasis. This is one of the most cited passages in economics.

2. BARTER PROBLEM: Smith's vivid illustration of why barter fails (the butcher has meat but doesn't need more bread/beer) is preserved in full -- it builds nicely on the butcher/brewer/baker from Chapter 2.

3. HISTORICAL CURRENCY EXAMPLES: Kept all of Smith's examples (cattle, salt, shells, dried cod, tobacco, sugar, nails in a Scottish village) as they're charming and illustrative. The Scottish nails anecdote connects back to the nail-making example from Chapter 1.

4. COIN DEBASEMENT: Smith's passionate point about rulers cheating their subjects by debasing currency is preserved with its moral force -- "the greed and dishonesty of rulers" for "the avarice and injustice of princes." This is one of those moments where Smith's quiet outrage breaks through.

5. MONETARY HISTORY: Kept the detailed history of Roman, English, French, and Scottish currency weights, as it serves Smith's argument about debasement. Clarified the Tower pound/Troy pound distinction with a parenthetical.

6. VOCABULARY: "clogged and embarrassed" to "frustrated and hampered"; "superfluity" to "surplus"; "inconveniency" to "inconvenience/deadlock"; "assaying" kept but explained; "aulnagers and stampmasters" explained as "official inspectors"; "per centum" does not appear but "farthing" kept with context.

7. REMOVED: All superscript reference numbers (156-181).

8. SMITH'S CLOSING: Preserved his charming direct address to the reader asking for "patience and attention" -- and his self-deprecating note about risking tedium for the sake of clarity. Changed "perspicuous" to "clear" (a word no modern reader would use).

9. BIBLICAL REFERENCE: Abraham paying for the field of Machpelah -- kept in full as Smith uses it to illustrate the transition from weighing to counting money.
