Major changes in this chapter:

1. TITLE CHANGE: "Of the Division of Stock" -> "The Division of Capital." Since Smith's entire point is about how accumulated wealth divides into categories, and his key distinction is between capital (which earns returns) and consumption stock (which doesn't), "Capital" is the clearer modern term.

2. SENTENCE RESTRUCTURING: This chapter is more definitional and taxonomic than argumentative, so the restructuring focused on making Smith's categories crystal clear. His longer sentences were broken up, especially in the section on farmers' different types of capital (draft animals vs. fattening cattle vs. breeding stock vs. seeds).

3. VOCABULARY MODERNIZATION: "stock" -> "stock" or "capital" depending on context (used "stock" for the broader concept including consumption goods, "capital" for the revenue-earning portions); "master artificer" -> "master craftsman"; "instruments of trade" -> "tools of the trade" / "equipment"; "husbandry" -> "farming" / "agriculture"; "provisions" -> "provisions" (kept, as it's still clear); "slitt-mill" -> "slitting mill"; "labouring servants" -> "laborers" / "workers"; "Indostan" -> "India."

4. BOLD AND ITALIC FORMATTING: Used bold for the three major divisions (First, Second, Third) and italic for the initial definition of "capital" to help readers navigate this taxonomic chapter.

5. SECTION BREAKS: Added horizontal rules (---) to separate the individual-level analysis from the society-level analysis, and to separate the taxonomy from the discussion of how the categories interact.

6. PRESERVED SMITH'S KEY INSIGHTS:
   - The dwelling-house distinction (a home you live in is NOT capital) is presented clearly
   - The masquerade costume rental example is preserved as a charming illustration
   - Smith's remarkably modern insight about human skills as fixed capital ("the acquired and useful abilities of all the inhabitants") is highlighted
   - The observation about people hiding wealth in unstable countries is kept as a vivid illustration

7. PRESERVED SMITH'S CONCRETE EXAMPLES: The progression from tailor (needles) to shoemaker (slightly more) to weaver (much more) is maintained. The farming examples (draft animals, fattening cattle, breeding stock, seeds) are all kept but clarified.

8. REMOVED SUPERSCRIPT REFERENCES: Stripped editorial endnote numbers (671-673).

9. TREASURE TROVE PASSAGE: Smith's vivid passage about people burying wealth in unstable countries is preserved, including the historical detail about treasure trove laws in feudal Europe.
