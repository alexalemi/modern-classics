THE SIXTH STORY

[Day the Third]

RICCIARDO MINUTOLO, BEING IN LOVE WITH FILIPPELLO FIGHINOLFI'S WIFE AND KNOWING HER JEALOUSY OF HER HUSBAND, DEVISES A PLAN TO BRING HER TO A BATHHOUSE, WHERE, THINKING SHE IS MEETING HER HUSBAND, SHE INSTEAD FINDS THAT SHE HAS BEEN WITH RICCIARDO

After Elisa finished speaking, the queen instructed Fiammetta to share a story, and Fiammetta began with a smile, "It would be fitting to depart from our city, which, like any place, is full of interesting events, and recount something that has happened in other parts of the world. So I shall tell you of how a woman, who pretends to be so shy of love, was brought by a lover's cleverness to taste the fruits of love before she had known its flowers. This will teach you caution in the things that may happen and provide amusement in the things that have already occurred.

In Naples, an ancient and delightful city, there was a young man named Ricciardo Minutolo, who was renowned for his noble lineage and great wealth. Although he had a very beautiful and charming wife, he fell in love with a woman who, in the general opinion, far surpassed all the other ladies of Naples in beauty. Her name was Catella, and she was the wife of another young gentleman, Filippello Fighinolfi, whom she loved and cherished above all else as a truly virtuous woman.

Ricciardo loved this Catella and did everything common for winning a lady's love and favor, but to no avail. Despairing of his passion and unable to rid himself of it, he knew not whether to live or die. 

One day, certain ladies of Ricciardo's family urgently advised him to abandon this love, seeing that he was merely wearying himself in vain, for Catella had no affection but for Filippello, of whom she was so jealous that she imagined every bird that flew would take him from her. Hearing of Catella's jealousy, Ricciardo quickly devised a plan to achieve his desires. He began to feign that he was no longer in love with Catella but had instead set his heart on another lady, for whose love he started to joust and perform other public displays. 

It wasn't long before nearly all the people of Naples, including Catella herself, were convinced that Ricciardo no longer loved Catella but was ardently in love with this second lady. Catella, believing this, lost the reserve she had previously shown him out of regard for his love, and she would greet him familiarly as she did others when they met.

One day, when many ladies and gentlemen had gone, as was the custom in Naples, to the seaside to dine and enjoy the pleasant weather, Ricciardo, knowing that Catella had gone with her companions, joined them. After allowing himself to be persuaded to stay, he acted as if he were deeply in love with his new lady, giving the ladies, including Catella, much reason to discuss it. 

When one of the ladies wandered off, leaving Catella alone with Ricciardo, he slyly hinted at an affair of Filippello, her husband. This immediately aroused Catella's jealousy, and she became impatient to know what he meant. After restraining herself for a while, she begged Ricciardo, for the sake of the lady he loved most, to enlighten her about what he had said of Filippello. Ricciardo replied that he could not refuse her request, but she must swear never to repeat what he told her until she had seen for herself that it was true.

Catella agreed and swore never to reveal what he would tell her. Ricciardo then said, "Madam, if I still loved you as I once did, I would not dare tell you anything that I thought might upset you. But since that love has passed, I shall be less cautious in revealing the whole truth. I do not know if Filippello has ever resented the love I bore you or believed that you returned my affections. Be that as it may, he has never shown me any sign of it. But now, perhaps thinking I would be less suspicious, it seems he would like to have my wife at his pleasure, just as I fear I may have done to him. 

"I have learned that for some time he has been secretly sending her messages, all of which she has told me, answering them as I have instructed her. Just this morning, before I came here, I found a woman in my house who I immediately recognized as Filippello's agent. My wife told me that the woman said Filippello wishes to meet your privately in a bathhouse in this city. I did not send my wife there, but if I were in your place, I would arrange to be there instead of her, and when he arrives thinking to be with her, you can let him know with whom he has been and give him the dishonor he seeks to inflict on you and me."

Catella, without considering who was telling her this or suspecting his design, immediately, as is the way of jealous people, believed his words and began fitting certain past events to his story. Filled with sudden anger, she replied that she would certainly do as he advised - it was no great matter - and that she would certainly shame Filippello if he came there. Ricciardo, well pleased that his plan seemed likely to succeed, confirmed her purpose with many other words and strengthened her belief in his story, begging her, nonetheless, never to say that she had heard it from him, which she promised.

The next morning, Ricciardo went to a good woman who kept the bathhouse he had mentioned to Catella and, telling her his plan, asked her to assist him, which she, being much beholden to him, agreed to do. In the bathhouse, there was a very dark chamber with no window, and the woman prepared this room and a bed there for Ricciardo, who laid himself down to wait for Catella after he had dined.

Catella, having heard Ricciardo's words and giving more credence to them than she should have, returned home that evening full of resentment. When Filippello also came home, and perhaps did not show her his usual affection as he was preoccupied with other thoughts, her suspicions rose even higher, and she said to herself, "Truly, his mind is occupied with that other lady with whom he thinks to take his pleasure tomorrow; but this shall not come to pass."

With this thought, she spent nearly the whole night considering how she should speak to him when she was with him in the bathhouse. When the hour of noon arrived, she took her waiting-woman and, without changing her plan, went to the bathhouse Ricciardo had named. There, she found the good woman, who, as instructed by Ricciardo, asked her if she was the lady who was to meet Filippello. Catella answered that she was, and the woman told her to go inside to him. Catella, who was seeking what she would have been better off not finding, had herself taken to the chamber where Ricciardo was, and entering with her head covered, locked the door behind her.

Ricciardo, seeing her enter, rose joyfully to his feet and, embracing her, softly said, "Welcome, my darling!" She, in order to pretend to be someone else, embraced and kissed him and made much of him without speaking, fearing that her voice would betray her. The chamber was very dark, which pleased them both, and for a long time they remained there, to the greater delight and pleasure of one party than the other.

But presently, it seeming to Catella time to vent the resentment she felt, she began, all aflame with rage and spite, to speak thus: "Alas, how wretched is women's lot and how ill-bestowed the love that many of us bear our husbands! I, unhappy that I am, these eight years have I loved you more than my life, and you, as I have felt, are all afire and all consumed with love of a strange woman, wicked and perverse man that you are! Now with whom do you think you have been? You have been with her whom you have too long deceived with your false flatteries, making a show of love to her and being enamored elsewhere. I am Catella, not Ricciardo's wife, disloyal traitor that you are! Listen if you know my voice; it is indeed I; and it seems to me a thousand years till we are in the light, so I may shame you as you deserve, scurvy discredited cur that you are!"

Ricciardo inwardly rejoiced at these words and, without making any reply, embraced and kissed her and caressed her more than ever. Catella, seeing this, continued her speech, saying, "Ah, you think to cajole me with your feigned caresses, you wicked dog, and to appease and console me; but you are mistaken; I shall never be comforted for this until I have put you to shame therefor in the presence of all our friends and kinsmen and neighbors. Am I not as fair as Ricciardo's wife, you villain? Am I not as good a gentlewoman? Why do you not answer, you sorry dog? What has she more than I? Keep your distance; touch me not; you have done enough for today. Now that you know who I am, I am well assured that all you could do would be by force; but, so God grant me grace, I will yet cause you suffer want thereof, and I know not what hinders me from sending for Ricciardo, who has loved me more than himself and could never boast that I once even looked at him; nor know I what harm it were to do it. You thought to have his wife here and it is as if you had had her, inasmuch as it is none of your fault that the thing has miscarried; wherefore, were I to have himself, you could not with reason blame me."

At last, Ricciardo, realizing that if he let her go in that belief, much harm might come of it, decided to reveal himself and undeceive her. Catching her in his arms and holding her fast, so she might not get away, he said, "Sweet my soul, be not angered; that which I could not have of you by simply loving you, Love has taught me to obtain by practice; and I am your Ricciardo."

Catella, hearing this and recognizing his voice, would have thrown herself out of the bed, but could not. Ricciardo stopped her mouth and said, "Madam, this that has been may henceforth on no wise be undone, though you should cry all the days of your life; and if you cry out or cause this ever anywise to be known of any one, two things will come thereof; the one (which should no little concern you) will be that your honor and fair fame will be marred, for that, albeit you may avouch that I brought you hither by practice, I shall say that it is not true, nay, that I caused you come hither for monies and gifts that I promised you, whereof for that I gave you not so largely as you hoped, you waxed angry and made all this talk and this outcry; and you know that folk are more apt to credit ill than good, wherefore I shall more readily be believed than you. Secondly, there will ensue thereof a mortal enmity between your husband and myself, and it may as well happen that I shall kill him as he me, in which case you are never after like to be happy or content. Wherefore, heart of my body, go not about at once to dishonor yourself and to cast your husband and myself into strife and peril. You are not the first woman, nor will you be the last, who has been deceived, nor have I in this practised upon you to bereave you of your own, but for the exceeding love that I bear you and am minded ever to bear you and to be your most humble servant."

Catella, though greatly provoked and complaining grievously, nevertheless, her reason allowed so much force to his true words that she knew it to be possible that it should happen as he said. Quitting her resentment, she agreed to make peace with him, and from that day forth, having learned how much more savory were a lover's kisses than a husband's, she loved Ricciardo very tenderly, and they many a time had joy of their loves, ordering themselves with the utmost discretion.
