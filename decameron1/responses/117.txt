The Ninth Story

[Day the Tenth]

SALADIN, DISGUISED AS A MERCHANT, IS GENEROUSLY HOSTED BY MESSER TORELLO D'ISTRIA. TORELLO THEN JOINS THE CRUSADE, ASKING HIS WIFE TO WAIT A YEAR AND A DAY BEFORE REMARRYING IF SHE DOES NOT HEAR FROM HIM. TORELLO IS CAPTURED BY THE SARACENS AND COMES UNDER THE NOTICE OF SALADIN, WHO RECOGNIZES HIM AND TREATS HIM WITH GREAT HONOR. TORELLO FALLS ILL, AND SALADIN MAGICALLY TRANSPORTS HIM BACK TO PAVIA, WHERE HE IS REUNITED WITH HIS WIFE ON THE EVE OF HER REMARRYING.

Filomena finished her tale, and all praised the great gratitude shown by Titus. The king then turned to Dioneo, saying, "Dear ladies, Filomena is right that friendship is sadly underappreciated in our times. If our purpose here was to correct the faults of the age, I could speak at length on this topic. But since our aim is different, I will instead tell you a story - perhaps a bit long, but surely delightful - about the magnificence of Saladin. Even if our own failings prevent us from fully earning the friendship of others, perhaps the events you hear will inspire you to take pleasure in acts of service, in the hope that we may one day be rewarded for them.

I must tell you that, according to many, a great Christian crusade was undertaken in the time of Emperor Frederick the First to reclaim the Holy Land. Saladin, a noble and powerful prince who was then the Sultan of Babylon, heard of this preparation and decided to see the Christian princes' plans for himself, so he could better prepare. Ordering his affairs in Egypt, he set out on a pilgrimage in merchant's clothing, accompanied by only two of his wisest officers and three servants.

As they rode through Lombardy, intending to cross the mountains, they encountered near Pavia a gentleman named Messer Torello d'Istria, who was on his way to his country estate. Seeing that the travelers were gentlemen, Torello told them they could not reach Pavia before nightfall. When they asked where they could find lodging, he offered to send one of his men to guide them to a suitable place. 

The servant led the travelers by backroads to Torello's own home, where Torello welcomed them warmly. Saladin, quick to understand, realized that Torello had intentionally brought them there so they could not refuse his hospitality. Nevertheless, Saladin graciously thanked him, saying, "Sir, were it not for your courtesy, we might complain of you for leading us astray." Torello replied that their lodging would be humble compared to what they deserved, but that they could find no decent inn nearby. He then had his servants attend to the travelers' horses.

Torello brought the three gentlemen to their prepared chambers, where he entertained them with pleasant conversation until supper. Saladin and his companions, who all knew Latin, found Torello to be the most charming and well-mannered man they had ever met. Torello, in turn, sensed that his guests were far more important than he had first thought, and he was disappointed that he could not honor them with more distinguished company and entertainment that evening. However, he had already sent a servant to his wife in Pavia to make preparations for a magnificent banquet the next day.

When it was time to sup, Torello left the seating arrangements to his guests, out of deference to their rank. After the meal, judging them to be weary, he bid them retire to their beds, while he also went to rest. 

The servant sent to Pavia informed Torello's wife, who, with a regal spirit, quickly called in many of Torello's friends and servants to prepare an extraordinary banquet. She also invited some of the city's nobles to attend. By torchlight, she ensured that everything was arranged magnificently.

The next morning, Saladin and his companions arose, and Torello took them to a nearby river to demonstrate his falcons' skills. Then, when Saladin asked to be directed to the best inn in Pavia, Torello said he would guide them there himself. To Saladin's surprise, they were instead taken to Torello's own house, where nearly fifty of the city's most prominent citizens had gathered to receive the strangers. Saladin protested, but Torello insisted that, while he could not take credit for their chance meeting the previous night, their visit this morning was entirely due to the gentlemen themselves, and his fellow citizens wished to welcome them.

Overcome, Saladin and his companions dismounted and were joyfully received. They were led to sumptuously prepared chambers, where they changed out of their travel clothes. When they entered the grand hall, they found a magnificent banquet awaiting them, fit for an emperor. Torello's wife, a beautiful and noble lady, came to greet them, accompanied by her two young sons who seemed like angels. She graciously offered them fine robes and other luxurious gifts, saying that as women, their offerings may be small, but they are given with great goodwill.

Torello then took the strangers on a tour of his home, showing them his most precious possessions. When the time came for them to depart the next morning, Torello provided them with fresh, strong horses in place of their weary mounts. Saladin was deeply impressed by Torello's generosity and hospitality, remarking that if the Christian kings were like this gentleman, the Sultan of Babylon would have little hope of prevailing against them.

After thanking Torello profusely, the travelers continued on their way. Torello accompanied them some distance outside the city, and when they finally parted, he said, "Gentlemen, I do not know who you are, nor do I ask - but you will never convince me that you are mere merchants." Saladin replied that perhaps they would yet show him their "merchandise," which would confirm his belief. Then Saladin departed with his companions, resolved that if he survived the coming war, he would honor Torello no less than Torello had honored him.

Torello returned to Pavia, wondering who his guests truly were, but never guessing the truth. As the time for the Crusade approached, Torello, despite his wife's tears and pleas, was determined to go. Before departing, he made her promise that if she did not have certain news of his survival, she would wait a year and a month and a day before remarrying. The lady wept, but agreed, saying she would remain his wife in life and memory.

Torello then set out on his journey, eventually reaching Genoa, where he embarked for the Holy Land. There, the Christian army was soon struck by a terrible illness, and nearly all were captured by Saladin without a battle being fought. Torello was among those taken prisoner and brought to Alexandria, where, fearful of revealing his identity, he took up the training of hawks, at which he excelled. This brought him to the attention of Saladin, who valued him as a falconer, though neither recognized the other.

Torello longed to return to Pavia, and when some Genoese ambassadors came to Saladin to negotiate ransoms, Torello seized the opportunity to write to his wife, letting her know he was alive and would return as soon as possible. He entrusted the letter to one of the ambassadors, asking that it be delivered to his uncle, the Abbot of San Pietro in Ciel d'Oro.
