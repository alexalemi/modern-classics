THE SIXTH STORY

[Day the Fourth]

Andrevuola Loves Gabriotto and Tells Him a Dream She Has Had, Whereupon He Tells Her One of His Own and Then Suddenly Dies in Her Arms. As She and a Maid of Hers Carry His Body to His Own House, They Are Taken by the Officers of Justice and Brought Before the Provost, to Whom She Explains What Has Happened. The Provost Tries to Force Her, but She Resists, and Her Father, Upon Learning of the Matter, Arranges for Her Release, as She Is Found Innocent; Whereupon, Refusing to Remain in the World, She Becomes a Nun.

Filomena's story was warmly received by the ladies, as they had often heard the song it referenced but never learned the reason behind it. But the king, having heard the end of the tale, instructed Pamfilo to continue the sequence. Pamfilo said, "The dream in the previous story gives me cause to recount one that mentions two dreams, which foretold a future event, just as the earlier one had revealed a past occurrence. You must know, dear ladies, that it is a common human experience to see various things in our sleep. While these visions may appear utterly real to the dreamer, upon waking, some are found to be true, others plausible, and still others highly unlikely. 

Many people place as much faith in their dreams as they would in things they see while awake, rejoicing or sorrowing based on the hopes or fears they inspire. Others, however, believe none of them, until they find themselves facing the peril the dream had foretold. I approve of neither extreme view, for dreams are neither always true nor always false. That they are not all true, each of us has had occasion to know; and that they are not all false has already been shown in Filomena's story, and I also intend to demonstrate it in mine. 

Therefore, I believe that in matters of virtuous living and conduct, one should have no fear of any contrary dream nor abandon good intentions on account of them. But as for perverse and wicked things, no matter how favorable the dreams may appear or how much they may encourage the dreamer with propitious omens, none of them should be credited, while full faith should be accorded to all that tend to the contrary.

But to the story. In the city of Brescia, there was a gentleman named Messer Negro da Ponte Carraro, who among his various children had a young, unmarried daughter named Andrevuola, who was very beautiful. It happened that she fell in love with a neighbor of hers, a man named Gabriotto, who was of humble status but possessed commendable manners and a pleasing, handsome appearance. With the aid of a maid in their house, Andrevuola arranged for Gabriotto not only to know of her love for him, but also to be frequently brought to the delight of them both in a fine garden belonging to her father.

In order that nothing other than death might ever part their delightful love, they secretly became husband and wife, continuing their clandestine meetings. It happened that one night, the young lady dreamed she was in her garden with Gabriotto, holding him in her arms with great pleasure. But as they remained thus, she seemed to see something dark and frightening emerge from his body, the form of which she could not discern. This thing tore Gabriotto violently from her embrace and carried him away underground, and she never saw either of them again.

This dream filled her with inexpressible grief, and though she was relieved to find upon waking that it had not been real, fear nonetheless entered her heart because of what she had seen. When Gabriotto shortly desired to visit her that night, she did her utmost to prevent his coming. However, seeing his eagerness and wishing not to make him suspicious, she received him in the garden. There, she had gathered a great abundance of white and red roses, for it was the season, and they went to sit at the foot of a beautiful, clear fountain. After they had taken great and long delight together, Gabriotto asked her why she had tried to keep him from coming that night. She then recounted to him the dream she had had the previous night and the fear it had instilled in her.

Hearing this, Gabriotto laughed it off, saying it was folly to put any faith in dreams, for they arose from an excess or lack of food and were daily proven to be entirely vain. He added, "Were I inclined to heed dreams, I would not have come here, not so much on account of yours as of one I myself dreamed last night." He then described a dream he had had about hunting a beautiful, familiar hind in a fair and delightful wood, only to have a fierce black greyhound come and tear out his heart. But, he concluded, "After all, what does this dream mean? I have dreamed many such, and far more frightening, yet nothing in the world has ever befallen me because of them. So let us put it aside and think only of enjoying ourselves."

The young lady, already greatly afraid because of her own dream, became even more so upon hearing his. Yet she hid her fear as best she could, not wishing to cause Gabriotto any distress. Nevertheless, as she took delight with him, embracing and kissing him repeatedly while he did the same, she often gazed intently at his face, fearing she knew not what, and looked around the garden, lest she see anything black approaching.

Presently, as they remained thus, Gabriotto heaved a great sigh and, embracing Andrevuola, said, "Alas, my love, help me, for I am dying!" And with that, he fell to the ground upon the lawn. The young lady, seeing this, drew him into her lap and asked, almost weeping, "What ails you, my dear one?" But he gave no answer, only panting heavily and sweating all over, and shortly after departed this life.

Andrevuola's grief and anguish at losing the man she loved more than her own life can scarcely be imagined. She wept bitterly and called out to him in vain. But after touching his body in every part and finding him cold throughout, realizing that he was completely dead, and knowing not what to do or say, she went, all in tears and full of distress, to summon her maid, who was privy to their love, and shared her misery and grief with her. Then, after they had lamented a while over Gabriotto's lifeless face, the young lady said to the maid, "Since God has taken from me the one I love, I purpose to remain no longer in this life. But before I go about to take my own life, I would ensure that my honor and the secret of the love between us is preserved, and that the body, from which the gracious spirit has departed, is properly buried."

"My daughter," the maid answered, "do not speak of seeking to slay yourself, for if you have lost him in this world, by taking your own life you would lose him in the next as well, for you would go to hell, whereas I am sure his soul has not gone there, for he was a virtuous young man. It would be far better to comfort yourself and think of providing aid to his soul through prayers and other good works, if perchance he has need thereof for any sin committed. The means of burying him are here in this garden, and none will ever know of the matter, for none knows that he ever came hither. Or, if you will not have it so, let us put him out of the garden and leave him be; he will be found in the morning and carried to his home, where his family will see to his burial."

The young lady, though filled with bitter sorrow and weeping unceasingly, listened to her maid's counsel. Declining the first suggestion, she replied to the second, saying, "God forbid that I should allow so dear a youth, beloved by me and my husband, to be buried like a dog or left lying in the street! He has had my tears, and as much as I am able, he shall have those of his family as well. I have already decided what we must do in this matter."

With that, she sent her maid to fetch a piece of fine silk cloth she had in a chest, and spreading it on the ground, laid Gabriotto's body upon it, with his head on a pillow. Then, with many tears, she closed his eyes and mouth, and weaving a garland of roses, covered him entirely, he and she together. Afterwards, she said to the maid, "His house is not far away; therefore, you and I will carry him there, arrayed as he is, and lay him before the door. It will not be long before morning, and he will be taken up; and although this may be no consolation to his family, it will be a comfort to me, in whose arms he died." Saying this, she once more cast herself upon his face in abundant tears. Then, pressed by her maid to hurry, for day was dawning, she rose to her feet and, removing the ring with which Gabriotto had espoused her, placed it on his finger, saying through her weeping, "Dear lord, if your soul now sees my tears, or if any sense or awareness remains in the body after its departure, graciously receive this last gift from her whom, living, you loved so well." With that, she collapsed in a faint upon him, but soon revived and, taking up the cloth with the maid, carried Gabriotto's body from the garden toward his house.

As they went, they were discovered and apprehended, along with the dead body, by the officers of the provostry, who happened to be abroad at that hour on some other matter. Andrevuola, more desirous of death than of life, recognizing the officers, said frankly, "I know who you are, and it would avail me nothing to try to flee; I am ready to go with you before the Seignory and declare how the matter stands. But let none of you dare to touch me, provided I am obedient to you, or to remove anything from this body, if he would not be accused by me." Accordingly, without being touched by any, she proceeded with Gabriotto's body to the palace, where the Provost, upon hearing what had occurred, summoned her into his chamber and questioned her about the event. He had various physicians examine the body, who all declared that the young man had not been poisoned or otherwise killed, but rather had died from the bursting of some internal abscess near his heart, which had suffocated him.

The magistrate, hearing this and finding her guilty of only a small matter, sought to take advantage of the situation, telling her that if she would consent to his desires, he would release her. But these words availed him nothing, and when he attempted to use force, the virtuous and indignant Andrevuola defended herself vigorously, rebuking him with proud and scornful words.

Meanwhile, as day broke and these events were reported to Messer Negro, he went sorrowfully to the palace, accompanied by many of his friends, and learning the whole matter from the Provost, demanded angrily that his daughter be returned to him. The Provost, preferring to accuse himself of the violence he had attempted rather than be accused by her, first praised the young lady and her steadfastness, then recounted what he had done, acknowledging that, seeing her of such excellent resolve, he had conceived an extraordinary love for her and would gladly, if it were agreeable to her father and to herself, take her as his wife, despite her having had a husband of humble status.

While they still conversed, Andrevuola presented herself and, weeping, cast herself at her father's feet, saying, "Father, I think there is no need for me to recount to you the story of my boldness and misfortune, for I am sure you have heard and know it. Wherefore, as best I may, I humbly ask your pardon for my fault, that is, for having taken as my husband, without your knowledge, one who was most pleasing to me. And this boon I ask of you, not that my life may be spared, but to die as your daughter and not your enemy." With these words, she fell weeping at his feet.

Messer Negro, an old and kindly man of an affectionate nature, hearing these words, began to weep, and with tears in his eyes, tenderly raised his daughter to her feet, saying, "My daughter, it would have pleased me more for you to have had such a husband as, in my opinion, befitted you. And that you should have taken one who was pleasing to you would also have been pleasing to me; but that you should have concealed him, out of your little confidence in me, grieves me, and all the more so as I see you have lost him before I knew of it. However, since the case is so, that which I would gladly have done for him, had he lived, to honor him as my son-in-law, be it done for him now that he is dead." Then, turning to his sons and kinsmen, he commanded that great and honorable funeral rites be prepared for Gabriotto.

Meanwhile, Gabriotto's kinsmen and the women of the city had flocked there, and the body, laid out in the courtyard on Andrevuola's silk cloth and strewn with all her roses, was not only mourned by her and his own family, but publicly lamented by nearly all the ladies of the city and many men. It was then borne to the sepulcher with the utmost honor, carried on the shoulders of the most noble citizens, not as that of a commoner, but as that of a nobleman.

Some days later, when the Provost pursued the matter he had demanded, Messer Negro proposed it to his daughter, who would hear nothing of it. Her father, being willing to comply with her in this, she and her maid made themselves nuns in a convent renowned for its sanctity, where they lived honorably for a great while thereafter.
