"Dearest ladies, in the days when the good King William ruled over Sicily, there was a gentleman on that island named Messer Amerigo Abate of Trapani. Among his many worldly goods, he had many children. When he needed servants, some Genoese corsair galleys arrived from the Levant, having captured many boys during raids along the coast of Armenia. Messer Amerigo bought some of these boys, thinking they were Turks. One of them, named Teodoro, seemed nobler and better-mannered than the rest, who seemed more like shepherds. 

Though treated as a slave, Teodoro was raised in Messer Amerigo's household alongside his children. Teodoro conformed more to his own innate nature than to the circumstances of his fortune, and so proved himself so accomplished and well-bred that Messer Amerigo freed him, still believing him to be a Turk. He then had Teodoro baptized and renamed Pietro, and made him the chief overseer of all his affairs, placing great trust in him.

As Messer Amerigo's children grew up, so did his daughter Violante, a fair and graceful young woman. Her father delayed too long in finding her a husband, and by chance, she fell in love with Pietro. She held his manners and bearing in high esteem, but was ashamed to reveal her feelings to him. However, Love spared her that trouble, for Pietro, having glimpsed her here and there, had become so passionately in love with her that he never found rest except when he saw her, though he greatly feared anyone might discover this, thinking it improper.

But fortune, as if it had deliberately decided that this should be, provided them with an opportunity to overcome their timidity.

Messer Amerigo had a very fine country estate about a mile from Trapani, to which his wife and daughter and other ladies would often go for recreation. One day of great heat, they went there, taking Pietro with them. While they were there, the sky suddenly became overcast with dark clouds, and the lady decided to return to Trapani before the bad weather arrived. She and her company hurried off, but Pietro and Violante, being young, outpaced them by a great distance, urged on not just by the weather, but by their love. After much thunder, a heavy, thick hail shower began to fall, and the lady and her group fled into a farmer's house for shelter.

Pietro and the young lady, having no readier refuge, took shelter in a small, dilapidated, abandoned hut. Pressed close together under the small patch of roof that remained intact, the intimacy of their situation emboldened their minds to reveal the amorous desires that consumed them both. Pietro said first, 'Would that this hail would never cease, so that I might remain as I am!' 'Indeed,' the girl answered, 'that would please me as well.' From these words, they progressed to holding hands, embracing, and kissing, all while the hail continued to fall. In short, before the storm had passed, they had experienced the full delights of love and made arrangements to continue their secret pleasure.

When the storm ended, they rejoined the lady at the city gate and returned home with her. Thereafter, with great discretion and secrecy, they met again and again in the same place, to their great mutual satisfaction. Their endeavors were so successful that the young lady became pregnant, which was most unwelcome to them both. She attempted many unnatural means to rid herself of the burden, but could not succeed. Pietro, fearing for his life, considered fleeing, but the lady threatened to kill herself if he left. So he remained, and she promised that her pregnancy would be discovered, but that his involvement would never be known if he kept silent about it.

After a while, the young lady, who had concealed her condition as much as possible, could no longer hide her growing belly. She confessed her state to her mother, begging her to save her. The lady was deeply distressed and scolded her harshly, demanding to know how this had happened. To protect Pietro, Violante told a fabricated story, and her mother, believing it, sent her away to one of their country estates to give birth in private.

There, when the time of her delivery came and the girl cried out as women do, her father, Messer Amerigo, unexpectedly returned from hawking and, hearing the commotion, entered the room. Seeing his daughter's condition, he flew into a rage, threatening to kill her unless she revealed her lover's identity. Fearing death, Violante broke her promise to Pietro and disclosed all that had passed between them. Furious, Messer Amerigo left to report the offense Pietro had done him to Messer Currado, the king's captain in Trapani.

Messer Currado promptly had the unsuspecting Pietro seized and tortured, whereupon he confessed everything. A few days later, Pietro was sentenced by the captain to be whipped through the city and then hanged. Messer Amerigo, whose wrath was not appeased by Pietro's impending execution, decided to rid the earth of the two lovers and their child all at once. He had a servant take a cup of poisoned wine and a naked dagger to Violante, instructing him to demand that she choose between those two deaths, or else be burned alive. The servant was then to take the newborn child and dash its head against the wall, throwing it to the dogs to eat.

Meanwhile, as Pietro was being led to the gallows, scourged by the officers, he passed by a hostelry where three Armenian noblemen, sent as ambassadors to the Pope, were resting. One of them, an elderly man named Fineo, noticed a large, reddish birthmark on Pietro's chest, which suddenly reminded him of his own son, stolen by corsairs fifteen years earlier. Calling out 'Teodoro!' in the Armenian language, Fineo discovered that this was indeed his long-lost son. Embracing him among the officers, Fineo pleaded with the captain to delay the execution until it could be determined whether Violante would accept Teodoro as her husband. 

The captain, realizing that the condemned man was Fineo's son, felt ashamed of the unjust fortune and immediately sent for Messer Amerigo. When Messer Amerigo learned that his daughter and grandson were still alive, he was greatly relieved, for he had believed them dead. A messenger was sent to Violante to stop the intended killings. The two fathers then met and agreed that Teodoro and Violante should be married, to the great joy of all. After the magnificent wedding, Fineo took his son, daughter-in-law, and grandson back with him to Lazistan, where the two lovers lived in peace and happiness for the rest of their days."
