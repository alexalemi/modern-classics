THE FOURTH STORY

[Day the Eighth]

THE RECTOR OF FIESOLE LOVES A WIDOW LADY, BUT SHE DOES NOT LOVE HIM BACK. THINKING HE WILL SLEEP WITH HER, HE INSTEAD SLEEPS WITH HER SERVING-MAID, WHILE HER BROTHERS HAVE THE BISHOP FIND HIM IN THIS SITUATION.

Elisa had finished her story, which had greatly entertained the entire company. The queen then turned to Emilia and signaled for her to continue with her own story. Emilia promptly began:

"Noble ladies, I have not forgotten how many times it has been shown in the previous stories how much we women are subjected to the unwanted advances of priests, friars, and clergy of all kinds. But since there is still more that can be said on this matter, I intend to tell you a story about a rector who, despite all opposition, was determined to have the affections of a gentlewoman, whether she wanted him or not. She, being a very wise woman, dealt with him as he deserved.

As you all know, the hill of Fiesole, which we can see from here, was once a great and ancient city. Although it is now in ruins, it remains the seat of a bishop. Near the cathedral church, there lived a noble widow named Madam Piccarda, who, not being very wealthy, spent most of the year in a modest house of hers, along with her two very courteous and worthy brothers.

It happened that the lady, frequenting the cathedral church, was still very young, beautiful, and charming. The rector of the church became so enamored of her that he could think of nothing else. After a while, he boldly confessed his feelings to her and begged her to accept his love and love him in return. 

Now, the rector was already old in years, but very young in wit, presumptuous and arrogant to the extreme, with manners and ways full of conceit and clumsiness. He was so ill-tempered and ill-mannered that no one wished him well. The lady in particular hated him more than anything. Being a wise woman, she replied to him, 'Sir, that you love me should please me greatly, for I am bound to love you and will gladly do so. But between your love and mine, nothing improper should ever happen. You are my spiritual father and a priest, and you are now well advanced in years - all of which should make you both modest and chaste. As for me, I am no longer a young girl, nor do these amorous games suit my current state as a widow. I therefore beg you to excuse me, for I shall never love you in the way you desire, nor do I wish to be loved by you in that manner.'

The rector could not get any other answer from her at that time, but, undaunted and undiscouraged by the initial rejection, he persisted in soliciting her again and again with the most presumptuous importunity, both by letter and by message, and even in person when he saw her come to the church. Feeling that this was too great and grievous an annoyance, she decided to rid herself of him in the manner he deserved, but only after consulting with her brothers. She informed them of the rector's behavior towards her and her intended plan, and, having their full approval, went to the church a few days later, as was her custom. 

As soon as the rector saw her, he approached her with his usual arrogance. The lady received him with a cheerful countenance and, withdrawing apart with him, after he had said many words to her in his usual style, she heaved a great sigh and said, 'Sir, I have heard that there is no fortress so strong that, being assailed daily, it does not eventually fall. And this I can clearly see has happened to me; for you have so persistently besieged me with soft words and one act of kindness after another, that you have made me break my resolve, and I am now disposed, since I please you so, to consent to be yours.'

'Thank you, my lady,' the rector answered, overjoyed. 'To tell you the truth, I have often wondered how you could hold out so long, considering that this has never happened to me with any other woman. Indeed, I have sometimes said, "If women were made of silver, they would not be worth a farthing, for not one of them would withstand the hammer." But let that pass for now. When and where can we be together?'

The lady replied, 'My sweet lord, as for the when, it may be at any time that pleases us, for I have no husband to whom I must account for my nights. But as for the where, I do not know how to arrange it.' 

'How?' exclaimed the priest. 'Why, in your house, of course.'

'Sir,' the lady answered, 'you know I have two young brothers, who come and go about the house with their companions day and night, and my house is not very large. Therefore, it cannot be there, unless one chose to remain silent and in the dark, like a mute, without making the least sound. If you are content to do this, it might be possible, for they do not meddle with my bedchamber, but their own is so close to mine that one cannot whisper the least word without it being heard.'

'Madam,' the rector replied, 'this shall not hinder us for a night or two, until I think of where we can meet more at ease.'

The lady said, 'Sir, leave that to you. But I pray you, let this remain a secret, so that no word of it is ever known.'

'Madam,' he replied, 'have no fear of that. But, if possible, arrange for us to meet this evening.' 

'With all my heart,' said the lady, and, making an appointment with him for how and when he should come, she took her leave of him and returned home.

Now, the lady had a serving-maid, not at all young, but with the ugliest and most ill-favored face ever seen. She had a flattened nose, a crooked mouth with thick, ill-set lips, and she tended to squint, never being without sore eyes. Her complexion was green and yellow, giving her the appearance of having spent the summer not in Fiesole, but in Sinigaglia. Furthermore, she was hunchbacked and a bit crooked on the right side. Her name was Ciuta, but because of her dog-like appearance, she was called by everyone 'Ciutazza'. Despite her misshapen person, she had a touch of roguishness about her.

The lady called her and said, 'Listen, Ciutazza, if you will do me a service tonight, I will give you a fine new shift.' Hearing of the shift, Ciutazza answered, 'Madam, so long as you give me a shift, I will throw myself into the fire, let alone anything else.'

'Well, then,' said her mistress, 'I would have you lie tonight with a man in my bed and shower him with caresses, but take care not to say a word, lest you be heard by my brothers, who, as you know, sleep in the next room. And after, I will give you the shift.'

Ciutazza replied, 'With all my heart. I will lie with half a dozen men, if need be, let alone just one.'

Accordingly, when night fell, the rector made his appearance, as agreed, while the two young men, per the lady's arrangement, were in their bedchamber, taking care to make themselves heard. The rector entered the lady's chamber in silence and darkness and went straight to the bed, where Ciutazza, who had been well instructed by the lady on what to do, came to him. The rector, thinking he had his mistress beside him, caught Ciutazza in his arms and began kissing her without a word, and she him. He then proceeded to enjoy himself with her, believing he had finally attained the long-desired prize.

The lady, having orchestrated this, instructed her brothers to carry out the rest of the plan. Softly leaving the chamber, they went to the great square, and fortune was more favorable to them than they had hoped, for, due to the heat, the bishop had inquired about the two young gentlemen, wishing to come to their house and drink with them. Seeing the brothers, he told them of his wish and returned with them to their home, where, entering a cool little courtyard with many lit torches, he drank with great pleasure of their excellent wine.

When he had finished drinking, the young men said to him, 'My lord, since you have done us the great favor of deigning to visit this our poor house, to which we had come to invite you, we would have you be pleased to witness a small matter with which we would like to show you.' The bishop answered that he would be pleased to do so. One of the young men, taking a lighted torch in hand, then led the way to the chamber where the rector lay with Ciutazza, followed by the bishop and all the others.

The rector, eager to reach his goal, had hurried to leave and had already ridden more than three miles before they arrived. Nonetheless, being somewhat weary, he had fallen asleep with Ciutazza in his arms, despite the heat. When the young man entered the chamber, torch in hand, and the bishop and the others followed, the rector was shown to the prelate in this plight. He awoke and, seeing the light and the people around him, was greatly ashamed and hid his head under the bedclothes out of fear.

The bishop scolded him soundly and made him put out his head and see with whom he had been lying. The rector, understanding the trick that had been played on him by the lady, became the most wretched man, both because of this and the disgrace he felt he had suffered. Then, having been ordered by the bishop to redress himself, he was sent to his house under guard to do penance for the sin he had committed.

The bishop, inquiring how it came about that he had gone there to lie with Ciutazza, the young men recounted everything to him in order. Having heard this, he greatly commended both the lady and her brothers for having treated the rector as he deserved, without choosing to stain their hands with a priest's blood. As for the rector, the bishop made him lament his offense for forty days, but love and shame caused him to rue it for more than forty-nine, especially since, for a long time afterward, he could never go out without the children pointing at him and saying, "There is the one who lay with Ciutazza," which was such a great annoyance to him that he was nearly driven mad by it.

In this way, the worthy lady rid herself of the importunity of the presumptuous rector, and Ciutazza gained both the shift and a merry night.
