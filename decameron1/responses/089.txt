Now it happened that, after some time, fortune provided him with an opportunity to satisfy his desire for revenge. The young man the widow loved had, without any regard for the love she felt for him, fallen in love with another woman and wanted nothing to do with her, causing her to pine away in tears and bitterness. But her maid, who felt great compassion for her mistress and saw no way to rouse her from the distress caused by losing her lover, had a clever idea. Seeing the scholar passing by on the street as usual, she conceived the notion that the lady's lover could be made to love her again through some form of necromancy, and that the scholar must be a master of such dark arts. She shared this thought with her mistress.

The lady, not very wise, without considering that if the scholar knew necromancy, he would have used it for himself, lent her mind to her maid's words and instructed her to learn from him if he would do it, promising to do whatever he wished in return. The maid carried out her errand well and diligently. When the scholar heard this, he was overjoyed and said to himself, "Praise be to you, my God! The time has come when, with your help, I can make that wicked woman pay the penalty for the harm she did me in return for the great love I bore her."

He then told the maid, "Tell my lady that she need not worry, for even if her lover were in the Indies, I would quickly cause him to come to her and beg her pardon for what he has done to displease her. But the means she must take to this end, I will impart to her directly, when and where it pleases her most." The maid relayed his answer to her mistress, and it was agreed that they would meet at Santa Lucia del Prato.

When the lady and the scholar met there in private, she, remembering not that she had once brought him nearly to his death, openly told him her situation and what she desired, begging him to help her. "Madam," he answered, "it is true that among the other things I learned in Paris was necromancy, which I do know. But because it is supremely displeasing to God, I had sworn never to practice it, either for myself or for others. Nevertheless, the love I bear you is so powerful that I know not how I can deny you anything you would have me do. So, though it would require me to go to the devil's abode, I am yet ready to do it, since it is your wish. But I must warn you that the task is more difficult than you perhaps imagine, especially when a woman wishes to recall a man to love her or a man a woman, for this can only be done by the very person to whom it pertains. And it must be done at night, in solitary places, without company—things I know not how you are disposed to do."

The lady, more enamored than wise, replied, "Love spurs me on so that there is nothing I would not do to have back the one who has wrongfully forsaken me. But if it pleases you, show me what I must do to prove myself of assured mind."

"Madam," the scholar replied, who harbored ill will, "I must make a pewter image in the name of the one you wish to recall, which, when I send to you, you must bathe with seven times, naked, in a running stream, at the hour of the first sleep, when the moon is waning. Then, still naked, you must climb a tree or the top of some uninhabited house and, facing north with the image in your hand, repeat certain words I will give you seven times. After that, two of the fairest maidens you have ever seen will come and courteously ask what you desire done. Tell them your wishes precisely, and they will depart. You may then come down, dress, and return home. By midnight of that same night, your lover will come weeping to beg your pardon and mercy, and from that time on, he will never leave you for another."

The lady, hearing all this and fully believing it, was half comforted, imagining she already had her lover back in her arms, and said, "Never fear, I will do these things most willingly. I have the perfect place—a farm I have up the Arno Valley, very close to the riverbank. And it is July now, so bathing will be pleasant. Moreover, I remember there is a little uninhabited tower nearby, where shepherds sometimes climb up a chestnut ladder to look for their stray animals. It is a very solitary, out-of-the-way spot. I will go there and do what you instruct me, the best in the world."

The scholar, who knew both the place and the tower the lady mentioned, was pleased to hear of her plan and said, "Madam, I have never been in these parts, so I do not know the farm or the tower. But if it is as you say, nothing in the world could be better. When the time comes, I will send you the image and the conjuration. And I pray you, as soon as you have obtained your desire and know that I have served you well, be mindful of me and remember to keep your promise."

She answered that she would without fail, then took her leave and returned home. The scholar, rejoicing that it seemed his desire was about to be fulfilled, made an image with certain talismanic characters of his own devising, wrote a spell in his own fashion, and when the time seemed right, sent them to the lady, telling her she must do that very night what he had instructed her. He then secretly went with a servant to the house of one of his friends, who lived very near the tower, so he could carry out his plan.

The lady set out with her maid and went to her farm. As soon as night fell, she pretended to go to bed and sent the maid away to sleep. But towards the first hour of the night, she quietly left the house and went to the bank of the Arno near the tower. After looking all around and seeing and hearing no one, she undressed, hid her clothes under a bush, and bathed seven times with the image. Then, still naked, she made her way to the tower, image in hand.

The scholar, who had hidden himself and his servant among the willows and other trees near the tower and had witnessed everything, saw her pass by in the darkness, her whiteness overcoming the night. As he considered her breasts and other fair parts, he felt compassion for her, while at the same time, the pricks of the flesh assailed him suddenly, tempting him to leave his hiding place and take her. Between the two, he was nearly overcome, but, recalling who he was and the injury he had suffered and at whose hands, and thus rekindled in resentment and compassion, he banished his carnal desire and let her go.

The lady, going up onto the tower and turning to the north, began to repeat the words the scholar had given her. He then quietly entered the tower and, little by little, removed the ladder that led to the sollar where she was, then waited to see what she would do and say. Meanwhile, the lady, having repeated her conjuration seven times, looked for the two maidens, but as she waited (and felt it colder than she would have liked), she saw the dawn appear. Distressed that it had not happened as the scholar had told her, she said to herself, "I fear yonder man meant to give me a night such as the one I gave him. But if that was his intent, he has not known well how to avenge himself, for this night has not been by a third as long as his, let alone that the cold was of a different sort."

Wanting to avoid being caught there by daylight, she tried to go down from the tower but found the ladder gone. Seized with fear, as if the world had crumbled beneath her feet, she fell swooning onto the tower platform. When her senses returned, she began to weep bitterly and lament, realizing all too well that this must have been the scholar's doing. She blamed herself for having offended others and for having trusted too much in one she had good reason to believe her enemy. She remained there a great while, lamenting and falling into bitter thoughts, saying to herself, "Alas, unhappy woman! What will be said of my brothers, kinsmen, and neighbors, and generally of all the people of Florence, when it is known that I have been found here naked? My reputation, hitherto so great, will be revealed as false. And if I try to make up excuses (if indeed there are any to be found), the accursed scholar, who knows all my affairs, will not let me lie."

She was so overcome with anguish that she was about to cast herself down from the tower to the ground. But as the sun rose and she drew near the side of the tower walls to see if any boy with cattle might pass, whom she could send for her maid, it chanced that the scholar, who had slept a while at the foot of a bush, awoke and saw her. He called out, "Good day, madam. Have the maidens come yet?"

Seeing and hearing him, the lady began to weep anew and begged him to come within the tower so she could speak with him. He courteously complied, and she, lying prone on the platform and showing only her head at the opening, said through her tears, "Assuredly, Rinieri, if I gave you an ill night, you have well avenged yourself on me, for, though it is July, I have thought I would freeze this night, naked as I am, all the more since I have wept so bitterly over the trick I played on you and my own foolishness in believing you. Therefore, I entreat you, not for love of me, whom you have no call to love, but for love of yourself, who are a gentleman, that you be content with the vengeance you have already taken and let me come down, nor seek to take from me that which you could not, if you would, restore to me, which is my honor. If I took from you the pleasure of being with me that night, I can give you many nights in its stead, whenever it pleases you. Let this suffice, and let it content you, as an honorable man, to have been able to avenge yourself and to have made me confess it. Seek not to use your strength against a woman; no glory is it for an eagle to have overcome a dove. For the love of God and your own honor, have pity on me."

The scholar, reflecting sternly on the injury he had suffered and seeing her weep and plead, felt both pleasure and distress: pleasure in the revenge he had desired more than anything, and distress that his humanity moved him to compassion for the unhappy woman. However, his humanity did not overcome the fierceness of his desire for vengeance. "Madam Elena," he answered, "if my prayers (which, it is true, I knew not how to bathe with tears nor make honeyed, as you now know how to offer yours) had availed, that night when I was dying of cold in your snow-filled courtyard, to obtain from you even a little shelter, it would be a light matter to me to heed yours now. But since you are now so much more concerned for your honor than you were then, and it is grievous to you to remain there naked, address these prayers of yours to him in whose arms you did not scruple, that night which you recall, to lie naked, while you heard me going about your courtyard, chattering with my teeth and trampling the snow. Get succor from him; let him fetch your clothes and set the ladder by which you may descend, and study to inspire him with tenderness for your honor, which you have not hesitated both now and a thousand other times to imperil for him. Why do you not call him to come help you? To whom does it pertain more than to him? You are his; and what should he regard or succor, if he regard not nor succor you? Call him, silly woman that you are, and prove if the love you bear him and your wit and his together can avail to deliver you from my folly, whereof, dallying with him the while, you questioned aforetime whether seemed the greater, his wit or the love you bore him."

The scholar continued on in this vein, mocking the lady and refusing to help her. When he had finished speaking, the wretched woman wept without ceasing, as the sun rose higher and higher. But when she saw that he was silent, she said, "Alas, cruel man, if the accursed night was so grievous to you and if my fault seems to you so heinous a thing that neither my youthful beauty nor my bitter tears and humble prayers may move you to any pity, at least let this one act of mine alone move you a little and abate the rigor of your resentment—that I just now trusted in you and disclosed to you every secret, opening withal to your desire a way whereby you might avail to make me cognizant of my sin. For God's sake, leave your anger and pardon me henceforth; I am ready, if you will but forgive me and bring me down, altogether to renounce yonder faithless youth and to have you alone as lover and lord, albeit you decriest my beauty, avouching it short-lived and little worth. And albeit I am cruelly entreated by you, I cannot believe that you would wish to see me die so unseemly a death as the casting myself down from this tower, as in desperation, before your eyes, wherein, an you were not a liar as you are become, I was erst so pleasing. Alas, have pity on me for God's sake and mercy! The sun begins to grow hot, and just as the excessive cold irked me this night, so does the heat now begin to do me sore annoy."
