THE SIXTH STORY

[Day the Seventh]

LADY ISABELLA, SPENDING TIME WITH HER LOVER LEONETTO, IS VISITED BY MESSER LAMBERTUCCIO, WHO ALSO DESIRES HER. WHEN HER HUSBAND UNEXPECTEDLY RETURNS, SHE SENDS LAMBERTUCCIO AWAY WITH A DRAWN SWORD, AND THEN HER HUSBAND ESCORTS LEONETTO HOME.

The group was greatly pleased with Fiammetta's story, all agreeing that the lady had acted admirably in dealing with such a boorish man. After the tale was finished, the king instructed Pampinea to continue, and she began, "There are many who, speaking without understanding, claim that love robs people of their senses and causes lovers to become foolish. I believe this is a foolish notion, as has been well demonstrated by the stories already told, and I intend to show it again.

In our city, which abounds in all good things, there was once a young lady of noble birth and great beauty, who was married to a highly respected and prominent gentleman. As often happens that people cannot forever enjoy the same fare, but desire at times to vary their diet, this lady, not wholly satisfied by her husband, fell in love with a young man named Leonetto, who was well-bred and agreeable, though of no great standing. He, likewise, became enamored of her, and as you know that what both parties desire rarely goes unfulfilled, it was not long before their love was consummated.

Now, it happened that this lady, being fair and engaging, caught the ardent affection of a gentleman called Messer Lambertuccio, whom, finding him disagreeable and tiresome, she could not bring herself to love in return. However, after he pursued her relentlessly with messages, and to no avail, he threatened to dishonor her, given his prominence, if she did not yield to his wishes. Fearing him, she thus submitted to his demands.

It chanced one day that the lady, whose name was Madam Isabella, had gone, as is our custom in summer, to reside at a splendid estate she owned in the countryside, and her husband had ridden away for a few days. She summoned Leonetto to join her there, and he was overjoyed to do so, hastening to the estate at once. Meanwhile, Messer Lambertuccio, hearing that her husband was abroad, took his horse and, arriving alone at her house, knocked at the door. The lady's maid, seeing him, went straight to her mistress, who was with Leonetto, and said, 'Madam, Messer Lambertuccio is downstairs, all alone.' Hearing this, the lady was the most troubled woman in the world, but, fearing Messer Lambertuccio greatly, she begged Leonetto to hide himself behind the curtains of her bed until the other had departed. Leonetto, no less afraid of him than the lady, did as she asked, and she then instructed the maid to admit Messer Lambertuccio.

Once he had dismounted in the courtyard and secured his horse, Messer Lambertuccio came up into the house. The lady greeted him with an amiable countenance and, going to the top of the stairs, welcomed him as best she could, asking what had brought him there. He embraced and kissed her, saying, 'My dear, I understood your husband was away and have come to spend time with you.' They then entered a bedchamber and locked themselves inside, where Messer Lambertuccio proceeded to enjoy her company.

As they were thus engaged, it happened, quite unexpectedly for the lady, that her husband returned, whom the maid saw approaching the house and quickly informed the lady. When the lady heard this, realizing she had two men in the house and knowing there was no way to conceal Messer Lambertuccio since his horse was in the courtyard, she feared for her life. Yet, taking a swift decision, she hurried down from the bed and said to Messer Lambertuccio, 'Sir, if you wish me well and would save me from harm, do as I instruct. Take your sword in hand and go down the stairs looking angry and disheveled, saying, "I swear by God I will find him elsewhere." And if my husband tries to detain you or question you, say nothing more than that, then mount your horse and do not linger with him for any reason.'

The gentleman agreed to do as she said, and drawing his sword, he acted as the lady had directed, with a face flushed from his recent activity and anger at the husband's return. The husband had by this time dismounted in the courtyard and was surprised to see the horse there; then, as he was about to go up into the house, he saw Messer Lambertuccio come down, and, wondering at both his words and his manner, said, 'What is this, sir?' Messer Lambertuccio, putting his foot in the stirrup and mounting his horse, said nothing but, 'By God, I shall find him again elsewhere,' and rode off.

The husband, going up, found his wife at the top of the stairs, distraught and fearful, and asked her, 'What is all this? Whom is Messer Lambertuccio angrily pursuing?' The lady, drawing closer to the chamber where Leonetto was so he could hear, replied, 'Sir, I have never been so frightened. A young man I do not know came fleeing here, pursued by Messer Lambertuccio with sword in hand, and finding the door of this chamber open by chance, said to me, trembling, "For God's sake, madam, help me, that I not be slain in your arms." I rose to my feet, about to ask who he was and what was wrong, when Messer Lambertuccio rushed in, saying, "Where are you, traitor?" I stood before the chamber door and prevented him from entering, and after many words, seeing it pleased me not that he should go in there, he went down as you have seen.' The husband said, 'Wife, you did well; it would have been a great shame had a man been slain in our house, and Messer Lambertuccio acted most uncivilly to pursue someone who had sought refuge here.'

Then he asked where the young man was, and the lady replied, 'Indeed, sir, I do not know where he has hidden himself.' The husband then said, 'Come out, you are safe.' Whereupon Leonetto, who had heard everything, emerged from his hiding place, still trembling with fear, and the gentleman asked him, 'What have you to do with Messer Lambertuccio?' 'Sir,' Leonetto answered, 'I have nothing in the world to do with him; I believe he is either not in his right mind or has mistaken me for another, for no sooner did he see me on the road not far from this house than he immediately drew his sword and said, "Traitor, you are a dead man!" I did not wait to ask why, but fled as best I could and found my way here, where, thanks to God and this gentlewoman, I have escaped.' The husband said, 'Go on; have no fear; I will escort you safely to your own home, and you can then discover what he has against you.' Accordingly, after they had supped, he mounted Leonetto on a horse and took him back to Florence, leaving him at his own house. As for Leonetto, that same evening, as the lady had instructed him, he privately spoke with Messer Lambertuccio and arranged matters with him, though there was much talk of the incident afterward, the husband never becoming aware of the trick his wife had played on him.
