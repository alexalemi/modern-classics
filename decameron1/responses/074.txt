THE FIFTH STORY

[Day the Seventh]

A JEALOUS HUSBAND, PRETENDING TO BE A PRIEST, CONFESSES HIS WIFE, WHO MAKES HIM BELIEVE SHE LOVES A PRIEST WHO VISITS HER EVERY NIGHT; WHILE THE HUSBAND SECRETLY WATCHES, THE LADY BRINGS IN HER LOVER THROUGH THE ROOF AND SPENDS THE NIGHT WITH HIM

After Lauretta finished her story, and everyone had praised the lady for her actions against her wretched husband, the king turned to Fiammetta and politely asked her to tell the next story. She began, "Noble ladies, the previous tale reminds me of another story about a jealous husband, which I believe was justly handled by his wife. In my opinion, all that wives do to such jealous men, especially when their suspicions are unfounded, should be praised rather than condemned. These jealous men plot against the lives of young women and actively seek their demise. 

Wives are confined to their homes all week, occupied with domestic duties and family matters. On holidays, they simply long for some respite and recreation, just as farmworkers, artisans, and law administrators do - following the example of God Himself, who rested on the seventh day. Yet jealous husbands refuse to grant their wives even this small pleasure, keeping them even more tightly restricted and miserable than on other days. Only those who have experienced it know the true torment and anguish this causes the poor women. Therefore, I say that what a woman does to a husband unjustly suspicious of her should be commended, not condemned.

There was a merchant in Rimini who was extremely wealthy in both lands and money. He had a very beautiful wife, but became excessively jealous of her, though he had no real reason for it. Believing that every man loved her and she sought to please them all as she did him, his suspicion arose from foolishness and lack of understanding. He kept such a strict watch over her and held her in such confinement that many condemned criminals are likely guarded less tightly by their jailers. She was not allowed to go to weddings, entertainments, church, or even step outside the house; she could not even stand at the window or look outside. Her life was utterly wretched, and she bore this mistreatment with increasing impatience, feeling she was unjustly accused.

Seeing herself wrongly suspected by her husband, the lady resolved to find a way to give him reason for his unjust treatment of her, in order to find some solace. Since she could not station herself at the window to show favor to any passersby, she searched the walls between her house and the neighboring one, where a handsome and agreeable young man lived. Finally, she found a crack in the wall, through which she could peer and eventually communicate with him. She arranged to have a secret affair with this young man to help pass the sorrowful time until her husband's jealousy subsided.

As the Christmas holiday approached, the lady told her husband that she wished to go to church on Christmas morning to confess and take communion, as other Christians did. He replied skeptically, asking what sins she could have committed, since he kept her confined. She answered that she was no saint, and that she committed sins like anyone else living in the world, but would not tell them to him since he was not a priest. The jealous man became suspicious at these words and decided he would find a way to hear her confession himself.

On Christmas Day, the lady went to the church her husband had specified, where he had already arranged with the chaplain to take her confession, having disguised himself in the chaplain's robes. When the lady asked to confess, the chaplain said he could not hear her but would send one of his brothers. The jealous husband, now wearing the priest's garments, approached the lady, who recognized him despite his disguise. Deciding to give him what he was seeking, she confessed to the false priest that she was in love with a real priest who came to her every night, describing how he could enter the locked doors of the house through some magic words.

The jealous man was deeply troubled by this, but continued the charade, saying he would pray for her and send a clerk to check on the efficacy of his prayers. After the lady left, the husband hurried home, intent on catching the "priest" with his wife. That night, as the lady had planned, she brought her lover into the house through the roof while her husband waited by the street door, hoping to surprise them. The lover spent the night with the lady, while the jealous man froze and went hungry on his fruitless watch. In the morning, when the husband questioned his wife further, she cleverly revealed that he was the very priest she had confessed to loving, and that his jealousy had made a fool of him. Realizing his mistake, the husband accepted his wife's virtue and discretion, and thereafter no longer troubled her with his unreasonable suspicions.
