The moon had reached its highest point in the sky and lost its glow, and the entire world was bright with the new, emerging light, when the queen arose and summoned her company. They all slowly made their way across the dewy grass, some distance from the beautiful hill, engaging in various conversations and debating the greater or lesser merit of the stories they had told. They continued to laugh at the different adventures recounted until the sun rose high and began to grow hot, at which point they decided it was time to return home. Retracing their steps, they went back to the palace, where, by the queen's command, the tables had already been set and strewn with fragrant herbs and lovely flowers. They proceeded to eat before the heat became too intense. After this joyful meal, they sang several pleasant and delightful songs. Some then went to sleep, while others played chess or other games, and Dioneo and Lauretta sang a duet about Troilus and Cressida. 

When the time came for them to reconvene in the usual manner, they were all summoned by the queen and seated themselves around the fountain. But just as she was about to call on the first storyteller, a great commotion was heard from the kitchen, where the maids and servants were arguing.

The steward, having been called and questioned about the source of the clamor and what might be the cause of the turmoil, replied that the outcry was between Licisca and Tindaro, though he did not know the reason, having only just come to order them to be quiet, as he had been summoned by the queen. She instructed him to immediately bring the two offenders before her, and when they arrived, she inquired about the cause of their shouting. Tindaro attempted to respond, but Licisca, who was older and somewhat domineering, angrily turned to him and said, "Listen to this brute of a man daring to speak before me! Let me speak."

Then, facing the queen again, Licisca said, "Madam, this fellow would presume to teach me, as if I didn't already know Sicofante's wife! He tried to convince me that the first night her husband lay with her, Sir Maul forcibly entered Black Hill, with blood and all, but I say that is not true. He entered peacefully and to the great satisfaction of those within. This fool is simple enough to believe that young women wait around for years, until their fathers and brothers finally decide it's time to marry them off. Well, they would be lucky indeed to wait so long! By the grace of God (and I know what I'm talking about when I swear this), I don't have a single friend who went to her husband a virgin. And as for wives, I know very well the tricks they play on their husbands. But this blockhead thinks he can teach me about women, as if I were born yesterday!"

As Licisca spoke, the ladies could not help but burst out laughing, nearly to the point of losing their teeth. The queen tried to silence her half a dozen times, but to no avail - Licisca continued until she had said her piece. When she was finally done, the queen turned to Dioneo and, laughing, said, "Dioneo, this is a matter for your jurisdiction. When we have finished our stories, you shall provide the final judgment on it."

To which he promptly replied, "Madam, the judgment is already given, without needing to hear more. I say that Licisca is right, and that Tindaro is a fool." Hearing this, Licisca laughed and turned to Tindaro, saying, "I told you so! Go away, and may God be with you. Do you think you know better than I, you whose eyes are not yet dry?" - meaning, he was still a child. "Thank goodness, I haven't lived down here for nothing!" 

Had the queen not sternly silenced her and sent both Licisca and Tindaro away, ordering them not to make any more noise or commotion, lest they be punished, they would have had nothing else to do that day but attend to her.
