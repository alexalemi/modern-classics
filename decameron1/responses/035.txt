The Tenth Story
[Day the Third]

Alibech, Turning Hermit, Is Taught by Rustico, a Monk, to Put the Devil in Hell, and Being After Brought Away Thence, Becomes Neerbale's Wife

Dioneo, who had listened carefully to the queen's story, seeing that it was over and that he was the only one left to tell, began to speak with a smile:

"Lovely ladies, perhaps you have never heard how one puts the devil in hell. Therefore, without straying too far from the topic you've discussed all day, I will tell you about it. You may just gain an understanding of it and come to know that, although Love dwells more happily in joyful palaces and luxurious chambers than in the hovels of the poor, he nonetheless sometimes makes his power felt in the midst of thick forests and rugged mountains and in desert caves. From this, it can be understood that all things are subject to his power.

To get to the facts, I'll say that in the city of Capsa in Barbary, there was once a very wealthy man who, among his other children, had a fair and lovely young daughter named Alibech. She was not a Christian, but hearing many Christians who lived in the town greatly extol the Christian faith and the service of God, one day she questioned one of them about how one might best serve God with the least hindrance. The other replied that those who most strictly avoided worldly things, like those who had taken to the solitudes of the deserts of Thebais, best served God. The girl, who was maybe fourteen years old and very simple, moved by no orderly desire but by some childish fancy, set off the next morning in secret and all alone to go to the desert of Thebais, without letting anyone know her intent. After some days, her desire persisting, she reached the deserts in question with no little toil and, seeing a hut in the distance, went there and found at the door a holy man, who marveled to see her there and asked what she sought. She replied that, being inspired by God, she was going to seek to enter His service and was now looking for someone to teach her how to serve Him.

The worthy man, seeing her young and very fair and fearing that, if he entertained her, the devil might beguile him, commended her pious intent and, giving her something to eat of roots, herbs, wild apples, and dates, and to drink of water, told her, 'My daughter, not far from here is a holy man who is a much better master than I of what you are seeking; go to him.' And he put her on the way. However, when she reached the man in question, she had the same answer from him, and, traveling farther, came to the cell of a young hermit, a very devout and good man named Rustico, to whom she made the same request. He, wishing to test his own constancy, did not send her away, as the others had done, but received her into his cell. When night came, he made her a little bed of palm fronds and told her to lie down to rest on it. 

Temptations did not delay in attacking his powers of resistance, and he, finding himself grossly deceived by these, turned tail, without waiting for many assaults, and admitted defeat. Then, laying aside devout thoughts, prayers, and mortifications, he began to think about the girl's youth and beauty and consider what course he should take with her to win what he desired, without her taking him for a debauched man.

Accordingly, having questioned her, he found that she had never known a man and was indeed as simple as she seemed. So he considered how, under the guise of serving God, he might bring her to his pleasures. First, he showed her with many words how great an enemy the devil was of God the Lord, and then gave her to understand that the most acceptable service that could be rendered to God was to put the devil back into hell, where He had condemned him. The girl asked how this could be done, and he said, 'You will soon learn that; just do as you see me do.' So saying, he proceeded to take off the few garments he had and remained stark naked, as did the girl, whereupon he fell on his knees, as if to pray, and had her kneel facing him.

As they stood thus, Rustico, more inflamed in his desires than ever by seeing her so beautiful, experienced the resurrection of the flesh. Alibech noticed this and, marveling, said, 'Rustico, what is that I see on you that sticks out so, and which I do not have?' 'My daughter,' said Rustico, 'this is the devil I told you about, and you see, he is giving me great trouble, so much that I can barely stand it.' Then the young woman said, 'Praised be God, for I see that I am better off than you, since I do not have that devil.' Rustico said, 'You are right, but you have something else that I do not have, and you have it instead of this.' 'What is that?' asked Alibech, and he said, 'You have hell, and I tell you that I believe God has sent you here for the health of my soul, for when this devil troubles me, if you will have pity on me and allow me to put him back into hell, you will give me the greatest consolation and do God a very great pleasure and service, if indeed you have come to these parts to do as you say.'

The girl answered in good faith, 'Well then, my father, since I have hell, let us do it whenever you please.' Rustico said, 'Blessed daughter, let us go then and put him back there, so that he may afterward leave me in peace.' So saying, he led the girl to one of their little beds and taught her how she should act to imprison that accursed one of God. The girl, who had never before put any devil in hell, felt a little pain the first time; wherefore she said to Rustico, 'Truly, my father, this devil must be a wicked thing and truly an enemy of God, for it pains even hell itself when he is put back into it.' Rustico said, 'Daughter, it will not always happen thus.' And to ensure that this did not happen, six times, before they got up from the bed, they put him back in hell, so much that for that time they took the conceit out of his head, and he willingly remained at peace.

But as the devil returned to him again and again in the following days, and the obedient girl continued to lend herself to take him out, it happened that the game began to please her, and she said to Rustico, 'I see now that those good people in Capsa spoke truly, that serving God is such a sweet thing, and truly I do not remember ever doing anything that gave me as much delight and pleasure as putting the devil in hell; and therefore I judge any other person who attends to anything other than serving God to be a beast.' So she often went to Rustico and said, 'My father, I have come here to serve God, and not to be idle; let us go put the devil in hell.' And as they did this, she sometimes said, 'Rustico, I don't know why the devil flees from hell, for if he stayed there as willingly as hell receives and holds him, he would never come out.'

Thus the girl often invited Rustico and encouraged him in God's service, so much that she took the stuffing out of his doublet, so that he sometimes felt cold when another would have sweated. Therefore, he began to tell the girl that the devil was not to be chastised or put into hell, except when he raised his head in pride, 'and we,' he added, 'by God's grace, have so defeated him that he prays our Lord to let him be at peace.' And in this way, he imposed silence on her for a while. But when she saw that he no longer asked her to put the devil in hell, she said to him one day, 'Rustico, if your devil is chastised and no longer troubles you, my hell does not let me be; therefore, you would do well to help my hell with your devil, as I have helped your devil with my hell.'

Rustico, who lived on roots and water, could hardly meet her demands, and told her that it would take many devils to appease hell, but he would do what he could. So he satisfied her sometimes, but so rarely that it was like throwing a bean into the lion's mouth; and the girl, thinking she was not serving God as diligently as she would have liked, complained somewhat. But while this debate was going on between Rustico's devil and Alibech's hell, due to excessive desire on the one part and lack of power on the other, it happened that a fire broke out in Capsa and burned Alibech's father in his own house, with as many children and other family members as he had. As a result, she became heir to all his wealth.

Thereupon, a young man named Neerbale, who had spent all his substance in gallantry, hearing that she was alive, went in search of her and, finding her before the authorities had seized her father's estate as that of a man dying without an heir, to Rustico's great satisfaction but against her own will, brought her back to Capsa, where he took her as his wife and succeeded, by her right, to her father's ample inheritance.

There, when asked by the women what service she had rendered to God in the desert, she answered (Neerbale not yet having lain with her) that she had served Him by putting the devil in hell, and that Neerbale had committed a grave sin in taking her away from such service. The ladies asked, 'How does one put the devil in hell?' And the girl, with words and gestures, explained it to them, whereat they broke out in such great laughter that they still laugh, and said, 'Do not worry, child; in fact, that is also done here, and Neerbale will serve our Lord well with you in this way.' Thereafter, telling it from one to another throughout the city, they brought it to a common saying there that the most acceptable service one could render to God was to put the devil in hell, which saying, having crossed the sea hither, is yet current here. Wherefore, all you young ladies who have need of God's grace, learn to put the devil in hell, for this is highly acceptable to Him and pleasing to both parties, and much good may grow and ensue thereof.
