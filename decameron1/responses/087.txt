THE SIXTH STORY
[Day the Eighth]

BRUNO AND BUFFALMACCO STEAL A PIG FROM CALANDRINO, THEN TRICK HIM INTO THINKING HE ATE IT HIMSELF

As soon as Filostrato had finished his story, which had elicited much laughter, the queen instructed Filomena to continue. She began: "Dear ladies, just as Filostrato was prompted by the mention of Maso to tell the story you have just heard, I too am moved by the tale of Calandrino and his friends to share another one with you, which I believe you will enjoy.

You already know who Calandrino, Bruno, and Buffalmacco were, so I can proceed with my story. Calandrino owned a small farm not far from Florence, which he had received as part of his wife's dowry. From this farm, he would get a pig each year, and it was his custom to go there with his wife to slaughter and salt the pig. 

One year, when Calandrino's wife was somewhat unwell, he went to the farm alone to kill the pig. Bruno and Buffalmacco, hearing about this and knowing that his wife had not accompanied him, went to stay with a priest who was a close friend of theirs and lived nearby. When Calandrino had killed the pig that morning, he saw Bruno and Buffalmacco with the priest and called out to them, saying, "You're just in time. I'd like you to see what a good husband I am."

He then took them into the house and showed them the pig, which they recognized was a very fine one. Realizing that Calandrino intended to salt it for his family, Bruno said to him, "Good heavens, you fool! Sell it and let's have a good time with the money, then tell your wife it was stolen."

"No," replied Calandrino, "she'd never believe me, and she'd throw me out of the house. Don't bother, I won't do it." Despite their many attempts, they could not persuade him.

Calandrino invited them to supper, but he did so so grudgingly that they refused and took their leave of him. Then Bruno and Buffalmacco said to each other, "What do you think about stealing that pig from him tonight?"

"How can we do it?" the other replied. 

"I can see how," said Bruno, "if he doesn't move it from where it is now."

"Then let's do it," his companion said. "Why not? Afterward, we can have a good time with it and the priest here."

The priest agreed, and Bruno said, "We'll need to use a little cunning. You know how stingy Calandrino is and how gladly he drinks when others pay. Let's take him to the tavern, where the priest will pretend to pay the whole bill in our honor and not let him pay anything. Calandrino will soon get tipsy, and then it will be easy, since he'll be home alone."

As they had planned, so they did. Calandrino, seeing that the priest would not let anyone else pay, gave himself over to drinking and got quite drunk, though it didn't take much to make him so. Late at night, when they left the tavern, Calandrino, without bothering about supper, went straight home, leaving the door open as he thought he had closed it and went to bed. 

Bruno and Buffalmacco then went to sup with the priest, and afterward quietly made their way to Calandrino's house, bringing the tools they needed to break in where Bruno had arranged. But finding the door open, they simply entered, unhooked the pig, and carried it off to the priest's house, where they stored it and went to sleep.

The next morning, Calandrino, having slept off the wine, got up and missed his pig, seeing the door open. He asked everyone he could if they knew who had taken it, but getting no news of it, began to shout, "Woe is me, wretched man that I am!" for the pig had been stolen.

As soon as Bruno and Buffalmacco had gotten up, they went to Calandrino's house to hear what he would say about the pig. When they saw him, he called out to them, almost in tears, "Alas, my friends, my pig has been stolen!"

Bruno came up to him and said softly, "It's strange that for once you've been wise." 

"Alas," Calandrino replied, "it's the truth."

"That's right," said Bruno. "Make a big fuss, so it looks like it really happened."

Calandrino shouted even louder, "Damn it, I'm telling you the truth, the pig has been stolen from me!"

"Good, good," replied Bruno. "That's how to speak, shout it loudly so it seems true."

Calandrino said, "You're trying to drive me crazy! I'm telling you, and you don't believe me, that the pig was stolen from me last night."

"Good heavens!" cried Bruno. "How can that be? I saw it here just yesterday. Do you think you can make me believe it flew away?"

"It's as I'm telling you," Calandrino insisted.

"If that's the case," said Buffalmacco, "we'll have to figure out a way to get it back, if we can manage it."

"But what way can we find?" asked Calandrino.

Buffalmacco replied, "We can be sure that no one from the Indies came to steal your pig; the thief must have been one of your neighbors. If you can gather them together, I know how to use the test with bread and cheese, and we'll soon see for certain who had it."

"Ah," put in Bruno, "you'd make a fine thing of that with the people around here. I'm sure one of them took the pig, and he'd see through the trap and wouldn't come."

"Then how shall we do it?" Buffalmacco asked, and Bruno said, "We'll have to do it with ginger pills and good white wine. We'll invite them to drink, and they'll suspect nothing and come, and the ginger pills can be blessed just like the bread and cheese."

"Indeed, you're right," said Buffalmacco. "What do you say, Calandrino? Shall we do it?"

"No, please, for the love of God," Calandrino replied. "If I only knew who had it, I'd be half consoled already."

"Well then," said Bruno, "I'm ready to go to Florence to get the things you need, if you'll give me the money."

Calandrino had maybe forty shillings, which he gave to Bruno. Bruno went to Florence and bought a pound of fine ginger pills from a friend of his who was an apothecary. He also had a couple of dog-balls compounded with fresh aloe, covered them in sugar like the others, and marked them secretly so he wouldn't mix them up. Then, getting a flask of good white wine, he returned to Calandrino in the country and said to him, "Tomorrow morning, invite those you suspect to come and drink with you. It's a holiday, and they'll all be happy to come. Meanwhile, Buffalmacco and I will consecrate the pills tonight and bring them to you tomorrow morning at your house. And for your sake, I'll administer them myself and do and say what needs to be done."

Calandrino did as he was told and gathered a good company of young Florentines and farmers who were in the area. Bruno and Buffalmacco came with a box of pills and the flask of wine and had the people stand in a circle. Then Bruno said, "Gentlemen, I must tell you the reason why you are here, so that if anything happens that displeases you, you won't have cause to complain about me. Calandrino here was robbed of a fine pig last night, and he can't find out who had it. Since none of us here could have stolen it from him except one of you, he offers each of you one of these pills to eat and a drink of wine. You should know that the one who had the pig won't be able to swallow the pill; it will seem more bitter than poison, and he'll spit it out. So rather than be shamed in front of everyone, he'd better confess it to the priest, and I won't go any further with this."

Everyone there said they would gladly eat the pills. Bruno lined them up and put Calandrino among them. Starting at one end, he began giving each one a pill. When he reached Calandrino, he gave him one of the dog-balls. Calandrino immediately put it in his mouth and started chewing it, but as soon as his tongue tasted the aloe, he spat it out, unable to bear the bitterness.

Meanwhile, everyone was looking at each other, waiting to see who would spit out their pill. As Bruno, pretending not to notice Calandrino, continued handing out the pills, he heard someone behind him say, "Hey, Calandrino, what's this?" He turned around quickly and, seeing that Calandrino had spit out his pill, said, "Wait, maybe something else made him spit it out. Take another one."

Then he put the other dog-ball in Calandrino's mouth. If the first one had seemed bitter to him, this one was even more so, but, ashamed to spit it out, he kept it in his mouth for a while, chewing it and shedding tears as big as hazelnuts, until finally, unable to hold out any longer, he expelled it like the first one.

Meanwhile, Buffalmacco and Bruno gave the company wine to drink, and they all declared that Calandrino had certainly stolen the pig himself. Some even scolded him roundly.

After the others had gone and the two tricksters were alone with Calandrino, Buffalmacco said to him, "I was certain all along that you had taken the pig yourself and wanted to make us believe it was stolen, to avoid having to share the money with us." 

Calandrino, who still hadn't gotten over the bitter taste of the aloe, began to swear that he hadn't had it, and Buffalmacco said, "But really, my friend, how much did you get for it? Was it six florins?"

Hearing this, Calandrino became desperate, and Bruno said, "Listen, Calandrino, there was someone in the group who ate and drank with us who told me you have a mistress over there, and you give her whatever you can scrape together, and he was sure you had sent her the pig. You've learned some new tricks lately - you dragged us down the Mugnone the other day, picking up black stones, and when you had got us on the boat without any biscuits, you took off and then tried to make us believe you'd found the magic stone. And now you think you can make us believe by swearing that the pig, which you gave away or more likely sold, was stolen from you. But we're used to your tricks and won't let you play any more of them on us. And to be plain with you, since we went to the trouble of doing the consecration, we expect you to give us two pairs of capons; otherwise, we'll tell your wife everything."

Seeing that he wasn't believed and thinking he'd had enough torment without adding his wife's scolding, Calandrino gave them the two pairs of capons. They took them back to Florence, after salting the pig, leaving Calandrino to digest the loss and the mockery as best he could.
