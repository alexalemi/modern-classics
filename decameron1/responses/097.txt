THE FIRST STORY

[Day the Ninth]

LADY FRANCESCA, COURTED BY RINUCCIO PALERMINI AND ALESSANDRO CHIARMONTESI BUT LOVING NEITHER, CLEVERLY RIDS HERSELF OF BOTH BY HAVING ONE ENTER A TOMB PRETENDING TO BE A DEAD MAN AND THE OTHER RETRIEVE HIM, SUCH THAT THEY CANNOT FULFILL THE CONDITION SHE IMPOSED

"Since it is your wish, Lady, I am happy to be the first to share a story in this open and free field of storytelling that your grace has set before us. If I do well, I have no doubt that those who follow will do well and even better. Many times, charming ladies, our discussions have shown the power and great influence of love. Yet, I believe it has not been fully explored (nor would it be, even if we spoke of nothing else for a year) that love not only brings lovers into various dangers of death, but causes them to even enter tombs of the dead as if dead themselves. So, I will tell you a story about this, beyond those already told, so that you may not only understand the force of love, but also know the wit used by a worthy lady to rid herself of two who loved her against her will.

You should know that there was once in the city of Pistoia a very beautiful widow, whom two of our townsmen, one called Rinuccio Palermini and the other Alessandro Chiarmontesi, both banished from Florence, loved passionately without knowing of the other's affection. The lady in question, whose name was Lady Francesca de' Lazzari, was constantly being pursued by the two with messages and pleas, to which she had sometimes unwisely listened, and wishing, but in vain, to discreetly withdraw, thought of how she might rid herself of their importunity by requiring them to perform a service, which, though possible, she believed neither of them would render, so that, by their failure to do what she asked, she might have a fair and reasonable excuse to refuse to listen to their messages anymore. The plan that occurred to her was as follows.

There had died that very day in Pistoia a man who, although his ancestors were gentlemen, was considered the worst person not only in Pistoia, but in the entire world. Moreover, he was so misshapen and monstrous in appearance that whoever saw him for the first time, even if they did not know him, would have been afraid of him. He had been buried in a tomb outside the church of the Minor Friars. This circumstance she thought would be very suitable for her purpose, and accordingly she said to a maid of hers, 'You know the annoyance and vexation I suffer all day long from the messages of those two Florentines, Rinuccio and Alessandro. Now I am not disposed to gratify either of them with my love, and to rid myself of them, I have thought of something, given the great offers they make, to test them in a way that I am certain they will not do. This shall do away with their importunity, and you shall see how. You know that Scannadio, as the wicked man of whom we have spoken was called, was buried this morning in the burial place of the Minor Brethren, Scannadio, of whom, even when alive, the bravest men of this city were afraid; therefore, go quietly first to Alessandro and tell him, "Lady Francesca informs you that the time has now come when you may have her love, which you have so desired, and be with her, if you will, in this way. This night, for a reason which you shall know later, the body of Scannadio, who was buried this morning, is to be brought to her house by one of her kinsmen, and she, being in great fear of him, even though dead, would prefer not to have him there; therefore, she prays that it may please you, as a great service to her, to go this evening, at the first hour of sleep, to the tomb where he is buried, and, by putting on the dead man's clothes, remain there as if you were he until they come for you. Then, without moving or speaking, you must allow yourself to be taken up from the tomb and brought to her house, where she will receive you, and you may afterwards stay with her and depart at your leisure, leaving the rest to her.' If he says he will do it, well and good; but, should he refuse, tell him that on my part he is never to show himself where I may be and to look, as he values his life, that he send me no more letters or messages. Then you shall go to Rinuccio Palermini and say to him, 'Lady Francesca says that she is ready to grant your every desire, if you will render her a great service, namely, that tonight, towards midnight, you go to the tomb where Scannadio was buried this morning and take him out softly from there and bring him to her at her house, without saying a word of anything you may hear or feel. There you shall learn what she would have with him and have your pleasure of her; but, if it does not please you to do this, she charges you never again to send her letter or message.'"

The maid went to the two lovers and delivered her message faithfully to each, to which each replied that, if it pleased her, they would go, not only into a tomb, but into hell itself. The maid conveyed their responses to the lady, and she waited to see if they would be foolish enough to do it. When night came and it was the hour of the first sleep, Alessandro Chiarmontesi, having stripped down to his doublet, went forth from his house to take Scannadio's place in the tomb; but, on the way, a very frightening thought came into his head, and he began saying to himself, "Good heavens, what a fool I am! Where am I going? How do I know that the lady's kinsmen, having perhaps realized that I love her and believing what is not true, have not arranged for me to do this, so they can kill me in that tomb? If that were to happen, I would suffer for it, and nothing in the world would ever be known to their detriment. Or what do I know but that perhaps some enemy of mine has procured this for me, whom she may love and seek to oblige in this matter?" Then he said, "But, granting that neither of these things is the case and that her kinsmen are simply going to carry me to her house, I must believe that they do not want Scannadio's body to hold it in their arms or to place it in hers; nay, it is rather to be conceived that they mean to do some mischief to it, as to the body of one who may have displeased them in some way before. She says that I am not to say a word for anything that I may feel. But, should they put out my eyes or pull out my teeth or cut off my hands or play me any other such trick, how shall I do? How could I remain silent? And if I speak, they will know me, and perhaps do me some harm, or, though they do me no hurt, yet shall I have accomplished nothing, for they will not leave me with the lady; whereupon she will say that I have broken her command and will never do anything to please me." So saying, he had almost returned home; but, nevertheless, his great love urged him on with counter arguments of such power that they brought him to the tomb, which he opened and, entering therein, stripped Scannadio of his clothes; then, putting them on and closing the tomb behind him, he laid himself in the dead man's place. Thereupon he began to call to mind what manner of man the latter had been and, remembering all the things he had heard tell of as having befallen by night, not only in the sepulchres of the dead, but even elsewhere, every hair on his head began to stand on end, and it seemed to him each moment as if Scannadio should rise upright and butcher him then and there. However, aided by his ardent love, he overcame these and the other fearful thoughts that beset him and, remaining as if he were the dead man, he waited for what should come to pass.

Meanwhile, Rinuccio, midnight being now at hand, left his house to do what had been enjoined him by his mistress, and as he went, he entered into many and various thoughts of the things which might possibly befall him; as, to wit, that he might fall into the hands of the police, with Scannadio's body on his shoulders, and be condemned to the fire as a sorcerer, and that he should, if the thing came to be known, incur the ill-will of his kinsmen, and other like thoughts, whereby he was like to have been deterred. But afterwards, reflecting again, "Alas," said he, "shall I deny this lady, whom I have so loved and love, the first thing she requires of me, especially as I am thereby to gain her favor? God forbid, though I were certainly to die thereof, but I should set myself to do that which I have promised!" Accordingly, he went on and presently, coming to the sepulchre, opened it easily; which Alessandro hearing, remained still, although he was in great fear. Rinuccio, entering in and thinking to take Scannadio's body, laid hold of Alessandro's feet and drew him forth of the tomb; then, hoisting him on his shoulders, he made off towards the lady's house.

Going thus and taking no manner of heed to his burden, he jolted it many a time now against one corner and now another of certain benches that were beside the way, more by token that the night was so cloudy and so dark he could not see whither he went. He was already well nigh at the door of the gentlewoman, who had posted herself at the window with her maid, to see if he would bring Alessandro, and was ready armed with an excuse to send them both away, when it chanced that the officers of the watch, who were ambushed in the street and abode silently on the watch to lay hands upon a certain outlaw, hearing the scuffling that Rinuccio made with his feet, suddenly put out a light, to see what was to do and whither to go, and rattled their targets and halberds, crying, "Who goeth there?" Rinuccio, seeing this and having scant time for deliberation, let fall his burden and made off as fast as his legs would carry him; whereupon Alessandro arose in haste and made off in his turn, for all he was hampered with the dead man's clothes, which were very long. The lady, by the light of the lantern put out by the police, had plainly recognized Rinuccio, with Alessandro on his shoulders, and perceiving the latter to be clad in Scannadio's clothes, marvelled amain at the exceeding hardihood of both; but, for all her wonderment, she laughed heartily to see Alessandro cast down on the ground and to see him after take to flight. Then, rejoiced at this accident and praising God that He had rid her of the annoy of these twain, she turned back into the house and betook herself to her chamber, avouching to her maid that without doubt they both loved her greatly, since, as it appeared, they had done that which she had enjoined them.

Meanwhile Rinuccio, woeful and cursing his ill fortune, for all that returned not home, but, as soon as the watch had departed the neighbourhood, he came back whereas he had dropped Alessandro and groped about, to see if he could find him again, so he might make an end of his service; but, finding him not and concluding that the police had carried him off, he returned to his own house, woebegone, whilst Alessandro, unknowing what else to do, made off home on like wise, chagrined at such a misadventure and without having recognized him who had borne him thither. On the morrow, Scannadio's tomb being found open and his body not to be seen, for that Alessandro had rolled it to the bottom of the vault, all Pistoia was busy with various conjectures anent the matter, and the simpler sort concluded that he had been carried off by the devils. Nevertheless, each of the two lovers signified to the lady that which he had done and what had befallen and excusing himself withal for not having fully accomplished her commandment, claimed her favor and her love; but she, making believe to credit neither of this, rid herself of them with a curt response to the effect that she would never consent to do aught for them, since they had not done that which she had required of them."
