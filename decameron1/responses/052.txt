THE FIFTH STORY

[Day the Fifth]

GUIDOTTO DA CREMONA LEAVES HIS DAUGHTER TO GIACOMINO DA PAVIA BEFORE DYING. GIANNOLE DI SEVERINO AND MINGHINO DI MINGOLE FALL IN LOVE WITH THE GIRL IN FAENZA AND COME TO BLOWS OVER HER. ULTIMATELY, SHE IS PROVEN TO BE GIANNOLE'S SISTER AND IS GIVEN TO MINGHINO AS HIS WIFE.

All the ladies, having listened to the story about the nightingale, had laughed so much that even though Filostrato had finished telling it, they could not yet stop laughing. But after they had laughed for a while, the queen said to Filostrato, "Truly, if you distressed us ladies yesterday, you have so delighted us today that none of us can rightfully complain about you." Then, turning to Neifile, she instructed her to tell the next story, and Neifile began to speak cheerfully:

I say, then, that there once lived in the city of Fano two Lombards, one named Guidotto da Cremona and the other Giacomino da Pavia, both men of advanced years who had spent much of their youth as soldiers engaged in acts of war. Guidotto, being on his deathbed and having no son, kinsman, or friend he trusted more than Giacomino, left him a little daughter of about ten years old, as well as all his worldly possessions. After speaking at length to Giacomino about his affairs, Guidotto passed away. 

At that time, the city of Faenza, which had long been at war and in a poor state, was restored to somewhat better condition, and permission was freely granted for all who wished to return there. So Giacomino, who had lived there before and had an affection for the place, returned there with all his belongings and took the girl left to him by Guidotto, whom he loved and cared for as his own child.

The girl grew up to become as beautiful a young woman as any in the city, and as virtuous and well-mannered as she was fair. As a result, she began to be courted by many, but especially two very agreeable young men of equal worth and standing, who professed a great love for her. This led them to hate each other greatly out of jealousy. Their names were Giannole di Severino and Minghino di Mingole, and each would have gladly taken the young lady, who was now fifteen years old, as his wife if it had been permitted by their families. Seeing that she was denied to them in an honorable way, each set out to try to obtain her for himself as best he could.

Now Giacomino had an old serving-woman in his house and a serving-man named Crivello, who was a very merry and obliging person. Giannole struck up a great acquaintance with Crivello and, when he thought the time was right, revealed his passion to him, begging him to be favorable to his efforts to obtain his desire and promising him great rewards if he did so. Crivello replied, "Look, I can do nothing for you in this matter other than that, when Giacomino next goes out to supper, I will bring you where she may be; for if I were to say a word to her in your favor, she would never stop to listen to me. If this pleases you, I promise it to you and will do it; and do you, if you know how, that which you think will best serve your purpose." Giannole answered that he desired nothing more, and they came to this understanding.

Meanwhile, Minghino had corrupted the maidservant and worked with her so that she had several times carried messages to the girl and had almost inflamed her with love for him; moreover, she had promised him that she would bring him to meet her, as soon as Giacomino should happen to go out in the evening for any reason.

It then came about that, through Crivello's contrivance, Giacomino went to sup with a friend of his. Crivello informed Giannole of this and made an arrangement with him that, when he gave a certain signal, he should come and would find the door open. The maid, for her part, knowing nothing of all this, let Minghino know that Giacomino was to sup abroad and bade him wait near the house, so that when she made a signal, he might come and enter.

When evening came, the two lovers, knowing nothing of each other's plans but each mistrusting his rival, came with various armed companions to take possession. Minghino, with his troop, took up his quarters in the house of a friend of his, a neighbor of the young lady's, while Giannole and his friends stationed themselves at a little distance from the house. Meanwhile, Crivello and the maid, Giacomino being gone, each tried to send the other away. But Crivello, seeing the hour come that he had appointed with Giannole, said to himself, "What do I care about her? If she doesn't stay quiet, she's likely to regret it."

Accordingly, giving the appointed signal, he went to open the door, whereupon Giannole, coming up in haste with two companions, entered and, finding the young lady in the salon, seized her to carry her off. The girl began to struggle and make a great outcry, as did the maid. Hearing this, Minghino ran there with his companions and, seeing the young lady being dragged out the door, they drew their swords and cried, "Oh, traitors, you are dead men! This shall not be done. What is this violence?" They then attacked them, while the neighbors, coming out with lights and weapons at the clamor, began to blame Giannole's behavior and to side with Minghino. After a long struggle, Minghino rescued the young lady from his rival and restored her to Giacomino's house. But before the fray was over, the town guards arrived and arrested many of them, including Minghino, Giannole, and Crivello, who were taken to prison.

Once things had settled down, Giacomino returned home, greatly distressed by what had happened. But upon inquiring into the matter and finding that the girl was in no way at fault, he was somewhat appeased and determined to marry her as soon as possible, so that such an event would not happen again.

The next morning, the kinfolk of the two young men, hearing the truth of the case and knowing the harm that might befall the imprisoned youths if Giacomino chose to act as he reasonably could, went to him and pleaded with gentle words for him to have regard, not so much for the affront he had suffered from the young men's lack of judgment, as for the love and goodwill they believed he bore toward themselves who now besought him. They submitted themselves and the young men who had caused the mischief to any amends it should please him to take. Giacomino, who had seen many things in his time and was a man of sense, answered briefly, "Gentlemen, were I in my own country, as I am in yours, I hold myself so much your friend that neither in this nor in anything else would I do other than as it should please you; moreover, I am the more bound to comply with your wishes in this matter, inasmuch as you have therein offended against yourselves, for the girl in question is not, as many may suppose, from Cremona or Pavia; nay, she is a native of Faenza, although neither I nor she nor he from whom I received her may ever learn whose daughter she was. Therefore, concerning that which you pray me, so much shall be done by me as you yourselves shall enjoin me."

The gentlemen, hearing this, marveled and, thanking Giacomino for his gracious answer, begged him to tell them how she came into his hands and how he knew her to be from Faenza. To this, Giacomino replied, "Guidotto da Cremona, who was my friend and comrade, told me, on his deathbed, that when this city was taken by the Emperor Frederick and everything given up to pillage, he entered with his companions into a house and found it full of plunder, but deserted by its inhabitants, save only this girl, who was then about two years old, and who, seeing him mount the stairs, called him 'father.' Thereupon, taking pity on her, he carried her off with him to Fano, together with all that was in the house, and, dying there, left her to me with what he had, charging me to marry her in due time and give her as her dowry that which had been hers. Since she has come of marriageable age, I have not yet found an occasion to marry her to my liking, though I would gladly do it, rather than that another mischance like that of last night should befall me on her account."

Now among the others there was a certain Guiglielmino da Medicina, who had been with Guidotto at the time of the sack of Faenza and knew very well whose house it was that he had plundered. Seeing the person in question there among the rest, he accosted him, saying, "Bernabuccio, do you hear what Giacomino is saying?" "Yes, I do," answered Bernabuccio, "and I was just thinking about it, the more so as I remember losing a little daughter of the age of which Giacomino speaks in those very troubles." Quiglielmino said, "This is she for certain, for I was once in company with Guidotto when I heard him tell where he had done the plundering, and I knew it to be your house that he had sacked; wherefore do you bethink you if you may credibly recognize her by any token and let search be made therefor; for you will assuredly find that she is your daughter."

Accordingly, Bernabuccio bethought himself and remembered that she should have a little cross-shaped scar above her left ear, from a tumor he had caused to be cut for her not long before that occurrence. Without further delay, he accosted Giacomino, who was still there, and begged him to take him to his house and let him see the young woman. To this Giacomino readily consented, and, taking him there, had the girl brought before him. When Bernabuccio set eyes on her, he seemed to see the very face of her mother, who was still a handsome lady; nevertheless, not content with this, he asked Giacomino's leave to lift her hair a little above her left ear, to which the other consented. Going up to the girl, who stood bashfully, he lifted up her hair with his right hand and found the cross; whereupon, knowing her to be indeed his daughter, he fell to weeping tenderly and embracing her, despite her resistance. Then, turning to Giacomino, he said, "Brother mine, this is my daughter; it was my house that Guidotto plundered, and this girl was, in the sudden alarm, forgotten there by her mother and myself; and until now we believed that she had perished with the house, which was burned down that same day."

The girl, hearing this, and seeing him to be an older man, gave credence to his words and, submitting herself to his embraces, as moved by some innate instinct, fell to weeping tenderly with him. Bernabuccio presently sent for her mother and other female relatives, as well as her brothers and sisters, and presented her to them all, recounting the matter. Then, after a thousand embraces, he took her home to his house with the utmost rejoicing, to the great satisfaction of Giacomino. The town captain, who was a man of worth, learning this and knowing that Giannole, whom he had in prison, was Bernabuccio's son and therefore the lady's own brother, determined indulgently to overlook the offense committed by him and released Minghino and Crivello and the others who were implicated in the affair. Moreover, he interceded with Bernabuccio and Giacomino concerning these matters and, making peace between the two young men, gave the girl, whose name was Agnesa, to Minghino as his wife, to the great contentment of all their kinfolk. Thereupon Minghino, greatly rejoiced, made a great and splendid wedding and, taking her home, lived with her many years thereafter in peace and prosperity.
