The Fourth Story
[Day the Tenth]

Messer Gentile de' Carisendi Recovers a Woman from the Grave and Reunites Her with Her Husband

It seemed remarkable to everyone that a man would be so generous with his own blood, and they declared that Messer Gentile's generosity surpassed even that of the King of Spain and the Abbot of Cluny. But after enough had been said about this, the king looked at Lauretta and signaled for her to tell the next story. She promptly began, "Young ladies, the things that have been recounted are magnificent and wonderful, and I don't believe there is anything left for us to discuss that has not already been thoroughly covered, except for matters of love, which provide an abundance of material for discourse on every subject. For this reason, and because the theme is one to which our age is naturally inclined, I am pleased to tell you an act of magnanimity performed by a lover, which, when all things are considered, may perhaps appear to you no less impressive than any of those already related, if it is true that treasures are lavished, enmities forgotten, and life itself, indeed, honor and renown, exposed to a thousand perils, so that we may possess the thing beloved.

There was, then, in the noble city of Bologna in Lombardy, a gentleman of great virtue and noble blood, called Messer Gentile Carisendi. While he was young, he fell in love with a noble lady named Madam Catalina, the wife of one Niccoluccio Caccianimico. Because his love for her was not reciprocated, when he was appointed governor of Modona, he went there, despairing of her.

Meanwhile, Niccoluccio was away from Bologna, and the lady, being with child, had gone to stay at a country house she owned about three miles from the city. She was suddenly seized by a severe illness that extinguished all signs of life in her, and she was pronounced dead by various physicians. Since her closest relatives said they had been told by her that she had not been pregnant long enough for the child to be fully formed, they buried her, after much lament, in a nearby church vault, without further inquiry.

The news was quickly conveyed to Messer Gentile by a friend of his. Though he had still been denied her favor while she lived, he grieved deeply for her and ultimately said to himself, 'Madam Catalina, you are dead, you whom I could never obtain so much as a glance from while you lived; therefore, now that you can no longer defend yourself, I must take a kiss or two from you, even though you are dead.' With this thought, he made arrangements for a secret journey, and that night he rode with one of his servants until he reached the place where the lady was buried. He opened the tomb with haste and, entering it, laid himself beside her, kissing her repeatedly with many tears. But then, as we often see that the desires of men, and especially those of lovers, are never content within any limit but always crave for more, he said to himself, 'Now that I am here, why should I not touch her breast a little? I may never touch her again, and I have never done so before.' Overcome by this desire, he put his hand into her bosom and, holding it there for a while, felt her heart beating faintly. Putting aside his fear, he examined her more closely and found that she was not truly dead, though her life was barely flickering. With the help of his servant, he carefully took her from the tomb and, setting her before him on his horse, carried her secretly to his house in Bologna.

There, his mother, a worthy and discreet gentlewoman, after hearing everything from her son, moved by compassion, set about reviving the lady through the use of hot baths and great fires. The lady soon came to herself and, heaving a great sigh, asked, "Where am I?" The good lady replied, "Be of good comfort; you are in safety." Madam Catalina, regaining her senses, looked around and did not quite know where she was, but seeing Messer Gentile before her, she was filled with wonder and begged his mother to tell her how she had come there. Messer Gentile then recounted everything in order. At this, she was deeply distressed, but soon expressed her gratitude to him and besought him, by the love he had once borne her and out of courtesy, that she might not suffer in his house anything contrary to her honor or that of her husband, and that he would allow her to return to her own home as soon as day broke. "Madam," Messer Gentile replied, "whatever my desires may have been in the past, I do not intend, either now or ever hereafter (since God has granted me the grace of restoring you to life from death, through the love I have borne you), to treat you here or elsewhere as anything other than a dear sister. But this service I have done you tonight deserves some recompense; therefore, I would ask a favor of you."

The lady graciously replied that she was ready to do his wish, as long as it was honorable. He then said, "Madam, your relatives and all the people of Bologna believe and hold you to be certainly dead, so no one is expecting you at home. Therefore, I would ask that you be pleased to stay quietly here with my mother until such time as I shall return from Modona, which will be soon. The reason I request this of you is that I intend to make a dear and solemn present of you to your husband in the presence of the most notable citizens of this place." The lady, acknowledging her debt to the gentleman and finding his request an honorable one, determined to do as he asked, however much she desired to gladden her kin with news of her life. She promised this to him on her faith. Hardly had she finished speaking when she felt the time of her delivery come, and soon after, being tenderly cared for by Messer Gentile's mother, she gave birth to a fine baby boy, which greatly increased his joy and her own. Messer Gentile made sure that all necessary provisions were made and that she was attended to as if she were his own wife, and then he returned in secret to Modona.

There, having served out his term of office and being about to return to Bologna, he arranged for a great and splendid banquet to be held at his house on the morning of his arrival, to which he invited many gentlemen of the city, including Niccoluccio Caccianimico. When he returned and dismounted, he found them all waiting, as well as the lady, more beautiful and in better health than ever, and her little son in good condition. With indescribable joy, he seated his guests at the table and had them magnificently served with various dishes.

As the meal was nearing its end, having first told the lady what he meant to do and agreed with her on the course she should follow, he began to speak: "Gentlemen, I recall having heard that there is a custom in Persia, which I find pleasant, that when anyone wishes to honor a friend above all others, he invites him to his house and shows him the thing he holds most dear, be it wife, mistress, daughter, or whatever else, declaring that, just as he shows him this, so would he show him his very heart if he could. I intend to observe this Persian custom in Bologna. You have honored my banquet with your presence, and I in turn mean to honor you, in the Persian fashion, by showing you the most precious thing I have or may ever have in the world. But before I proceed to do this, I pray you tell me what you think of a doubtful case I shall put to you. A certain person has in his house a very faithful and good servant who falls grievously ill, whereupon the former, without awaiting the sick man's end, has him carried into the middle of the street and pays him no further heed. A stranger comes, who, moved to compassion for the sick man, takes him to his own house and, with great diligence and expense, brings him back to his former health. Now I would like to know whether, if the stranger keeps him and makes use of his services, the former master can justly complain of or blame the latter if, when he demands the servant back, the latter refuses to return him."

The gentlemen, after various discussions among themselves, all agreeing in one opinion, entrusted the response to Niccoluccio Caccianimico, for he was a fine and eloquent speaker. Niccoluccio, having first commended the Persian custom, declared that he and all the rest were of the opinion that the first master had no further right in his servant, since he had, in such a circumstance, not only abandoned him, but cast him away, and that, for the kind offices done him by the second, it seemed to them the servant had justly become his; therefore, in keeping him, he did the first no harm, no violence, no injustice whatsoever. The other guests at the table (and there were men there of worth and distinction) all said in one accord that they held to what had been answered by Niccoluccio; and Messer Gentile, well pleased with this response and that Niccoluccio had made it, avowed himself also to be of the same opinion. Then he said, "It is now time that I honor you according to my promise," and calling two of his servants, he sent them to the lady, whom he had had magnificently dressed and adorned, requesting that she come gladden the company with her presence. Accordingly, she took her little son, who was very handsome, in her arms and, coming into the banquet hall, attended by two servants, seated herself as Messer Gentile directed, next to a gentleman of high standing. Then he said, "Gentlemen, this is the thing which I hold and purpose to hold dearer than any other; look if it seems to you that I have reason to do so."

The guests, having paid her the utmost honor and commended her greatly, declared to Messer Gentile that he might well hold her dear. They began to gaze upon her, and there were many there who would have recognized her as Madam Catalina, had they not believed her to be dead. But Niccoluccio gazed upon her above all and, unable to contain himself, asked her, (Messer Gentile having withdrawn for a moment,) as one who burned to know who she was, if she were a woman of Bologna or a foreigner. The lady, seeing herself questioned by her husband, could hardly restrain herself from answering; but yet, to observe the appointed order, she remained silent. Another asked her if the child was hers, and a third if she were Messer Gentile's wife or in any way related to him; but she made them no reply. 

Presently, Messer Gentile coming up, one of his guests said to him, "Sir, this is a fair creature of yours, but she seems to us mute; is she so?" "Gentlemen," Messer Gentile replied, "her not having spoken at this present is no small proof of her virtue." And the other said, "Then tell us who she is." Messer Gentile said, "That I will gladly do, provided you all promise me that no one will leave his place until I have finished my story."

They all agreed to this, and the tables being removed, Messer Gentile, seating himself beside the lady, said, "Gentlemen, this lady is that loyal and faithful servant, of whom I questioned you a while ago and who, being little cared for by her people and thus cast out as a worthless thing no longer of use, was taken up by me; yes, through my solicitude and by my own efforts I brought her back from the jaws of death, and God, having regard to my good intent, has caused her, by my means, to become thus beautiful from a frightful corpse. But that you may more clearly understand how this came about, I will briefly relate it to you." Then, starting from his falling in love with her, he particularly recounted to them all that had happened up to that time, to the great wonder of the listeners, and added, "By reason of these events, if you, and especially Niccoluccio, have not changed your opinion since a while ago, the lady is rightfully mine, nor can anyone justly demand her back from me." To this none made answer; nay, all awaited what he should say further, while Niccoluccio and the lady and certain of the others who were there wept for compassion.

Then Messer Gentile, rising to his feet and taking the little child in his arms and the lady by the hand, went to Niccoluccio and said to him, "Rise up, my friend; I do not return to you your wife, whom your kin and hers cast away; nay, but I will gladly give you this lady, my friend, together with this her little son, whom I am assured was begotten by you and whom I held at baptism and named Gentile; and I pray you that she be no less dear to you for having stayed near upon three months in my house; for I swear to you—by that God who perhaps caused me to fall in love with her aforetime, to the end that my love might be, as in effect it has been, the occasion of her deliverance—that never, whether with father or mother or with you, has she lived more chastely than she has done with my mother in my house." So saying, he turned to the lady and said to her, "Madam, from this time forth I absolve you of every promise made to me and leave you free to return to Niccoluccio." Then, giving the lady and the child into Niccoluccio's arms, he returned to his seat.

Niccoluccio received them with the utmost eagerness, so much the more rejoiced as he was the farther removed from hope thereof, and thanked Messer Gentile as best he might and knew; while the others, who all wept for compassion, commended the latter greatly for this deed. The lady was received in her house with wonderful rejoicing and long beheld with amazement by the Bolognese, as one raised from the dead; and Messer Gentile ever after remained a friend of Niccoluccio and of his kin and those of the lady.

What, then, dear ladies, will you say of this case? Is the king's having given away his scepter and crown, or an abbot's having, without cost to himself, reconciled a wrongdoer with the Pope, or an old man's having offered his throat to the enemy's knife, to be compared with this deed of Messer Gentile, who, being young and ardent and thinking he had a just claim to what the carelessness of others had cast away and he had by good fortune taken up, not only honorably restrained his passion, but, having in his possession that which he was still wont to covet and seek to steal away, freely restored it to its owner? Surely, none of the noble deeds already recounted can equal this.
