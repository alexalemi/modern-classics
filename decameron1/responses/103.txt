TALANO OF MOLESE DREAMS THAT A WOLF ATTACKS HIS WIFE'S NECK AND FACE, BUT SHE IGNORES HIS WARNING AND IT HAPPENS TO HER AS HE DREAMED

After Pamfilo's story ended and everyone praised the quick-witted woman, the queen ordered Pampinea to tell her tale. She began, "We have often discussed the truths that dreams can foretell, which many women scoff at. But despite what has been said, I will tell you in a few words what recently happened to a neighbor of mine who did not believe her husband's dream.

I don't know if you were acquainted with Talano of Molese, a respectable man who married a young woman named Margarita, beautiful beyond all others. But she was so moody, ill-tempered, and stubborn that she would do nothing according to others' judgment, nor could anyone please her. As difficult as this was for Talano to endure, he had no choice but to put up with it.

One night, while staying with this Margarita at a country estate they owned, Talano dreamed he saw his wife walking in a lovely forest near their home. Suddenly, a large and fierce wolf emerged from the bushes, leapt at her throat, and knocked her to the ground, trying to drag her away as she screamed for help. After she freed herself from his jaws, the dream showed the wolf had marred her whole throat and face.

When Talano woke up, he said to his wife, "Although your stubbornness has never allowed me a good day with you, I would be grieved if harm came to you. So, if you will heed my advice, you will not leave the house today." When she asked why, he recounted his dream to her.

Margarita shook her head and said, "Those who wish you ill, dream you ill. You pretend to care for me, but you dream of what you wish would happen to me. Be assured that I will be careful today and always not to give you cause for such misfortune." Talano replied, "I knew you would say that, for he who combs a scald-head gets little thanks. But believe as you wish - I tell you this for your own good, and again I advise you to stay home today or at least beware of going into our forest."

"Very well, I will do it," she answered. Then, she said to herself, "Did you see how cleverly that man thinks to frighten me from going to our forest today? Surely, he has made a tryst there with some mistress and does not want me to find him. Well, it will be a fine meal for him if I am blind to his tricks, and I would be a simpleton not to see his scheme and believe him! But he shall not have his way; even if I stay there all day, I must see what business he is about today."

So, after her husband left through one door, she went out the other and made her way as secretly as she could to the forest, hiding in the thickest part and watching intently, hoping to see someone arrive. As she waited there, with no thought of danger, a huge and terrible wolf suddenly emerged from a nearby thicket. Scarcely could she cry, "Lord, help me!" when the wolf leapt at her throat and began to carry her off, just as a lamb. She could neither cry out nor defend herself, so tightly was her throat gripped. The wolf would surely have strangled her, had some shepherds not shouted at him, forcing him to release her.

The shepherds recognized her and carried the pitiful woman home, where the physicians tended to her for a long time. Though she eventually recovered, she was never fully healed, and the wolf had marred her whole throat and part of her face, so that where she had once been beautiful, she now appeared disfigured and very ugly. Ashamed to be seen in public, she often bitterly regretted her stubbornness and her refusal to believe her husband's true dream, which had cost her so dearly.
