After Elisa finished her sorrowful story, Queen Filomena, who was tall and beautiful, with a pleasant and agreeable demeanor, gathered herself and said, "Dioneo's agreement must be honored, so since he and I are the only ones left to speak, I will tell my story first, and he, having requested it, will be the last to speak."

She began, "There is a common saying that the deceiver ends up at the feet of the deceived. This seems to me to be true, as events have shown. While following the appointed theme, I will demonstrate to you, dear ladies, that this is indeed the case, so that you may know how to protect yourselves from deceivers.

There were once in Paris some very prominent Italian merchants, who had come there on various business matters. One evening, as they dined together merrily, they began discussing their wives, whom they had left at home. One said jokingly, "I know not how my wife fares; but I know that whenever a woman pleases me here, I set aside the love I bear my wife and take my pleasure with the other." Another replied, "I do likewise, for whether I believe my wife is unfaithful or not, she is, so tit for tat. A wise man gets as good as he gives." A third echoed similar sentiments, and they all seemed to agree that the wives they had left behind had no intention of wasting time in their husbands' absence.

Only one, Bernabo Lomellini of Genoa, maintained the opposite, swearing that his wife was the most virtuous and accomplished woman in all of Italy. He extolled her beauty, grace, and skills, claiming that she was so chaste and loyal that even if he were away for ten years, she would never stray with another man. This provoked laughter from a young man named Ambrogiuolo of Piacenza, who mocked Bernabo's boast and challenged him, saying that if he could not seduce Bernabo's wife within three months, he would forfeit a thousand gold florins, but if he succeeded, Bernabo would owe him five thousand.

Bernabo, confident in his wife's virtue, accepted the wager. Ambrogiuolo then traveled to Genoa, where he bribed a servant to hide him in a chest in the lady's bedchamber. Over several nights, he observed the room and took some of the lady's belongings as proof. Upon returning, Ambrogiuolo presented these items to the merchants, convincing Bernabo that he had indeed seduced his wife.

Overcome with rage and shame, Bernabo ordered a servant to kill his innocent wife. The servant, however, took pity on her and allowed her to flee, providing her with a man's clothing. Disguised as a sailor named Sicurano, she eventually made her way to Alexandria, where she entered the service of the Sultan.

Some time later, Sicurano learned that Ambrogiuolo was also in Alexandria, boasting of his deception. Seizing the opportunity for revenge, Sicurano arranged for both Ambrogiuolo and Bernabo to be brought before the Sultan. There, Ambrogiuolo confessed his lies, and the Sultan punished him severely. Sicurano then revealed her true identity as Bernabo's wife, Ginevra, and was happily reunited with her husband, who had believed her dead.
