THE EIGHTH STORY

[Day the Ninth]

BIONDELLO TRICKS CIACCO OUT OF A DINNER, AND CIACCO CLEVERLY SEEKS REVENGE

The lively group unanimously agreed that Talano's vision in his dream was no dream at all, but a true vision, as it had unfolded exactly as he had seen it. With everyone silent, the queen instructed Lauretta to continue, and she said, "Just as those, most discreet ladies, who have spoken before me today, have been inspired to tell their tales by something previously mentioned, the harsh revenge taken by the scholar that Pampinea told us about yesterday moves me to recount a piece of revenge that, while not as barbaric, was still deeply hurtful to the one who endured it.

I must tell you, then, that there was once a man in Florence whom everyone called Ciacco, a glutton as great as any who ever lived. His means were not enough to support his gluttony, but he was a very well-mannered man, full of pleasant and witty sayings, and he made himself not quite a fool, but a sponger, accompanying the wealthy who delighted in good food, often dining and supping with them, even if he was not always invited. There was also in Florence, in those days, a man named Biondello, a small, dapper fellow with a carefully styled wig and clothing always impeccably neat, who pursued the same trade as Ciacco.

One morning in Lent, as Biondello was bargaining for two very fine lampreys for Messer Vieri de' Cerchj, he was spotted by Ciacco, who approached him and asked, 'What is the meaning of this?' Biondello replied, 'Last night, three even finer lampreys and a sturgeon were sent to Messer Corso Donati, but that was not enough for the dinner he is hosting for some gentlemen, so he wants me to buy these other two. Won't you come along?' Ciacco said, 'You know I'll be there.'

When the time seemed right, Ciacco went to Messer Corso's house, where he found him with some of his neighbors, not yet seated for the meal. When Messer Corso asked what he was doing there, Ciacco answered, 'Sir, I've come to dine with you and your company.' Messer Corso said, 'You're welcome; since it's time, let's sit at the table.' They sat down, and for the first course they had chickpeas and pickled tuna, and then a dish of fried fish from the Arno, and no more. Realizing Biondello had tricked him, Ciacco was inwardly quite angry and determined to get even. He did not have to wait long, for he soon encountered Biondello, who had already regaled many with the trick he had played.

Biondello, seeing Ciacco, greeted him and jokingly asked how he had enjoyed Messer Corso's lampreys. Ciacco replied, 'You'll find out much better than I in less than a week.' Then, without further ado, he took his leave of Biondello and, making a deal with a sharp-witted peddler, had him go near the Cavicciuoli gallery and point out a certain Messer Filippo Argenti, a large, burly, and incredibly ill-tempered and moody man. The peddler was instructed to approach Messer Filippo with a large glass flask and say, 'Biondello sent me to you and asks that you be so kind as to fill this flask with your good red wine, for he wishes to make merry with his friends.' But the peddler was told to be careful that Messer Filippo did not lay hands on him, 'or he will give you a bad morning, and you will have ruined my plan.'

The peddler did as instructed, and when he told Messer Filippo the message, the man, knowing Biondello and easily ruffled, concluded that Biondello meant to mock him. Turning bright red, Messer Filippo exclaimed, 'What "fill this flask" and "his friends"? May God curse you and him!' Then, jumping up, he tried to grab the peddler, but the peddler, who was on his guard, quickly ran off and came back to Ciacco, who had seen everything, reporting Messer Filippo's response. Pleased, Ciacco paid him and wasted no time in finding Biondello, to whom he said, 'Have you been near the Cavicciuoli gallery lately?' 'No,' answered the other. 'Why do you ask?' 'Because,' replied Ciacco, 'I must tell you that Messer Filippo is looking for you. I don't know what he wants.'

'Good,' said Biondello, 'I'll go speak with him.' And off he went, with Ciacco following to see how it would play out.

Meanwhile, Messer Filippo, having failed to catch the peddler, was quite upset and fuming with rage, unable to make any sense of the peddler's words, except that Biondello, at someone's behest, meant to mock him. As he stewed, Biondello appeared, and no sooner did Messer Filippo see him than he rushed at him and struck him hard in the face. 'Alas, sir,' cried Biondello, 'what is this?' Whereupon Messer Filippo, grabbing him by the hair and tearing off his cap, threw it to the ground and, hitting him without mercy, said, 'You scoundrel, you'll soon see what this is! What's this you sent to say to me with your "fill this flask" and your "friends"? Do you think I'm a child, to be mocked in this way?' And with that, he battered Biondello's whole face with his fists, which were as hard as iron, leaving not a hair unruffled; then, rolling him in the mud, he tore all the clothes off his back, until Biondello could not say a word to him or ask why he was being so mistreated. He had indeed heard the mention of "fill this flask" and "friends," but did not know what it meant.

Finally, the bystanders, of whom many had gathered around by this time, with great difficulty dragged Messer Filippo off the battered and bruised Biondello, and told him why the gentleman had done this, blaming Biondello for the message he had sent and telling him he should have known by then that Messer Filippo was not one to be joked with. Biondello, in tears, protested his innocence, declaring he had never sent any message to Messer Filippo for wine, and as soon as he was somewhat recovered, he went home, sick and sorrowful, realizing this must have been Ciacco's doing. When, many days later, his bruises had healed and he began to go out again, he chanced to meet Ciacco, who asked him laughingly, 'Hey, Biondello, how did you like Messer Filippo's wine?' 'Just as you liked Messer Corso's lampreys,' the other replied; and Ciacco said, 'The matter is in your hands from now on. Whenever you go about to feed me as you did, I will give you to drink in the fashion of the other day.' Biondello, knowing full well it was easier to wish Ciacco ill than to put it into practice, begged God for peace between them and was careful not to offend him again.
