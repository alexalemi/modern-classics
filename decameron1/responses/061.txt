THE THIRD STORY

[Day the Sixth]

MADAM NONNA DE' PULCI SHUTS DOWN A BISHOP'S INAPPROPRIATE JEST WITH A QUICK-WITTED REPLY

After Pampinea finished her story and everyone praised both Cisti's witty response and his generosity, the queen decided it was Lauretta's turn to tell the next tale. Lauretta began cheerfully, "Dear ladies, Pampinea and Filomena have both spoken truthfully about our limited worth and the power of clever remarks. To build upon what they've said, I want to remind you that the nature of a quick-witted retort is that it should gently prick the listener, not attack them aggressively. If a remark bites like a dog, it's no longer a clever quip but an insult.

Madam Oretta and Cisti both struck the right balance. However, if a clever remark is used to retaliate, and the response is as harsh as the initial jab, the respondent is not as much to blame as they would be otherwise. We must therefore consider carefully how, when, and with whom we exchange jests. Our bishop here did not heed this advice, and ended up receiving as sharp a reprimand as he intended to give, as I will now show you.

Bishop Antonio d'Orso of Florence was a learned and respectable prelate. One day, a Catalan nobleman named Messer Diego della Ratta, who was the marshal for King Robert, came to the city. Diego was a very handsome man and a notorious flirt. He took a liking to one of the bishop's own relatives, a beautiful young woman married to a rather miserly and stingy husband. Diego offered the husband five hundred gold florins if he would let Diego spend the night with his wife. The husband agreed, and Diego paid him in gilded silver coins that looked like gold florins. This scandal became widely known, but the bishop, being a discreet man, pretended to know nothing about it.

The bishop and the marshal continued to associate together. One St. John's Day, as they were riding side by side watching the women who had come to see the palio race, the bishop spotted a young lady named Madam Nonna de' Pulci. Nonna was the cousin of Messer Alessio Rinucci, and she was a lively, attractive newlywed in the Porta San Piero district. The bishop pointed her out to the marshal and, when they were close to her, he put his hand on the marshal's shoulder and said, "Nonna, what do you think of this fine gentleman? Do you think you could win him over?"

Nonna felt that the bishop's words were a slight against her honor and threatened to tarnish her reputation in front of the many people there. Rather than try to brush it off, she decided to give the bishop a taste of his own medicine with a sharp retort. She quickly replied, "Perhaps, sir, he would not win me over - but in any case, I would require good money for it." 

The marshal and the bishop were both stung by her remark. The marshal was reminded of the deception he had played on the bishop's own relative, while the bishop was offended on behalf of his kinswoman. They rode off in embarrassment and silence, not even looking at each other again that day. So the young lady had been bitten, but she was not forbidden from biting back her attacker with an equally clever reply.
