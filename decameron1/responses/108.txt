The First Story

[Day the Tenth]

A Knight in the Service of the Spanish King, Thinking Himself Poorly Rewarded, Is Shown by the King That This Is Not His Fault, but Rather His Own Misfortune, and Is Then Generously Compensated

"Dear ladies, I must consider it a great honor to be the first to tell a tale of the king's magnificence, which, just as the sun is the glory and adornment of the entire heavens, is the light and luster of every other virtue. I will, therefore, tell you a rather amusing and pleasant little story about this, which I believe can only be useful to recall.

You must know that among the other noble gentlemen who have long graced our city, there was one (perhaps the most worthy of them) named Messer Ruggieri de' Figiovanni. He was both wealthy and high-spirited, and seeing that, given the way of life and customs of Tuscany, he might have little opportunity to showcase his merits if he remained there, he resolved to seek service for a time with Alfonso, the King of Spain, whose reputation for valor surpassed that of any other prince of his time. So, very honorably equipped with arms, horses, and attendants, he went to Alfonso in Spain and was graciously received by him. He took up residence there, living splendidly and performing remarkable feats of arms, and soon made himself known as a man of great worth and valor.

After he had spent quite some time there and had paid close attention to the king's ways, it seemed to him that the king bestowed castles, cities, and baronies rather indiscriminately, giving them to those who were unworthy. And since nothing was given to him, who considered himself worthy of such rewards, he felt that his reputation would be much diminished as a result. Therefore, he determined to depart and asked the king's leave to do so. The king granted him the leave he sought and gave him one of the best and finest mules that had ever been ridden, which was very welcome to Messer Ruggieri for the long journey he had to make. Moreover, the king instructed a discreet servant of his to accompany Messer Ruggieri in such a way that he would not appear to have been sent by the king, and to observe everything Messer Ruggieri said about him, so that the servant could report it back. Accordingly, the servant, lying in wait for Messer Ruggieri's departure, joined him as he left the city, pretending that he too was bound for Italy.

As they rode, Messer Ruggieri spoke with the servant, and when they had gone nearly until the third hour, he said, 'I think it would be best to let our beasts relieve themselves.' They then put up their animals in a stable, and all of them relieved themselves except the mule. As they continued on their way and came to a river, the mule urinated in the stream, which Messer Ruggieri seeing, he said, 'God confound you, beast, for you are made just like the prince who gave you to me!' The servant noted these words, and although he collected many others as he journeyed with Messer Ruggieri that day, he heard him say nothing but praise for the king.

The next morning, as they were mounted and Ruggieri was about to ride toward Tuscany, the servant conveyed the king's command to him, whereupon he immediately turned back. When he arrived at the court, the king, learning what he had said about the mule, summoned him and, receiving him with a cheerful manner, asked him why he had likened the mule to him, or rather why he had likened him to the mule. 'My lord,' replied Ruggieri frankly, 'I likened her to you because, just as you give where it is unwarranted and do not give where it is warranted, so she relieved herself where it was unwarranted, but not where it was warranted.' Then the king said, 'Messer Ruggieri, if I have not given to you as I have to many who are of no account compared to you, it is not because I did not know you to be a most valiant knight and worthy of great gifts. Rather, it is your own fortune that has failed to allow me to reward you according to your merits, and not I. And to prove that I speak the truth, I will demonstrate it to you clearly.'

'My lord,' replied Ruggieri, 'I was not distressed because I have received no largesse from you, for I do not desire to be richer than I am, but because you have in no way borne witness to my merit. Nevertheless, I accept your excuse as good and honorable, and I am ready to see what it shall please you to show me, although I believe you without proof.' The king then took him into a great hall of his, where, as he had ordered beforehand, there were two large locked chests, and said to him, in the presence of many, 'Messer Ruggieri, in one of these chests is my crown, the royal scepter and orb, together with many fine girdles, brooches, and rings of mine, and in short, all my most precious jewels; and the other is full of earth. Take one, and that which you take shall be yours; and you will thus see which of the two has been ungrateful to your worth, whether I or your ill fortune.'

Messer Ruggieri, seeing that it was the king's pleasure, took one of the chests, which, being opened by Alfonso's command, was found to be the one filled with earth. Whereupon the king, laughing, said, 'Now you can see, Messer Ruggieri, that what I tell you of your fortune is true; but certainly your worth deserves that I should oppose its power. I know you have no desire to become a Spaniard, and therefore I will bestow upon you neither castle nor city in these parts; but this chest, of which fortune deprived you, I will, in spite of her, make yours, so that you may carry it off to your own country and justly glorify yourself of your worth in the sight of your countrymen by the witness of my gifts.' Messer Ruggieri accordingly took the chest and, having rendered the king the thanks that befitted such a gift, joyfully returned with it to Tuscany."
