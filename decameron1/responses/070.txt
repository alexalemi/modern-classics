THE FIRST STORY

[Day the Seventh]

GIANNI LOTTERINGHI HEARS A KNOCK AT HIS DOOR AT NIGHT AND WAKES HIS WIFE, WHO MAKES HIM BELIEVE IT IS A GHOST; WHEREUPON THEY GO TO EXORCISE IT WITH A CERTAIN PRAYER AND THE KNOCKING STOPS

"My dear ladies, it would have pleased me greatly to allow someone else to begin this wonderful tale we are about to tell. But since it is your wish that I set the example for the rest of you, I will gladly do so. And I will try to share something that may be useful to you in the future, for if you are as fearful as I am, especially of ghosts (though what they may be, God only knows, for I have never found a woman who truly understands them, though all are equally terrified of them), you may learn a holy and effective prayer to banish them, should they come to you.

There once lived in Florence, in the quarter of San Brancazio, a wool-comber named Gianni Lotteringhi. He was more successful in his craft than wise in other matters, for, being rather simple-minded, he was often made the captain of the Laudsingers of Santa Maria Novella and had the governance of their confraternity, and he held various other minor offices, which he greatly valued. This came about because, being a man of means, he gave many generous donations to the clergy, who, receiving from him often a pair of stockings, a robe, or a scapular, taught him a wealth of pious prayers and gave him the Our Father in the common tongue, the Song of Saint Alexis, the Lamentations of Saint Bernard, the Canticles of Lady Matilda, and other such trifles, all of which he held dear and kept diligently for the health of his soul.

Now, he had a very fair and lovely wife named Mistress Tessa, the daughter of Mannuccio dalla Cuculia, who was extremely discreet and well-advised. Knowing her husband's simplicity and being enamored of Federigo di Neri Pegolotti, a lively and handsome young man, and he of her, she arranged with a maidservant that he should come speak with her at a lovely country house that her husband owned in Camerata, where she stayed all summer and where Gianni would sometimes come to dine and sleep, returning in the morning to his shop and occasionally to his Laudsingers.

Federigo, who desired this above all else, took the opportunity when appointed and went there towards evening, and Gianni not coming that night, he supped and spent the night in great ease and delight with the lady, who, being in his arms, taught him that night a good half dozen of her husband's lauds. Then, neither she nor Federigo intending this to be the first and last time, they made a plan together, so that it would not be necessary to send the maid for him each time. This was that every day, as he came and went to and from a place he had a little farther on, he should keep an eye on a vineyard adjoining the house, where he would see a donkey's skull set up on one of the vine poles. If the muzzle was turned towards Florence, he should without fail and with complete assurance come to her that evening after dark; and if he found the door shut, he should knock softly three times and she would open it for him. But if the muzzle was turned towards Fiesole, he should not come, for that would mean Gianni was there; and following this plan, they met many times.

But once, among other times, it happened that Federigo was to sup with Mistress Tessa, and she had had two fat capons cooked, when Gianni, who was not expected there that night, came very late, which greatly vexed the lady. Having supped with her husband on a piece of salted pork that she had had boiled separately, she had the maid wrap the two boiled capons in a clean napkin and carry them, along with a good quantity of fresh eggs and a flask of good wine, into a garden she had, where she was wont to sup sometimes with her lover, instructing her to lay them at the foot of a peach tree that grew beside a lawn there. But she was so troubled and annoyed that she forgot to tell the maid to wait until Federigo came and inform him that Gianni was there and he should take the provisions from the garden. So she and Gianni went to bed, and the maid did likewise, and it was not long before Federigo came to the door and knocked softly once. The door was so near the bedchamber that Gianni heard it immediately, as did the lady; but she pretended to be asleep so her husband would have no suspicion of her. After waiting a little, Federigo knocked a second time, whereupon Gianni, marveling, nudged his wife somewhat and said, 'Tessa, do you hear what I hear? It seems to me there is a knocking at our door.'

The lady, who had heard it much better than he, pretended to awake and said, 'Eh? What do you say?' 'I say,' answered Gianni, 'that it seems to me there is a knocking at our door.' 'Knocking!' cried she. 'Alas, my Gianni, don't you know what it is? It is a ghost, which has terrified me greatly these past few nights, so that whenever I hear it, I put my head under the covers and dare not bring it out again until it is broad daylight.' Gianni said, 'Come now, wife, have no fear, if that is the case; for I said the "Te Lucis" and the "Intemerata" and certain other pious prayers before we went to bed, and crossed the bed from side to side, in the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost, so that we have no need to fear, for whatever power it may have, it cannot harm us.'

The lady, fearing that Federigo might suspect something and be angered with her, decided at all costs to get up and let him know that Gianni was there; therefore, she said to her husband, 'That may all be very well, but for my part, I shall never feel safe or secure unless we exorcise it, since you are here.' 'And how is it to be exorcised?' he asked; and she said, 'I know very well how to exorcise it; for the other day, when I went to the Pardon at Fiesole, a certain hermit (the holiest of creatures, my Gianni, God only knows how holy she is) seeing me thus fearful, taught me a pious and effective prayer and told me that she had tried it several times before becoming a recluse, and that it had always worked for her. God knows I would never have dared to try it alone; but now that you are here, I would have us go and exorcise the ghost.'

Gianni answered that he was willing, and accordingly they both got up and went softly to the door, behind which Federigo, who now began to suspect something, was still waiting. When they reached the door, the lady said to Gianni, 'You must spit when I tell you.' And he answered, 'Very well.' Then she began the conjuration and said, 'Ghost, ghost that goes by night, with tail upright[1] you came to us; now begone with tail upright. Go into the garden to the foot of the great peach tree; there you shall find an anointed twice-anointed one[2] and a hundred turds of my sitting hen[3]; set your mouth to the flask and be gone again, and do no harm to my Gianni nor to me.' Then to her husband, 'Spit, Gianni,' she said, and he spat. Federigo, who heard all this from outside and was now free of jealousy, had, for all his vexation, so great a desire to laugh that he was like to burst, and when Gianni spat, he said under his breath, 'May it be your teeth!'

The lady, having thrice conjured the ghost in this manner, returned to bed with her husband, while Federigo, who had not supped, expecting to sup with her, and had very well understood the words of the conjuration, went to the garden and finding the capons and wine and eggs at the foot of the great peach tree, carried them off to his house and there supped at his ease; and afterwards, when he next met the lady, he had a hearty laugh with her about the said conjuration. Some say indeed that the lady had actually turned the donkey's skull towards Fiesole, but that a farmer, passing through the vineyard, had given it a blow with a stick and caused it to spin round, so that it became turned towards Florence, wherefore Federigo, thinking himself summoned, had come thither, and that the lady had made the conjuration in this way: 'Ghost, ghost, be gone in God's name; for it was not I who turned the donkey's head; but another it was, God put him to shame! and I am here with my Gianni in bed'; whereupon he went away and remained without supper or lodging. But a very ancient neighbor of mine tells me that, according to what she heard in her youth, both the one and the other were true; but that the latter happened, not to Gianni Lotteringhi, but to one Gianni di Nello, who lived at Porta San Piero and was no less exquisite a simpleton than the other. Wherefore, dear ladies, it remains at your election to take whichever of the two prayers most pleases you, or even both if you will. They have great virtue in such cases, as you have seen in the story you have heard; learn them, therefore, by heart, and they may yet avail you.

[Footnote 1: _i.e._ with erect penis.]
[Footnote 2: _i.e._ a well-fattened capon.]
[Footnote 3: _i.e._ eggs.]
