THE TENTH STORY

[Day the Second]

PAGANINO OF MONACO KIDNAPS THE WIFE OF MESSER RICCIARDO DI CHINZICA, WHO, LEARNING WHERE SHE IS, GOES THERE AND MAKES FRIENDS WITH PAGANINO, ASKING HIM TO RETURN HER. PAGANINO AGREES IF SHE WISHES, BUT SHE REFUSES TO RETURN WITH HIM, AND MESSER RICCIARDO DIES, AND SHE BECOMES PAGANINO'S WIFE

The entire honorable company highly praised the story told by their queen, especially Dioneo, who was the only one left to tell a tale that day. After showering the preceding tale with many praises, Dioneo said, "Dear ladies, one part of the queen's story has caused me to change the story I had in mind and instead tell you another - and that is the foolishness of Bernabo (even though good came of it for him) and of all others who believe what he pretended to believe, and who, while traveling the world, diverting themselves now with this woman and now with that, imagine that the ladies left at home remain with their hands in their girdles, as if we who are born and raised among them do not know what they desire.

In telling you this story, I shall at once show you how great is the folly of these men and how even greater is that of those who, thinking themselves more powerful than nature itself, believe that through cunning arguments they can achieve what is beyond their ability and try to bring others to what they themselves are, when the constitution of those on whom they practice does not allow it.

There was, then, in Pisa a judge, named Messer Ricciardo di Chinzica, who was more gifted with wit than with physical strength. Thinking perhaps to satisfy a wife in the same way that he managed his studies, and being very wealthy, he sought with great diligence to have a beautiful and young lady as his wife. But had he known how to advise himself as he advised others, he should have avoided both the one and the other. The thing came to pass as he wished, for Messer Lotto Gualandi gave him his daughter Bartolomea in marriage, one of the fairest and most beautiful young ladies of Pisa, although there are few there who are not very charming to look upon. 

The judge accordingly brought her home with the utmost pomp and, having held a magnificent wedding, managed on the first night to perform the consummation of the marriage, but came within an ace of making a stalemate of it. Thereafter, being now a better judge of his own powers than he was before, he began teaching his wife a calendar fit for children learning to read, which had perhaps been made in Ravenna, for according to what he told her, there was no day in the year that was not sacred not to one saint only, but to many, in reverence of whom he showed by various reasons that man and wife should abstain from carnal conversation. To these he added fast days and Ember days and the vigils of the Apostles and of a thousand other saints and Fridays and Saturdays and Sundays and all Lent and certain seasons of the moon and a multitude of other exceptions, perhaps believing that he should keep holiday with women in bed just as he did sometimes while pleading in the courts of civil law.

This practice (to the great displeasure of the lady, whom he handled perhaps once a month, and hardly that) he followed for a long time, still keeping close watch over her, lest by chance someone else should teach her to know working days, just as he had taught her holidays. Things standing thus, it chanced that, the heat being great and Messer Ricciardo having a mind to go for pleasure to a very beautiful country estate he had near Monte Nero, and there remain some days to take the air, he betook himself thither, taking with him his fair lady. There sojourning, to give her some diversion, he caused them to go fishing one day, and they went out to sea in two boats, he in one with the fishermen, and she in another with other ladies. The sport luring them on, they drifted some miles out to sea, almost without perceiving it, and whilst they were intent upon their diversion, there came up suddenly a galley belonging to Paganino da Mare, a famous corsair of those days. He, seeing the boats, made for them, nor could they flee so fast but he overtook that in which were the women and, seeing therein the judge's fair lady, he carried her aboard the galley, in full sight of Messer Ricciardo, who had now come to land, and made off without caring for aught else. When my lord judge, who was so jealous that he doubted even the air, saw this, it is needless to ask if he was distressed; and in vain, both at Pisa and elsewhere, did he complain of the villainy of the corsairs, for he knew not who had taken his wife from him nor whither he had carried her. As for Paganino, finding her so fair, he deemed himself fortunate and, having no wife, resolved to keep her for himself. Accordingly, seeing her weeping sore, he studied to comfort her with soft words till nightfall, when, his calendar having dropped from his girdle and saints' days and holidays gone clean out of his head, he began to comfort her with deeds, thinking that words had availed little by day; and after such a fashion did he console her that, ere they came to Monaco, the judge and his ordinances had altogether escaped her mind and she began to lead the merriest of lives with Paganino. The latter carried her to Monaco and there, over and above the comforts with which he plied her night and day, he treated her honorably as his wife.

After a while, it came to Messer Ricciardo's ears where his wife was, and he, being consumed with the most ardent desire to have her again and bethinking himself that none other might thoroughly suffice to do what was needful to that end, resolved to go thither himself, determined to spend any quantity of money for her ransom. Accordingly, he set out by sea and, coming to Monaco, both saw and was seen by the lady, who told it to Paganino that same evening and acquainted him with her intent. The next morning, Messer Ricciardo, seeing Paganino, approached him and quickly formed a great familiarity and friendship with him, while the other feigned not to know him and waited to see what he aimed at. Accordingly, when it seemed to him time, Messer Ricciardo disclosed to him, as best and most courteously he knew, the reason for his coming and prayed him take what he pleased and restore him the lady. To which Paganino made answer with a cheerful countenance, 'Sir, you are welcome, and to answer you briefly, I say thus; it is true I have a young lady in my house, if she be your wife or another's I know not, for I know you not nor indeed her, save in so much as she has abided awhile with me. If you be, as you say, her husband, I will, since you seem to me a courteous gentleman, take you to her, and I am assured that she will know you very well. If she says it is as you claim and is willing to go with you, you shall, for the sake of your courtesy, give me what you yourself will to her ransom; but, if it be not so, you would do ill to seek to take her from me, for I am a young man and can entertain a woman as well as another, and especially such a one as she, who is the most pleasing I ever saw.'

Quoth Messer Ricciardo, 'For certain she is my wife, and if you bring me where she is, you shall soon see it; for she will immediately throw herself into my arms.' 'Then,' said Paganino, 'let us be going.' Accordingly, they betook themselves to the corsair's house, where he brought the judge into a hall of his and called the lady, who came forth of a chamber, all dressed and adorned, and approached where they were, but greeted Messer Ricciardo no otherwise than she would any other stranger who might have come home with Paganino. The judge, who expected to have been received by her with the utmost joy, marveled greatly at this and began saying to himself, 'Perchance the distress and long grief I have suffered, since I lost her, have so changed me that she knows me not.' Wherefore he said to her, 'Wife, it has cost me dear to take you fishing, for never was grief felt like that which I have suffered since I lost you, and now it seems to me you do not know me, so distantly do you greet me. Do you not see that I am your own Messer Ricciardo, come hither to pay what this gentleman, in whose house we are, shall require for your ransom and to take you away? And he, of his favor, restoreth you to me for what I will.' The lady turned to him and said, smiling somewhat, 'Speak you to me, sir? Look you mistake me not, for, for my part, I do not remember ever to have seen you.'

Quoth Ricciardo, 'Consider me well; if you will but recollect yourself, you will see that I am your own Ricciardo di Chinzica.' 'Sir,' answered the lady, 'you will pardon me; it may not be as seemly a thing as you imagine for me to look much on you. Nevertheless, I have seen enough of you to know that I never before set eyes on you.' Ricciardo, concluding that she did this for fear of Paganino and chose not to confess to knowing him in the latter's presence, besought him of his favor that he might speak with her in a room alone. Paganino replied that he would well, so but he would not kiss her against her will, and bade the lady go with him into a chamber and there hear what he had to say and answer him as it should please her. Accordingly, the lady and Messer Ricciardo went into a room apart, and as soon as they were seated, the latter began to say, 'Alas, heart of my body, sweet my soul and my hope, do you not know your Ricciardo, who loves you more than himself? How can this be? Am I so changed? I pray you, fair one, do but look on me a little.'

The lady began to laugh and, without letting him say more, replied, 'You may be assured that I know very well you are Messer Ricciardo di Chinzica, my husband; but, what time I was with you, you showed that you knew me very ill, for you should have had the sense to see that I was young and lively and playful and should consequently have known what is needful to young ladies, besides clothes and food, although for modesty they name it not. How you performed this, you know. If the study of the law was more pleasing to you than your wife, you should not have taken her, although it never seemed to me that you were a judge; nay, you appeared to me rather a common crier of saints' days and sacraments and fasts and vigils, so well you knew them. And I tell you this, that, had you allowed the laborers who till your lands to keep as many holidays as you allowed him who had the tilling of my poor little field, you would never have reaped the least grain of corn. However, as God, having compassion on my youth, hath willed it, I have happened on yonder man, with whom I abide in this chamber, wherein it is unknown what manner of thing is a holiday (I speak of those holidays which you, more assiduous in the service of God than in that of the ladies, did so diligently celebrate) nor ever yet entered in at this door Saturday nor Friday nor vigil nor Ember day nor Lent, that is so long; nay, here we toil day and night and thump our wool; and this very night after matins, I know right well how the thing went, once he was up. Wherefore I mean to abide with him and work; whilst I am young, and leave saints' days and jubilees and fasts for my keeping when I am old; so get you gone about your business as quickly as you may, good luck go with you, and keep as many holidays as you please, without me.'

Messer Ricciardo, hearing these words, was distressed beyond endurance and said, when he saw she had made an end of speaking, 'Alas, sweet my soul, what is this you say? Have you no regard for your kinfolk's honor and your own? Will you rather abide here for this man's mistress and in mortal sin than at Pisa as my wife? He, when he is weary of you, will turn you away to your own exceeding reproach, whilst I will still hold you dear and still (even though I willed it not) you shall be mistress of my house. Will you for the sake of a lewd and disorderly appetite, forsake your honor and me, who love you more than my life? For God's sake, dear my hope, speak no more thus, but consent to come with me; henceforth, since I know your desire, I will strive to satisfy it; wherefore, sweet my treasure, change counsel and come away with me, who have never known joy since you were taken from me.'

Whereto answered the lady, 'I have no mind that any, now that it availeth not, should be more tender of my honor than I myself; would my kinfolk had had regard thereto, when they gave me to you! But, as they then had no care for my honor, I am under no present concern to be careful of theirs; and if I am herein in mortal sin, I shall abide though it be in pestilent sin. And let me tell you that here methinks I am Paganino's wife, whereas at Pisa I seemed to be your mistress, seeing that there, by reason of the moon and geometrical figures, planets must needs concur to couple betwixt you and me, whereas here Paganino holdeth me all night in his arms and straineth me and biteth me, and how he serveth me, let God tell you for me. You say forsooth you will strive; to what? To do it in three attempts and cause it stand by dint of cudgeling? I warrant me you are grown a doughty cavalier since I saw you last! Begone and strive to live, for methinketh indeed you do but sojourn here below upon sufferance, so peaked and out of breath you show to me. And yet more I tell you, that, should he leave me (albeit methinketh he is nowise inclined thereto, so I choose to stay,) I purpose not therefor ever to return to you, of whom squeeze you as I might, there were no making a porringer of sauce; for that I abode with you once to my grievous hurt and loss, wherefore in such a case I should seek my advantage elsewhere. Nay, once again I tell you, here be neither saints' days nor vigils; wherefore here I mean to abide; so get you gone in God's name as quickly as you may, or I will cry out that you would fain force me.'

Messer Ricciardo, seeing himself in ill case and now recognizing his folly in taking a young wife, when he himself was spent, went forth the chamber saddened and woebegone, and spoke many words to Paganino that availed not a jot. Ultimately, leaving the lady, he returned to Pisa, without having accomplished anything, and there, out of chagrin, fell into such dotage that, as he went about Pisa, to whoso greeted him or asked him of anything, he answered nothing but 'The ill place will have no holidays;' and there, no great while after, he died. Paganino, hearing this and knowing the love the lady bore himself, espoused her as his lawful wife, and thereafter, without ever observing saints' day or vigil or keeping Lent, they worked as long as their legs would carry them and led a jolly life of it. Wherefore, dear ladies, it seems to me Bernabo, in his dispute with Ambrogiuolo, rode the she-goat down the steep.
