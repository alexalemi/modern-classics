THE NINTH STORY

[Day the Sixth]

GUIDO CAVALCANTI COURTEOUSLY REBUKES SOME FLORENTINE GENTLEMEN WHO HAD CAUGHT HIM UNAWARES

The queen, seeing that Emilia had finished her story and that it was now her turn to speak, began, "Although, lively ladies, you have already taken from me at least two stories that I had planned to tell, I still have one left to share, the end of which contains a remark so fitting that perhaps none other of equal pertinence has yet been cited to us.

You should know that in our city, in times past, there were many fine and commendable customs, none of which remain today, thanks to the greed that has grown with wealth and banished them all. Among these was the practice that the gentlemen of the various districts of Florence would gather together in different places around the city and form companies of a certain number, carefully admitting only those who could suitably bear the expense. Each day, one member would host the whole company. At these gatherings, they often entertained both visiting gentlemen and those from the city; and likewise, at least once a year, they would dress alike and process through the city on the most notable days, and sometimes hold tournaments, especially on major holidays or when the city received joyful news of a victory or the like.

Among these companies was one led by Messer Betto Brunelleschi, and he and his companions had tried their utmost to draw Guido, son of Messer Cavalcante de' Cavalcanti, into their group, and not without reason. For besides being one of the best logicians in the world and an excellent natural philosopher (which things they cared little about), Guido was very lively and well-mannered, an excellent speaker, and knew better than anyone how to do everything that a gentleman should, all the more so because he was very wealthy and knew how to entertain anyone he deemed worthy of honor. But Messer Betto had never been able to win him over and have him join, and he and his companions believed this was because Guido, being often engaged in abstract contemplations, became much distracted from mankind; and since he inclined somewhat to the views of the Epicureans, it was rumored among the common folk that his contemplations consisted only of trying to discover whether God might not exist.

It happened one day that Guido set out from Orto San Michele and came by way of the Corso degli Ademari, which was often his path, to San Giovanni. There were at that time various large marble tombs around the church (which are now at Santa Reparata) and many others. As Guido was between the porphyry columns and the tombs in question, with the church door closed, Messer Betto and his company, riding horses, came along the Piazza di Santa Reparata and spotted him among the tombs. They said, 'Let us go tease him.' Spurring their horses, they charged down upon him in sport, coming upon him before he was aware of them, and said, 'Guido, you refuse to be part of our company; but tell us, when you have discovered that God does not exist, what will you have accomplished?'

Guido, seeing himself surrounded by them, answered promptly, 'Gentlemen, you may say what you will to me in your own house.' Then, placing his hand on one of the large tombs and being very agile, he took a leap and landed on the other side, escaping from them.

The gentlemen were left looking at one another, saying that Guido was a madman and that his response amounted to nothing, since they were no more in their own house there than all the other citizens, nor Guido less than any of them. But Messer Betto turned to them and said, 'It is you who are the madmen if you have not understood him. He has courteously and in a few words given us the sharpest rebuke in the world; for, if you consider it rightly, these tombs are the houses of the dead, where they are laid and abide, and these, he says, are our house, meaning thereby to show us that we and other foolish and unlettered men are, compared to him and other learned men, worse than dead people; and therefore, being here, we are in our own house.' Then each understood what Guido had meant to say and was ashamed, nor did they ever again tease him, but henceforth held Messer Betto to be a gentleman of subtle wit and understanding."
