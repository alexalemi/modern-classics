The Fourth Story

[Day the Sixth]

CHICHIBIO, COOK TO CURRADO GIANFIGLIAZZI, WITH A QUICK RESPONSE
SAVES HIMSELF FROM PUNISHMENT THREATENED BY HIS MASTER

After Lauretta finished speaking and Nonna had been greatly praised by all, the queen instructed Neifile to continue the storytelling. Neifile said, "Lovely ladies, although a quick wit often provides people with prompt and useful words that are appropriate to the situation, sometimes fortune comes to the aid of the fearful and suddenly puts into their mouths answers that the speaker could never have devised on purpose. I will show you this with my story.

Currado Gianfigliazzi, as you all know, has long been a noble and generous citizen of our city who has led a knight's life, taking pleasure in hawks and hounds, even when not occupied with weightier matters. One day, he used his falcon to bring down a crane and, finding it young and fat, he sent it to his talented Venetian cook, Chichibio, instructing him to roast it and prepare it well for supper.

Chichibio, who was quite the simpleton, trussed the crane and set it to cook. As it was nearly done and giving off a delicious aroma, a local woman named Brunetta, with whom Chichibio was deeply infatuated, happened to enter the kitchen. Smelling the crane and seeing it, she immediately begged Chichibio to give her a thigh. He responded playfully, singing, "You shall not have it from me, Mistress Brunetta, you shall not have it from me." This vexed her, and she said, "By God, if you don't give it to me, you'll never receive anything pleasing from me again." After many words between them, Chichibio, not wanting to anger his sweetheart, cut off one of the crane's thighs and gave it to her.

When the bird was later served to Messer Currado and his guests, lacking a thigh, Currado was surprised and called Chichibio, asking what had become of the other thigh. The lying Venetian answered without hesitation, "Sir, cranes have only one thigh and one leg." "What the devil?" cried Currado angrily. "They have only one thigh and one leg? Haven't I seen cranes before?" "Sir," replied Chichibio, "it is as I tell you, and if you'd like, I can show you a live one to prove it."

Currado, out of consideration for his guests, chose not to argue the matter further, but said, "Since you claim you can show me a live one with only one thigh and leg, which I have never seen or heard of, I want to see it tomorrow morning. If it's not as you say, I swear by Christ's body that I will have you served in such a way that you'll remember my name with sorrow as long as you live." That ended the discussion for the night. 

The next morning, Currado, his anger still not abated, had the horses brought and made Chichibio ride with him to a place where cranes could be seen at dawn. Chichibio, fearing for his safety, rode after Currado, looking everywhere for cranes standing on one leg, hoping to somehow make good on his lie. As they approached the river, he spotted a dozen cranes standing on one leg, as they do when they sleep. He quickly pointed them out to Currado, saying, "Now, sir, you can see that I told you the truth last night - cranes have only one thigh and one leg." 

Currado replied, "Wait, and I'll show you they have two," and as he approached the cranes, he shouted, "Ho! Ho!" At this, the cranes put down their other legs and took flight. Currado then said to Chichibio, "How about now, you deceitful rascal? Do you still think they have only one leg?" Chichibio, thoroughly confused and unsure which way was up, answered, "Yes, sir, but you didn't shout 'Ho! Ho!' to the crane last night. If you had, it would have put out its other thigh and leg, just like those did." 

This quick and amusing response so delighted Currado that his anger turned to mirth and laughter. He said, "Chichibio, you're right; I should have done that." And so, with his prompt and comical answer, Chichibio avoided misfortune and made peace with his master."
