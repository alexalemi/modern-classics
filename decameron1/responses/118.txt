Things being in this state with him, it happened one day that, as Saladin was discussing his hawks with him, Messer Torello happened to smile and make a movement with his mouth, which the former had noted well while he was in his house in Pavia. This brought the gentleman to his mind, and looking steadfastly upon him, it seemed to him that it was the man himself; wherefore, leaving the former discussion, 'Listen, Christian,' said he, 'What country are you from in the West?' 'My lord,' replied Torello, 'I am a Lombard from a city called Pavia, a poor man and of humble condition.'

Saladin, hearing this, was in a manner certain of the truth of his suspicion and said joyfully to himself, 'God has granted me an opportunity to show this man how grateful his courtesy was to me.' Accordingly, without saying anything further, he had all his clothes laid out in a chamber and, taking him there, said to him, 'Look, Christian, if there is any among these gowns that you have ever seen.' Torello looked and saw those which his lady had given Saladin; but, nevertheless, not conceiving that they could possibly be the same, he answered, 'My lord, I know none of them; although, in truth, these two do resemble certain gowns with which I, together with three merchants who came to my house, was previously clad.'

Thereupon Saladin, unable to contain himself further, embraced him tenderly, saying, 'You are Messer Torello d'Istria and I am one of the three merchants to whom your lady gave these gowns; and now is the time come to show you what manner of merchant I am, even as I told you, at my parting from you, might chance to befall.' Messer Torello, hearing this, was at once rejoiced and ashamed; rejoiced to have had such a guest and ashamed that it seemed to him he had entertained him but poorly. Then said Saladin, 'Messer Torello, since God has sent you here to me, henceforth consider that not I, but you are master here.' Accordingly, after they had greatly rejoiced in each other, he clad him in royal apparel and, carrying him into the presence of all his chief barons, commanded, after saying many things in praise of his worth, that he should be honored as himself by all who held his favor dear, which was thenceforth done by all, but above all by the two gentlemen who had been Saladin's companions in his house.

The sudden height of glory to which Messer Torello thus found himself advanced put his Lombard affairs somewhat out of his mind, more by token that he had good reason to hope that his letters were by this come to his uncle's hands. Now there had died and been buried in the camp or rather in the host of the Christians, the day they were taken by Saladin, a Provençal gentleman of little account, by name Messer Torello de Dignes, by reason whereof, Messer Torello d'Istria being renowned throughout the army for his magnificence, whosoever heard say, 'Messer Torello is dead,' believed it of Messer Torello d'Istria, not of him of Dignes. The hazard of the capture that ensued thereupon suffered not those who had been thus misled to be undeceived; wherefore many Italians returned with this news, amongst whom were some who scrupled not to affirm that they had seen him dead and had been at the burial. This, coming to be known of his wife and family, was the cause of grievous and inexpressible sorrow, not only to them, but to all who had known him.
