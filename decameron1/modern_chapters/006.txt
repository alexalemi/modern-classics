THE FOURTH STORY

[Day the First]

A MONK, HAVING FALLEN INTO A SIN DESERVING OF VERY GRIEVOUS PUNISHMENT, ADROITLY REPROACHING THE SAME FAULT TO HIS ABBOT, QUITTETH HIMSELF OF THE PENALTY

Filomena had finished her story, and Dioneo, who sat next to her, knowing it was his turn to speak according to the established order, began without waiting for further instruction from the queen:

"Charming ladies, if I have understood your intentions correctly, we are here to entertain ourselves with storytelling. Therefore, as long as it is not contrary to our purpose, I believe each of us is permitted (as our queen mentioned earlier) to tell whatever story we think will provide the most amusement. 

Having heard how Jehannot de Chevigné's good counsel saved Abraham's soul and how Melchizedek's quick thinking protected his riches from Saladin's schemes, I intend to briefly recount how a monk cleverly freed himself from a very severe punishment.

There was once a monastery in Lunigiana, a region not far from here, that was once more abundant in sanctity and monks than it is today. Among them was a young monk whose vigor and lustiness could not be subdued by fasts or vigils. One day, when the other monks were sleeping at around noon, he was walking alone around the convent, which stood in a very secluded place. There he spotted a very attractive young woman, likely a farmer's daughter, gathering herbs in the fields. As soon as he saw her, he was seized by carnal desire. Approaching her, he struck up a conversation that eventually led them both to his cell, unseen by anyone. But as he indulged in his ardor with her less cautiously than was prudent, it happened that the abbot awoke from his sleep and softly passed by the monk's cell, hearing the noise the two were making together.

The abbot quietly came to the door to listen, clearly recognizing a woman's voice. At first, he was inclined to have the door opened, but then he reconsidered and returned to his chambers to await the monk's departure. The monk, fully absorbed in the girl and his great pleasure in her company, was nevertheless on guard. Sensing some rustling in the dormitory, he peeked through a crack and clearly saw the abbot listening at the door. Realizing the abbot must have discovered the girl's presence in his cell and knowing he would face severe punishment, the monk was greatly distressed. However, without betraying any of his concern to the girl, he quickly considered various options, seeking a way to escape, and soon hit upon an ingenious plan.

Pretending he had spent enough time with the damsel, he said to her, 'I must go find a way for you to leave here unseen; wait quietly until I return.' Then, locking the cell door on her, he went straight to the abbot's chamber and, presenting him with the key as each monk did when going out, said, 'Sir, I was unable to finish bringing in all the firewood I had cut this morning; with your permission, I will go to the woods now to fetch the rest.' The abbot, assuming the monk was unaware of having been seen, was glad to have this opportunity to learn more about the offense committed and readily gave him leave to go.

As soon as the monk had left, the abbot began to consider whether he should open the cell in front of all the other monks, so they could witness the default and have no reason to murmur when he punished the offender, or if he should first speak to the girl herself to learn how the matter had occurred. Thinking that she might be the wife or daughter of someone he would not want to shame in front of the whole monastery, he decided to see her first before deciding what to do. So he went to the cell, opened it, and entered, closing the door behind him.

The girl, seeing the abbot enter, was terrified and began to weep for fear of scandal. But the old abbot, upon catching sight of her youth and beauty, suddenly felt the pangs of carnal desire no less than his young monk had, and he said to himself, 'Why should I not take a little pleasure, when I may, especially since displeasure and trouble are always at hand whenever I wish for them? This is a lovely girl, and she is unknown to anyone here. If I can make her do my will, I see no reason why I should not. Who will ever know? No one will ever find out, and a sin that is kept hidden is half forgiven. Perhaps this chance will never come again. I consider it great wisdom to make use of a good thing when the Lord God sends it our way.'

Thus having completely changed the purpose with which he had come, he approached the girl and began gently comforting her, asking her not to weep. From one thing to another, he revealed his desire to her. The girl, who was neither made of iron nor diamond, readily yielded to the abbot's pleasure. After embracing and kissing her repeatedly, he lay down on the monk's bed. But out of regard for the gravity of his office and the girl's tender age, fearful of overburdening her, he did not mount her, but instead placed her upon himself and there enjoyed her company for a good while.

Meanwhile, the monk, who had only pretended to go to the woods and had hidden himself in the dormitory, was completely reassured when he saw the abbot enter his cell alone, having no doubt that his plan would succeed. Emerging from his hiding place, he stealthily made his way to a crack through which he both heard and saw all that the abbot said and did. When the abbot seemed to have tarried long enough with the girl, he locked her in the cell and returned to his own chamber. After a while, hearing the monk returning, he thought to severely rebuke him and have him thrown in prison, so he could alone possess the prey he had obtained. Therefore, sending for the monk, he harshly reprimanded him and ordered him to be imprisoned.

The monk readily replied, 'Sir, I have not yet been a monk long enough under the order of St. Benedict to have learned every particular, and you had not yet shown me that monks should make use of women as a means of mortification, as with fasts and vigils. But now that you have shown me, I promise you that, if you pardon me this fault, I will never offend in this way again, and will do as I have seen you do.' The abbot, who was a quick-witted man, readily understood that the monk not only knew more than he, but had seen what he had done. Therefore, his conscience pricking him for his own default, he was ashamed to inflict on the monk a punishment that he himself had deserved. So he pardoned the monk and, charging him to keep silent about what he had seen, they secretly sent the girl away, and it is believed they brought her back there more than once afterward."
