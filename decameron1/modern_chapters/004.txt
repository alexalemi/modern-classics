THE SECOND STORY 

[Day the First]

ABRAHAM THE JEW, AT THE INSTIGATION OF JEHANNOT DE CHEVIGNÉ, 
GOES TO THE COURT OF ROME AND, SEEING THE DEPRAVITY OF THE
CLERGY, RETURNS TO PARIS AND THERE BECOMES A CHRISTIAN

Pamfilo's story amused and impressed the ladies, and when it ended, the queen instructed Neifile, who sat next to him, to continue the storytelling. Neifile, who was known for both her beauty and her kind manners, happily agreed and began:

"Pamfilo has shown us that God's kindness looks past our errors when they stem from things beyond our control. In my story, I aim to demonstrate how this same kindness, patiently enduring the failings of those who are especially bound to bear true witness of it through both words and deeds, yet practice the opposite, provides us with incontrovertible proof of itself. This is so that we may pursue with steadfast minds that which we believe.

As I have been told, gracious ladies, there was once in Paris a great and very honest and upright merchant named Jehannot de Chevigné, who had a thriving business in silks and fabrics. He had a particular friendship with a very wealthy Jewish merchant named Abraham, who was also an honest and trustworthy man. Seeing Abraham's worth and loyalty, Jehannot was deeply troubled that the soul of such a worthy, discreet, and good man should be lost for lack of faith. He therefore began earnestly entreating Abraham to abandon the errors of the Jewish faith and embrace the Christian truth, which he could see was still growing and prospering as a holy and good religion, while the Jewish faith was manifestly waning and dwindling away.

The Jew responded that he held no faith as holy or good except the Jewish, that he had been born into it, and that he meant to live and die in it, and that nothing would ever make him leave it. Jehannot, however, did not desist, but some days later returned to the attack with similar words, crudely explaining (as merchants are generally better able) the reasons why our religion is better than the Jewish. And although the Jew was a master of their law, whether it was the great friendship he bore Jehannot that moved him or perhaps the words that the Holy Spirit put into the good simple man's mouth, the latter's arguments began to greatly please him. Yet, persisting in his own belief, he would not allow himself to be converted.

As he remained obstinate, so Jehannot never stopped pressing him, until at last the Jew, overcome by such constant insistence, said, 'Look, Jehannot, you want me to become a Christian, and I am disposed to do it. Indeed, I intend to first go to Rome and see the one you call God's Vicar on earth, and observe his manners and customs, as well as those of his chief clergy. If these appear to me such that, along with your words, I may apprehend that your faith is better than mine, as you have tried to show me, I will do as I have said; and if it is not so, I will remain a Jew as I am.'

When Jehannot heard this, he was greatly distressed and said to himself, 'I have wasted my efforts, which I thought I had well spent, thinking to have converted this man; for if he goes to the court of Rome and sees the lewd and wicked life of the clergy, not only will he never become a Christian, but if he were already one, he would infallibly turn Jew again.' Then, turning to Abraham, he said, 'Alas, my friend, why do you want to undertake this journey and such a great expense as it will be for you to go from here to Rome? Moreover, both by sea and by land, the road is full of perils for a rich man like you. Do you not think to find here who shall give you baptism? Or, if you have any doubts concerning the faith which I have proposed to you, where are there greater doctors and more learned men in the matter than are here, or better able to resolve you of what you wish to know or ask? Wherefore, in my opinion, this journey of yours is unnecessary. Consider that the prelates there are even such as those you may have seen here, and indeed so much the better as they are nearer to the Chief Pastor. Therefore, if you will be guided by me, you will reserve this journey for another time, for some jubilee or other, when perhaps I will bear you company.' To this the Jew replied, 'I doubt not, Jehannot, that it is as you tell me; but, to sum up many words in one, I am altogether determined, if you want me to do what you have so earnestly besought me, to go there; otherwise I will never do anything of the sort.' Jehannot, seeing his determination, said, 'Go, and good luck go with you!' And inwardly assured that he would never become a Christian, once he had seen the court of Rome, but to no avail, he desisted.

The Jew mounted his horse and as quickly as he could made his way to the court of Rome, where he was honorably received by his brethren. There, without telling anyone the reason for his coming, he began diligently to inquire into the manners and customs of the Pope and Cardinals and other prelates and all the members of the papal court. Both from his own keen observations, as he was a very quick-witted man, and from what he gathered from others, he found all of them, from the highest to the lowest, to be shamefully given to the sin of lust, not only in the natural way, but in the Sodomitical fashion as well, without any restraint of remorse or shame. Moreover, he clearly saw them to be universally gluttonous, heavy drinkers, drunkards, and slaves to their appetites, more like beasts than anything else, after lust. Looking further, he saw them all covetous and greedy for money, so much so that they bought and sold human, even Christian, blood, as well as sacred things pertaining to the altar or the church benefices, indifferently for a price, making a greater traffic and having more brokers of it than people in Paris do of silks and fabrics or anything else. What they called 'procuration' was blatant simony, and what they called 'sustenance' was gluttony, as if God apprehended not the meaning of words, but only the intention of depraved minds, and would suffer Himself, after the fashion of men, to be deceived by the names of things. All this, together with much else which must be left unsaid, was supremely displeasing to the Jew, who was a sober and modest man, and feeling he had seen enough, he decided to return to Paris, which he did.

As soon as Jehannot knew of his return, he went to him, hoping for nothing less than that he should become a Christian, and they greeted each other with the utmost joy. Then, after Abraham had rested some days, Jehannot asked him how he found the Holy Father and the cardinals and others of the papal court. To which the Jew promptly replied, 'May God give them all ill! And I say this because, if I was able to observe correctly, I saw no piety, no devotion, no good work or example of life or anything else in any who was a churchman; rather, lust, greed, gluttony, and the like, and worse (if worse can be), seemed to be in such favor with all that I hold it for a forge of diabolical rather than divine things. And as far as I can judge, it seems to me that your chief pastor and consequently all the others endeavor with all diligence and all their wit and every art to bring to naught and banish from the world the Christian religion, whereas they should be its foundation and support. And for that I see that this which they strive for comes not to pass, but that your religion continually increases and grows brighter and more glorious, it seems to me that I manifestly discern that the Holy Spirit is verily the foundation and support thereof, as of that which is true and holy over any other. Wherefore, whereas, aforetime I abode obdurate and insensible to thine exhortations and would not be persuaded to embrace thy faith, I now tell thee frankly that for nothing in the world would I forbear to become a Christian. Let us, then, to church and there have me baptized, according to the rite and ordinance of your holy faith.'

Jehannot, who expected the complete opposite conclusion, was the happiest man that could be when he heard him speak thus, and repairing with him to the Church of Our Lady in Paris, required the clergy there to give Abraham baptism. They, hearing that the Jew himself demanded it, immediately proceeded to baptize him, whilst Jehannot raised him from the sacred font and named him Giovanni. After this, he had him thoroughly instructed by men of great worth and learning in the tenets of our holy faith, which he quickly grasped, and thenceforth he was a good, devout, and worthy man."
