THE EIGHTH STORY
[Day the Second]

THE COUNT OF ANTWERP, BEING FALSELY ACCUSED, GOES INTO EXILE AND LEAVES HIS TWO CHILDREN IN DIFFERENT PLACES IN ENGLAND, WHERE, AFTER A WHILE, RETURNING IN DISGUISE AND FINDING THEM IN GOOD CONDITION, HE TAKES SERVICE AS A GROOM IN THE SERVICE OF THE KING OF FRANCE AND, BEING PROVEN INNOCENT, IS RESTORED TO HIS FORMER ESTATE

The ladies sighed deeply over the misfortunes of the beautiful Saracen woman; but who knows what prompted those sighs? Perhaps some of them sighed just as much out of envy of such frequent weddings as out of pity for Alatiel. But, leaving that aside for now, after they had laughed at Pamfilo's last words, the queen, seeing his story ended, turned to Elisa and told her to continue with one of her own.

Elisa cheerfully obeyed and began as follows: "The field in which we wander today is ample indeed, and each of us could easily run not just one, but several courses through it, so abundant has Fortune made it with her strange and grievous chances. Therefore, to come to telling one of these latter, which are innumerable, I say that:

When the Roman Empire was transferred from the French to the Germans, a great enmity arose between the two nations, and a severe and continuous war. Because of this, both to defend their own country and to attack that of others, the King of France and his son, with all the power of their realm and of such friends and relatives as they could command, raised a mighty army to go against the enemy. And before they set out - so as not to leave the realm without governance - knowing Gautier, Count of Antwerp, to be a noble and discreet gentleman and their very faithful friend and servant, and since (although he was well-versed in the art of war) he seemed to them more suited to delicate matters than to martial toils, they left him as their vicar general over the entire governance of the French kingdom and went on their way. 

Gautier accordingly addressed himself with both order and discretion to the office committed to him, still conferring on everything with the queen and her daughter-in-law, whom, although they were left under his custody and jurisdiction, he honored no less as his liege ladies and mistresses.

Now this Gautier was exceedingly handsome, being perhaps forty years old and as agreeable and well-mannered a gentleman as might be; and withal, he was the sprightliest and most fashionable cavalier known in those days and the one who went most adorned of his person. His countess was dead, leaving him two little children, a boy and a girl, and it happened that, the King of France and his son being at the war aforesaid and Gautier frequenting much at the court of the aforesaid ladies and speaking often with them of the affairs of the kingdom, the wife of the king's son cast her eyes on him and, considering his person and his manners with very great affection, was secretly fired with a fervent love for him.

Feeling herself young and lusty and knowing him to be widowed, she doubted not but her desire might easily be accomplished, and thinking nothing hindered her thereof but shame, she bethought herself altogether to put that away and reveal to him her passion. Accordingly, being one day alone and it seeming to her time, she sent for him into her chamber, as though she would discuss other matters with him.

The count, whose thoughts were far from that of the lady, hastened to her without any delay and, at her bidding, seated himself by her side on a couch; then, they being alone together, he twice asked her the reason for which she had summoned him there, but she made him no reply. At last, urged by love and grown all crimson with shame, nearly in tears and all trembling, with broken speech she thus began to say: 'Dearest and sweet friend and my lord, you may easily as a man of understanding apprehend how great is the frailty both of men and of women, and that more, for divers reasons, in one than in another...'
