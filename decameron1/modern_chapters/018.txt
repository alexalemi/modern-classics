THE FIFTH STORY

[Day the Second]

ANDREUCCIO OF PERUGIA, COMING TO NAPLES TO BUY HORSES, IS IN ONE NIGHT OVERTAKEN WITH THREE GRIEVOUS ACCIDENTS, BUT ESCAPETH THEM ALL AND RETURNETH HOME WITH A RUBY

Fiammetta began her tale, saying, "The story of the jewels found by Landolfo has reminded me of another tale, no less perilous than the one Lauretta told, but differing in that the events happened all in one night, as you shall hear.

There was once in Perugia a young horse trader named Andreuccio di Pietro. Hearing that horses were selling cheap in Naples, he put five hundred gold florins in his purse and traveled there with other merchants, having never before left his hometown. He arrived on a Sunday evening and, after consulting his host, went to the horse market the next morning. He saw many horses and bargained for several, but could not agree on a price. In his eagerness to buy, he would occasionally, like an unwary country bumpkin, pull out his purse of florins in front of the onlookers.

A Sicilian woman, who was quite beautiful but not above a little mischief, happened to see the purse and thought to herself, 'If only that money were mine!' She passed by without Andreuccio noticing her, but an old Sicilian woman with her saw him and, recognizing him, embraced him warmly. When the young woman saw this, she waited for the old woman, who then questioned her about Andreuccio - who he was, where he was from, and how she knew him. The old woman told the young woman everything about Andreuccio, including where he was staying and the reason for his visit.

Armed with this information, the young woman devised a plan. She kept the old woman busy for the rest of the day so she could not return to Andreuccio. Then she sent a maid to Andreuccio's inn to invite him to the young woman's house, claiming she wished to speak with him. Andreuccio, thinking the lady must be enamored with him, readily agreed and followed the maid to a disreputable part of town called Malpertugio, which means 'ill hole.'

The young woman greeted Andreuccio warmly, embracing and kissing him, and then told him an elaborate tale about being his long-lost sister. Andreuccio, naive and flattered by her attention, believed her story. She kept him there late into the night, eventually sending him to her chamber to sleep. When Andreuccio needed to relieve himself, the young woman directed him to a hidden chamber, where he fell through a faulty floorboard into a pit of filth. 

Realizing he had been tricked, Andreuccio tried in vain to get back into the house and recover his money. Just then, two thieves arrived planning to rob the tomb of a recently deceased archbishop. They took pity on Andreuccio's misfortune and invited him to join them. Against his better judgment, he agreed. 

At the cathedral, the thieves lowered Andreuccio into the archbishop's tomb, where he managed to steal the precious ring from the dead prelate's finger. But the thieves then trapped him inside, leaving him to face either starvation or being discovered as a thief. Miraculously, Andreuccio was able to scare off a priest who came to rob the tomb, allowing him to escape unharmed. 

Shaken but wiser, Andreuccio returned to his inn, gathered his companions, and departed Naples immediately, having turned his investment into a valuable ruby ring.
