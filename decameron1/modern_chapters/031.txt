THE SEVENTH STORY

[Day the Third]

TEDALDO ELISEI, HAVING FALLEN OUT WITH HIS LOVER, LEAVES FLORENCE AND RETURNING THERE LATER, IN A PILGRIM'S DISGUISE, SPEAKS WITH THE LADY AND MAKES HER AWARE OF HER ERROR. AFTER THIS, HE DELIVERS HER HUSBAND, WHO HAD BEEN CONVICTED OF MURDERING HIM, FROM DEATH AND RECONCILING HIM WITH HIS BROTHERS, THEN DISCREETLY ENJOYS HIMSELF WITH HIS LOVER AGAIN.

With Fiammetta's tale now concluded and praised by all, the queen promptly passed the storytelling duties to Emilia, who began thus: "I'm pleased to return to our city, from which the last two speakers had departed, and show you how one of our townsmen regained his lost lover.

There was in Florence a noble young man named Tedaldo Elisei, who was deeply in love with a woman called Madam Ermellina, the wife of one Aldobrandino Palermini. Tedaldo's admirable qualities made him deserving of enjoying her affection. However, Fortune, the enemy of the happy, denied him this comfort. For some reason, the lady, after complying with Tedaldo's wishes for a time, suddenly withdrew her favor from him entirely and refused to hear any of his messages or even see him. This plunged Tedaldo into a dire and cruel melancholy, though his love for her had been so well-hidden that no one guessed it to be the cause of his distress.

After trying in various ways to regain the love he felt he had lost through no fault of his own, and finding all his efforts futile, Tedaldo resolved to withdraw from the world, so that she who was the cause of his suffering would not have the pleasure of seeing him waste away. Without saying anything to friend or family, except for a confidant who knew everything, he took what money he could and, departing in secret, went to Ancona, where, under the name of Filippo di Sanlodeccio, he made the acquaintance of a wealthy merchant and took service with him, accompanying him to Cyprus aboard one of his ships.

Tedaldo's manners and behavior so pleased the merchant that he not only gave him a good wage but also made him a partial business partner, entrusting him with a large part of his affairs, which Tedaldo handled so well and diligently that within a few years he himself became a rich, renowned, and prominent merchant. Although in the midst of these dealings he often remembered his cruel lover and was greatly tormented by love, yearning intensely to see her again, his constancy was such that he prevailed over this battle for seven long years. But one day, by chance hearing in Cyprus a song he had composed earlier, which recounted the love he bore his lover and the pleasure he had with her, and thinking she could not have forgotten him, he was inflamed with such a desire to see her again that, unable to endure it any longer, he resolved to return to Florence.

Accordingly, having set all his affairs in order, he went with only one servant to Ancona, sent all his goods there to a friend of the Anconese merchant in Florence, and, disguised as a pilgrim returning from the Holy Sepulchre, followed secretly after with his servant. Coming to Florence, he lodged at a small inn kept by two brothers, near his lover's house, to which he immediately went to see if he might catch a glimpse of her. However, he found the windows, doors, and everything else closed, which made him fear she was dead or had moved away. Troubled, he then went to the house of his brothers, before which he saw four of them dressed in black. Marveling greatly at this, and knowing himself so changed in appearance and dress from when he had last been there that he would not easily be recognized, he boldly approached a nearby cobbler and asked why they were in mourning. The cobbler told him that the men in black were Tedaldo's brothers, who had recently found out that one of their brothers, who had not been there for a long time, had been murdered, and they had proven to the authorities that a man named Aldobrandino Palermini, who was in prison, had killed him, as he had been a lover of Aldobrandino's wife.

Tedaldo was amazed that someone so resembled him as to be mistaken for him, and he was grieved by Aldobrandino's misfortune. Then, having learned that his lover was alive and well, and with night now fallen, he returned, full of various thoughts, to the inn. After supping with his servant, he was put to sleep near the top of the house. There, with the many thoughts troubling him, the poor quality of the bed, and perhaps also due to the meager supper, half the night passed without him falling asleep. Awake, around midnight, he seemed to hear people come down into the house from the roof, and through the cracks in the chamber door, he saw a light come up there. He quietly stole to the door, peeked through the crack, and saw a rather pretty girl holding the light, while three men who had come down from the roof approached her. After some greetings, one of the men said to the girl, "Praise be to God, from now on we can rest easy, since we know for certain that the death of Tedaldo Elisei has been proven by his brothers against Aldobrandino Palermini, who has confessed to it, and the judgment has now been recorded. Nevertheless, it is necessary to keep strict silence, for if it ever became known that it was we who killed him, we would be in the same danger as Aldobrandino."

Hearing this, Tedaldo began to consider the many and great errors that can befall people's minds. He thought first of his brothers, who had mourned and buried a stranger in his place, and then of the innocent man accused on false suspicion and brought to the brink of death by false testimony, as well as the blind severity of the laws and rulers, who often, under the pretense of diligently investigating the truth, prove falsehoods to be true through their cruelties, styling themselves ministers of justice and God, when in fact they are executors of iniquity and the devil. Then he turned his thoughts to delivering Aldobrandino and determined what he should do.

Rising in the morning, he left his servant at the inn and went alone, when he thought the time was right, to his lover's house, where he happened to find the door open and saw the lady seated, full of tears and bitterness, in a small ground floor room. At this sight, he nearly wept out of compassion for her, and drawing near, said, "Madam, do not afflict yourself; your peace is at hand." The lady, hearing this, lifted her eyes and said, weeping, "Good man, you seem to me a strange pilgrim; what do you know of my peace or my affliction?" "Madam," answered Tedaldo, "I am from Constantinople and have just come here, sent by God to turn your tears into laughter and to deliver your husband from death."
