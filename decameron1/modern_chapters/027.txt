The Third Story

[Day the Third]

A Lady, Pretending Concern Over a Suitor's Advances, Tricks a Gullible Friar into Helping Her Pursue Her Own Desires

After Pampinea finished speaking and the group had marveled at the boldness and cunning of the stableman as well as the king's wisdom, the queen turned to Filomena and asked her to continue the storytelling. Filomena then began cheerfully, "I intend to recount a deception that was indeed carried out by a beautiful lady upon a serious-minded friar. This should be all the more pleasing to every layperson, as these friars - who are mostly foolish and eccentric - consider themselves superior and wiser than others, when in reality they are of far less worth than the rest of us, being men who, lacking the means to provide for themselves due to the smallness of their spirits, take refuge wherever they can find sustenance.

This story, dear ladies, I shall tell you not only to continue the order set forth, but also to show you that even the clergy, to whom we women, being overly credulous, yield too much trust, can be and are sometimes cleverly deceived, and not just by men, but even by some of our own sex.

In our city, which is fuller of deceit than of love or faith, there was, not long ago, a gentlewoman endowed with beauty, charm, and all the engaging manners, loftiness of spirit, and subtle wit that nature can bestow upon a woman. I shall not reveal her name or any other details that pertain to this story, as there are still people alive who would take offense, whereas it should be received with laughter.

This lady, seeing herself married to a wool merchant, despite her noble lineage, and unable to shed the haughtiness of her spirit, which made her deem no man of humble condition, no matter how wealthy, worthy of a gentlewoman - and moreover observing her husband to be skilled at nothing more significant than setting up a loom or bargaining with a spinner over her yarn - resolved not to accept his embraces except when absolutely necessary, but to seek, for her own satisfaction, someone who she deemed more deserving of her favors than the wool merchant. She consequently fell so fervently in love with a man of very good standing and middle age that, whenever she did not see him during the day, she could not pass the following night without unease. The gentleman, unaware of her feelings, paid her no heed, and she, being very discreet, dared not make her affection known to him, either by sending women or by letter, fearing the potential dangers.

However, noting that he often kept company with a churchman who, though a dull fellow, was nonetheless reputed by nearly all to be a most worthy friar due to his devout life, she thought that this friar would make an excellent intermediary between herself and her lover. Having considered the means she should employ, she went, at an opportune time, to the church where he dwelt and, summoning him, told him that, if he pleased, she would like to confess to him. The friar, seeing her and judging her to be a woman of standing, willingly heard her confession, and she then said to him, 'My father, I must turn to you for aid and counsel regarding a matter that you shall hear. I know, as I have told you, that you are acquainted with my family and my husband, who loves me more than his own life, and there is nothing I desire that he does not immediately provide, he being a very wealthy man and able to do so. Therefore, I love him more than myself, and were I even to think, let alone do, anything that might go against his honor and pleasure, there would be no woman more wicked or more deserving of the fire than I.

'Now, there is a man - whose name, in truth, I do not know, but who seems to me to be a man of good standing and is, if I am not mistaken, frequently in your company, a well-built and tall man dressed in very decent somber clothing - who, unaware perhaps of my steadfast purpose, appears to have laid siege to me, and I cannot show myself at door or window nor go out of the house without him immediately presenting himself before me. I marvel that he is not here now, and I am greatly concerned, for such behavior often brings virtuous women into disrepute, without their fault. I have sometimes thought of having my brothers tell him of this, but then I have considered that men often deliver messages in such a way that ill responses ensue, leading to words and then to deeds. Therefore, to avoid any mischief or scandal, I have kept silent about the matter and have determined to disclose it to you rather than to another, both because you seem to be his friend and because it is fitting for you to rebuke not only friends but also strangers of such things. I beseech you, for the sake of God, that you rebuke him of this and pray him to abandon these his ways. There are women enough who may perhaps be inclined to such trifles and would take pleasure in being followed and courted by him, whereas to me, who have no mind for such matters, it is a great annoyance.' With these words, she bowed her head as if she were about to weep.

The holy friar immediately understood of whom she spoke and firmly believing what she said to be true, greatly commended her righteous intent and promised her to act in such a way that she would have no further annoyance from the person in question. Knowing her to be very wealthy, he commended to her works of charity and almsgiving, recounting to her his own need. The lady said, 'I implore you to do this for God's sake, and if he denies it, do not hesitate to tell him that it was I who informed you of this and complained to you about it.' Then, after making her confession and receiving her penance, remembering the friar's exhortations to acts of charity, she stealthily filled his hand with money, asking him to say masses for the souls of her deceased relatives. Having done this, she rose from his feet, took leave of him, and returned home.

Not long after, the gentleman, according to his custom, came up, and after they had conversed for a while, the friar, drawing his friend aside, very politely rebuked him for the manner in which, as he believed, he pursued and spied upon the aforementioned lady, according to what she had told him. The other, as was to be expected, marveled, having never laid eyes on her and being accustomed to pass by her house very rarely, and would have excused himself, but the friar did not allow him to speak, saying, 'Now make no show of wonder nor waste words in denying it, for it will avail you nothing. I did not learn these matters from the neighbors; nay, she herself told them to me, complaining bitterly of you. And besides that such antics are unbecoming for a man of your age, I may tell you this much of her, that if ever I saw a woman averse to these follies, it is she. Wherefore, for your own credit and her comfort, I pray you desist therefrom and let her be in peace.'

The gentleman, quicker of wit than the friar, quickly grasped the lady's scheme and, feigning some embarrassment, promised that he would no longer meddle with her. Then, taking leave of the friar, he went to the lady's house, where she was still waiting at a small window, so that she might see him if he passed that way. When she saw him come, she showed herself so joyful and gracious to him that he could very well understand that he had correctly interpreted the friar's words, and from then on, under the pretext of other business, he began to pass continually through the street, to his own pleasure and to the exceeding delight and solace of the lady.

After a while, perceiving that she pleased him as much as he pleased her and wishing to inflame him further and to assure him of the love she bore him, she went again, choosing her time and place, to the holy friar and, seating herself at his feet in the church, began to weep. The friar, seeing this, asked her affectionately what was troubling her anew. 'Alas, my father,' she answered, 'what ails me is none other than yonder friend of yours, or rather, that incarnate devil, of whom I complained to you the other day, for I believe he was born to torment me and make me do something that I would never be happy about nor ever dare to sit at your feet again.' 'How?' cried the friar. 'Hath he not ceased to annoy you?' 'No, indeed,' she answered. 'Nay, since I complained to you about him, as if out of spite, perhaps taking it ill that I should have done so, for whereas he used to pass by my house once, I verily believe he hath passed seven times. And would to God he were content with passing and spying upon me! Nay, he hath grown so bold and so impertinent that but yesterday he sent a woman to me at home with his idle tales and trinkets, and sent me a purse and a girdle, as if I did not have purses and girdles enough. These I took and take so ill that I believe, had it not been for my regard for the sin of it and afterwards for the love of you, I had played the devil. However, I contained myself and would not do or say anything without first letting you know. Nay, I had already returned the purse and the girdle to the messenger who brought them, that she might carry them back to him, and had given her a rough dismissal, but afterwards, fearing she might keep them for herself and tell him that I had accepted them, as I hear women of her sort sometimes do, I called her back and took them, full of disgust, from her hands and have brought them to you, so you may return them to him and tell him I want none of his trash, for that, thanks be to God and my husband, I have purses and girdles enough to smother him with. Moreover, if hereafter he desist not from this, I tell you, as a father, you must excuse me, but I will tell it, come what may, to my husband and my brothers; for I had far liefer he should brook an affront, if needs he must, than that I should suffer blame for him; wherefore let him look to himself.'

So saying, still weeping bitterly, she pulled out from under her surcoat a very handsome and rich purse and a quaint and costly girdle and threw them into the lap of the friar, who, fully crediting what she told him and incensed beyond measure, took them and said to her, 'Daughter, I marvel not that thou art provoked at these doings, nor can I blame thee therefor; but I much commend thee for following my counsel in the matter. I rebuked him the other day and he hath ill performed that which he promised me; wherefore, as well for that as for this that he hath newly done, I mean to warm his ears for him after such a fashion that methinketh he will give thee no farther concern; but do thou, God's benison on thee, suffer not thyself to be so overcome with anger that thou tell it to any of thy folk, for that overmuch harm might ensue thereof unto him. Neither fear thou lest this blame anywise ensue to thee, for I shall still, before both God and men, be a most constant witness to thy virtue.'

The lady pretended to be somewhat comforted and, leaving that topic, said, as one who knew his greed and that of his fellow churchmen, 'Sir, these some nights past there have appeared to me sundry of my kinsfolk, who ask nought but almsdeeds, and meseemeth they are indeed in exceeding great torment, especially my mother, who appeareth to me in such ill case and affliction that it is pity to behold. Methinketh she suffereth exceeding distress to see me in this tribulation with yonder enemy of God; wherefore I would have you say me forty masses of Saint Gregory for her and their souls, together with certain of your own prayers, so God may deliver them from that penitential fire.' So saying, she put a florin into his hand, which the holy father blithely received and, confirming her devotion with fair words and many pious examples, gave her his blessing and let her go.

The lady having departed, the friar, never suspecting he had been duped, sent for his friend, who, coming and finding him troubled, at once divined that he was to receive news of the lady and awaited what the friar should say. The latter repeated what he had previously said to him and, addressing him angrily and reproachfully, rebuked him severely for what, according to the lady's report, he had done. The gentleman, not yet perceiving the friar's drift, faintly enough denied having sent her the purse and the girdle, so as not to undeceive the friar, in case the lady should have led him to believe that he had done this; whereat the good man was sore incensed and said, 'How canst thou deny it, wicked man that thou art? See, here they are, for she herself brought them to me, weeping; look if thou knowest them.' The gentleman feigned to be somewhat abashed and answered, 'Yes, I do indeed know them and I confess to you that I did ill; but I swear to you, since I see her thus disposed, that you shall never more hear a word of this.'

In short, after many words, the simpleton of a friar gave his friend the purse and the girdle and dismissed him, after scolding him soundly and beseeching him to no longer occupy himself with these follies, which he promised to do. The gentleman, overjoyed both at the assurance that it seemed to him he had of the lady's love and at the fine gift, was no sooner quit of the friar than he betook himself to a place where he made shift to let his mistress see that he had the one and the other thing; whereat she was mightily rejoiced, more by token that it seemed to her the scheme went from good to better. She now awaited nought but her husband's going abroad to give completion to the work, and it befell not long after that it behoved him repair to Genoa on some occasion or other. No sooner had he mounted to horse in the morning and gone his way, than the lady betook herself to the holy man and after many lamentations, said to him, weeping, 'Father mine, I tell you now plainly that I can brook no more...'
