THE NINTH STORY 

[Day the Third]

GILLETTE OF NARBONNE CURES THE KING OF FRANCE OF A FISTULA AND ASKS FOR BERTRAND OF ROUSSILLON AS HER HUSBAND, WHO MARRIES HER AGAINST HIS WILL. BERTRAND THEN GOES TO FLORENCE, WHERE HE COURTS A YOUNG LADY. GILLETTE, DISGUISING HERSELF, LIES WITH HIM AND HAS TWO SONS BY HIM. AFTERWARD, HOLDING HER IN HIGH REGARD, HE ACCEPTS HER AS HIS WIFE.

With Lauretta's story now finished, it was the queen's turn to speak, and without waiting for her companions to urge her, she cheerfully began: "Who can tell a tale that will seem good after Lauretta's? Truly, it was fortunate that hers was not the first, for few of the others would have pleased after it, as I fear will happen with those still to be told today. Nevertheless, I will recount to you what comes to my mind on the proposed theme.

There was in the kingdom of France a nobleman called Isnard, Count of Roussillon, who, because he was often unwell, kept in his service a physician named Master Gerard of Narbonne. The count had one young son, named Bertrand, who was exceedingly handsome and agreeable, and was raised together with other children of his own age. Among these was the physician's daughter, Gillette, who had an infinite and fervent love for Bertrand, greater than befitted her tender years. 

When the count died and left his son in the king's care, Bertrand had to go to Paris, which left the young lady deeply saddened. Shortly after, her own father also passed away, and though Gillette longed to go to Paris to see Bertrand, as she was closely guarded due to her wealth and lack of family, she found no honorable way to do so. Being now of marriageable age but unable to forget Bertrand, she had, without good reason, refused many suitors her relatives wished her to accept.

It happened that while Gillette burned more than ever with love for Bertrand, upon hearing that he had grown into a very handsome gentleman, news came to her that the King of France had developed a fistula, a painful ailment that had not been successfully treated by any physician, despite many attempts. Despairing of a cure, the king would no longer seek counsel or aid from anyone.

Gillette was exceedingly pleased by this, seeing it as not only a legitimate excuse to go to Paris, but also an opportunity to potentially have Bertrand as her husband. Having previously learned many things from her father, she made a powder from certain useful herbs and, taking horse, went to Paris. 

First, she studied to see Bertrand, and then presented herself before the king, asking to examine his ailment. The king, seeing her as a fair and engaging young woman, could not refuse her, and showed her the affliction. Upon seeing it, she was certain she could heal it and said, 'My lord, if it pleases you, I hope in God to make you whole of this infirmity within eight days, without any discomfort or fatigue on your part.' The king scoffed inwardly, thinking, 'What the best physicians in the world have been unable to do, how can a young woman know?' He thanked her for her goodwill but said he was resolved to heed no more counsel from physicians. 

The young lady replied, 'My lord, you dismiss my skill because I am young and a woman, but I do not practice medicine of my own knowledge, but with the aid of God and the expertise of Master Gerard of Narbonne, who was my father and a renowned physician in his lifetime.'

The king, hearing this, thought to himself, 'Perhaps this woman has been sent to me by God; why should I not try her knowledge, since she says she can cure me quickly and without my discomfort?' So he decided to give her a chance and said, 'Damsel, and if you do not cure us, after causing us to break our resolve, what will you have happen to you?' 'My lord,' she answered, 'set a guard over me, and if I do not cure you within eight days, let me be burned alive; but if I do cure you, what reward shall I have?' The king said, 'You seem as yet unwed; if you do this, we will marry you well and honorably.' 'My lord,' the young lady replied, 'I am pleased that you would marry me, but I will have a husband of my own choosing, excepting only any of your sons or of the royal house.' He readily promised her that, and she began her treatment. In short order, before the specified time, she restored him to health.

The king, feeling himself cured, said, 'Damsel, you have well earned your husband.' To which she answered, 'Then, my lord, I have earned Bertrand of Roussillon, whom I began to love even in my childhood and have loved above all others ever since.' The king deemed it a grave matter to give Bertrand to her, yet, having promised and unwilling to break his word, he summoned the count and said to him, 'Bertrand, you are now of age and accomplished; therefore, it is our wish that you return to govern your county, and take with you a young woman whom we have given you to be your wife.'

'And who is the young woman, my lord?' Bertrand asked. The king answered, 'It is she who has restored our health through her medicines.' 

Bertrand, who had seen and recognized Gillette, knowing her (though she seemed very fair to him) to be of no such lineage as accorded with his status, disdainfully said, 'My lord, will you then marry me to a physician? Now God forbid I should ever take such an one to wife!' 'Then,' said the king, 'will you have us break our faith, which, to regain our health, we pledged to the young woman, who in return demanded you as her husband?' 'My lord,' Bertrand answered, 'you may take from me whatever I possess, or, as your vassal, bestow me on whomever pleases you; but I will never willingly consent to such a marriage.' 'Nay,' the king rejoined, 'but you shall, for the young woman is fair and wise and loves you dearly; wherefore we doubt not but you will have a far happier life with her than with a lady of higher birth.' Bertrand fell silent, and the king ordered grand preparations for the marriage.

When the appointed day came, Bertrand, greatly against his will, in the presence of the king, espoused the young woman who loved him more than herself. Then, having already determined what he would do, he sought leave of the king to depart, saying he wished to return to his county and there consummate the marriage. But instead of going there, he went to Tuscany, where, hearing the Florentines were at war with those of Siena, he decided to join the Florentines, who joyfully received him and made him captain over a certain number of men-at-arms, and there he remained for some time, well provided for.

The newly made wife, discontented with such a fate but hoping through her good conduct to recall him to his county, went there, where she was received by all as their rightful lady. Finding everything in disrepair and disorder from the long time the land had been without a lord, with great diligence and care, as a wise lady, she set everything in order again, which greatly pleased the count's vassals, who held her in high esteem and blamed the count sorely for not accepting her.

The lady, having thoroughly ordered the county, notified the count thereof through two knights she sent to him, praying that if it was on her account he stayed away, he would let her know, and she would depart to please him. But he answered them very harshly, saying, 'For that, let her do as she will; I, for my part, will return there to live with her only when she has this ring on her finger and a son begotten by me in her arms.' Now, the ring in question he prized highly and never parted with, for it was said to have a certain virtue.

The knights understood the hardship of the conditions he set, which were nearly impossible, but seeing they could not move him from his purpose by their words, they returned to the lady and reported his reply. This greatly afflicted her, but after much consideration, she determined to see if and where the two things he required might be accomplished, so that she might regain her husband.
