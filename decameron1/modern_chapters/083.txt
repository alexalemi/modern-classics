The Second Story
[Day the Eighth]

The Parish Priest of Varlungo Lies with Mistress Belcolore and Leaves Her a Cloak of His in Pledge; Then, Borrowing a Mortar of Her, He Sends It Back to Her, Demanding in Return the Cloak Left by Way of Token, Which the Good Woman Grudgingly Gives Him Back

The men and women alike commended what Gulfardo had done to the greedy Milanese lady, and the queen, turning to Pamfilo, smiled and charged him to continue. He said, "Fair ladies, it occurs to me to tell you a little story against those who constantly offend against us, without being open to retaliation on our part - that is, the clergy, who have proclaimed a crusade against our wives and who, when they manage to get one of the latter under them, believe they have gained forgiveness of fault and pardon of penalty no otherwise than as they had brought the Sultan bound from Alexandria to Avignon. The wretched laymen cannot return them the like, although they vent their anger upon the priests' mothers and sisters, mistresses and daughters, assailing them with no less fervor than the former do their wives. Therefore, I intend to recount to you a village love affair, more laughable for its conclusion than long in words, from which you may yet gather, by way of fruit, that priests are not always to be believed in everything.

You must know, then, that there was once at Varlungo - a village very near here, as each of you ladies either knows or may have heard - a worthy priest and a lusty of his person in the service of the ladies, who, although he knew not overwell how to read, nonetheless regaled his parishioners with a store of good and pious sayings at the elm tree on Sundays and visited their women, when they went abroad anywhere, more diligently than any priest who had been there before, carrying them trinkets and holy water and a stray candle-end or so, and even to their houses. Now it chanced that, among other his female parishioners who were most to his liking, one pleased him over all, by name Mistress Belcolore, the wife of a farmer who styled himself Bentivegna del Mazzo, a jolly, buxom country wench, brown-favored and tightly built, as skilled at operating the mill as any woman alive. Moreover, it was she who knew how to play the tambourine and sing 'The water runs to the ravine' and lead up the dance and the round, when need was, with a fine handkerchief in her hand and a quaint, better than any woman of her neighborhood; by reason of which things my lord priest became so enamored of her that he was like to lose his wits therefor and would prowl about all day long to get a sight of her...

(The story continues with the priest's pursuit of Belcolore and their eventual tryst, after which he devises a scheme to retrieve the cloak he had left with her as a pledge.)

The clerk accordingly repaired to her house and there, finding her at table with Bentivegna, set down the mortar and did the priest's errand. Belcolore, hearing the demand for the cloak again, would have answered; but her husband said, with an angry air, "You take a pledge of his reverence? I vow to Christ, I have a mind to give you a good clout over the head! Go, give it quickly back to him, pox take thee! And in future, let him ask what he will of ours, (aye, though he should seek our ass,) look that it be not denied him." Belcolore rose, grumbling, and pulling the cloak out of the chest, gave it to the clerk, saying, "Tell his reverence from me, Belcolore says, she vows to God you shall never again pound sauce in her mortar; you have done her no such fine honor of this bout."

The clerk made off with the cloak and did her message to the priest, who said, laughing, "Tell her, when thou seest her, that, an she will not lend me her mortar, I will not lend her my pestle; and so we shall be quits." Bentivegna concluded that his wife had said this, because he had chided her, and took no heed thereof; but Belcolore bore the priest a grudge and held him at arm's length till vintage-time; when, he having threatened to cause her go into the mouth of Lucifer the great devil, for very fear she made her peace with him over must and roast chestnuts and they after made merry together time and again. In lieu of the five crowns, the priest let put new parchment to her tambourine and string thereto a cast of hawk's bells, and with this she was fain to be content.
