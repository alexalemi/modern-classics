THE NINTH STORY

[Day the First]

THE KING OF CYPRUS, ROUSED BY A GASCON LADY, TRANSFORMS FROM A TIMID PRINCE INTO A MAN OF WORTH AND COURAGE

The queen's final command fell to Elisa, who, without waiting, began merrily, "Young ladies, it has often happened that what countless scoldings and severe punishments have failed to accomplish in a man has been achieved by a single word, spoken more by chance than with deliberate intent. This is clearly shown in the story Lauretta told, and I, in turn, intend to prove the same to you through another, very brief tale. For since good things can still be of service, they should be received attentively, no matter who the speaker.

I say, then, that in the days of the first King of Cyprus, after the Holy Land was conquered by Godfrey of Bouillon, it happened that a gentlewoman from Gascony went on a pilgrimage to the Holy Sepulcher. Upon her return, she came to Cyprus, where she was shamefully abused by certain disreputable men. Having complained of this without receiving any satisfaction, she thought to appeal to the king for redress. But she was told that her efforts would be in vain, for he was of such a base and worthless character that, instead of defending others from wrongs, he endured with shameful cowardice countless affronts to himself. Indeed, anyone with a grievance was accustomed to vent their spite by doing him some shame or insult.

Hearing this and despairing of justice, the lady resolved, as a small solace to her distress, to rebuke the king's cowardice. Therefore, presenting herself in tears before him, she said, 'My lord, I have not come into your presence seeking redress for the wrong done to me. But in compensation for it, I beg you to teach me how you endure the affronts I understand are inflicted upon yourself, so that I may learn from you to patiently endure my own, which, God knows, I would gladly give to you, since you are such an excellent bearer of them.'

The king, who until then had been sluggish and passive, awoke as if from sleep. Beginning with the wrong done to the lady, which he cruelly avenged, he thereafter became a very rigorous punisher of all who committed any offense against the honor of his crown."

4. Preserved the author's narrative voice and tone: I aimed to maintain Boccaccio's distinctive style, including his use of descriptive language, moral commentary, and subtle humor. For instance, the line "so haply I may learn of thee patiently to endure mine own" was modernized to "so that I may learn from you to patiently endure my own."

Overall, the goal was to make the text more accessible to a modern high school audience while staying true to the original meaning, tone, and literary quality.
