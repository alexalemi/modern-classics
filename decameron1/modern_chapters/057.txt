THE TENTH STORY

[Day the Fifth]

PIETRO DI VINCIOLO GOES OUT TO DINE, WHEREUPON HIS WIFE SUMMONS A YOUTH TO KEEP HER COMPANY, AND HER HUSBAND RETURNING, UNEXPECTEDLY, SHE HIDES HER LOVER UNDER A CHICKEN COOP. PIETRO TELLS HER HOW A YOUNG MAN HAD BEEN FOUND IN THE HOUSE OF ONE ARCOLANO, WITH WHOM HE WAS TO HAVE DINED, AND BROUGHT IN BY HIS WIFE, AND SHE BLAMES THE LATTER. PRESENTLY, A DONKEY, BY ACCIDENT, STEPS ON THE FINGERS OF THE ONE WHO IS UNDER THE COOP AND HE CRIES OUT, WHEREUPON PIETRO RUNS THERE AND SEEING HIM, DISCOVERS HIS WIFE'S UNFAITHFULNESS, BUT ULTIMATELY COMES TO AN AGREEMENT WITH HER FOR HIS OWN IMMORAL ENDS.

The queen's story had ended, and all having praised God for rewarding Federigo as he deserved, Dioneo, who never waited for command, began in this way: "I don't know whether to say if it is a common vice, grown up in mankind through the perversity of customs and habits, or a defect inherent in our nature, that we laugh more at bad things than at good deeds, especially when they do not concern us. Therefore, seeing that the effort I have sometimes made and am now about to make aims at nothing else than to rid you of melancholy and provide you with laughter and merriment - although the subject of my present story may not be entirely proper, nevertheless, dear ladies, because it may provide amusement, I will tell it to you, and you, listening to it, as you are accustomed to do when you enter into gardens, where, putting out your delicate hands, you pluck the roses and leave the thorns be. In this way must you do with my story, leaving the wicked man of whom I shall tell you to his infamy and ill-luck, while you merrily laugh at the amorous devices of his wife, having compassion, when necessary, for the misfortunes of others.

There was, then, in Perugia, not long ago, a rich man called Pietro di Vinciolo, who, perhaps more to deceive others and to reduce the general suspicion in which he was held by all the Perugians, than out of any desire of his own, took a wife, and fortune in this was so far conformable to his inclination that the wife he took was a stocky, red-haired, hot-tempered woman, who would have preferred to have two husbands than one, whereas she happened upon one who had a mind far more disposed to other things than to her. Becoming, in time, aware of this and seeing herself fair and fresh and feeling herself buxom and lusty, she began by being greatly angered by it and came once and again to unseemly words thereof with her husband, with whom she was well-nigh always at variance. Then, seeing that this might result rather in her own exhaustion than in the amendment of her husband's depravity, she said to herself, 'Yonder scoundrel forsakes me to go of his lewdness on pattens through the dry, and I will study to carry others on shipboard through the wet. I took him to husband and brought him a fine large dowry, knowing him to be a man and supposing him desirous of that whereunto men are and should be inclined; and had I not believed that he would play the part of a man, I had never taken him. He knew that I was a woman; why, then, did he take me to wife, if women were not to his mind? This is not to be suffered. Were I minded to renounce the world, I should have made myself a nun; but, if, choosing to live in the world, as I do, I look for delight or pleasure from yonder fellow, I may belike grow old, expecting in vain, and whenas I shall be old, I shall in vain repent and bemoan myself of having wasted my youth, which latter he himself is a very good teacher and demonstrator how I should solace, showing me by example how I should delight myself with that wherein he delights, more by token that this were commendable in me, whereas in him it is exceeding blameworthy, seeing that I should offend against the laws alone, whereas he offendeth against both law and nature.'

Accordingly, the good lady, having thus bethought herself and likely more than once, to give effect privately to these considerations, made the acquaintance of an old woman who appeared like Saint Verdiana, that gives the serpents to eat, and still went to every pardon, rosary in hand, nor ever talked of aught but the lives of the Holy Fathers or of the wounds of St. Francis and was reputed a saint by nearly all, and when it seemed to her time, openly shared her intent with her. 'My daughter,' replied the old woman, 'God who knows all knows that you will do exceedingly well, and if for nothing else, yet should you do it, you and every other young woman, not to lose the time of your youth, for that to whoso hath understanding, there is no grief like that of having lost one's time. And what a devil are we women good for, once we are old, save to keep the ashes about the fire-pot? If none else knows it and can bear witness thereof, that do and can I; for, now that I am old, I recognize without avail, but not without very sore and bitter remorse of mind, the time that I let slip, and albeit I lost it not altogether (for that I would not have thee deem me a ninny), still I did not what I might have done; whereof whenas I remember me, seeing myself fashioned as thou seest me at this present, so that thou wouldst find none to give me fire to my tinder, God knoweth what chagrin I feel.'

The lady, finding that her husband was satisfied, arose and had the table reset straightway; then, ordering the supper she had prepared to be brought, she supped merrily in company with her scoundrel of a husband and the young man. After supper, what Pietro devised for the satisfaction of all three has escaped my memory; but this much I know that on the following morning the youth was escorted back to the public place, not altogether certain which he had the more been that night, wife or husband. Wherefore, dear ladies, this will I say to you, 'Whoso doth it to you, do you it to him'; and if you cannot presently, keep it in mind till such time as you can, so he may get as good as he gives."
