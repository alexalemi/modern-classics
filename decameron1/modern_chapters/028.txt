The Fourth Story
[Day the Third]

Dom Felice Teaches Fra Puccio How to Become Blessed by Performing a Special Penance, Which the Other Does, While Dom Felice Lives Merrily with the Good Man's Wife

Filomena finished her story, and Dioneo, with sweet words, greatly praised the lady's cleverness and the prayer with which Filomena had concluded. The queen then turned to Pamfilo with a smile and said, "Come, Pamfilo, continue our entertainment with some pleasant tale." Pamfilo promptly replied that he would, and began as follows:

Madam, there are many people who, while striving to enter Paradise, inadvertently send others there; and this is what recently happened to a neighbor of ours, as you shall hear.

According to what I have been told, there lived near San Pancrazio an honest and wealthy man named Puccio di Rinieri, who, devoting himself in his later years entirely to religious practices, became a tertiary of the Franciscan order, and was thus called Fra Puccio. Pursuing this pious life, he frequently visited the church, as he had no family aside from a wife and one maid, and therefore did not need to work. Being an ignorant and simple-minded fellow, he said his prayers, attended sermons and Mass, and never failed to be present at the Lauds chanted by the laity. He fasted and mortified himself, and it was even rumored that he was one of the Flagellants.

His wife, Mistress Isabetta, a woman of twenty-eight to thirty years of age, fresh and fair and plump, kept much longer and more frequent fasts than she would have wished, due to her husband's piety and likely his age. When she would have slept or perhaps frolicked with him, he would recount to her the life of Christ, the sermons of Fra Nastagio, or the Lament of Mary Magdalene.

Meanwhile, a monk named Dom Felice, a Conventual of San Pancrazio, had returned home from Paris. He was young and handsome, keen-witted and a learned scholar. Fra Puccio became close friends with him, as Dom Felice ably resolved all his doubts and feigned great piety, which led Fra Puccio to frequently bring him home to dine and sup. The lady, for her husband's sake, also became familiar with the monk and willingly honored him.

Seeing the lady so fresh and plump, the monk guessed what she likely lacked the most and decided, if he could, to provide it himself, thus sparing Fra Puccio the effort. He skillfully made eyes at her, and at the first opportunity, spoke to her of his desires. Although he found her well-disposed to act on them, he could find no way to be alone with her, for she would trust herself nowhere in the world except her own house, and there it could not be, since Fra Puccio was always present.

Troubled by this, the monk eventually devised a plan to meet with the lady in her own home without arousing suspicion, even with Fra Puccio there. One day, when Fra Puccio came to visit him, the monk said, "I have often understood, Fra Puccio, that your greatest desire is to become a saint, and you go about it by a long road, when there is another, much shorter way that the Pope and other great prelates, who know and practice it, will not make known, for if the common people learned it, the clergy, who live mostly on alms, would be ruined, as the laity would no longer feel the need to make offerings to them. But since you are my friend and have entertained me so honorably, I will teach it to you, if I can be assured that you will practice it and not reveal it to anyone."

Eager to know the secret, Fra Puccio begged the monk to teach it to him, swearing he would never tell anyone, except as the monk permitted. The monk then said, "You must know that the church doctors hold that whoever wishes to become blessed must perform the penance you shall hear. I do not say that after the penance you will not still be a sinner as you are now, but the sins you have committed up to that time will all be purged and pardoned, and those you commit afterward will not be held against you, but will pass away like venial sins.

First, you must confess your sins most diligently, and then keep a strict fast and abstinence for forty days, during which time you must not touch your own wife, nor any other woman. You must also have a place in your house where you can see the sky at night, and there, standing upright with your back against a wide plank, keeping your feet on the ground and your arms outstretched like a crucifix, you must remain gazing at the sky until matins, saying three hundred Paternosters and as many Ave Marias, in honor of the Trinity, while keeping in mind that God is the Creator of heaven and earth and the Passion of Christ. When the bell rings for matins, you may go to bed, and in the morning, hear at least three Masses and say fifty Paternosters and Aves. The rest of the day, you may attend to your business, if you have any, and dine. In the evening, you must return to the church and say certain prayers I will provide you. Then, towards compline, return to your nighttime devotions. If you perform this penance with devotion and contrition, I doubt not that you will feel something marvelous of eternal blessedness before it is done."

Fra Puccio found this not a very burdensome matter, and decided to begin on Sunday. Returning home, he related everything in order to his wife, with the monk's permission. The lady understood very well what the monk meant by having him stand fast without stirring until matins, and the plan seemed excellent to her. She replied that she was pleased with it and any other good work he did for the health of his soul, and that she would fast with him, but no more.

So, on the appointed Sunday, Fra Puccio began his penance, and Dom Felice, having agreed with the lady, came most evenings to sup with her, bringing fine food and drink, and then remained with her until matins, when he would depart, while Fra Puccio returned to bed. The place Fra Puccio had chosen for his penance adjoined the lady's chamber, separated only by a thin wall, so that one night, as the monk and lady were enjoying themselves more freely than usual, it seemed to Fra Puccio that he felt the floor shaking. Having said a hundred of his Paternosters, he stopped and called out to his wife to ask what she was doing.

The lady, who was mischievous, and likely riding atop one of the saints at that moment, answered, "Indeed, husband, I toss and turn as best I can." "How? You toss?" said Fra Puccio. "What does this tossing mean?" The lady, laughing, for she was a playful woman and no doubt had reason to laugh, replied merrily, "How? Don't you know what it means? You've said a thousand times, 'Whoever doesn't sup at night must toss till morning light.'" Fra Puccio, in the simplicity of his heart, thought the fasting was causing her to be unable to sleep and that was why she was tossing so much in bed. "Wife," he said, "I told you not to fast, but since you insist on it, think no more of it, and try to rest; the shaking you make of the bed disturbs everything here."

"Don't worry about that," she answered, "I know what I'm doing; you just focus on doing well, and I'll do as well as I can." So Fra Puccio remained silent and returned to his Paternosters. After that night, the monk and lady made a bed in another part of the house, where they enjoyed themselves in great delight for the duration of Fra Puccio's penance. At the same hour, the monk would depart, and the lady return to her own bed, where Fra Puccio would join her a little later, having completed his devotions. In this way, Fra Puccio continued his penance, while his wife took her pleasure with the monk, saying to him merrily from time to time, "You have put Fra Puccio to performing a penance that has earned us Paradise." Indeed, the lady, finding herself in good case, took such a liking to the monk's fare, having been long kept on a sparse diet by her husband, that even after Fra Puccio's penance was accomplished, she still found means to feed her fill with him elsewhere, using discretion to continue her enjoyment for a long time. Thus, then, that my last words may not be out of accord with my first, it came to pass that, whereas Fra Puccio, by doing penance, thought to win Paradise for himself, he put therein the monk, who had shown him the speedy way thither, and his wife, who lived with him in great lack of that whereof Dom Felice, like a charitable man as he was, vouchsafed her great plenty.
