THE SIXTH STORY

[Day the First]

AN HONEST MAN, WITH A CHANCE PLEASANTRY, PUTS TO SHAME THE PERVERSE HYPOCRISY OF THE RELIGIOUS ORDERS

Emilia, who sat next after Fiammetta, began boldly to speak as follows, by the queen's pleasure: "I too will not keep silent about a biting rebuke given by an honest layman to a greedy monk, with a speech that was no less amusing than commendable.

There was, dear ladies, not long ago in our city, a Franciscan friar and inquisitor of heretical beliefs, who, though he tried hard to appear a devout and loving follower of the Christian religion, as they all do, was no less diligent in inquiring about who had a well-filled purse than about who might be lacking in matters of faith. Thanks to this diligence, he happened upon a simple, good-natured man, far richer in coin than in wit, who, out of no lack of faith, but speaking thoughtlessly and perhaps intoxicated by wine or excess of merriment, happened one day to say to a group of his friends that he had a wine so good that Christ himself might drink it. This was reported to the inquisitor, and understanding that the man's means were ample and his purse well-filled, he rushed in haste, with swords and clubs, to bring a serious case against him, not seeking to correct any misbelief in the defendant, but to fill his own hand with gold coins (as indeed he did). Summoning the man, he asked if what had been alleged against him was true.

The good man replied that it was, and explained how it had happened. Whereupon the most holy inquisitor, who was a lover of money, said, 'Then have you made Christ a drunkard and connoisseur of fine wines, as if he were some notorious drinker or tavern-haunter? And now you speak lightly, as if this were a trivial matter! It is not as you think; you have deserved the flames for it, if we were inclined to deal with you as we ought.' With these and many other words, he addressed the man with as threatening a countenance as if the poor wretch had been Epicurus denying the immortality of the soul, and in short so terrified him that the good, simple soul, through certain intermediaries, greased his palm with a good dose of the ointment of St. John Goldenmouth (which is a sovereign remedy for the pestilential greed of the clergy, especially the Franciscans, who dare not touch money), so that he might deal mercifully with him.

This ointment, being of great virtue (although Galen speaks not of it in any part of his Medicines), worked to such effect that the fire denounced against him was by favor commuted into the wearing, by way of penance, of a cross, and to make the finer banner, as if he were to go on a crusade beyond the seas, the inquisitor imposed it upon him in yellow on black. Moreover, after he had gotten the money, he detained the man in his company for some days, ordering him, by way of penance, to hear mass every morning at Santa Croce and present himself before him at dinnertime, and after that he might do what most pleased him the rest of the day; all of which the man diligently performed.

One morning, among others, it happened that at the mass he heard a gospel, in which these words were sung: 'For every one ye shall receive an hundred and shall possess eternal life.' This he laid fast in his memory, and according to the commandment given him, presented himself at the eating hour before the inquisitor, whom he found at dinner. The friar asked him if he had heard mass that morning, to which he promptly answered, 'Yes, sir.' The inquisitor said, 'Didst thou hear anything therein whereof thou doubtest or wouldst question?' 'Certainly,' replied the good man, 'I doubt not of anything that I heard, but firmly believe all to be true. I did indeed hear something that caused and still causes me to have the greatest compassion for you and your brother friars, considering the wretched state in which you will find yourselves in the next life.' 'And what was it that moved thee to such compassion for us?' asked the inquisitor. 'Sir,' answered the other, 'it was that verse of the Gospel, which says, "For every one ye shall receive an hundred." 'That is true,' rejoined the inquisitor; 'but why did these words move thee thus?' 'Sir,' replied the good man, 'I will tell you. Since I have been accustomed to come here, I have seen given out to a multitude of poor folk every day now one and now two vast great cauldrons of broth, which had been taken away from before yourself and the other brethren of this convent, as being in excess; wherefore, if for each one of these cauldrons of broth there be rendered you an hundred in the world to come, you will have so much thereof that you will assuredly all be drowned therein.'

All who were at the inquisitor's table fell to laughing; but the latter, feeling the hit at the gluttonous hypocrisy of himself and his brethren, was greatly incensed, and but that he had already earned blame for what he had done, he would have burdened the man with another prosecution, for that with a humorous speech he had rebuked him and his good-for-nothing companions. Therefore, out of spite, he told the man to henceforth do as he pleased and not come before him again.
