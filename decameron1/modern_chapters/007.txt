THE FIFTH STORY 

[Day the First]

THE MARCHIONESS OF MONFERRATO, WITH A DINNER OF HENS AND CERTAIN LIVELY WORDS, CURBS THE EXCESSIVE PASSION OF THE KING OF FRANCE

The story told by Dioneo initially caused some embarrassment among the listening ladies, evident in the modest reddening of their faces. But soon after, glancing at one another and barely able to contain their laughter, they listened, chuckling to themselves. When the story had ended, they gently scolded Dioneo, making him understand that such tales were not suitable to be told among ladies. The queen then turned to Fiammetta, who sat next to him on the grass, and instructed her to continue the sequence.

Fiammetta began gracefully and with a cheerful countenance, "It has occurred to me, dear ladies, that we should not only demonstrate through stories the great power of prompt and eloquent responses, but also consider how it is wise for women to avoid being captivated by the love of men of higher rank than themselves, just as it is for men to seek the love of ladies of nobler lineage. Therefore, I will tell you a story of how a noble lady protected herself and diverted another from this.

The Marquis of Monferrato, a man of great worth and the standard-bearer of the church, had traveled overseas for a general crusade undertaken by the Christians. One day, his merits were discussed at the court of King Philip the One-Eyed, who was also preparing to depart France for the same crusade. A gentleman present declared that there was no couple under the stars to match the marquis and his lady, for as he was renowned among knights for every virtue, so she was the fairest and noblest of all ladies in the world. These words took such hold on the king's mind that, without having seen the marchioness, he suddenly fell ardently in love with her. Determined to go to the crusade not by any other route than through Genoa, he planned to make an honorable visit to the marchioness, convinced that, with her husband absent, he could fulfill his desire.

As he had conceived, so he put his plan into action. Having sent his troops ahead, he set out accompanied by only a few gentlemen. Upon arriving within a day's journey of the marquis's domains, he sent a messenger to inform the lady that he would dine with her the following morning. The marchioness, who was well-advised and discreet, replied cheerfully that this would be a great honor, and that she would welcome him. She then considered what the meaning of the king's visit might be, and was not mistaken in concluding that the report of her beauty had drawn him there. Nevertheless, like a brave lady, she decided to receive him with honor. Summoning several gentlemen who remained there, she made arrangements for everything necessary. The planning of the meal and the dishes, however, she reserved solely for herself.

The king arrived at the appointed time and was received by the lady with great honor and rejoicing. When he beheld her, she seemed to him even fairer, nobler, and more gracious than he had conceived from the courtier's words, which filled him with great admiration. He commended her greatly, and his desire grew even hotter as he found the lady exceeding his previous impression of her. After he had rested somewhat in chambers adorned to the utmost for the entertainment of such a king, the dinner hour arrived, and the king and the marchioness seated themselves at the same table, while the others were honorably entertained at separate tables according to their rank. The king, served with many successive dishes of the finest and most costly wines, and delighting in the sight of the lovely marchioness, was greatly pleased with the hospitality.

However, after a while, as the courses continued, he began to marvel somewhat, noticing that all the dishes were made from nothing but hens, despite the abundance of game in the surrounding area, which he had anticipated the lady would have had hunted in preparation for his arrival. Nonetheless, he chose not to engage her in discussion about this, other than to remark playfully, "Madam, are hens the only fowl bred in these parts, with no cocks to be found?" The marchioness, who understood the king's question very well, sensing that God had provided her with an opportune occasion to express her mind, turned to him and boldly replied, "No, my lord, but women, though they may differ somewhat in apparel and rank from others, are nevertheless of the same fashion here as elsewhere."

The king, hearing this, fully comprehended the meaning of the hen-based banquet and the virtue hidden in her speech. He realized that words would be wasted on such a lady and that violence was out of the question. Just as he had rashly become enamored with her, he now wisely stifled his ill-conceived passion for the sake of his own honor. Without further discussion with her, for fear of her replies, he dined, his hopes dashed. At the end of the meal, he thanked her for the honorable entertainment and, commending her to God, departed for Genoa, thus making amends for his inappropriate visit through his prompt departure.
