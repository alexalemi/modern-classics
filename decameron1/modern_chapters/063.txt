THE FIFTH STORY

[Day the Sixth]

MASTER FORESE DA RABATTA AND PAINTER GIOTTO
RETURNING FROM MUGELLO, EACH TEASES THE OTHER ABOUT
THEIR UNIMPRESSIVE APPEARANCES

After Neifile finished speaking and the ladies had greatly enjoyed Chichibio's clever response, Pamfilo, at the queen's request, spoke as follows:

"Dear ladies, it often happens that, just as fortune sometimes hides great treasures of worth and virtue under humble conditions, as Pampinea has shown us, so too can marvellous wits be found hidden within the most unappealing of human forms. This was clearly demonstrated in two of our fellow citizens, about whom I wish to tell you briefly. 

One of them was called Master Forese da Rabatta. Though small in stature and misshapen, with a flat, homely face that would have been an eyesore even on the ugliest beggar in Florence, he was nonetheless so excellent in his interpretation of the law that he was regarded by many learned men as a veritable treasury of civil jurisprudence. The other, whose name was Giotto, had such an exceptional talent that there was nothing in all of nature, that endless source and mover of all things under the heavens, that he could not depict with his pencil, pen, and brush so accurately that it seemed not merely similar, but the very thing itself. So convincing were his works that people's eyes were often deceived, taking the painted images for real. By reviving this art, which had lain buried for many years under the errors of certain painters more intent on dazzling the ignorant than pleasing the discerning, Giotto deserves to be counted among Florence's greatest glories. This is all the more true since he bore the honours he had gained with the utmost humility, refusing to be called 'master' - a title greedily usurped by those less skilled than he or by his own disciples, and which shone all the brighter for his rejection of it.

Yet despite Giotto's exceptional talents, he was no more handsome or better-favoured in appearance than Master Forese. But to come to my story:

I should tell you that Master Forese and Giotto each had a country estate in Mugello. When Master Forese had been to visit his property there, during the court's summer recess, he was returning to Florence mounted on a sorry old nag. Along the way, he happened to encounter the aforementioned Giotto, who had also been to Mugello on some business and was likewise making his way back to the city, no better equipped in horse or attire. The two men joined company and continued on their way at a leisurely pace, like the old men they were. 

Eventually, as often happens in summer, a sudden shower caught them out. Seeking shelter, they went to the home of a farmer, a mutual friend of theirs. After a time, the rain showing no signs of letting up and wishing to reach Florence before nightfall, they borrowed two old homespun cloaks and hats, badly worn with age, from their host, since there was nothing better available, and set off again.

As they rode along, drenched and splattered with mud from their horses' hooves - hardly a sight to add to one's dignity - the weather began to clear. The two men, who had been silent for some time, then began conversing. As Messer Forese listened to the eloquent Giotto, he examined his companion from head to toe and, seeing him so poorly attired and in such a sorry state, could not help bursting out laughing. Without a thought for his own wretched appearance, he said to Giotto, 'Tell me, Giotto, if a stranger who had never seen you before happened upon us now, do you think he would believe you to be, as you are, the finest painter in the world?'

'Indeed, sir,' Giotto replied without hesitation, 'I think he might well believe it, just as he would surely believe, seeing you, that you had never learned your ABC.' Hearing this, Master Forese realized his error and saw that he had been paid back in kind."
