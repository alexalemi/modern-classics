THE TENTH STORY

[Day the Second]

PAGANINO OF MONACO KIDNAPS THE WIFE OF MESSER RICCIARDO DI CHINZICA, WHO LATER GOES TO MONACO TO DEMAND HER RETURN. THOUGH RICCIARDO PLEADS WITH HIS WIFE TO COME BACK, SHE REFUSES, PREFERRING TO REMAIN WITH PAGANINO AS HIS WIFE. WHEN RICCIARDO DIES, PAGANINO MARRIES THE LADY.

After the queen's tale was highly praised by the entire company, especially by Dioneo, who now had the duty of telling the next story, he said, "Dear ladies, the queen's story has made me change my mind about the tale I had planned to tell you. Instead, I will recount the foolishness of Bernabo and all others who believe that their wives remain faithful while they are away. In doing so, I shall demonstrate the great folly of these men and the even greater folly of those who, thinking themselves more powerful than nature, believe they can control what is beyond their means through clever tricks.

There was in Pisa a judge named Messer Ricciardo di Chinzica, who, though more gifted with wit than physical strength, sought a young and beautiful wife, thinking he could satisfy her as he did his studies. He married Bartolomea, one of the fairest young ladies in Pisa, but on their wedding night, he was scarcely able to complete the act. Thereafter, Ricciardo taught his wife a calendar filled with saints' days, fasts, and other religious observances when they should abstain from lovemaking, keeping her under close watch to ensure she did not learn "working days."

One day, while Ricciardo and his wife were out fishing, the pirate Paganino da Mare spotted their boats and seized Bartolomea, carrying her off to Monaco. When Ricciardo discovered where his wife was, he went to Monaco, hoping to ransom her back. However, when Bartolomea saw him, she did not acknowledge him as her husband, telling Paganino that she had never met Ricciardo before. She then explained to Ricciardo that while she was with him, he had shown little understanding of her needs as a young and lively woman. But with Paganino, she said, there were no such restrictions, and she had no intention of returning to Ricciardo.

Messer Ricciardo, seeing his efforts were in vain, returned to Pisa, where he fell into a state of distress and madness, wandering the streets muttering, "The ill hole will have no holidays." He died soon after, and Paganino, knowing of the lady's affection for him, made her his lawful wife. They then lived a joyful life together, without observing any saints' days or Lenten fasts.
