THE FOURTH STORY

[Day the Ninth]

CECCO FORTARRIGO GAMBLES AWAY ALL HIS MASTER CECCO ANGIOLIERI'S MONEY AND POSSESSIONS; THEN, CHASING AFTER HIM IN HIS SHIRT, CLAIMING ANGIOLIERI HAS ROBBED HIM, HE CAUSES THE COUNTRY FOLK TO APPREHEND ANGIOLIERI; AFTERWARDS, DONNING ANGIOLIERI'S CLOTHES AND RIDING HIS HORSE, HE ESCAPES, LEAVING ANGIOLIERI BEHIND IN HIS SHIRT

Calandrino's foolish tale had been met with much laughter from the whole company; then, after Filostrato fell silent, Neifile, as the queen desired, began, "Noble ladies, it would be no easier for men to show their wit and worth to others than it is for them to display their folly and vice, and many would tire themselves in vain trying to bridle their tongues; this has been made quite clear to you by Calandrino's foolishness, who had no need, in seeking to be cured of the ailment in which his simplicity caused him to believe, to reveal the private affairs of his wife. This has brought to my mind something quite the opposite, namely, a story of how one man's deception got the better of another's wit, to the grievous harm and confusion of the deceived, which I am pleased to relate to you.

Not many years ago, there were in Siena two grown men, each named Cecco. One was the son of Messer Angiolieri and the other the son of Messer Fortarrigo, and although they differed in most other ways, they were nonetheless united in one particular, which was that they were both hated by their fathers, and this had caused them to become friends and frequent companions. After a time, Angiolieri, who was a handsome and well-mannered man, deciding that he could not live adequately in Siena on the allowance provided by his father, and hearing that a certain cardinal, a great patron of his, had come to the Marches of Ancona as the Pope's Legate, determined to go and visit him, thinking thus to improve his circumstances. Accordingly, informing his father of his intention, he arranged to receive at once what his father was to give him in six months, so that he might clothe and horse himself and make an honorable appearance. As he went in search of someone he might take with him as a servant, the matter came to Fortarrigo's knowledge, whereupon he promptly went to Angiolieri and begged him, as best he knew how, to take him along, offering to be his lackey and servant without any wage beyond his expenses paid. Angiolieri answered that he would not take him, not because he did not know him to be quite capable of every kind of service, but because he was a gambler and sometimes a drunkard as well. But the other replied that he would certainly refrain from both of these faults and affirmed it to him with many oaths, adding so many pleas that Angiolieri was persuaded and said that he was content.

Accordingly, they both set out one morning and went to dine at Buonconvento, where, after the meal, the heat being great, Angiolieri had a bed prepared at the inn and, undressing with Fortarrigo's help, went to sleep, charging the latter to wake him at the hour of none. As soon as his master was asleep, Fortarrigo went to the tavern and there, after drinking for a while, he fell to gambling with certain men, who in a trice won from him the money he had and then the very clothes he had on his back; whereupon, desiring to recoup his losses, he returned, in his shirt as he was, to Angiolieri's chamber and, seeing him fast asleep, took from his purse what money he had and, going back to the gaming, lost these as he had lost the others. Presently, Angiolieri awoke and, arising, dressed himself and inquired for Fortarrigo. The latter was nowhere to be found, and Angiolieri, concluding him to be asleep, drunk, somewhere, as was often his wont, decided to leave him be and get himself another servant at Corsignano. Accordingly, he had his saddle and valise put on a horse he had and, thinking to pay the reckoning so he might be on his way, found himself without a penny; whereupon there was great outcry, and all the inn was in an uproar, Angiolieri declaring that he had been robbed there and threatening to have the host and all his household carried prisoners to Siena.

At this moment Fortarrigo came in, in his shirt, thinking to take his master's clothes, as he had taken his money, and seeing the latter ready to mount, said, 'What's this, Angiolieri? Must we be off so soon? Good heavens, wait a bit; there'll be someone here directly who has my doublet in pawn for thirty-eight shillings; and I'm sure he'll let us have it for thirty-five, cash in hand.' As he spoke, someone came who informed Angiolieri that it was Fortarrigo who had robbed him of his money, showing him the sum that the latter had lost at play; wherefore he was greatly incensed and heaped reproaches upon Fortarrigo; and had he not feared others more than he feared God, he would have done him harm; then, threatening to have him hanged or banished from Siena, he mounted his horse. Fortarrigo, as if he were not speaking to him but to another, said, 'Good heavens, Angiolieri, let's not discuss this useless talk for now, and consider this: by redeeming it right away, we can get it back for thirty-five shillings; whereas, if we wait till tomorrow, he won't take less than the thirty-eight he lent me on it; and he's doing me this favor because I staked it on his advice. Really, why not save those three shillings?'

Angiolieri, hearing him speak thus, lost all patience (more so because he saw himself being looked at askance by the bystanders, who clearly believed, not that Fortarrigo had gambled away his money, but that he still had Fortarrigo's money in hand) and said to him, 'What do I care about your doublet? May you be hanged, since not only have you robbed me and gambled away my money, but you're also hindering me in my journey, and now you mock me.' However, Fortarrigo still persisted, as if he were not speaking to him, and said, 'For heaven's sake, why won't you let me have those three shillings? Don't you think I'll be able to return the favor another time? Please, do it, if you have any regard for me. Why all this haste? We'll still reach Torrenieri in good time this evening. Come, find the purse; you know I might search all of Siena and not find a doublet that suits me as well as this; and to think I should let that fellow have it for thirty-eight shillings! It's worth at least forty shillings or more, so you'd be doing me a double wrong.'

Angiolieri, beyond measure exasperated to see himself first robbed and now held in parley after this fashion, made him no further answer, but, turning his horse's head, took the road to Torrenieri, whilst Fortarrigo, devising a cunning trick, proceeded to trot after him in his shirt for about two miles, still demanding his doublet. Presently, Angiolieri riding swiftly to rid himself of the annoyance, Fortarrigo espied some farmhands in a field adjoining the highway ahead of him and cried out to them, saying, 'Stop him, stop him!' Accordingly, they ran up, some with spades and others with hoes, and placing themselves in the road before Angiolieri, concluding that he had robbed the one who came running after him in his shirt, they stopped and seized him. It availed him little to tell them who he was and how the matter stood, and Fortarrigo, coming up, said with an angry air, 'I don't know what's stopping me from killing you, disloyal thief that you were to make off with my belongings!' Then, turning to the farmhands, 'See, gentlemen,' quoth he, 'in what a state he left me at the inn, having first gambled away all his own! I may well say that by God and by you I've gotten back this much, and for that I shall still be in your debt.'

Angiolieri told them his own story, but his words were not heeded; nay, Fortarrigo, with the aid of the farmhands, pulled him off his horse and stripped him, then dressed himself in Angiolieri's clothes; and, mounting the horse, he left Angiolieri in his shirt and barefoot, and returned to Siena, proclaiming everywhere that he had won the horse and clothes from Angiolieri, whilst the latter, who had thought to go, as a wealthy man, to the cardinal in the Marches, returned to Buonconvento, poor and in his shirt, nor dared for shame go straight back to Siena, but, some clothes being lent him, he mounted the nag that Fortarrigo had ridden and betook himself to his kinfolk at Corsignano, with whom he remained until such time as he was re-outfitted by his father. In this manner, Fortarrigo's deception thwarted Angiolieri's good plan, although his villainy was not left unpunished by the latter in due time and place.
