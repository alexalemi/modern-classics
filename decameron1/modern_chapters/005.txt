THE THIRD STORY

[Day the First]

MELCHIZEDEK THE JEW, WITH A STORY OF THREE RINGS, ESCAPETH A
DANGEROUS TRAP SET FOR HIM BY SALADIN

After Neifile finished her story, which everyone praised, Filomena, with the queen's permission, spoke up: "Neifile's story reminds me of a dangerous situation that once befell a Jew. Since we've already discussed matters of God and the truth of our faith, I think it's appropriate to now turn to the deeds and experiences of mankind. You should know, my dear companions, that just as foolishness often leads people from a happy state into the greatest misery, so too can good sense extract the wise from the gravest perils and place them in safety and tranquility. The many examples of how foolishness brings people from a fine condition into misery are all around us every day, and there's no need to recount them now. But I will briefly show you, as I promised, how good sense can be a source of comfort, through a little story.

Saladin - whose valor was such that it raised him from a man of little account to the Sultan of Babylon, and gained him many victories over both Saracen and Christian kings - had spent all his treasure on diverse wars and extraordinary acts of generosity. When he found himself in urgent need of a large sum of money and could not see where to obtain it as quickly as was required, he remembered a wealthy Jewish moneylender named Melchizedek who lived in Alexandria and knew he had the means to assist him. But Melchizedek was so miserly that he would never have done so willingly, and Saladin was reluctant to use force on him. Necessity therefore compelled Saladin to devise a way to make the Jew serve him in this matter.

Accordingly, Saladin summoned Melchizedek and, receiving him warmly, seated him by his side. Then he said, "Honest man, I have heard from many people that you are a very learned individual, deeply versed in matters of divinity. I would therefore like to know from you which of the three faiths - the Jewish, the Saracen, or the Christian - you believe to be the true one."

The Jew, who was indeed a man of learning and understanding, easily perceived that Saladin was trying to trap him in his words, so that he might find cause to quarrel with him. He therefore sharpened his wits, as one who felt the need for a clever answer that would not put him at a disadvantage. The following soon occurred to him as the proper response:

"My lord, the question you pose is a delicate one, and to inform you of my thoughts on the matter, I must tell you a little story, which you shall hear. If I am not mistaken, I have often heard tell that there was once a great and wealthy man who, among his other very precious jewels, had a magnificent and costly ring. Wishing to honor this ring for its worth and beauty, and to leave it as an heirloom to his descendants, he declared that whichever of his sons should be found in possession of the ring at his death, by his bequest to that son, would be recognized as his heir and held in honor and reverence by the others as their chief and head. The son to whom the ring was left followed the same course with his own descendants, and so did all who came after him. In the end, the ring passed through many generations into the hands of a man who had three fine and virtuous sons, all of whom he loved equally. The young men, knowing the custom of the ring, each desiring to be the most honored among his people, begged their father, who was now an old man, to leave the ring to him upon his death. The worthy man, loving them all alike and unable to choose which one he preferred to succeed him, secretly had two other rings made, so like the first that he himself could scarcely tell them apart. When he came to die, he privately gave each of his sons his own ring. And so, after the father's death, each son, seeking to claim the inheritance and the honor, produced his ring as proof of his right. But since the three rings were found to be so alike that the true one could not be identified, the question of which son was the true heir remains unresolved and still pending.

"And so, my lord, I say to you of the three Faiths given by God the Father to the three peoples: each people deems itself to possess His true Law and commandments, just as with the rings, and the question of which one truly has them is still pending."

Saladin realized that the Jew had cleverly contrived to escape the trap he had set for him. He therefore decided to confess his needs to him and see if he would be willing to assist him, which he accordingly did. The Jew readily provided Saladin with all that he required, and the Sultan fully repaid him. Moreover, he gave the Jew great gifts and continued to hold him in high esteem and honor in his service.</modernized_text>
