THE FOURTH STORY

[Day the Seventh]

Tofano Locks His Wife Out of the House, but She Tricks Him and Returns Inside

The king wasted no time in turning to Lauretta, signaling her to begin her tale. Without hesitation, she spoke: "Oh, Love, how great and varied is your power! How many resources and devices you possess! What philosopher or scholar could ever hope to teach the stratagems, feints, and subterfuges that you instantly suggest to those who follow in your footsteps? Truly, all others' teachings pale in comparison to yours, as you shall see from the clever scheme I am about to recount, one practiced by a woman of modest wit, but which I know none but Love could have taught her.

There was once a wealthy man named Tofano who lived in Arezzo. He had a very beautiful wife, Madam Ghita, of whom he quickly grew jealous for no apparent reason. The lady, perceiving his jealousy, was vexed and repeatedly questioned him about the cause, but he could offer no justification beyond general suspicions. This led her to devise a plan to make him die of the very ailment he feared without reason.

Noticing that Tofano greatly enjoyed drinking, the lady began not only to encourage this habit, but would often craftily incite him to inebriation. This became such a habit that, whenever it suited her, she would put him to bed drunk and then secretly meet with her lover, which she did many times thereafter with complete freedom. Indeed, she grew so confident in her husband's drunkenness that she would sometimes bring her gallant into their home, or even spend a good part of the night at his house, which was not far away.

This went on until one evening, when the unfortunate husband, not having drunk that day, feigned extreme drunkenness in words and behavior. The lady, believing this, quickly put him to bed and then went, as was her custom, to visit her lover. As soon as she had left, Tofano arose, locked the doors from within, and stationed himself at the window to watch for her return and confront her.

When the lady came back and found herself locked out, she was deeply distressed and tried in vain to force the door open. After she had struggled for a while, Tofano called out, 'Wife, you are wasting your efforts - you cannot come back in here. Go back to where you have been until now, and be assured you shall not return until I have informed your relatives and neighbors of your behavior.'

The lady begged him, for the love of God, to open the door, claiming she had been keeping vigil with a neighbor, not where he suspected. But her brutish husband was determined to shame her publicly, so her pleas fell on deaf ears. Seeing that her entreaties were to no avail, the lady resorted to threats, saying, 'If you don't open the door, I will make you the most miserable man alive.'

'And what can you do to me?' Tofano asked. Quick-witted Mistress Tessa, with Love's counsel, replied, 'Rather than endure the shame you would wrongfully inflict, I will cast myself into the well nearby. When they find me dead, no one will believe anything other than that in your drunkenness, you pushed me in. You will then be forced to flee, losing all you have, or face execution as my murderer, which you will truly be.'

Tofano remained unmoved by these words, so the lady said, 'Listen, I can no longer tolerate your behavior. God forgive you! Place my distaff here by the well.' Then, in the dark of night, she picked up a large stone and dropped it into the water, crying out, 'God forgive me!'

Hearing the splash, Tofano truly believed she had thrown herself in. He rushed to the well with a bucket and rope, intending to save her. Meanwhile, the lady had hidden near the door and, as soon as she saw him run to the well, slipped back into the house and locked herself inside. Then, from the window, she shouted, 'You should water your wine when you drink it, not after and at night!'

Tofano, realizing he had been tricked, returned to the door but could not gain entry. The lady then abandoned her soft tone and began to shout, almost screaming, 'By Christ's Cross, you drunken fool, you shall not come in tonight! I can no longer tolerate your behavior, and I must let everyone see what kind of man you are and at what hour you come home.'

Tofano grew enraged and started yelling back. The neighbors, hearing the commotion, came to their windows and asked what was happening. The lady, weeping, told them that her wretched husband, who often returned home drunk in the late hours, had finally forced her to lock him out in order to shame him for his ways. Tofano, like the fool he was, told them the truth of the matter, but the neighbors sided with the lady, scolding Tofano.

The lady's relatives soon arrived, heard the neighbors' accounts, and then beat Tofano severely. They then took the lady's belongings and brought her back to their home, threatening Tofano with worse. Realizing the sorry state he was in and still loving his wife dearly, Tofano managed to make peace with her through the intercession of friends, promising never to be jealous again and giving her leave to do as she pleased, as long as he remained ignorant of it. In this foolish way, the churl made peace after suffering harm. Long live Love, and down with war and all its company!
