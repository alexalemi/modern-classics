THE EIGHTH STORY

[Day the Tenth]

SOPHRONIA, THINKING TO MARRY GISIPPUS, BECOMES THE WIFE OF TITUS QUINTIUS FULVUS AND GOES WITH HIM TO ROME, WHERE GISIPPUS COMES IN POOR CONDITION AND, THINKING TITUS HAS SLIGHTED HIM, DECLARES HE HAS KILLED A MAN SO HE MAY DIE. TITUS, RECOGNIZING HIM, CLAIMS TO HAVE COMMITTED THE CRIME TO SAVE HIM, AND THE TRUE MURDERER, SEEING THIS, REVEALS HIMSELF; WHEREUPON THEY ARE ALL THREE FREED BY OCTAVIANUS, AND TITUS GIVES GISIPPUS HIS SISTER IN MARRIAGE AND SHARES ALL HIS WEALTH WITH HIM.

After Pampinea had finished speaking and all had commended King Pedro, especially the Ghibelline lady, Fiammetta, at the king's command, began as follows: "Renowned ladies, who does not know that kings, when they so wish, can accomplish great deeds, and that it is especially required of them to be generous? Whoever, then, having the power, does what is expected of them, does well. But people should not marvel at this so much or exalt them with the highest praise as they would someone else, from whom less would be expected due to a lack of means. Therefore, if you praise the actions of kings with such words and they seem fair to you, I have no doubt that the deeds of our peers, when they are equal to or greater than those of kings, will please you even more and be praised even more highly by you. And so I intend to recount to you, in a story, the praiseworthy and magnanimous actions of two citizens and friends toward each other.

You must know, then, that at the time when Octavianus Caesar (not yet called Augustus) ruled the Roman Empire as part of the Triumvirate, there was in Rome a gentleman named Publius Quintius Fulvus, who, having a son of remarkable intelligence named Titus Quintius Fulvus, sent him to Athens to study philosophy and entrusted him as best he could to a nobleman there called Chremes, his very old friend, with whom Titus was lodged in his own house, along with a son of his named Gisippus, and set to study with the latter, under the guidance of a philosopher named Aristippus. The two young men, coming to associate with each other, found each other's habits so compatible that a brotherhood and a friendship so great arose between them that it was never severed by any event other than death, and neither of them knew happiness or peace except in being together. Applying themselves to their studies and each being equally endowed with the highest intelligence, they ascended with equal step and remarkable commendation to the glorious heights of philosophy; and they continued in this way of life for about three years, to the exceeding contentment of Chremes, who in a manner regarded the one as no less his son than the other. At the end of this time, it happened, as it happens to all things, that Chremes, now an old man, departed this life, at which the two young men suffered a like sorrow, as for a common father, nor could his friends and kinsmen discern which of the two was in greater need of consolation for what had befallen them.

Some months later, the friends and kinsmen of Gisippus came to him and, along with Titus, urged him to take a wife, to which he consenting, they found him a young Athenian lady of remarkable beauty and very noble parentage, whose name was Sophronia and who was about fifteen years old. As the time of the future nuptials drew near, Gisippus one day asked Titus to go visit her with him, for he had not yet seen her. Accordingly, they having come into her house and she seated between the two, Titus proceeded to consider her with the utmost attention, as if to judge of the beauty of his friend's bride, and every part of her pleasing him beyond measure, what while he inwardly commended her charms to the utmost, he fell, without showing any sign thereof, as passionately enamored of her as ever yet man of woman. After they had been with her awhile, they took their leave and returned home, where Titus, betaking himself alone into his chamber, fell a-thinking of the charming damsel and grew the more enkindled the more he enlarged upon her in thought; which, perceiving, he fell to saying in himself, after many ardent sighs, 'Alas, the wretchedness of thy life, Titus! Where and on what settest thou thy mind and thy love and thy hope? Knowest thou not that it behooveth thee, as well for the kindness received from Chremes and his family as for the entire friendship that is between thee and Gisippus, whose bride she is, to have yonder damsel in such respect as a sister?'...
