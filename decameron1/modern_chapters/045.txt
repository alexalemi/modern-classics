THE NINTH STORY

[Day the Fourth]

SIR GUILLAUME DE ROUSSILLON GIVES HIS WIFE THE HEART OF SIR GUILLAUME DE GUARDESTAING, WHOM HE HAS KILLED AND WHO WAS LOVED BY HIS WIFE; SHE, UPON REALIZING THIS, THROWS HERSELF FROM A HIGH WINDOW TO THE GROUND AND, DYING, IS BURIED WITH HER LOVER

After Neifile had finished her story, which had moved all the ladies to great sympathy, the king, who did not intend to deny Dioneo his privilege, since there were only the two of them left to tell a tale, began, "Dear ladies, since you have shown such compassion for ill-fated love affairs, it has occurred to me to tell you a story that will likewise deserve your pity, for the people involved were of higher rank than those we have heard about, and the misfortune that befell them was even more cruel.

You should know that, according to what the Provençals relate, there were once in Provence two noble knights, each of whom had castles and vassals under his command, called the one Sir Guillaume de Roussillon and the other Sir Guillaume de Guardestaing. And since they were both men of great valor in arms, they loved each other with an exceeding love and were accustomed to go always together and dressed in the same colors to every tournament or joust or other martial endeavor. Although they dwelled each in his own castle, which were about ten miles apart, it came to pass that, Sir Guillaume de Roussillon having a very beautiful and charming lady for a wife, Sir Guillaume de Guardestaing, notwithstanding the friendship and fellowship that was between them, became deeply enamored of her, and so contrived, now by one means and now by another, that the lady became aware of his passion. And knowing him to be a most valiant knight, it pleased her, and she began to return his love, so that she desired and cherished nothing more than him, nor awaited anything else than to be solicited by him; which did not take long in coming to pass, and they met together many times.

Loving each other exceedingly and conversing together less discreetly than they should have, it happened that the husband became aware of their familiarity and was greatly incensed thereat, insomuch that the great love he had borne toward Guardestaing was turned into mortal hatred; but this he knew better how to keep hidden than the two lovers had known to conceal their love, and he was fully resolved in himself to kill him. Roussillon being in this mind, it happened that a great tournament was proclaimed in France, which he immediately made known to Guardestaing and bade him come to him, if it pleased him, so they might take counsel together whether and how they should go thither; to which the other very joyfully answered that he would without fail come to sup with him on the following day. Roussillon, hearing this, thought the time had come when he might be able to kill him, and accordingly on the morrow he armed himself and, mounting to horse with a servant of his, lay in ambush, maybe a mile from his castle, in a wood where Guardestaing must pass.

There, after he had awaited him a good while, he saw him come, unarmed and followed by two servants in like case, as one who apprehends nothing from him; and when he saw him come where he would have him, he rushed out upon him, lance in hand, full of rage and malice, crying, 'Traitor, thou art dead!' And to say thus and to plunge the lance into his breast were one and the same thing. Guardestaing, without being able to make any defense or even to say a word, fell from his horse, transfixed by the lance, and a little after died, whilst his servants, without waiting to learn who had done this, turned their horses' heads and fled as quickly as they might, towards their lord's castle. Roussillon dismounted and, opening the dead man's breast with a knife, with his own hands tore out his heart, which he let wrap in the pennon of a lance and gave to one of his men to carry. Then, commanding that none should dare make words of the matter, he remounted, it being now night, and returned to his castle.

The lady, who had heard that Guardestaing was to be there that evening to supper and looked for him with the utmost impatience, seeing him not come, marveled greatly and said to her husband, 'How is it, sir, that Guardestaing is not come?' 'Wife,' answered he, 'I have had word from him that he cannot be here till to-morrow'; whereat the lady remained somewhat troubled. Roussillon then dismounted and, calling the cook, said to him, 'Take this wild boar's heart and look thou make a delicious dish thereof, the best and most delectable to eat that thou knowest, and when I am at table, send it to me in a silver porringer.' The cook accordingly took the heart and, putting all his art and all his diligence thereto, minced it and, seasoning it with a great quantity of rich spices, made of it a very delicious ragout.

When it was time, Sir Guillaume sat down to table with his wife, and the dishes were brought; but he ate little, being hindered in thought for the evil deed he had committed. Presently the cook sent him the ragout, which he caused to be set before the lady, feigning himself slightly indisposed that evening and commending the dish to her highly. The lady, who was by no means squeamish, tasted thereof and, finding it good, ate it all; which when the knight saw, he said to her, 'Wife, how dost thou find this dish?' 'In good truth, my lord,' answered she, 'it pleases me exceedingly.' Whereupon, 'So God be mine aid,' quoth Roussillon; 'I do indeed believe it you, nor do I marvel if that please you, dead, which, alive, pleased you more than aught else.' The lady, hearing this, hesitated a while, then said, 'How? What have you made me eat?' 'This that you have eaten,' answered the knight, 'was in very truth the heart of Sir Guillaume de Guardestaing, whom you, disloyal wife as you are, so loved; and know for certain that it is his very heart, for that I tore it from his breast with these hands a little before my return.'

It need not be asked if the lady were distressed, hearing this of him whom she loved more than aught else; and after a while she said, 'You have done the deed of a disloyal and base knight, as you are; for, if I, unenforced of him, made him lord of my love and therein offended against you, not he, but I should have borne the penalty thereof. But God forbid that ever other victual should follow upon such noble meat the heart of so valiant and so courteous a gentleman as was Sir Guillaume de Guardestaing!' Then, rising to her feet, without any manner of hesitation, she let herself fall backward through a window which was behind her and which was exceeding high above the ground; wherefore, as she fell, she was not only killed, but well-nigh broken in pieces.

Sir Guillaume, seeing this, was sore dismayed and thought he had done ill; wherefore, being afraid of the country people and of the Count of Provence, he had his horses saddled and made off. On the morrow it was known all over the country how the thing had passed; whereupon the two bodies were, with the utmost grief and lamentation, taken up by Guardestaing's people and those of the lady and laid in one same sepulchre in the chapel of the latter's own castle; and thereover were verses written, signifying who these were that were buried therewithin and the manner and occasion of their death.
