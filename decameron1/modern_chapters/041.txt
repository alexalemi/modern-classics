THE FIFTH STORY

[Day the Fourth]

LISABETTA'S BROTHERS KILL HER LOVER, WHO APPEARS TO HER IN A DREAM AND SHOWS HER WHERE HE IS BURIED. SHE SECRETLY DIGS UP HIS HEAD AND KEEPS IT IN A POT OF BASIL, MOURNING OVER IT DAILY, UNTIL HER BROTHERS TAKE IT FROM HER, AND SHE DIES OF GRIEF SOON AFTER.

After Elisa's tale ended and received some praise from the king, Filomena was asked to tell her story. With pity for the tragic fates of Gerbino and his lover, Filomena began with a sigh:

"My story, dear ladies, will not involve people of such high status as Elisa's, but it may be no less sorrowful. What reminded me of it was the earlier mention of Messina, where this event took place.

There were three young merchant brothers living in Messina, who had inherited great wealth from their father, a man from San Gimignano. They had an only sister named Lisabetta, a beautiful and well-mannered young woman whom, for some reason, they had not yet married. These brothers had a Pisan youth named Lorenzo working in one of their warehouses, managing all their affairs. Lorenzo was quite handsome and agreeable in manner, and Lisabetta, seeing him often, began to find him increasingly pleasing. Lorenzo, likewise, turned his attention to her, leaving his other romantic interests behind. It was not long before their mutual attraction blossomed into a secret love affair.

Their intimate relationship continued with great joy and delight, but they were unable to keep it fully hidden. One night, Lisabetta's eldest brother, a prudent young man, happened to witness her visiting Lorenzo's quarters. Though troubled by this discovery, he decided, out of honor, not to confront them immediately. Instead, he discussed the matter with his brothers and they agreed to remain silent about it for the time being, planning to deal with the situation discreetly when the opportunity arose without causing themselves or their sister any public shame.

Feigning a trip out of the city, the three brothers one day took Lorenzo with them to a remote and secluded location. There, when the moment was right, they caught him unaware and killed him, burying his body so that no one would know what they had done. When they returned to Messina, they claimed they had sent Lorenzo away on business, which was easily believed given their frequent use of him for such tasks.

Lisabetta often asked her brothers about Lorenzo's prolonged absence, growing increasingly distraught. One day, one of the brothers sharply warned her to stop questioning them about him, threatening her with an unspecified punishment. Fearful and grief-stricken, Lisabetta dared not inquire further, but at night she would mournfully call out Lorenzo's name and weep bitterly, awaiting his return.

One night, as Lisabetta wept herself to sleep, Lorenzo appeared to her in a dream. Pale and disheveled, he told her, "Lisabetta, you do nothing but call for me and grieve over my long delay, accusing me cruelly with your tears. Know that I can never return to you, for on the last day you saw me, your brothers killed me." He then revealed the location where they had buried him and instructed her not to call for him or expect him anymore, before vanishing. Lisabetta awoke, believing the vision to be true.

The next morning, without telling her brothers, Lisabetta decided to go to the place Lorenzo had shown her in the dream and see if it was real. Leaving the city on the pretense of going out for recreation, she went to the spot, accompanied by a servant who knew her affairs. There, clearing away the leaves, she began to dig where the earth seemed softer. She soon uncovered the body of her ill-fated lover, unchanged and not decayed, confirming the truth of her dream vision. Utterly distraught, yet knowing she could not lament there, Lisabetta carefully cut off Lorenzo's head and wrapped it in a cloth, placing it in her servant's lap. She then covered the body again and returned home, shutting herself in her room with the severed head.

Lisabetta wept over the head bitterly and at length, bathing it with her tears and kissing it all over. She then placed it in a large, beautiful flowerpot, wrapping it in a fine linen cloth and covering it with earth. In this she planted several sprigs of lovely Salerno basil, watering them only with her tears, rosewater, or orange-flower water. Lisabetta would then sit near the pot, gazing longingly at it as if it held her beloved Lorenzo, and weep profusely, the tears nourishing the basil, which grew lush and fragrant due to the decomposing head within.

Lisabetta's neighbors often observed this ritual and reported it to her brothers, marveling at her wasted appearance and weeping eyes. The brothers, having tried in vain to stop her, eventually had the pot secretly taken from her. Distraught at its loss, Lisabetta fell ill and continued to ask for the pot of basil until she died, her ill-fated love reaching its tragic end. After her death, the truth of the matter became known to many, inspiring a song that is still sung:

"Alas! Alas! Who can the cruel Christian be,
That stole my pot of basil away from me?"
