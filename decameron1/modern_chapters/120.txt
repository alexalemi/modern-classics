Gualtieri realized that his wife's demeanor had not changed at all, and he was certain that this was not due to a lack of understanding, as he knew her to be very intelligent. He felt he had now seen as much as he desired of his lady's patience, and he decided it was time to free her from the bitterness he was sure she had been hiding beneath her constant composure. So, calling her to him, he said to her with a smile, in front of everyone, "What do you think of our bride?"

"My lord," she answered, "I think very highly of her, and if, as I believe, she is as discreet as she is beautiful, I have no doubt that you will be the happiest man in the world with her. But I beseech you, as much as I can, not to inflict upon her the pains that you inflicted upon the one who was once yours, for I think she might scarcely be able to endure them, both because she is younger and because she has been delicately raised, whereas the other had been in continual hardships from a very young age."

Thereupon, Gualtieri, seeing that she firmly believed the young lady was to be his wife and therefore spoke only well of her, seated her by his side and said to her, "Griselda, the time has now come for you to reap the fruits of your long patience, and for those who have considered me cruel, unjust, and brutish to know that what I have done, I have done with a purpose in mind - to teach you to be a wife, to show them how to take and use one, and to gain for myself the perpetual peace I desired in living with you. When I decided to take a wife, I was greatly afraid this might not come to pass, and therefore, to test it, I probed and afflicted you as you know. And it seems to me that, since I have never seen you depart from my wishes in word or deed, I have gained from you the solace I desired. Therefore, I now intend to restore to you at once what I took from you in many parts, and to reward you with the greatest joy for the pains I have inflicted upon you. Therefore, with a joyful heart, take this woman whom you believed to be my bride, and her brother, as your children and mine; for these are they whom you and many others have long believed I had barbarously had killed. And I am your husband, who loves you above all else, and I can boast that there is no one else who can be as content with his wife as I can."

So saying, he embraced and kissed her; then, rising up, he went with Griselda, who wept for joy, while the daughter, hearing these things, sat all bewildered, and he tenderly embraced her and her brother, revealing the truth to her and many others who were there. Then the ladies arose from the table, overjoyed, and withdrew with Griselda into a chamber, where, with happier omens, they removed her plain attire and clothed her anew in a magnificent dress of her own, bringing her again to the hall as a true gentlewoman, which indeed she appeared, even in rags. There she rejoiced in her children with immense joy, and all being overjoyed at this happy outcome, they redoubled their feasting and merriment, prolonging the festivities for several days, considering Gualtieri a very wise man, albeit they held the trials he had made of his lady to be too harsh, indeed intolerable; but above all, they considered Griselda most wise.
