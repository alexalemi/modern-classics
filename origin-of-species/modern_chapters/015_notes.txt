KEY CONCEPTS ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:
- Classification reflects genealogy (descent), not an unknown plan of creation
- The "Natural System" = genealogical arrangement
- Traits useful for classification are those inherited from common ancestors
- Physiological importance of an organ =/= classificatory importance
- Combination of traits more valuable than any single trait
- Analogical resemblances (convergent evolution) vs. true homology
- Bates's mimicry explained by natural selection (rare species mimicking protected ones)
- Morphology: same bones in different animals (homology) explained by shared ancestry
- Serial homology: repeated parts in the same individual (vertebrae, segments, leaf-derived
  flower parts) explained by ancestral repetition + subsequent modification
- Lankester's homogenous/homoplastic distinction
- Embryology: embryos resemble each other more than adults do
- Two principles: (1) variations appear at not very early age, (2) inherited at corresponding age
- These explain why embryos are similar, why adults diverge, why larvae may differ from adults
- Embryo as "picture" of the ancestor
- Rudimentary organs: useless structures explained by inheritance from ancestors who used them
- Disuse + natural selection lead to reduction
- Rudimentary organs larger in embryo (inheritance at corresponding ages)
- Difficulty of explaining final stages of organ reduction (Darwin honest about this gap)
- Silent letters analogy for rudimentary organs
- All of these converging lines of evidence support descent with modification
