KEY CONCEPTS ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:
- Instinct is analogous to habit; can be inherited; varies in nature
- Complex instincts explained by gradual accumulation of small beneficial variations
- Cuckoo parasitism: gradation from American cuckoo to European cuckoo; Molothrus
  gradation series (M. badius -> M. bonariensis -> M. pecoris) shows stages
- Slave-making ants: gradation from F. sanguinea (partial dependency) to
  F. rufescens (complete dependency); possible origin from pupae stored as food
- Honeybee cells: gradation from bumblebee (irregular) to Melipona (spherical
  with flat intersections) to honeybee (perfect hexagonal); explained by simple
  instincts of equal-sphere excavation at set distances
- Family-level selection (kin selection precursor): selection acting on the family
  rather than the individual explains sterile insect castes
- Sterile insect castes: the "most serious special difficulty" for the theory;
  resolved by family selection + graduated variation within nests
- Anti-Lamarckian argument: sterile workers' specialized habits can't be inherited
  through use/habit since they don't reproduce; proves natural selection not habit
