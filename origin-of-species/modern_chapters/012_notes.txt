KEY CONCEPTS ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:
- New species appear gradually, not simultaneously; different groups change at different rates
- Once a species disappears, it never reappears (explained by divergent inheritance)
- Groups follow same rules as species: gradual rise, peak, gradual decline, continuous existence
- Extinction and production of new forms are intimately connected under natural selection
- Rarity precedes extinction (the "sickness before death" analogy)
- Competition is strongest between the most similar forms
- Life forms change roughly simultaneously worldwide because dominant species spread and vary
- Extinct forms bridge gaps between living groups (extensive paleontological examples)
- The more ancient a form, the more it tends to connect now-distinct groups
- Organization has generally (but not inevitably) progressed over geological time
- Same types succeed each other in the same geographical areas ("law of succession of types")
- All major patterns in paleontology are explained by descent with modification
